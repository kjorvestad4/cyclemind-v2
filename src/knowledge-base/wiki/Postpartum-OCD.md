# Postpartum OCD & Perinatal Intrusive Thoughts

> **Related:** [[Postpartum-Depression]] · [[Postpartum-Psychosis]] · [[Pregnancy-Anxiety-Disorders]] · [[Trauma-Reproductive-Health]]

---

## Overview
Perinatal OCD is **significantly underdiagnosed** and frequently misidentified as postpartum depression or, more dangerously, confused with [[Postpartum-Psychosis]]. Correct identification is critical because treatment differs substantially.

---

## Prevalence
- OCD affects **up to 4%** of pregnant and postpartum women
- New-onset or significant worsening common in **third trimester and early postpartum**
- Affects both mothers AND fathers

---

## Intrusive Thoughts — Normal vs. OCD

### Normal Intrusive Thoughts (>90% of new parents experience)
- Fleeting, unwanted images of accidentally dropping the baby
- Brief scary "what if" thoughts
- **Quickly dismissed**; do not cause sustained distress or behavioral change
- No compulsions

### Perinatal OCD
- Intrusive thoughts are **frequent, recurring, and ego-dystonic** (unwanted; person knows they are wrong)
- Cause **intense guilt, shame, and distress**
- Lead to **compulsive behaviors** (avoidance of infant, excessive checking, repeated reassurance-seeking)
- Significantly interfere with daily functioning and bonding

---

## Common OCD Presentations in Perinatal Period

### Harm Obsessions
- Fear of **intentionally or accidentally harming the baby**
- Fear of using knives, stairs, bathing near water
- **Key:** Ego-dystonic — person does NOT want to act on these thoughts; deeply distressing

### Contamination Obsessions
- Fear of passing illness or germs to the infant
- Excessive hand-washing, avoidance of others, over-sterilizing

### "Bad mother" Obsessions
- Intrusive doubts about being a good enough parent
- Compulsive checking on the baby; seeking constant reassurance

### Religious/Moral Obsessions
- Blasphemous or morally unacceptable intrusive thoughts

---

## Critical Distinction: OCD vs. Postpartum Psychosis

| Feature | Perinatal OCD | Postpartum Psychosis |
|---|---|---|
| Insight | **Intact** — knows thoughts are wrong/irrational | **Impaired** — may believe delusions are real |
| Thoughts | Ego-dystonic; deeply unwanted | Ego-syntonic delusions/commands |
| Response to thoughts | Distress, shame, avoidance | May act on delusional beliefs |
| Risk to infant | Very low (ego-dystonic) | **Elevated risk** — requires immediate action |
| Treatment | CBT/ERP ± SSRI | Emergency hospitalization |

---

## Treatment

### First-Line: CBT with Exposure & Response Prevention (ERP)
- **Most effective treatment** for OCD; effective in perinatal period
- **ERP:** Gradually confront feared situations (exposure) without performing compulsions (response prevention)
- Requires trained OCD specialist
- Online/telehealth delivery effective

### Pharmacologic: SSRIs
- Used when CBT/ERP is insufficient or not accessible, or for severe symptoms
- **Sertraline** preferred (best perinatal safety data)
- Fluoxetine also effective
- Higher doses often needed for OCD than depression
- Response takes 8–12 weeks; trials of ≥12 weeks recommended before switching

### Combined Therapy
- CBT/ERP + SSRI superior to either alone for moderate-severe OCD

---

## Safety Assessment
- Despite content of intrusive thoughts, **perinatal OCD does NOT increase risk of harm to infant**
- However, **always rule out** postpartum psychosis (see table above)
- Screen for suicidal ideation — OCD causes significant suffering

---

## Luna Clinical Boundaries
> Luna can normalize that intrusive thoughts are common and do not mean the user will act on them. Luna directs users with distressing intrusive thoughts to seek professional evaluation (not self-diagnosis). Luna never interprets intrusive thoughts as safe or unsafe — that requires clinical assessment.

---

## Psychoeducation Script for Luna
*"Many new parents experience scary, unwanted thoughts — it's more common than you might think and doesn't mean you're a bad parent or that you'll act on them. If these thoughts are very distressing or are changing how you care for your baby, please speak with a mental health professional who specializes in perinatal OCD. Treatment works really well."*
