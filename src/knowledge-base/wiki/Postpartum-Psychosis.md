# Postpartum Psychosis

> ⚠️ **PSYCHIATRIC EMERGENCY** — requires immediate evaluation and hospitalization
> 
> **Related:** [[Bipolar-Disorder-Reproductive-Context]] · [[Postpartum-Depression]] · [[Postpartum-OCD]] · [[Schizophrenia-Reproductive-Context]]

---

## Overview
Postpartum psychosis (PP) is a rare but **life-threatening psychiatric emergency** occurring in the early postpartum period. It is most commonly an episode of **bipolar disorder** precipitated by parturition.

---

## Epidemiology
- Incidence: **1–2 per 1,000 births**
- Peak onset: **first 10 days** postpartum; >90% within first 4 weeks
- Untreated, carries significant risk of:
  - Maternal suicide (~5%)
  - Infanticide (rare but documented)
  - Chronic psychiatric morbidity

---

## Risk Factors

### Highest Risk
- Personal history of **bipolar disorder** (~20–30% lifetime risk of PP)
- **Previous postpartum psychosis** (~50–80% recurrence risk)
- Family history of bipolar disorder or PP
- First pregnancy

### Moderate Risk
- Schizophrenia — see [[Schizophrenia-Reproductive-Context]]
- Sleep deprivation (may be precipitant)

---

## Clinical Presentation — Prodrome
The earliest sign is often **severe insomnia** not explained by infant care demands:
- Racing thoughts; elated or irritable mood
- Marked anxiety, agitation
- Unusual behavior or confusion

## Full Presentation
- **Delusions** — often infant-related (e.g., baby is possessed, must be harmed to be saved)
- **Hallucinations** (auditory and/or visual)
- **Disorganized thinking and speech**
- Rapid mood cycling (mania → depression within hours)
- Confusion, disorientation
- Labile affect

---

## Differential Diagnosis
| Condition | Key Differentiator |
|---|---|
| Bipolar I with psychosis | Most common diagnosis at follow-up |
| Schizophrenia | Prior history; insidious onset |
| Severe PPD with psychosis | Depressive rather than manic/mixed features |
| Delirium | Underlying medical cause; fluctuating consciousness |
| Autoimmune encephalitis | Anti-NMDA-R antibodies; fever, seizures |
| Postpartum thyroiditis | TSH abnormal |

---

## Emergency Management

### Immediate Steps
1. **Hospitalization** — ideally mother-baby unit if available
2. Ensure **infant safety** — mother should not be alone with infant until stable
3. Involve family/support system
4. Rapid medical workup (TSH, CBC, BMP, urine drug screen, anti-NMDA-R if indicated)

### Pharmacologic Treatment

#### First-Line (Bipolar PP)
- **Lithium** (mood stabilizer) + **atypical antipsychotic** (olanzapine, quetiapine, risperidone)
- Benzodiazepines for acute agitation and sleep

#### Refractory Cases
- **ECT** — safe, rapidly effective; consider early in severe cases or when medication response inadequate

### Breastfeeding
- May need temporary interruption during acute stabilization
- Discuss with psychiatric team based on medication choice and stability

---

## Prophylaxis in Future Pregnancies
- Women with prior PP: **prophylactic lithium** immediately postpartum (or pre-delivery) reduces recurrence risk
- Requires close monitoring; advance planning with reproductive psychiatrist essential

---

## Recurrence Risk
- Prior PP in context of bipolar disorder: **50–80% recurrence** in future postpartum periods
- With prophylaxis: risk substantially reduced

## Luna Clinical Boundaries
> If Luna detects language suggesting confusion, unusual beliefs about the baby, or severe sleep disruption in the early postpartum period, it immediately surfaces crisis resources and urges emergency contact. Luna never attempts to assess or manage psychotic symptoms.

---

## Luna Response Template for Suspected PP
*"What you're describing sounds like it could be a medical emergency. Please call 911 or go to your nearest emergency room right now. If you can, call your OB or perinatal psychiatrist immediately. You and your baby's safety comes first."*
