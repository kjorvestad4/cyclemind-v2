# Perinatal Bipolar Disorder — Detailed Management

> **Related:** [[Bipolar-Disorder-Reproductive-Context]] · [[Postpartum-Psychosis]] · [[Breastfeeding-Psychotropics]] · [[Antidepressants-Pregnancy-Safety]] · [[Perinatal-Suicide-Safety]]

---

## Why Perinatal Bipolar Is a High-Stakes Condition
- Highest psychiatric relapse risk of any condition in the postpartum period
- Postpartum psychosis occurs in **25–50%** of women with bipolar I who discontinue mood stabilizers
- Suicide and infanticide risk are real — require proactive safety planning
- **Medication continuation is almost always the safer choice** despite fetal/infant exposure concerns

---

## Preconception Planning — Ideal Approach

### Goals
1. Identify the **optimal mood stabilizer** for pregnancy before conception
2. Transition off highest-risk agents (valproate) before conception
3. Establish psychiatric monitoring schedule for pregnancy
4. Discuss prophylactic lithium plan for postpartum
5. Create a postpartum safety plan with specific action steps

### Medication Hierarchy for Preconception Planning

| Agent | Status |
|---|---|
| **Lamotrigine** | Preferred for maintenance if mood profile allows |
| **Quetiapine** | Useful for both poles; lower teratogenicity |
| **Lithium** | Effective; requires careful management in pregnancy |
| **Valproate** | ❌ Change before conception if at all possible |
| **Carbamazepine** | Avoid — teratogenic; neural tube risk |

## Lithium in Pregnancy — Detailed Management

### Historical Concern (Ebstein's Anomaly)
- 1970s Scandinavian Register: highly elevated risk of Ebstein's anomaly (tricuspid valve malformation)
- **Modern re-analysis:** Original study was severely biased; absolute risk is very small
- Current estimate: **~1–2 per 1,000** lithium-exposed pregnancies have Ebstein's vs. ~1 per 20,000 in general population
- Risk is real but small; must be weighed against massive relapse risk of discontinuation

### Management Protocol
1. **Fetal echocardiogram** at 16 weeks (if continuing lithium)
2. Level 2 ultrasound at 18–20 weeks
3. **Serum lithium levels** every 4 weeks in pregnancy → every 2 weeks in third trimester
4. **Dose adjustments:** Renal clearance increases ~30–50% in pregnancy → may need dose increase
5. **Peripartum:** Creatinine and lithium level drop rapidly with delivery + fluid shifts → TOXICITY RISK
   - Many experts reduce dose by 25% at onset of labor or rupture of membranes
   - Hold lithium during labor; restart at pre-pregnancy dose immediately postpartum
   - Monitor lithium levels days 1, 3, 5 postpartum

### Signs of Lithium Toxicity
- Tremor worsening, coarse tremor
- GI: nausea, diarrhea, vomiting
- Neurological: ataxia, confusion, slurred speech
- Severe: seizures, arrhythmia, coma
- **Therapeutic range in pregnancy:** 0.6–1.0 mEq/L (some lower targets acceptable)

## Lamotrigine in Pregnancy

### Pharmacokinetic Changes
- Renal clearance of lamotrigine **doubles or triples** in pregnancy (glucuronidation increases)
- Serum levels may drop by 50–65% → **relapse risk if dose not increased**
- **Monitor levels monthly** during pregnancy; adjust dose to maintain pre-pregnancy serum level
- Postpartum: clearance normalizes rapidly → may need dose reduction in days to weeks after delivery to prevent toxicity

### Safety Profile
- **Teratogenicity:** No consistent major malformation signal in large registries
- Historical cleft palate concern: not replicated in North American registries
- **Neural tube defects:** Not associated (unlike valproate)
- **Rash risk:** Advise any rash to be reported immediately (Stevens-Johnson syndrome risk)

### Dosing Strategy
- Check level at baseline (preconception or early pregnancy)
- Increase dose proactively at 2nd trimester onset
- Recheck every 4 weeks
- Target: maintain serum level at patient's known therapeutic level

## Valproate in Pregnancy — Why to Avoid

### Teratogenicity ❌
- **Major malformation rate:** 9–10% (vs. 2–3% background)
- **Neural tube defects (NTD):** 1–2% (vs. 0.1% background)
- **Valproate embryopathy:** Facial dysmorphology, limb defects, cognitive impairment
- **Fetal valproate syndrome:** NTDs, spina bifida, autism spectrum, intellectual disability

### Cognitive Outcomes
- Lower IQ in children exposed in utero (dose-dependent)
- Elevated autism spectrum disorder risk (odds ratio ~3)
- Higher rates of ADHD and learning disabilities

### Current Guidance
- **First-line in epilepsy and bipolar during reproductive years:** Strongly avoid; use alternatives
- If already stable on valproate: careful discussion; if must continue, **high-dose folic acid (4mg/day)**; detailed surveillance
- **Regulatory:** FDA Black Box; REMS program in USA

## Quetiapine in Pregnancy
- Increasingly used as mood stabilizer in pregnancy (especially lower doses)
- Good tolerability; dual manic/depressive pole coverage
- Gestational diabetes risk: elevated; screen early and repeat
- Weight gain: significant; monitor
- **No consistent teratogenic signal** in large registries
- Neonatal effects: jitteriness, sedation — monitor

---

## Postpartum Bipolar Management — The Critical Window

### Why Postpartum = Highest Risk
- Abrupt estrogen/progesterone crash → HPA dysregulation
- Sleep deprivation: powerful mood destabilizer in bipolar
- Psychosocial demands of new parenthood
- Medication often inappropriately reduced during pregnancy → low blood levels at highest-risk time

### Prophylaxis Protocol
- **Start (or restart) lithium immediately postpartum** if:
  - Bipolar I history
  - Prior postpartum psychosis
  - Stopped lithium during pregnancy
- Sleep protection: essential; partners/support people must take night feeds
- Psychiatric monitoring: weekly in first 4 postpartum weeks

### Postpartum Mania — Early Recognition
- Reduced sleep need (feeling energized despite little sleep)
- Racing thoughts, pressured speech
- Grandiosity; unusual plans or decisions
- Irritability or euphoria
- **Any of these → contact psychiatrist same day**

---

## Safety Planning for Bipolar Postpartum
1. **Psychiatric emergency contact:** Available 24/7 during first 4 weeks
2. **Sleep protection plan:** Written schedule of who handles each night feed
3. **Warning sign list:** Patient identifies her personal early warning signs
4. **Named support person:** Who checks in daily; authorized to call psychiatrist if concerned
5. **Infant safety plan:** If acutely psychotic or manic → who cares for baby
