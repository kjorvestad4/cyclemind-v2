# Menarche & Adolescent Reproductive Mental Health

> **Related:** [[PMDD-Overview]] · [[Cycle-Tracking-Mental-Health]] · [[Contraception-Mental-Health]]

---

## Overview

The onset of puberty and first menstruation (menarche) marks a critical neurobiological transition. The **gap in depression prevalence between boys and girls emerges at puberty** — before puberty, rates are roughly equal; after, women have 2× the rate of depression. This gap directly implicates reproductive hormones in mood vulnerability.

---

## Pubertal Timing and Mental Health

| Timing | Mental Health Impact |
|---|---|
| **Early menarche** (<11 years) | Higher risk of depression, anxiety, eating disorders; social maturity mismatch |
| **On-time menarche** (11–14 years) | Baseline risk |
| **Late menarche** (>15 years) | Social anxiety; possible endocrine investigation warranted |

### Why Early Menarche Increases Risk
- Physical maturity precedes emotional/social maturity
- Early objectification and sexualization by peers
- Early entry into "adult" expectations and experiences
- Brain still developing — emotional regulation not fully mature until ~age 25

## Adolescent PMDD

PMDD can and does occur in adolescents. It is often missed because:
- Mood symptoms are attributed to "being a teenager"
- DRSP tracking is not offered to teens
- Parents and providers normalize significant cyclical distress

### Key Differences in Adolescent PMDD
- Cycles may be irregular in early years post-menarche (first 1–2 years)
- Diagnosis requires same DRSP criteria as adults — but cycle irregularity complicates timing
- SSRIs are first-line (same as adults) — approved for adolescents
- Drospirenone OCP (Yaz) approved for PMDD from age 14+

### Red Flags in Adolescent Cyclical Symptoms
- Missing school during the luteal phase
- Cyclical self-harm, suicidal ideation, or aggression
- Cyclical disordered eating
- Relationship crises that resolve with menstruation

## Adolescent-Specific Assessment Tools

| Tool | Use |
|---|---|
| **DRSP** | Gold standard — can be used from menarche onward |
| **PHQ-A** | Adolescent version of PHQ-9 — includes school/activity domain |
| **SCARED** | Screen for child anxiety-related emotional disorders |
| **CRAFFT** | Substance use (often co-occurring with mood disorders) |
| **CAMS-2** | Collaborative Assessment and Management of Suicidality (adolescent version) |

## Communication and Consent Considerations

### Developmentally Appropriate Language
- Use concrete examples: "Do you feel really different the week before your period compared to the week after?"
- Normalize cyclical mood: "Many people notice their mood changes at certain times of their cycle — have you noticed that?"
- Involve the adolescent as the expert on their own experience

### Confidentiality
- HIPAA allows adolescents to have confidential reproductive health care in most US states
- Parental involvement: involve when needed for safety; otherwise defer to adolescent
- Document clearly what was shared with parents vs kept confidential

## First Menstrual Experience

Menarche can be a positive, neutral, or traumatic experience depending on:
- Preparation and education beforehand
- Privacy and dignity in the moment (especially if at school)
- Cultural/family attitudes toward menstruation
- Experience of dysmenorrhea (menstrual pain)

**Dysmenorrhea (Menstrual Pain):**
- Affects up to 70% of adolescents
- Often undertreated — dismissed as "normal"
- Severe dysmenorrhea: consider endometriosis (may present early)
- NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen) are first-line: most effective when started the day before expected onset
- Hormonal suppression (OCP) for refractory pain

---

## School Impact

Cyclical symptoms significantly impact academic performance:
- Concentration difficulties in the luteal phase = lower grades, learning gaps
- School avoidance during heavy flow or severe pain
- Impact on standardized testing if test day falls in symptomatic phase

**Practical accommodations:**
- Flexible bathroom access
- Extra absences policy for menstrual symptoms
- Option to reschedule high-stakes tests

---

## Luna's Role with Adolescent Users

Luna adjusts its communication for younger users:
- More concrete, less clinical language
- More emphasis on normalizing and validating
- Stronger emphasis on talking to a trusted adult or school counselor
- Parental safeguarding: if a user appears to be a minor in crisis, immediate escalation to 988 and encouragement to involve a trusted adult
