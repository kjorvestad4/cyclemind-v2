# Menarche & Adolescent Reproductive Mental Health

> **Related:** [[PMDD]] · [[Premenstrual-Disorders-Spectrum]] · [[Contraception-Mental-Health]] · [[Eating-Disorders-Perinatal]] · [[Reproductive-Psychiatry-Overview]]

## Menarche as a Neuropsychiatric Transition
- Menarche (average age 12.4 years in USA) initiates the **HPG axis cycling** that continues for ~40 years
- The 2× female:male depression ratio **emerges at puberty** — not before
- Puberty represents a window of vulnerability AND plasticity for mental health

---

## Normal Pubertal Mood Changes
- Emotional lability: normal; related to estrogen-progesterone cycling
- Heightened social sensitivity: peers become paramount; rejection exquisitely painful
- Increased HPA reactivity: heightened cortisol response to social stress vs. pre-puberty
- Sleep: pubertal circadian shift → later sleep phase (not laziness — biology)

## PMDD in Adolescents

### Considerations
- Must have established regular cycles (typically 2+ years post-menarche)
- Under-diagnosed — symptoms attributed to "teenage moodiness"
- **Prevalence:** ~3–5% of post-menarcheal adolescents
- Higher rates of comorbid anxiety, depression, ADHD

### Assessment
- Prospective DRSP charting essential — even more important than in adults (baseline mood more variable)
- Rule out: major depression, bipolar disorder, ADHD, substance use, trauma

### Treatment Considerations
- CBT adapted for adolescents is first-line
- Exercise: highly effective; leverage sports/school PE
- Combined OCP: effective; consider drospirenone-containing formulations
- SSRIs: effective; parental involvement in consent; FDA black box warning (increased suicidality in adolescents — monitor closely)

---

## Dysmenorrhea — Often Undertreated in Adolescents
- Affects **~75–85%** of adolescent girls
- Most common cause of school absenteeism in adolescent females
- **Under-treated:** Dismissed as "normal period pain"
- Treatment: NSAIDs (naproxen, ibuprofen); hormonal contraception; heat therapy
- Persistent severe dysmenorrhea: screen for endometriosis (often diagnosed late — average 7–10 years from symptom onset to diagnosis)

---

## Endometriosis in Adolescents
- Estimated **~10%** of adolescent girls with chronic pelvic pain have endometriosis
- Presentation different from adults: cyclic pain often primary feature; classic chocolate cysts uncommon
- Diagnosis often delayed because:
  - Symptoms attributed to "normal periods"
  - Reluctance to perform laparoscopy in young patients
  - Superficial lesions missed on ultrasound
- **Management:** Combined OCP (first-line); progestin-only; GnRH agonist (add-back essential due to bone density impact in adolescents)

---

## Eating Disorders in Adolescence
- Peak onset: **14–18 years**
- Anorexia nervosa: highest mortality of any psychiatric disorder (cardiac complications, suicide)
- Puberty-related body changes: major trigger for eating disorder onset
- **Hypothalamic amenorrhea:** Common in restrictive eating → estrogen deficiency → bone loss → irreversible if prolonged
- Menstrual restoration requires sufficient caloric intake and weight restoration — not OCP (OCPs mask hypothalamic amenorrhea and falsely reassure but don't restore bone)

---

## Self-Harm in Adolescence
- **Non-suicidal self-injury (NSSI):** Cutting, burning — peaks in adolescence, particularly girls
- Premenstrual exacerbation of NSSI: common; pain tolerance changes across cycle
- Premenstrual worsening may drive escalation of cutting behavior
- Assess cycle-phase timing when evaluating NSSI patterns

---

## OCP in Adolescents — Specific Considerations
- **Mood effects:** More pronounced in adolescents (Skovlund: HR 1.80 for antidepressant use)
- Screen for depression before prescribing
- Assess at 3-month follow-up specifically for mood changes
- Drospirenone-containing formulations: best evidence for PMDD/mood management
- **Non-contraceptive indications in adolescents:**
  - Dysmenorrhea
  - Endometriosis
  - Heavy menstrual bleeding
  - PMDD/premenstrual symptoms

---

## Contraception Counseling in Adolescents
- **All LARC methods** are appropriate for adolescents (ACOG/AAP position)
- IUD does not require prior pregnancy
- Hormonal implant: highly effective; addresses compliance challenges
- Counsel on STI prevention (OCP does NOT protect against STIs)
- Confidentiality: know your state's laws; most states allow minors to consent to contraception

---

## Supporting Menstrual Literacy in Adolescents
- Many adolescents have minimal accurate knowledge about their own menstrual cycle
- CycleMind's teen-appropriate education content reinforces cycle literacy
- Key education points:
  - Normal cycle length: 21–45 days in first 2 years post-menarche (wide range)
  - Heavy bleeding definition: soaking pad/tampon in <2 hours; passing clots >quarter size
  - Severe pain is NOT normal — seek evaluation
  - Cycle tracking helps identify patterns and supports health conversations with providers
