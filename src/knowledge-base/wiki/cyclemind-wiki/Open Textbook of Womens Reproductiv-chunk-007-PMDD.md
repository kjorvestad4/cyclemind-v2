Key Points 
• Women’s reproductive years begin with menarche, when reproductive hor -
mones initiate the menstrual cycle and the development of primary and sec-
ondary sexual characteristics. 
• The first half of the menstrual cycle is the follicular phase (days 1–14), when 
follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH) spur the de-
velopment of a dominant follicle, and estrogen and progesterone levels rise 
gradually. 
• Ovulation occurs at approximately day 14 and is followed by the luteal phase, 
when the uterine lining is prepared for implantation and the dominant follicle 
develops into the progesterone-producing corpus luteum. 
• When pregnancy does not occur, the uterine lining is shed, and the next cycle 
begins with menses. 
• The enormous changes of pregnancy are not confined to the reproductive 
system; they include changes in other endocrine systems as well as the cen-
tral nervous, cardiovascular, hematological, pulmonary, gastrointestinal, gen-
itourinary, and immune systems. 
• There are four phases to parturition: quiescence (most of pregnancy), activa-
tion (beginning of contractions), stimulation (active labor), and involution (re-
turn of the uterus to pre-pregnancy size). 
• The three stages of active labor are cervical dilation, delivery of the fetus, and 
delivery of the placenta. 
• Changes in breast anatomy and physiology in pregnancy prepare for lacta -
tion, which is regulated by prolactin and oxytocin. 
• Menopause, which represents the end of a woman’s reproductive life, is in-
duced by changes in reproductive hormones, with a decrease in estrogen and 
progesterone and an increase in LH and FSH.

and Mental Health 
Brittney Bastow, M.D., M.Sc. 
Pooja Lakshmin, M.D. 
Health care guidelines from the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecolo-
gists (ACOG) recommend an annual well-woman visit. This visit presents an excellent 
opportunity to screen, evaluate, and counsel women about their current health, as well 
as to provide anticipatory guidance for up coming life changes, including contracep-
tion, preconception counseling, menopause, and so forth. During these visits, gynecol-
ogists and primary care providers often diagnose other gynecological conditions, such 
as chronic pelvic pain (CPP), endometriosis, polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS), or 
sexual dysfunction (genito-pelvic pain/penetration disorder [GPPPD]). Gynecological 
care is aimed at treating the physical symptoms that women experience; however, sig-
nificant comorbidity is known to exist between gynecological issues and psychiatric 
disorders. Patients with such comorbidities are typically referred to a psychiatrist or 
psychotherapist for further workup and treatment of their mental health conditions. 
Mental health professionals working with women with gynecological conditions can 
benefit from having a basic understanding of these medical conditions. This chapter 
includes sections on four common classes of gynecological cond itions. Each section 
offers a brief overview of epidemiology, clinical features, pathophysiology, and treat-
ment options, with a focus on gynecological basics and treatment from a psychiatric lens.

Impression 
Ms. A has vulvodynia with high-tone pelvic floor muscle dysfunction. Her pain treat -
ment plan included pelvic floor PT with biofeedback and dry needling techniques, as 
well as pharmacological intervention with amitriptyline to treat the neuropathic com-
ponent of her vulvodynia. She was also refe rred to a psychiatrist  for evaluation and 
treatment of her generalized anxiety and de pressive symptoms. Her psychiatrist pre-
scribed duloxetine for treatment of her anxiety disorder and referred Ms. A to a psycho-
therapist who specialized in chronic medical conditions. With her therapist, she learned 
tools from cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT), guided imagery, and mindfulness to re-
duce her anxiety and pain symptoms. She was encouraged to engage in aerobic exercise, 
but this was difficult in the setting of her pain. 
Outcome 
Ms. A has continued to work with her gynecological pain doctor, physical therapist, and 
psychiatry team. Her pain has improved, and she is now able to sit more comfortably 
and was able to travel for a golf vacation. As her anxiety improved, she and her partner 
became motivated to start couples therapy, where they learned communication skills 
training. Ms. A says that their relationship has improved, and she feels more confident 
in communicating with her partner about her pain and mental health. Through CBT and 
mindfulness, she has much bett er awareness about her pain and her triggers. She re -
ports an improvement in her pain to a consistent 2/10 rating from her previous rating 
of at least 4/10 with exacerbations of 8/10. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
In 2006, the World Health Organization recognized CPP as the most common reason 
for referral to a women’s health specialist, representing 20% of all referrals (Latthe et 
al. 2006a, 2006b). Prevalence data are limi ted due to the multifactorial and multidi -
mensional characteristics of CPP , as well as limitations in pelvic pain research. Most 
sources put the prevalence around 15%, which would make it as common as asthma 
in the general population (Ahangari 2014; Allaire et al. 2018).

Risk Factors 
Reviewing patient history for risk factors can improve diagnosis of CPP . An important 
risk factor for the development of both pain and psychiatric distress related to pain is 
a history of physical or sexual abuse. An estimated 45%–65% of patients experiencing 
pelvic pain have such a history (As-Sanie et  al. 2014). It is important to clarify two 
points: 1) that a history of abuse is not necessary for the diagnosis of CPP and 2) that 
a history of abuse does not mean that the abuse survivor will automatically develop 
CPP . Instead, there is merely an association between abuse and CPP , with several the-
ories of how abuse could lead to pain. One theory is that chronic psychological stress 
from abuse can dampen immune response, which can predispose the patient to gyne-
cological disorders, including pain (Schliep et al. 2016). Another theory involves 
chronic sensitization of the limbic system by chronic pain stimuli. Sensitization of the 
limbic system has been shown to correlate with higher depression scores in women 
with pain, and the depression in turn increases the morbidity from chronic pain and 
leads to more pain-related disability (As-Sa nie et al. 2014). An a dditional risk factor 
to consider is genetics, although twin studies have shown only a 14% correlation be-
tween twins with CPP (Vehof et al. 2014). 
Screening Methods 
Screening during routine care visits can help providers care for patients with CPP. The 
first step is to ask patients about sympto ms of pain with menses, urination, bowel 
movements, intercourse, and so on. Pain scales and questionnaires can be useful ad -
juncts to the clinician interview and can allow the clinician to assess severity, effect of 
functioning, and type of pain (nociceptive vs. neuropathic). A visual analog scale can 
be a good first step in assessing severity of perceived pain; ask the patient to record her 
highest level of pain, her lowest level, and the level experienced on the day of evalua-
tion. Multidimensional pain scales, such as the McGill Pain Questionnaire (Melzack 
1975), can help also to assess the nature and location of pain, and neuropathic-specific 
questionnaires can further clarify the nature of the patient’s pain. Several question-
naires are designed specifically for pelvic pain, some focusing on urological complaints 
and others focusing on a wider variety of complaints. The Pelvic Pain and Urgency/ 
Frequency Patient Symptom Scale (Parsons et al. 2002) is widely used to assess pain, 
urinary symptoms of frequency and urgenc y, and sexual dysfun ction. The Interna -
tional Pelvic Pain Society (IPPS; www.pelvicpain.org) has developed an integrated Pel-
vic Pain Assessment Form that is specific to women and includes all of the above types 
of pain assessments along with relevant clinical history (Passavanti et al. 2017). 
Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
CPP is defined as noncyclic pain in the pelvis present for >6 months with pain suffi -
cient to interfere with normal activities and quality of life (QoL) (Ahangari 2014). The 
Royal College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists defines CPP as “intermittent or con-
stant pain in the lower abdomen or pelvis of at least 6 months duration, not occurring 
exclusively with menstruation or interc ourse and not associated with pregnancy” 
(Ahangari 2014, p. 2). The IPPS developed a publicly available form on their website 
(www.pelvicpain.org) that can help patients and providers synthesize pertinent infor-

Clinical Presentation 
Presentation of symptoms can be variable because CPP is multifactorial, with contri-
butions from structures that are not only gynecological but also urological, gastroin-
testinal, musculoskeletal, and psychological. CPP is an overlapping pain syndrome 
and can be thought of as a complex neuromuscular-psychosocial disorder (Speer et 
al. 2016). Patients often report other somatic pain syndromes, such as irritable bowel 
syndrome and chronic fatigue syndrome, in addition to mental health disorders such 
as PTSD and depression (Speer et al. 2016) . Patients with CPP report higher rates of 
depression than do their pain-free counterparts, which can also lead to functional im-
pairments with sleep, household activities, absences from work, and difficulties with 
sexual relationships, among others (Miller-Matero et al. 2016). 
Course and Prognosis 
The course and prognosis of CPP are also variable and dependent on underlying eti-
ologies. For patients with primarily gynecological etiologies, pain can persist even af-
ter standard gynecological management of underlying conditions (Allaire et al. 2018). 
A multidisciplinary approach can improve patient outcomes, but even in that setting 
the prognosis can be variable. Factors associated with higher pain severity even after 
starting a multidisciplinary treatment prot ocol include greater pain catastrophizing 
(P=0.02), abdominal wall pain (P=0.02), history of sexual assault (P=0.04), total number 
of comorbidities (P=0.02), and patients discharged from a pain clinic who require re-
referral to return to treatment (P=0.008) (Allaire et al. 2018). 
Differential Diagnosis 
The differential diagnosis of CPP is broad, with patients often reporting pain in mul-
tiple organ systems. For example, in a study of patients with interstitial cystitis, 93% 
had concurrent pelvic pain, 70% had dyspareunia, 60% had vulvodynia, 52% had con-
stipation, 49% had irritable bowel syndrome, and 17% had fibromyalgia (Hoffman 
2011). Clearly, the diagnosis of CPP requires a thorough approach with consideration 
of the entire person and not merely the location of the reported pain. 
Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
The multifactorial nature of CPP means that  it has various causes. For patients with 
underlying gynecological conditions, an inciting event can lead to pain. For example, 
a hemorrhagic ovarian cyst can lead to pain with hemoperitoneum. Similarly, patients 
experiencing pregnancy and delivery can have acute pain after delivery that persists 
into CPP . The process of acute pain transitioning to chronic pain is related to several

lead to efferent stimulation, allowing inflammatory mediators to be released into vis-
cera. In viscerovisceral convergence, nerves from painful viscera converge on the dorsal 
horn with nerves from nonpainful viscera, which can lead to pain separate from the 
organ of origin. A good example of this type of convergence is endometriosis, in 
which pain from endometrial implants in the pelvis or on the ovaries can lead to blad-
der voiding dysfunction and pain. In viscerosomatic convergence, noxious afferent sig-
nals from a visceral pain source converge with somatic afferents in the spinal cord, 
which leads to areas of the body that are seemingly unconnected but share a der -
matome for experiencing pain. For exam ple, noxious stimuli from dysmenorrhea 
(painful periods) converge with somatic afferents and lead to abdominal wall pain 
and hyperalgesia. Finally, in somatovisceral convergence, noxious afferents from muscle 
and somatic structures converge with visceral afferents in the spinal cord and lead to 
pain in an end organ. This is seen when pelvic floor dysfunction and muscle hyper -
tonicity lead to constipati on with painful bowel movements or incomplete bladder 
emptying and pain (Hoffman 2011). 
The plasticity of the CNS can also lead to worsening pain and pain that is distant 
from the initial nidus. Central sensitization causes expanded areas of pain beyond the 
original noxious stimuli due to long-term changes in perception from repeated stimuli 
of pain (Hoffman 2011). When nociceptive stimuli enter the spinal column at the dorsal 
horn, stimuli can spread to segments above, below, and contralateral to the site of ori-
gin. The persistent aberrant stimuli lead to a maladaptive response in the CNS, which 
causes hyperalgesia, allodynia, and pain remote from the site of origin (Hoffman 2011). 
Concurrent to the abnormal spread of the painful stimuli, central sensitization can 
cause de novo pain without any noxious stimuli. Studies have shown that these central 
changes with chronic pain are not specific to any specific pain etiology but are common 
to all disease states that cause chronic pain (Hoffman 2011; Morotti et al. 2017). 
Psychological Factors 
Patients with CPP have been found to have higher rates of depression and anxiety than 
those without CPP , with rates as high as 40% and 73%, respectively (Miller-Matero et 
al. 2016; Walker et al. 1988). The psycholo gical profile of patients with CPP includes 
high levels of psychological distress and related increased pain severity (Gomide et al. 
2012). There is also a positive correlation between depression symptoms and pain in-
terference, pain catastrophizing, and limiting self-efficacy beliefs (Bryant et al. 2016). 
Pain catastrophizing is associated with higher pain levels and decreased QoL (Sewell 
et al. 2018). One theory for the presence of depressive symptoms in patients with CPP 
is dysregulation of the hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axis. Patients with CPP 
who also have depression have demonstrated enhanced cortisol response to adreno-
corticotropic hormone stimulation testing (Wingenfeld et al. 2009). 
Although sexual abuse itself is unlikely to be a specific etiological factor in the 
pathogenesis of CPP , the literature sh ows an association between CPP and sexual 
abuse in some subset groups (Fry et al. 1993; Randolph and Reddy 2006). Studies have 
found that patients with CPP who also experienced childhood sexual abuse have 
higher scores on psychological distress, somatization, and dissociation measures.

Patients who have CPP experience consider able deficits in social and professional

When considering social support factors,  qualitative research has found the most

lack of empathy. Patients reported that tangible support was most helpful when com-

Treatment 
Psychopharmacology 
Pharmacological treatment of patients with CPP is targeted toward minimizing pain 
symptomatology and treating associated mood and anxiety disorders. In general, psy-
chopharmacological agents that increase the availability of serotonin and norepineph-
rine benefit patients who experience chronic pain and depression, whereas agents that 
increase the availability of norepinephrine alone benefit patients who have CPP with-
out depressive symptoms (Atkinson et al. 1998). 
Tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs), which elevate levels of norepinephrine and sero-
tonin by inhibiting reuptake, are some of the most common medications used to treat 
chronic pain conditions, including CPP . Although few empirical studies have looked 
at the efficacy of TCAs for CPP , the efficacy of TCAs as analgesics is well established 
for chronic pain in general (Lynch 2001). Clinically, pain tends to respond more 
quickly to TCAs than do mood symptoms and requires lower doses than needed for 
treatment of mood or anxiety disorders (Carey et al. 2017; Guay 2001). These medica-
tions have considerable side effects, ho wever, including sedation, dizziness, blurry 
vision, dry mouth, and other anticholinergic effects. Side effects are typically worse 
with tertiary amines, such as amitriptyline and imipramine, leading to poor adher -

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Endometriosis is a chronic gynecological condition in which endometrial glands and 
stroma are found in aberrant locations outside the uterus. The cyclic stimulation of 
these implants with the normal menstrual cycle results in release of inflammatory me-
diators and can result in worsening pain. Women with endometriosis represent 7%– 
15% of the general population, about 200 million women worldwide, and upward of 
one-third of women with CPP (Howard 2009; Morotti et al. 2017). Of women under -
going pelvic laparoscopy for pelvic pain, at least 33% will ultimately be diagnosed 
with endometriosis (Howard 2009). The ACOG  reports that 38% of infertile women 
are ultimately diagnosed with endometriosis (American College of Obstetricians and 
Gynecologists 2010).

family history of endometriosis (Mowers et al. 2016). In fact, monozygotic twin stud-

Most women with endometriosis present with pelvic pain or infertility, and thus,

Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
Endometriosis is diagnosed by surgical confirmation of ectopic growths of endome -
trial glands and stroma (Howard 2009). Neither serum markers nor imaging studies 
are able to replace surgical evaluation. En dometriosis implants have been described 
as “powder-burn” black, red, or white lesions (American College of Obstetricians and 
Gynecologists 2010). At the time  of surgery , histological evaluation with excision of 
endometrial implants is the preferred way to confirm diagnosis. Findings consistent 
with endometriosis as visualized by surgeo ns are often used to make the diagnosis; 
however, due to the variable patterns of intraoperative presen tation, research has 
shown a discrepancy between visual diagnosis and histological diagnosis (American 
College of Obstetricians an d Gynecologists 2010). Imaging studies are not sufficient 
for diagnosis but can be useful if any adnexal or pelvic masses are present, and in this 
setting, transvaginal ultrasound is the imaging modality of choice. 
Clinical Presentation 
Several theories have been formed about the etiology of the ectopic endometrial 
growths, including retrograde menstruation, hematogenous or lymphatic spread, or 
stem cells from bone marrow (American Co llege of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 
2010). No matter the underlying etiology of the ectopic endometrium, endometriosis 
is characterized as a chronic condition. Although most patients report pain and some 
have difficulty conceiving, a proportion of  patients with endometriosis do not have 
any pain or fertility manifestations. Their disease is typically diagnosed incidentally 
at the time of surgery for a different indication, such as a tubal ligation. Endometriosis 
is much more commonly diagnosed in women presenting with “classic symptoms,” 
including abdominal and pelvic pain (OR 5.2), dysmenorrhea (OR 8.1), menorrhagia 
or heavy periods (OR 4.0), an d dyspareunia (OR 6.0) (American College of Obstetri-
cians and Gynecologists 2010; Howard 2009). Dysmenorrhea in this case is pain start-
ing prior to menses, typically around ovulation, and continuing through the period.

Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
The principal manifestations of endometriosis include pain and infertility. As dis -
cussed by Howard (2009) and Morotti et al. (2017), endometriosis-related pelvic pain 
is established through well-understood mechanisms: inflammatory, nociceptive, and 
neuropathic. Inflammatory medi ators (tumor necrosis factor-  , cytokines, interleu -
kin-1 , interleukin-6) and nerve growth factor are increased in the peritoneal fluid in 
women with endometriosis. The inflammatory markers in the peritoneal fluid lower 
the threshold of sensory nerves and lead to de novo pain generation. Nerve growth 
factor causes neuroangiogenesis at the sites of ectopic endometriosis implants with 
increased nerve growth. Noxious stimuli trav el through these sensory nerves to the 
dorsal root ganglion, where they converge with visceral nerves and carry nociceptive 
signals to the brain. Once these nerves are damaged or dysfunctional, they cause neu-
ropathic pain with sensitization in the CNS (Howard 2009; Morotti et al. 2017). At this 
point in the chronic pain cycle, pain gene ration can be independent of ongoing pe -
ripheral stimuli. 
The mechanisms whereby endometriosis lead s to infertility are less well under -
stood, but the increased inflammatory milieu of the peritoneum is a likely contributor 
to both sperm and oocyte dysfunction (American College of Obstetricians and Gyne-
cologists 2010). 
Psychological Factors 
Women with endometriosis have a higher risk of developing major depression and 
anxiety disorders. It is unclear, however, whether that risk exists because of the CPP 
associated with endometriosis or is etiologically related to endometriosis or another 
factor, such as chronic inflammation (Chen et al. 2016; Pope et al. 2015). Women with 
endometriosis-related pelvic pain have higher rates of depression and poorer QoL in 
comparison with those with endometriosis and no chronic pain (Facchin et al. 2015; 
Lorençatto et al. 2006). Additionally, high levels of depressive or anxiety symptoms 
can increase pain severity, and higher levels of pain intensity have been associated 
with increased anxiety (Laganà et al. 2017; Sepulcri and do Amaral 2009). In compar-
ison with healthy control subjects, wome n living with endometriosis demonstrate 
higher levels of somatization, phobic anxi ety, depression, and emotional sensitivity 
and lower QoL scores (Laganà et al. 2015) . An association between endometriosis 
stage and psychiatric symptoms has not been found (Sepulcri and do Amaral 2009).

Social Considerations 
Women with endometriosis consistently score lower on QoL ratings than women with-
out the condition and may have more difficulty in interpersonal relationships than 
women with unexplained CPP (Laganà et al. 2015; Peveler et al. 1996; Sepulcri and do 
Amaral 2009). Extended treatment duration is associated with lower QoL scores (Sep-
ulcri and do Amaral 2009). 
Women living with endometriosis report a decreased capacity to maintain intimate 
relationships and report that their chronic pain condition leads to feeling isolated 
from friends and family. Focus group data from women living with endometriosis 
suggest that social isolation is a pervasive problem for patients partially because 
women perceive their relatives and friend s as lacking understanding about the con -
dition and the impairments it can cause. Women who experienced social isolation also 
felt resignation in the face of chronic pain and poor understanding about their symp-
toms (Mellado et al. 2016). Avoidance of intimate relationships and isolation from 
family and friends were both aspects of overall social isolation.

though dyspareunia is a major reported sexual symptom in patients with endometrio-
sis, it is not the only factor implicated in sexual dysfunction. Women with endometriosis 
report being afraid of pain before or during sexual intercourse, and when pain with sex 
is present, women have fewer episodes of intercourse per month and report greater fear 
that their partner will separate from them be cause of their coital pain (Fritzer et al.

Treatment 
The goals of treatment for endometriosis ar e multifactorial, meaning that treatment 
should include pain control, improvement in QoL, reduction of psychiatric symptoms 
and psychological distress, prevention of disease recurrence and tissue damage, and 
fertility preservation. Given this complicated set of goals, a multidisciplinary team is 
required. 
Psychopharmacology 
Treatment of psychiatric disorder in pati ents with endometriosis requires two main 
considerations: the symptoms related to endometriosis and the symptoms related to 
the use of GnRH agonists, which are effective in reducing endometriosis symptoms 
(Laganà et al. 2017; Pope et al. 2015; Warnock et al. 1998a). Trials of psychotropic med-
ications, such as antidepressants and anticonvulsants, are commonly used for both of 
these indications; however, limited data are available on efficacy in this patient pop -
ulation. The combination of gabapentin, which is a GABA analogue, and amitriptyline 
has been found to be effective, along with regional anesthesia techniques for manag-
ing pain specifically in patients with endometriosis who continue to have symptoms 
after surgical intervention (Malec-Milewska et al. 2015). Animal studies indicate that 
the molecular makeup of gabapentin support s its use for chronic pain in endometri -
osis patients (Bellessort et al. 2018). 
It is also important to evaluate existing psychiatric comorbidities that could increase 
the risk of decompensation related to hormonal therapy (Pope et al. 2015). One retro-
spective study considered the use of sertraline in patients receiving GnRH agonist 
therapy. Warnock et al. (1998b) retrospectively studied 42 women with endometriosis 
who were treated with 24 weeks of GnRH agonist therapy, with or without sertraline. 
Participants also receiving sertraline repo rted significantly fewer depressive symp-
toms, as measured by the Hamilton Depressi on Rating Scale, but these patients did 
not differ in reporting of physical symptoms compared with those receiving only the 
GnRH agonist.

Endometriosis is a lifelong disease that requires ongoing management. The use of hor-

has been a mainstay of treatment in en dometriosis due to the hormonally mediated 
and cyclic pain seen in the early stages of the disease. Providers prescribe birth control 
and other hormones to induce anovulation and amenorrhea. The FDA has specifically 
approved oral norethindrone acetate and subcutaneous depot-medroxyprogesterone 
acetate (DMPA) for use with endometriosis, with DMPA being as effective as GnRH 
agonists but without the concurrent bone loss (American College of Obstetricians and 
Gynecologists 2010). The use of an intrauterine progesterone, such as the levonorge -

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
PCOS is a disease characterized by irregular menstrual bleeding, hyperandrogenism, 
hyperandrogenemia, and polycystic-appearing ovaries. It is part of the spectrum of 
diseases leading to normogonadotropic no rmoestrogenic anovulation, and PCOS is 
diagnosed in 55%–91% of women with this anovulatory bleeding pattern (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b; Fauser 2004). Women with hyper-
androgenic anovulation/oligoovulation ma ke up 7% of reproductive-aged women, 
and PCOS represents one of the most common endocrinopathies among this patient 
population (Bednarska and Siejka 2017). Because PCOS is a diagnosis of exclusion, the 
exact prevalence varies depending on the diagnostic criteria used. 
Risk Factors 
PCOS has no known genetic or environmental risk factors, but insulin resistance and 
obesity may play a role because increased circulating insulin can lead to a decrease in 
sex hormone–binding globulin and thus increased androgens (American College of 
Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b). PCOS is diagnosed with a higher frequency 
in Hispanic and Native American women (Bednarska and Siejka 2017). 
Screening Methods 
According to the ACOG’s Practice Bulletin, at the time of diagnosis, patients should 
be screened for insulin resistance or diabetes with a 2-hour oral glucose tolerance test 
(American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b). Due to unopposed es-
trogen exposure at the level of the endometrium from anovulation, women with 
PCOS are at risk of developing metabolic syndrome and insulin resistance, type 2 di-
abetes, nonalcoholic fatty liver, obesity-related obstructive sleep apnea, and endome-
trial cancer (American College of Obst etricians and Gynecologists 2018b; Fauser 
2004). They have a three- to sevenfold incr eased risk of developing type 2 diabetes 
(Fauser 2004). Upon diagnosis of PCOS, whether made by a gynecologist or primary 
care physician, the patient should be counseled about implications related to her ob-
stetric and gynecological health, as well as her overall health and well-being. The 
ACOG recommends serial blood pressure measurements, monitoring and documen-
tation of BMI, measurement of waist circ umference, and evaluation for stigmata of 
PCOS, including hirsutism, acne, and acanthosis nigricans (American College of Ob-
stetricians and Gynecologists 2018b). Although the physiological changes are import-
ant to evaluate, treat, and follow, the psychological changes associated with PCOS are 
also essential to evaluate and treat.

Diagnostic Criteria 
No single set of criteria has yet been accepted for PCOS, and providers must first rule 
out other causes of symptoms. Patients wh o present with symptoms consistent with 
PCOS should first be evaluated with thyr oid hormone testing, a serum prolactin to 
rule out hyperprolactinemia, and other stud ies, including follicle-stimulating hor -

drogen-secreting tumor, intake of exogenous androgens, Cushing syndrome, nonclas-

and Gynecologists 2018b). Screening with serum androgens (free and total testoster-

Clinical Presentation 
Patients typically present with menstrual disorders or infertility (American College of 
Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b; Bednarska and Siejka 2017; Fauser 2004). Ab-
dominal obesity is diagnosed in 30%–70% of patients with PCOS (Bednarska and Sie-
jka 2017). Hyperandrogenism is also a common finding that can be expressed through 
clinical features, such as hirsutism and acne, or as hyperandrogenemia (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b; Fauser 2004). Comorbid insulin re-
sistance and type 2 diabetes are often diag nosed in these patients; up to 50% of both 
obese and nonobese women with PCOS demonstrate insulin resistance (Fauser 2004). 
Finally, there is a close correlation between PCOS and mental health disorders, includ-
ing an increased risk of depression, anxi ety, bipolar disorder, bulimia, and anorexia 
(Bednarska and Siejka 2017). 
Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
The underlying pathophysiology of the development of PCOS is unknown (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b). No certain genetic condition or en-
vironmental substance has been identified as a causative agent. Obesity is a common 
comorbidity, but it is not required for diagnosis. Insulin resistance may play a role be-
cause hyperinsulinemia may be linked to decreased levels of sex hormone–binding 
globulin; however, hyperandrogenism, which is central to PCOS, can be due to causes 
other than hyperinsulinemia.

Psychopharmacology 
No RCTs have yet assessed antidepressants for psychiatric symptoms in women with 
PCOS. A 2013 Cochrane Review on antidepressants for PCOS found no studies report-

women (Zhuang et al. 2013). A 2003 pilot st udy of 16 women found an association

these patients (Rasgon et al. 2003). Some studies support that some patients with PCOS

severity of the patient’s psychiatric symptoms. For example, if the patient presents

When deciding on a treatment plan for a woman with PCOS, the clinician should take 
into account the woman’s clinical presentation and desired clinical effect (Bednarska 
and Siejka 2017). A common early decision-making concern is whether the patient is 
trying to conceive. According to the ACOG  Practice Bulletin, for women hoping for 
menstrual regularity, combined OCPs are helpful in restoring normal cycles, decreas-
ing hyperandrogenism, and reducing the risk of endometrial hyperplasia (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologi sts 2018b). OCPs are the most commonly 
used and recommended first-line treatment option for PCOS because of their ability 
to suppress luteinizing hormone, increase sex hormone–binding globulin, and de -
crease ovarian androgen secretion. For patients who cannot or will not use OCPs, oral 
progestins, injection with DMPA, or progestin-secreting intrauterine devices can also 
be used to protect the endometrium from developing hyperplasia or malignancy. 
For women who wish to conceive, providers must focus on inducing ovulation in an-
ovulatory patients. A first-line treatment recommendation, per the ACOG (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b), is lifestyle modification with weight 
loss and exercise. Pharmacological management in the form of either clomiphene citrate 
or letrozole can induce ovulation as well. Clomiphene is a selective estrogen receptor 
modulator that acts centrally in the pituitary gland as an antagonist, increasing FSH and 
leading to ovulation. Letrozole is an aromatase inhibitor that decreases circulating estro-
gen levels and increases FSH production in the pituitary gland, with subsequent increases 
in ovulation. Clomiphene was once the first-line ovulation induction agent, but more re-

letrozole vs. 48.3% with clomiphene; P<0.001), clinical pregnancy rates (OR 1.4; 95% CI 
1.18–1.65), and live birth rates (OR 1.64; 95% CI 1.32–2.04). Metformin is another medica-
tion commonly used to treat PCOS. When used for infertility, it can improve ovulation 
rates by increasing sex hormone–binding globulin and decreasing circulating androgens 
(American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018b).

Rates/Incidence 
Although the prevalence of GPPPD is unknown, more is understood about specific di-
mensions of the disorder (dimensions that were considered separate disorders prior 
to being combined into on e disorder in DSM-5 [American Psychiatric Association 
2013]). Vulvodynia—chronic pain or discomfort around the vulva with no identifiable 
cause—is the most common cause of pain with  sex. It is estimated to affect 5%–17% of 
women in the general population, but both vulvodynia and the related genito-pelvic 
pain condition vestibulodynia (a more localized form of vulvodynia) are underdiag-
nosed and undertreated (Lahaie et al. 2010).  Dyspareunia (painful or difficult inter -
course) affects between 14% and 34% of younger women and between 6.5% and 45.0% 
of older women. Vaginismus (painful spasmodic contraction of the vagina in response 
to physical contact or pressure) is less commonly researched, and prevalence ranges 
from 1% to 6% (see van Lankveld et al. 2010 for a discussion of women’s sexual pain 
disorders). The prevalence of persistent genital arousal disorder (PGAD), a genital pain 
condition characterized by unwanted genital arousal, is currently unknown (Squibb et 
al. 2019). Although most of the literature studying genital pain has reported on hetero-
sexual women, one survey of more than 1,000 lesbian and heterosexual women looking 
at the prevalence of sexual problems found that sexual pain was the most frequent sex-
ual problem reported by both groups (Peixoto and Nobre 2015). 
Risk Factors 
The correlation between sexual or physical abuse and GPPPD remains unclear. Al -
though abuse is a commonly cited predictor of dyspareunia and vaginismus as defined 
in DSM-IV (American Psychiatric Associatio n 1994), Lahaie et al. (2010) found rela -
tively few good-quality research studies investigating this issue, and five of the six 
studies found no evidence of a higher pr evalence of sexual and physical abuse in 
women with these disorders. The medical literature frequently reports that women 
with dyspareunia and vaginismus more often endorse negative sexual attitudes; how-
ever, only one study has used a control group and standardized measures, and the re-
sults did not support this notion (Biswas and Ratnam 1995). Women with genito-pelvic 
pain report fewer sexual relationships and increased avoidance of sexual activity when 
compared with control subjects, yet it is unclear whether these findings are conse -
quences or causes of vaginismus and sexual dysfunction. A community-based epide-
miological study found that women with prior mood or anxiety disorders were four 
times more likely to develop vulvodynia. This work also demonstrated that vulvody-
nia was associated with recurrent or new-onset anxiety or mood disorders (Khandker 
et al. 2011). A more recent study found that women with PGAD are also more likely to 
report depressive symptoms, panic attacks, and obsessive-compulsive symptoms; 
however, no causal relationship has been identified (Squibb et al. 2019).

Diagnostic Criteria 
DSM-5 collapses the DSM-IV concepts of dyspareunia and vaginismus into the single 
diagnostic category, GPPPD (Box 2–1), based on the fact that the literature and clinical 
practice suggest that differentiating these conditions may not be possible (Sungur and 
Gündüz 2014). This expanded DSM-5 category includes four commonly comorbid 
symptom dimensions and requires the presence of only one of these for a duration of 
6 months for the diagnosis: 1) difficulty with vaginal penetration during intercourse, 
2) marked vulvovaginal or pelvic pain during vaginal penetration or attempts at pen-
etration, 3) fear or anxiety about pain with vaginal penetration, or 4) pelvic floor tight-

disease spectrum, with vaginismus accompanied by anxiety and avoidance at one 
end and pain with intercourse and hypertonic pelvic floor muscles at the other (Dero-
gatis et al. 2010). Current conceptualizations of vaginismus integrate pelvic floor mus-
cle tension, genital pain, and fear (Lahaie et al. 2010).

Clinical Considerations 
The prevalence of postpartum genito-pelvi c pain in women has been found to be as 
high as 27% at 3 months postpartum, with the literature reporting rates as high as 33% 
at 1 year postpartum. The prevalence of postpartum dyspareunia is higher; studies 
report that up to 62% of women report pa in with sex at 3 months postpartum, with 
rates as high as 33% lasting through 18 months postpartum. Perineal trauma increases 
the risk for CPP in the short term; however, the results are equivocal when looking at 
mode of delivery or parity status. Women who had deliveries requiring vaginal in -
strumentation are at a higher risk of ex periencing pelvic pain after childbearing 
(Rosen and Pukall 2016). 
When looking at conception and pregnancy patterns for women with preexisting 
GPPPD, clinicians should be aware that trying to conceive and the childbearing pro-
cess may trigger a worsening of mood or anxiety symptoms. The concerns for women 
with sexual pain disorders include managing pain during intercourse, the use of lu -
bricants or pain medications to allow for penetration and the women’s safety in preg-
nancy, the need to pursue advanced reproductive technologies for conception when 
intercourse is not possible, and fears arou nd pelvic examination during pregnancy 
and around the delivery itself (Rosenbaum and Padoa 2012). 
Research looking at this population is li mited, but the existing data indicate that 
women with GPPPD are more likely to have cesarean sections, with one study of al -
most 200,000 deliveries find ing that women with vaginismus also have higher rates 
of labor induction and vacuum extraction but no differences in birth outcomes (Gold-
smith et al. 2009). Women with vulvodynia have been found to be just as likely to carry 
a pregnancy to term but experience higher rates of cesarean section (Nguyen et al. 
2012). Qualitative data analyzing how women with vulvodynia regarded conception, 
pregnancy, and delivery indicate that dyspareunia itself plays an important role in a 
couple’s physical and emotional ability to conceive. To deal with pain around preg -
nancy, women report utilizing coping mechanisms including pain minimization, 
planning pregnancy-safe treatment, and timing intercourse around ovulation. These 
women report experiencing anxiety around how pregnancy may change pain symp-
toms, as well as having a mistrustful attitude toward dealing with the health care sys-
tem and clinicians in the context of prenatal care and delivery (Johnson et al. 2015).

Psychological Factors 
Women presenting with sexual complaints are likely to report mood or anxiety con -
cerns. Conversely, women presenting with mood or anxiety concerns are likely to re-
port difficulty in their sex lives. Meta-analysis data support a bidirectional relationship 
between sexual dysfunction and depressive  disorders (Atlantis and Sullivan 2012). 
For women with GPPPD, fear plays a critical role (Lahaie et al. 2010). Fear of pain has 
been reported as the primary reason that women with these conditions abstain from 
or avoid sexual intercourse (Reissing et al. 2003). Moreover, women with vulvodynia 
describe the emotional burden associated with their vulvar pain, as well as the nego-
tiation of sexual intercourse within heterosexual relationships, as causing them to feel 
inadequate as women and as partners (Ayling and Ussher 2008). Women with vulvar 
pain report higher rates of depression and affective distress (Pukall et al. 2007); one 
study found that one-fourth of women experiencing vulvar pain reported considering 
suicide (Gordon et al. 2003). A 2019 survey  of 51 women with PGAD found that de -
pressive symptoms significantly predicted sexual distress, but anxiety and stress mea-
sures did not (Squibb et al. 2019). 
Social Considerations 
GPPPD and related conditions have a pervasive impact on women’s interpersonal re-
lationships and QoL. Studies show that most women with vulvodynia report at least 
a moderate impact on their sex life; in one study, 40% of women reported an extreme 
effect (Arnold et al. 2006). Although some women abstain from intercourse altogether, 
others who continue to be sexually active  have reported experiencing emotional or 
physical pleasure that outweighs the pain (Gordon et al. 2003). The impact on rela -
tionship functioning for these women is so mewhat variable, with some couples de -
veloping a problematic cycle of pain, avoida nce, conflict, and distress (Pukall et al. 
2007). Some women with vulvodynia, however, reported that an understanding part-
ner helped them cope with pain (Gordon et al. 2003). Although most research on 
GPPPD and related conditions has involved heterosexual women, one survey of more 
than 800 lesbian, bisexual, and heterosexu al women investigated associations be-
tween relationship qualities and the influence of women’s perceptions of pain on their 
relationships. This study noted that the qual ity of genital pain reported by all three 
groups was similar. Women who were in re lationships with men, or those who had 
the potential to be in relationships with men, reported more pain than women who 
were less likely to be in a relationship  with men. Among same-sex couples, better 
communication between partners was associ ated with the perception that chronic 
pain had less of an effect on relationship functioning (Blair et al. 2015). Research on 
GPPPD in transgender and nonbinary individuals is lacking.

Treatment 
Psychopharmacology 
Treatment options for GPPPD should target the presenting symptoms (American Col-
lege of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019). Patients with genito-pelvic pain benefit 
from local therapy. Topical low-dose estr ogen is the first-line treatment for women 
who are able to use estrogen products. For women who are unable to use estrogen 
products, ospemifene (a selective estrogen receptor modulator) with agonist activity 
in the vagina can be an alternate. Intravaginal prasterone (a dehydroepiandrosterone 
preparation) can also be of use for wome n with dyspareunia. When compared with 
placebo, prasterone improved all domains of the female sexual function index (Amer-
ican College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019). 
Other Medical Interventions 
Patients with GPPPD may benefit from pelvic floor PT. They also may benefit from 
using lubricants, topical anesthesia, and vaginal moisturizers for symptomatic treat-
ment of dyspareunia (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019).
