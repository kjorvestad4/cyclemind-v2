Psychological Factors 
Psychological contributions to perimenopausal mood and anxiety disorders include at-
titudes toward menopause, personality traits, and cognitive styles, especially optimism 
and a self-critical cognitive style (Süss and Ehlert 2020). Most studies that include mea-
sures of attitudes toward menopause report that negative attitudes play a role in the 
etiology of menopausal depression and anxiety symptoms (Ayers et al. 2010). A bidi-
rectional relationship likely exists, because women who have more depressive symp-
toms also have more negative attitudes (Ayers et al. 2010). Similarly, the psychological

Social and Cultural Considerations 
In addition to biological and psychological factors, many social changes occur during 
the perimenopausal transition, and these may contribute to onset of depression and 
anxiety symptoms in vulnerable women. Role stress often escalates for women during 
this time as they attempt to juggle increased and varied responsibilities, which may in-
clude caring for aging family members, changing childcare needs, and new challenges 
at work and in their intimate relationships (Maki et al. 2018). To our knowledge, no 
large studies have attempted to quantify the contribution of these social and cultural 
differences to the etiology of perimenopausal depression. It is highly likely that differ-
ing cultural views of aging, and beliefs about the responsibilities associated with role 
changes, factor into the experience of stress and contribute to depressive symptoms ex-
perienced at this time of life. In their review of “empty nest psychosocial stress,” Bou-
gea et al. (2019) described the varying expectations that different families and cultures 
have regarding the care of elders, including whether living arrangements are indepen-
dent or shared, and how these expectations shift in times of economic or social crisis 
(Bougea et al. 2019). Social stresses associated with increased depression at other times 
of life, such as interpersonal violence, raci al discrimination, and poverty, likely also 
play a role in perimenopausal depressive symptoms. For instance, in a study of peri-
menopausal women in New York City, neighborhood characteristics of decreased 
safety and cohesiveness and increased raci al discriminatory actions were associated 
with increased depressive symptoms (Gerber and Sievert 2018). 
Treatment 
Complete Evaluation That Identifies Contributing Factors 
Treatment of a perimenopausal woman who has psychiatric symptoms begins with a 
comprehensive clinical interview that encompasses her current symptoms; prior his-
tory of psychiatric symptoms and treatment; medical history, including reproductive 
history; and social and family history (Stute et al. 2020). The interview should include 
a detailed review of current physical symptoms; list of current medications, including 
over-the-counter supplements; typical diet, sleep, and exercise habits; recent and ongo-
ing stressors; and consumption of caffeine, alcohol, marijuana, and other substances. 
Information about recent menstrual cycle patterns, sexual function, and VMS should 
also be elicited (Canonico et al. 2007; Joffe et al. 2003; Krause and Nakajima 2015). A 
one-time hormone level is not terribly informative in the assessment and treatment of

Psychopharmacology 
Antidepressants for perimenopausal depression. A number of open trials in 
perimenopausal women provide evidence for the efficacy of antidepressants, including 
citalopram, duloxetine, escitalopram, mirtazapine, venlafaxine, and vortioxetine (Free-
man et al. 2017; Joffe et al. 2001, 2007; Ladd et al. 2005; Soares et al. 2003, 2006). Use of 
desvenlafaxine is supported by two large RCTs, both of which show the medication’s 
superiority to placebo (Clayton et al. 2013; Kornstein et al. 2010). Only one published 
trial compared particular agents directly, and the results did not demonstrate an effi-
cacy difference between paroxetine and vort ioxetine (Callegari et al. 2019). No evi -
dence exists to contradict the conclusion that this efficacy is a class effect rather than

Hormonal treatment for depression. The extent to which MHT can have a direct 
positive effect on mood remains an open question. Multiple studies have attempted 
to determine whether estrogen therapy is effective as an antidepressant during and 
after the menopausal transition. Unfortunately, as discussed by Rubinow et al. (2015) 
in their assessment of 24 such studies (all RCTs) published between 1997 and 2014, 
drawing robust conclusions is made challenging by the studies’ methodological dif-
ferences; various studies used different definitions of the stages of menopause, as well 
as different forms of and timing of treatment with respect to the final menstrual pe -
riod. Even more problematically, few of the studies assessed whether subjects had a 
prior history of depression or current VMS (both known to increase risk of MDD 
during the menopausal transition), only five studied women with depression, and 
only two were restricted to women in pe rimenopause (Rubinow et al. 2015). There -
fore, although there is a theoretical basis and some evidence that estrogen therapy has 
direct positive effects on mood in wome n during perimenopause, perhaps particu -
larly in women who experience VMS, this is not sufficiently established for it to be a 
standard treatment option (Chester et al. 2018). The only extant head-to-head trial 
comparing MHT (ethinyl estradiol/norethin drone acetate) with an antidepressant 
(escitalopram) for the treatment of low mood, VMS, and sleep disturbance in peri -
menopausal women showed improvement in sleep, VMS, and quality of life in both 
treatment groups, but escitalopram was more effective in the alleviation of symptoms 
of depression (Soares et al. 2006). 
Another area in which knowledge is limited is whether MHT can prevent the occur-
rence of perimenopausal depression. The one extant study examining this in a prospec-
tive, placebo-controlled manner found that women who started MHT (transdermal 
estradiol plus sequential oral micronized progesterone) early, but not late, in the peri-
menopausal transition were less likely to experience depression during the 12-month 
study period than were those randomized to placebo. There was no obvious benefit of

In general, then, the paucity of data available to guide the use of estrogens for treat-
ment of depression during perimenopause leaves antidepressants as the current stan-
dard of care. However, estradiol should be considered if perimenopausal symptoms 
of sleep disruption and VMS do not resolve with antidepressant treatment and if the 
patient has no contraindication to the use of MHT (see “Hormonal Treatment” section 
later). Among women with a uterus, the endometrial proliferative effects of estradiol 
must be managed with either concurrent or  sequential use of progesterone. W e rec-
ommend that any use of MHT by psychiatri sts or psychiatric nurse practitioners be 
coordinated with the patient’s gynecologist or primary care provider.
