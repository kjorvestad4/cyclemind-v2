Course and differential diagnosis. Studies show both high prevalence and high se-
verity of depressive symptoms in the first 2–6 weeks following perinatal loss, but for 
many women the severity of these symptoms decreases with the passage of time, with a 
return to baseline 1 year following loss (Farren et al. 2018). In a large population study , 
Gold et al. (2016) reported that bereaved mothers had four times higher odds of depres-
sive symptoms and seven times higher odds of PTSD symptoms at 9 months postpartum 
as compared with nonbereaved mothers. Symptoms can often be triggered when trying 
to conceive or during a subsequent pregnancy (Farren et al. 2018; Hamama et al. 2010).

Acute Stress Disorder and PTSD 
Epidemiology. Acute stress disorder and PTSD are common after a perinatal loss 
and are distinguishable from grief and complicated grief (Horsch 2009; Lee and Slade 
1996). Rates of PTSD have been shown to be up to 45% at 3 months after loss in those 
with frequent miscarriages (Farren et al. 2016, 2018). Many studies do not separate out 
types of perinatal loss when looking at rates of acute stress and PTSD. In one large co-
hort study, criteria for PTSD were met by 29% of women at 1 month after an early mis-
carriage and by 18% at 9 months after the loss (Farren et al. 2016). 
Risk factors. Risk factors for developing PTSD in the context of a perinatal loss include 
maternal age (with younger age conveying higher risk), gestational age, lower educa-
tional level, a history of previous physical or sexual trauma, prior history of mental health 
problems, and current depression, anxiety, or perinatal grief symptoms (Dauggirdaite et 
al. 2015). Length of gestational age is corr elated with the intensity of reported PTSD 
symptoms, with later perinatal losses, such as stillbirth or neonatal death, associated 
with more severe intensity of reported symptoms (Dauggirdaite et al. 2015). 
Screening. The most common screening tool for PTSD following perinatal loss is 
the Perinatal Post-Traumatic Stress Disorder Questionnaire. This 14-item self-report 
screening tool measures symptoms of hy perarousal, reexperiencing, and avoidance 
behaviors (Callahan et al. 2006).
