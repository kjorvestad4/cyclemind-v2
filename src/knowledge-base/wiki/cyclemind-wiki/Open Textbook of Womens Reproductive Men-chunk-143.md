Psychopharmacology 
As discussed in Chapters 8 and 9, considerable controversy remains about treating de-
pression in pregnancy and during breastfeeding. We have no randomized controlled 
trials about medication risks and very few studies about medication efficacy, alterna-
tive treatments, or dosage alterations that may be required due to metabolic changes 
in pregnancy. Decisions about treatment must therefore rely on observational evidence 
only. Although there is some evidence of risks associated with antidepressant usage, 
those risks appear to be few now that studies (primarily of selective serotonin reuptake 
inhibitors [SSRIs]) are being conducted wi th appropriate control groups and con -
trolling for appropriate confounders. The substantial body of evidence we have con-
cerning the risks of untreated psychiatric illness to both mother and child (Field et al. 
2006, 2010; Li et al. 2009; Orr et al. 2007; Zuckerman et al. 1989) must be weighed 
against any known risks of medication.

To understand the risks of antidepressant usage in pregnancy, it is important to un-
derstand the differences between women who use antidepressants and those who do 
not. Women with depression have higher rates of diabetes mellitus, obesity, smoking, 
substance use, and cesarean section than women who do not have depression (De 
Hert et al. 2011). Therefore, studies that fa il to account for risks conferred either di -
rectly by the illness or symptoms themselves (e.g., by elevated cortisol or inflamma -
tion) or indirectly through health behaviors may find apparent associations between 
antidepressants and risks that may not be caused by the medication itself but rather 
by characteristics associated with the women taking the medication. This means that 
any association found when comparing women taking antidepressants with healthy 
women (the control group used in the vast majority of studies) is spurious. 
With these caveats in mind, current evidence shows few consistent and sustained 
risks associated with antidepressant usage. We review here current knowledge about 
several areas that have been investigated for risk. 
Risk Considerations During Early Pregnancy 
Congenital malformations. Some studies have found statistically significant but 
clinically negligible associations (i.e., with odds ratios just above 1) between overall 
congenital malformations and the use of antidepressants (Einarson 2012; Grigoriadis 
et al. 2013). Some but not all early studies found potential associations between use of 
antidepressants in pregnancy and heart defects (Chisolm and Payne 2016). In 2005, the 
FDA issued a warning concerning heart defects with paroxetine. These studies gener-
ally compared psychiatric with nonpsychiatric populations, an error of inaccurate con-
trol group that unfortunately continues, as shown in a recent large population study 
(Wemakor et al. 2015). Better-designed studies have not upheld these associations. 
Large studies (with populati ons approaching or exceeding 1 million women) and 
meta-analyses that compare women with depression who took antidepressants with 
those who did not take antidepressants have found no association between SSRI use

Spontaneous abortion (miscarriage). Several studies have found increased rates 
of spontaneous abortion in women using antidepressants (Einarson et al. 2009; Santone 
et al. 2009). Spontaneous abortion has also been associated with depression itself (Na-
kano et al. 2004). Large database studies and meta-analyses of the highest-quality stud-
ies indicate that antidepressants likely confer no added risk of spontaneous abortion 
beyond the risk conferred by the illness itself (Kjaersgaard et al. 2013; Ross et al. 2013). 
Other Pregnancy Risks 
Persistent pulmonary hypertension. In 2006, the FDA issued an alert for a poten-
tial, and seemingly alarming, association between SSRI usage and persistent pulmo -
nary hypertension of the newborn (Chambers et al. 2006). Subsequent studies were 
mixed, with about half confirming this association (albeit with considerably lower odds 
ratios) and the other half failing to find it (Andrade et al. 2009; Källén and Olausson 
2008; Kieler et al. 2012; Wichman et al. 2009; Wilson et al. 2011). A recent study in al-
most 4,000,000 women found an unadjusted OR of 1.51 (95% CI, 1.35–1.69) for an asso-
ciation between SSRI use and persistent pulmonary hypertension of the newborn, but 
when the analysis was adjusted for potent ial confounders, the relationship vanished 
(with the exception of remaining statistica lly significant when cases were limited to 
full-term infants only [OR 1.28; 95% CI 1.01–1.64]) (Huybrechts et al. 2015). 
Infant growth. Most studies that do not control for depression show a small, statisti-
cally significant relationship between antidepressant exposure and a reduction in birth 
weight, with very small effect size (<100 g) (Huang et al. 2014; Ross et al. 2013). Those 
studies that have directly compared the effects on infant growth of antidepressant ex-
posure with those of depression have found no differences; in one case, both groups 
were associated with slower growth (El Marroun et al. 2012), and in another, neither 
was associated (Wisner et al. 2013). It therefore seems likely that there is no significant 
effect of antidepressants directly on infant growth—and if there is any effect, it is very 
small and not clinically meaningful.
