Screening Methods 
Screening for perinatal OCD is similar to that for OCD in the general population, and 
screening tools are listed in Table 20–1. Commonly used tools include the Yale-Brown 
Obsessive Compulsive Scale (Y-BOCS; Goodman et al. 1989b, 1989c), Obsessive Com-
pulsive Inventory–Revised (Foa et al. 2002), Dimensional Obsessive-Compulsive 
Scale (Abramowitz et al. 2010a), and Perina tal Obsessive Compulsive Scale (POCS), 
which is the only scale specific to obsessive and compulsive symptoms in the perina-
tal period (Chaudron and Nirodi 2010; Lord et al. 2011b). The Y-BOCS is considered 
the gold standard for screening and diagnosing OCD due to its psychometric proper-
ties (Chaudron and Nirodi 2010; Lord et al . 2011b). It can also be used to measure 
symptom severity and track treatment response over time (Hudak and Wisner 2012). 
The POCS is a self-report scale developed to assess perinatal obsessions and com -
pulsions that was validated in comparison with the Y-BOCS (Lord et al. 2011b). The 
pilot study revealed good construct validity, high internal consistency, concurrent va-
lidity, and discriminative capacity (Lord et al. 2011b; Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). 
When compared with the Y-BOCS, the validation study for the POCS showed that 
perinatal women reported more overall symptoms and higher symptom severity us-
ing this instrument. It has both a prenatal and postpartum version, with questions on 
both thoughts and behaviors (Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). Additionally, because 
it is a self-report scale, it allows perinatal women the opportunity to discuss these sen-
sitive topics in an open way with their health care provider (Lord et al. 2011b). 
Practitioners screening perinatal or postpartum women for depression should pay 
close attention to the presence of anxiety symptoms. Studies indicate that perinatal de-
pression and anxiety and obsessive-compulsive symptoms are highly comorbid, yet 
traditional screening tools for symptoms of depression do not typically evaluate for 
anxiety symptoms (Abramowitz et al. 2010b). Furthermore, perinatal OCD is often 
misdiagnosed as depression or is comorbid with depression but not identified and, in 
both cases, is inadequately treated (Abramowitz et al. 2003; Speisman et al. 2011). If 
perinatal OCD is identified first, consider screening for comorbid mental illnesses with 
tools validated in the perinatal population, such as the Edinburgh Postnatal Depres -
