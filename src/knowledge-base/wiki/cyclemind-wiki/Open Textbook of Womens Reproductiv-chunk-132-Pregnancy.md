Special Considerations in Pharmacotherapy 
When prescribing for low-weight individuals, dosage adjustments should be consid-
ered due to an increased risk of side effects in the setting of decreased volume of dis-
tribution. Depleted protein stores can also increase the percentage of unbound or free 
drug in the serum, further supporting the recommendation to start at low initial doses 
and titrate slowly to effect. 
Weight Restoration and Medical Interventions 
Weight restoration and nutritional rehabilitation are the cornerstone of treatment in indi-
viduals with EDs, particularly when at low weight. Goals of nutritional rehabilitation are 
to stabilize the patient medically, reverse medical complications that occur in the low-
weight state, and restore weight and normal eating patterns. This improved nutritional 
state can also lead to improved cognitive function and emotional regulation and stability 
(Attia and Walsh 2009). Nutritional rehabilitation is best provided in a multidisciplinary 
approach, including behavioral management, nutritional counseling, and medical moni-
toring. A key component is often the introduction of regular, structured meals, with ad-
ditional monitoring of patients to ensure appropriate intake without the use of disordered 
eating behaviors, including compensatory behaviors such as exercise or purging. 
During the time of initial weight restoration, patients should be closely monitored 
given the risk of refeeding syndrome. Refeeding syndrome is a potentially fatal com-
plication of nutritional rehabilitation and occurs rarely in moderately severe cases 
(~6%) and more commonly in mildly severe cases (>20%) (Ornstein et al. 2003). This 
syndrome is marked by numerous electrolyte derangements and clinical sequelae, in-
cluding hypophosphatemia, hypokalemia, hypomagnesemia, thiamine deficiency, pe-
ripheral edema, rhabdomyolysis, and hemolysis. More severe medical complications 
have also been reported, including congestive heart failure, seizures, delirium, cardiac 
dysrhythmia, and even sudden death (Fuent ebella and Kerner 2009; Ornstein et al. 
2003). Of most import is phosphorus leve l because hypophosphatemia is considered 
the hallmark and predominant cause of refeeding syndrome. The pathogenesis of this 
relates to increased cellular uptake of phosphorus (along with potassium and magne-
sium) when insulin is released in the body in response to glucose upon refeeding. Risk 
of this syndrome is increased in more severely malnourished individuals (i.e., those 
who begin refeeding at lower weight/percent of ideal body weight), those not fed/un-
derfed for at least 10–14 days, and those with recent significant weight loss (>10% in 
last 1–2 months) (Fuentebella and Kerner 2009). 
Refeeding syndrome is avoided and mitigated through appropriate introduction 
of nourishment and close monitoring physically and biochemically. Initial caloric in-
take should approximate or be less than estimated energy expenditure, with slow in-
crease in caloric intake during early refeeding. Fluid intake should also be monitored 
and restricted as necessary to prevent fluid overload, particularly in those at risk of 
cardiac dysfunction. Electrolytes should be closely monitored and deficiencies imme-
diately corrected, with particular focus on phosphorus, potassium, and magnesium by 
daily measurement until stability is met (F uentebella and Kerner 2009). In addition, 
thiamine, vitamin B complex, and multivitamins should be started with refeeding be-
cause thiamine deficiency is a frequent complication of malnourishment and can have 
severe consequences (including Wernicke’s encephalopathy) (Mehanna et al. 2009).

Recent studies have looked at higher initial caloric intake with more rapid increases 
that have not shown worsening in medical complications and refeeding syndrome 
when electrolytes have been appropriat ely monitored and abnormalities corrected 
(Garber et al. 2016). Current recommendations now opt for more aggressive caloric 
values for those with mild to moderate malnutrition in the setting of close electrolyte 
monitoring. 
Behavioral Treatments 
Patients with EDs can be treated at various levels of care, and many require more in-
tensive treatment beyond typical outpatient care given the medical risks and complex-
ities of the disorder. Recommendations remain to offer outpatient treatment to those 
with EDs, with inpatient intervention reserved for those at greatest medical (e.g., high 
risk of refeeding syndrome) or psychiatric (e.g., comorbid depression with suicidality) 
risk (Treasure et al. 2010). Descriptions of the varied levels of care are found in Table 
22–7. Universal across treatment modalities is the involvement of a multidisciplinary 
team, often including a psychiatrist, psychologist/therapist, dietician, nurses, and a 
general medical provider. 
Psychotherapy is the preferred evidence-based intervention for most EDs. CBT has 
been well studied in the treatment of EDs an d has been found to be a superior treat -
ment modality for BN and BED (Davis and Attia 2019; Treasure et al. 2010). Due to its 
efficacy, CBT techniques have been adapted for group environment, telemedicine ser-
vices, and self-help modalities, including an internet-based manualized CBT group 
therapy delivered via group chat (Davis and Attia 2019). Interpersonal psychotherapy 
is also an effective treatment for EDs, although treatment response a p p e a r s  t o  b e  
slower and potentially less robust compared with CBT (Davis and Attia 2019; Treasure 
et al. 2010). Given the high prevalence of emotional dysregulation in EDs, dialectical 
behavioral therapy has also been proposed as a treatment, and data support its use in 
BN and BED, although evidence in AN is limited (Davis and Attia 2019). Acceptance 
and commitment therapy has also been considered as a treatment to target the under-
lying maladaptive cognitions and behaviors in EDs, although data remain limited 
(Davis and Attia 2019). 
For children and adolescents with EDs, fi rst-line treatment involves family-based 
therapy, in which responsibility for refeeding the child is placed back on the parents. 
Recommendations are for early and aggressi ve treatment, given that early response 
to treatment and initial weight gain are likely the greatest predictors of treatment out-
comes, including posttreatment remission (Davis and Attia 2019). 
Treatment Challenges 
One challenge to the treatment of EDs is th eir changing presentation over time. Pa -
tients may initially present with certain disordered eating symptoms (e.g., predomi-
nant restricting behaviors) and later prog ress to other symptoms (e.g., binge/purge 
behaviors). This can lead to treatment ch allenges, particularly when certain treat -
ments are less effective or even contraindicated when behaviors change. Most import-
ant to consider are medications associated with appetite reduction and weight loss; 
although individuals with BN may be of normal to high weight initially, they may de-
velop more severe symptoms that result in low weight and a subsequent diagnosis of 
AN. Such factors should be considered in the initial selection of pharmacotherapy.

Another challenge is managing patients with severe and enduring EDs (SEEDs). For 
those who have persistent symptoms that are not responding to highly aggressive treat-
ment, referral to a higher level of care may not be indicated despite serious symptoms. 
Intensive treatment focuses on amelioration of ED symptoms, which might not be a re-
alistic goal for those with SEED symptoms. Individuals with SEEDs may also remain 
symptomatic at discharge, which could be a reflection of external limitations on length 
of treatment, and patient factors related to ambivalence about change and inability to 
fully address the complex functions of disordered eating symptoms and their associa-
tions with comorbid psychopathology. Va luing a patient’s autonomy by assessing 
readiness for change and identifying specific goals of care is an important component 
of treatment in SEED cases. 
Conclusion 
EDs are a group of psychiatric diagnoses associated with numerous medical comor-
bidities and risk of severe complications and mortality . Care must be taken to identify 
individuals with disordered eating symptoms, including application of screening meth-
ods across various medical settings. Treatment should be focused initially on weight 
restoration and safe nutritional rehabilitation, with particular focus on avoiding com-
plications of refeeding, most notably refeeding syndrome. Further treatment should 
address underlying cognitive distortions related to food and body image and under-
lying psychosocial contributors to the development of the disease. 
Due to the associated changes in the HPO axis, most women struggling with disor-
dered eating have associated changes in reproductive functioning. The experience and 
symptoms associated with pregnancy may also affect the course of an ED. Providers
