The impact of ADHD on affected indivi duals and their loved ones is enormous. 
The direct health care expenditures for the treatment of ADHD, as well as the indirect 
economic deficits (e.g., loss of working hours), are also substantial. A recent study es-
timated that the total excess cost of ADHD in the United States in 2000 was $31.6 bil-
lion, including treatment of ADHD, other health care costs for patients and their family 
members, and loss of work time (Birnbau m et al. 2005). A recent study examining a 
claims database of more than 3,000 adult patients with ADHD noted that they were 
significantly more likely than the comparison cohort without ADHD to have psychi-
atric and medical comorbidities, higher outpatient and inpatient costs, and higher pre-
scription drug and total medical costs (Secnik et al. 2005). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors for perinatal ADHD have not yet been clearly delineated. Childhood pre-
dictors of adult ADHD include family hi story of ADHD, the combined subtype of 
ADHD in childhood, increased symptom severity, comorbid depression or other men-
tal health conditions, social adversity, and parental psychopathology (Table 23–1) (Bie-
derman et al. 1996; Lara et al. 2009). The prevalence does not appear to differ by race 
or ethnicity or in response to treatment, but Black and Hispanic children are diagnosed 
with ADHD at much lower rates than white children (Alvarado and Modesto-Lowe 
2017). Although less is known about these differences in adult ADHD, it is clear that 
rates of detection are substantially lowe r among racial and et hnic minority groups 
(Chung et al. 2019). 
Screening 
Screening for adult or perinatal ADHD is not in widespread practice, although there 
have been calls for increasing recognition and detection of ADHD given its significant 
impact on functioning and on physical and mental health across the lifespan (Ebert et 
al. 2003; National Institute for Health and Care Excellence 2008; Nutt et al. 2007). Sev-
eral validated rating scales are in clinical use for the assessment of ADHD (Rösler et al. 
2006); rating scales specific to the perinatal population have yet to be established. Rat-
ing scales applicable for adults include the Wender-Utah Rating Scale (Ward et al. 1993) 
and the Childhood Symptoms Scale (Barkley and Murphy 1998), which retrospectively 
address childhood symptoms consistent with ADHD; self-report scales that mainly fo-
cus on the DSM criteria, such as the Connors Adult ADHD Rating Scale (Conners et al. 
1999) and the Adult Self Report Scale (Adler et al. 2003); and observer scales that can 
be used by clinicians or family members, such as the Brown Attention-Deficit Disorder 
Rating Scale (Brown 1996). Both the self-report and the observer scales use Likert scales
