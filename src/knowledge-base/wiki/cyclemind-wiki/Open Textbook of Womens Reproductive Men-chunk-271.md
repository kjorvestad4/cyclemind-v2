Key Points 
• Eating disorders (EDs) are a group of diagnoses characterized by distur -
bance in eating that lead to functional impairment and are associated with 
numerous potentially severe medical comorbidities. 
• In addition to strong genetic correlation, risk factors for the development of 
EDs include cultural influences of the thin ideal, specific psychological traits, 
psychosocial factors, and comorbid psychiatric disorders. 
• The perinatal period can be a high-risk time for onset or relapse of disordered 
eating behaviors in some, whereas for others it can be a time of motivation 
for recovery. 
• Given the connection with reproductive hormones, EDs can be associated 
with effects on puberty, menstruation, fertility, and pregnancy. 
• Active ED symptoms during pregnancy carry risk for both mother and baby. 
• Initial treatment of EDs is focused on weight restoration and nutritional reha-
bilitation, with focus on medical monito ring given risk of negative sequelae 
from refeeding syndrome. 
• Treatment most often requires a multidisciplinar y team approach, including 
behavioral medicine, physical medicine, and nutrition, with the major focus on 
psychotherapy approaches to address ED cognitions and behaviors. 
• Data on use of psychopharmacology in EDs are somewhat limited, although 
antidepressants (particularly for bulimia nervosa), antipsychotics (for anorexia 
nervosa), and psychostimulants (for binge eating disorder) have been studied, 
with some FDA approvals. 
• Treatment of EDs often requires var ied levels of care, including inpatient, par-
tial hospitalization, intensive outpatient, and outpatient options.

ADHD is an early onset, highly prevalent neurobehavioral disorder characterized by 
significant deficits in two major domains: inattention and hyperactivity/impulsivity 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013; Kooij et al. 2010; Spencer et al. 2007). ADHD 
is typically diagnosed in childhood, but it often persists into adulthood, leading to 
functional impairment in school, work, and social settings throughout the person’s life 
(Agnew-Blais et al. 2016; Asherson 2010; Ba rbaresi et al. 2013; Faraone et al. 2015). 
ADHD in adults was recognized relatively recently (McCarthy et al. 2009), thus a com-
prehensive understanding is still evolving (Kooij et al. 2010); in turn, there are many 
more questions than answers regarding ADHD in women and in the perinatal period. 
Despite the lack of comprehensive information, ADHD is an important condition to 
address in the setting of reproductive mental health. An increasing number of women 
of reproductive age are diagnosed with ADHD (Leong et al. 2017; Louik et al. 2015), 
and they are at substantially increased risk of comorbidities such as depression, anxiety, 
self-injury, and substance use disorders (Owens et al. 2017). They are likely to face sig-
nificant difficulties with work, school, interpersonal relationships, and everyday func-
tioning (Corbisiero et al. 2017; Eddy et al. 2019). Although this hypothesis has yet to 
be examined rigorously, pregnancy and new motherhood may represent a time of ad-
ditional significant psychosocial stress related to the large number of tasks associated 
with the transition to motherhood (Freeman 2014). An increasing number of women

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
ADHD is common neurodevelopmental disorder with a high prevalence throughout 
the lifespan (Cho et al. 2017; Kessler et al . 2006; Kooij et al. 2010). The prevalence in 
children and adolescents is approximately 3%–7% (Thomas et al. 2015), and in adults 
it ranges from 2% to 5% (Fayyad et al. 2007; Kessler et al. 2005; Kooij et al. 2005; Simon 
et al. 2009). Adult ADHD is underdiagnosed and undertreated due to its relatively re-
cent recognition as a disorder (McCarthy et al. 2009), the presence of mental health co-
morbidities that may “mask” it (Kooij et  al. 2010), shifting symptomatology from 
childhood to adulthood (Biederman et al. 2000), and stigma about the condition itself, 
particularly around the use of stimulant medication (Buitelaar 2001; Biederman et al. 
1993). ADHD is commonly seen in women, with an estimated prevalence of 3.2% (Kes-
sler et al. 2006). In childhood, the male to female sex ratio is approximately 3:1, but in 
adults it is closer to 1:1 (Cortese et al. 2016; Rösler et al. 2006). It is unclear what drives 
the change in ratio over time; it has been hypothesized that at least one factor may be 
detection failure, leading to an underdiagnosis of female ADHD in childhood (Davies 
2014; Young et al. 2020).

The impact of ADHD on affected indivi duals and their loved ones is enormous. 
The direct health care expenditures for the treatment of ADHD, as well as the indirect 
economic deficits (e.g., loss of working hours), are also substantial. A recent study es-
timated that the total excess cost of ADHD in the United States in 2000 was $31.6 bil-
lion, including treatment of ADHD, other health care costs for patients and their family 
members, and loss of work time (Birnbau m et al. 2005). A recent study examining a 
claims database of more than 3,000 adult patients with ADHD noted that they were 
significantly more likely than the comparison cohort without ADHD to have psychi-
atric and medical comorbidities, higher outpatient and inpatient costs, and higher pre-
scription drug and total medical costs (Secnik et al. 2005). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors for perinatal ADHD have not yet been clearly delineated. Childhood pre-
dictors of adult ADHD include family hi story of ADHD, the combined subtype of 
ADHD in childhood, increased symptom severity, comorbid depression or other men-
tal health conditions, social adversity, and parental psychopathology (
