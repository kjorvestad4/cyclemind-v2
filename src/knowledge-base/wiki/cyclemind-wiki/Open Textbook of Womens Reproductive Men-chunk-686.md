665 Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder 
Conclusion 
ADHD is a common, lifelong neurodevelopmental disorder that has a significant im-
pact on functioning in daily life in school, work, and social settings and on physical 
and mental health throughout the lifespan (Agnew-Blais et al. 2016; Barbaresi et al. 
2013; Faraone et al. 2015). Contrary to a prior understanding of ADHD solely as a 
childhood disorder, it is now clear that ADHD persists through the lifespan (Asherson 
2010) and that there may be a distinct adult-onset version. However, a comprehensive 
understanding of ADHD in adulthood, in women specifically, and in the perinatal pe-
riod is just beginning to be elucidated (Freeman 2014; Kooij et al. 2010). 
Because of its high prevalence and significant impact, ADHD is an important con-
dition to address in the setting of reproductive mental health. Women of reproductive 
age are increasingly diagnosed with ADHD (Leong et al. 2017; Louik et al. 2015), and 
they are at substantially increased risk of comorbidities, including depression, anxiety, 
and substance use disorders (Owens et al. 2017) in addition to significant difficulties 
with work, school, interpersonal relationships, and everyday functioning (Corbisiero 
et al. 2017; Eddy et al. 2019). When wo men with ADHD enter pregnancy and new 
motherhood, they may be under increased psychosocial stress, although data regard-
ing this time period are very limited (Freeman 2014). The use of stimulant medications 
by women of reproductive age has increased significantly, and in turn these medica-
tions are among the most commonly prescrib ed in pregnancy (Anderson et al. 2018; 
Haervig et al. 2014; Louik et al. 2015; Renoux et al. 2016). Yet our understanding of the 
reproductive safety profile of prescribed medications is limited and is complicated by 
significant confounding variables (Bro et al. 2015; Diav-Citrin et al. 2016; Haervig et 
al. 2014; Källén et al. 2013; Newport et al . 2016; Wright et al. 2015). Because prenatal 
factors are thought to be environmental risk factors for ADHD in the offspring, a ro-
bust understanding of ADHD in women in the perinatal period would serve to ad -
dress the mental health needs of their children in an intergenerational manner. 
A great deal remains to be discovered regarding the epidemiology, diagnosis, clini-
cal presentation, diagnostic criteria, screening and assessment, and treatment of adults 
with ADHD generally, and women with ADHD specifically—both within and outside 
of the perinatal period. Future directions for an understanding of ADHD and repro-
ductive mental health remain a wide-open field, and a greater understanding of this 
highly prevalent and impairing disorder could improve the mental and physical health 
of women and their offspring for multiple generations. 
Key Points 
• ADHD is a common neurodevelopmental disorder that has a lifelong impact. 
It is associated with major functional impairment in multiple arenas, such as 
work, school, and social life. 
• Adult ADHD is highly comorbid with other mental health conditions, including 
depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders.
