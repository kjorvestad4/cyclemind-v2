Psychosocial Precipitants 
In addition to biological factors, several psychological, behavioral, cultural, and med-
ical factors may contribute to the perinata l onset or exacerbation of OCD. These in -
clude overvaluing fears pertaining to the infant, performing repetitive behaviors to 
protect the infant that exacerbate fears, sleep deprivation (which also has biological 
consequences), and coping with medical comorbidities such as autoimmune diseases 
(Maina et al. 1999). 
Fear of Infant Harm 
Mild obsessive-compulsive symptoms ar e widespread and normative among new 
parents, with both fathers and mothers in several studies reporting unwanted intrusive 
thoughts about causing harm, both accidental and intentional, to their baby and trying 
strategies to get rid of these thoughts (Abramowitz et al. 2003, 2006; Collardeau et al. 
2019; Fairbrother and Woody 2008). The ubiquity of postpartum intrusive fears of in-
fant harm and preventative behaviors, as well as their genetic and biological basis, has 
led theorists to hypothesize that such thoughts may have conferred evolutionary sur-
vival value in the past, perhaps by increasing vigilance to danger or protective behav-
iors in settings that were more threatening than are typical in modern times (Ross and 
McLean 2006). Unfortunately , in a vulner able subset of women, intrusive thoughts 
around fear of infant harm can grow out of control, becoming more frequent, lasting, 
and severe.

Repetitive Behaviors 
As intrusive thoughts grow more distressing, women commonly take protective mea-
sure in response to these thoughts, a tendency that is natural yet maladaptive, exacer-
bating rather than ameliorating symptoms. Typical repetitive behaviors to protect the 
baby can include avoiding spending time with the baby, checking on a sleeping baby 
more often than necessary, taking unnecessary precautions with the baby, and devel-
oping time-consuming cleaning rituals. Both avoidance and other compulsive behav-
iors contribute to the cycle of intrusive thoughts: they result in a short-term reduction 
in anxiety caused by the thought, reinforc e the thought’s significance by deeming it 
worth acting on (negatively interpreting a harmless thought as a threat), and trigger 
the thought to recur, which in turn triggers a compensatory or neutralizing behavior,
