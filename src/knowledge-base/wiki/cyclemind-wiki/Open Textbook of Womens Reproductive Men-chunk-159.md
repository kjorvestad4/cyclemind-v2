Diagnostic Criteria 
BDs comprise a spectrum of diagnostic subtypes distinguished primarily by the symp-
toms and symptom severity experienced duri ng the acute phases of the illness. The 
subtypes included in DSM-5’s (American Psychiatric Association 2013) “Bipolar and 
Related Disorders” section are BDI, BDII, cyclothymic disorder, other specified bipolar 
and related disorder, unspecified bipolar and related disorder, substance/medication-
induced bipolar and related disorder, and bipolar and related disorder due to another 
medical condition. DSM-5 also contains a range of specifiers that may further clarify a 
patient’s clinical features, course, or severity. The “with peripartum onset” specifier 
can be applied to the current or most recent manic, hypomanic, or depressive episode 
if onset occurs either during pregnancy or in the first 4 weeks postpartum. 
The essential feature of BDI is the occurrence of a manic episode (Box 15–1). Al-
though depressive episodes are not necessary to meet the diagnostic criteria for BDI, 
manic episodes are often preceded or followed by hypomanic or major depressive ep-
isodes. In contrast, the diagnosis of BDII disorder requires at least one major depres-
sive episode and at least one hypomanic episode over the disease course. Cyclothymic 
disorder is characterized by subthreshold presentation of depressive and hypomanic 
episodes (American Psychiatric Association 2013). Patients with BD often have one or 
more comorbid psychiatric diagnoses. Anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, im-
pulse control disorders, and personality disorders are the most common comorbidities 
(Yatham et al. 2018).

Box 15–1. DSM-5 Criteria for Bipolar I Disorder 
For a diagnosis of bipolar I disorder, it is necessary to meet the following criteria for a manic 
episode. The manic episode may have been preceded by and may be followed by hypo-
manic or major depressive episodes. 
Manic Episode 
A. A distinct period of abnormally and persistently elevated, expansive, or irritable mood 
and abnormally and persistently increased activity or energy, lasting at least 1 week and 
present most of the day, nearly every day (or any duration if hospitalization is necessary). 
B. During the period of mood disturbance and increased energy or activity, three (or more) 
of the following symptoms (four if the mood is only irritable) are present to a significant 
degree and represent a noticeable change from usual behavior: 
1. Inflated self-esteem or grandiosity. 
2. Decreased need for sleep (e.g., feels rested after only 3 hours of sleep). 
3. More talkative than usual or pressure to keep talking. 
4. Flight of ideas or subjective experience that thoughts are racing. 
5. Distractibility (i.e., attention too easily drawn to unimportant or irrelevant external stim-
uli), as reported or observed. 
6. Increase in goal-directed activity (either socially, at work or school, or sexually) or 
psychomotor agitation (i.e., purposeless non-goal-directed activity). 
7. Excessive involvement in activities that have a high potential for painful consequences 
(e.g., engaging in unrestrained buying sprees, sexual indiscretions, or foolish busi-
ness investments). 
C. The mood disturbance is sufficiently severe to cause marked impairment in social or oc-
cupational functioning or to necessitate hospitalization to prevent harm to self or others, 
or there are psychotic features. 
D. The episode is not attributable to the physiological effects of a substance (e.g., a drug 
of abuse, a medication, other treatment) or another medical condition. 
Note: A full manic episode that emerges during antidepressant treatment (e.g., medica-
tion, electroconvulsive therapy) but persists at a fully syndromal level beyond the physi-
ological effect of that treatment is sufficient evidence for a manic episode and, therefore, 
a bipolar I diagnosis. 
Note: Criteria A–D constitute a manic episode. At least one lifetime manic episode is required 
for the diagnosis of bipolar I disorder.

Although the sex ratio of BDI is approximately 1:1, there are sex differences in the 
clinical presentation. Compared with men, women are more likely to experience hypo-
manic episodes, and the prevalence rate of BDII is higher among women (Baldassano 
et al. 2005; Moses-Kolko et al. 2019). Women may be slightly older than men on initial 
presentation and are more likely to experience comorbid thyroid disease (Baldassano 
et al. 2005), anxiety disorders (Saunders et al. 2012), and eating disorders (McElroy et 
al. 2016). Alternatively, men are more likely than women to present with comorbid sub-
stance use disorders (Grover et al. 2019). Although some studies have indicated that 
women are more likely than men to experience predominant depressive episodes (Alt-
shuler et al. 2010; Grover et al. 2019) and rapid cycling subtype (Di Florio and Jones 
2010; Erol et al. 2015), this literature is mixed, and other studies have not found such 
associations (Baldassano et al. 2005). 
Course/Prognosis 
BD is a chronic illness that typically re quires ongoing management throughout all 
stages of the disease course. On average, people living with BD experience symptoms 
of the disease for approximately half of their lives (Yatham et al. 2018). Although the 
psychosocial functioning of people with BD varies greatly among individuals, overall, 
BD is associated with significant morbidity and is one of the leading causes of years lost 
to disability worldwide. Factors associated with more  significant functional impair -
ments include higher frequency of acute episodes, prominent depressive symptoms, 
and lower cognitive status (Yatham et al. 2018). Patients with BD have an elevated risk 
of suicide relative to the general popula tion, and approximately 6%–7% of patients 
with BD die by suicide (Schaffer et al. 2015). 
Influence of Reproductive Transitions 
Menses. Research suggests that most women with BD experience an exacerbation of 
mood symptoms in the premenstrual period and that this exacerbation is a predictor 
of more severe disease course for women with BD (Dias et al. 2011; Payne et al. 2007; 
Teatero et al. 2014). Women with premenstrual exacerbation have been found to have 
greater symptom severity and greater frequency of mood episodes over the course of 
the disease (Dias et al. 2011). Women with comorbid premenstrual dysphoric disorder 
(PMDD) have an earlier age of onset of BD, higher rates of rapid cycling, more frequent 
mood episodes, and higher rates of psychiatric comorbidity and are more likely to re-

The effect of BD and associated treatments on the menstrual cycle is also a develop-
ing area of research (Reynolds-May et al . 2014; Teatero et al. 2014). Women with BD 
are more likely than healthy control subjects to retrospectively report menstrual abnor-
malities early in the reproductive years that predate the onset of BD (Joffe et al. 2006; 
Rasgon et al. 2000). Medications used to treat BD also may adversely affect the hypo-
thalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, resulting in menstrual and other hormonal ab-
normalities (Kenna et al. 2009). Valproate has been the mood stabilizer most strongly 
linked to HPG dysfunction. A study by Joffe et al. (2006) showed that 10% of women 
with BD treated with valproate developed oligomenorrhea with hyperandrogenism. 
It is important therefore to monitor women with BD for menstrual and hormonal ab-
normalities. Encourage patients to record monthly onset of their cycles (particularly in 
the first several months after starting a new pharmacological agent), which will help 
them detect more subtle abnormalities or changes. Women with BD should also be ed-
ucated about signs of hormonal dysfunction, such as virilization and changes in the 
menstrual cycle (Kenna et al. 2009). All women of reproductive age who are treated 
with valproate must be counseled about the risks of valproate exposure in pregnancy 
(as detailed later) and the need for adequate contraception.
