Components of the psychological transitions involved in becoming a parent and 
building a family also may lead to the de velopment of perinatal anxiety. The period 
surrounding childbirth is one of profound change and is often experienced as stressful

Last, the process of pregnancy, giving bi rth, and becoming a parent is embedded 
within the societal context in which birth occurs, which can play a major role in the 
etiology of perinatal anxiety. Substantial data link anxiety, depressed mood, and poor 
sleep with chronic psychosocial stress both during pregnancy and over the course of 
a woman’s lifetime. Commonly cited stresso rs worldwide include low material re -
sources, poor employment or financial co nditions, and heavy family and household 
responsibilities (Dunkel Schetter and Tanner  2012). Chronic psychosocial stress also 
includes being a member of a racial minority group that experiences discrimination and 
systemic racism (Christian 2020; Giscombé and Lobel 2005). Racism and discrimina -
tion contribute to negative birth outcomes independent of other types of stress (Domin-
guez et al. 2008), and several studies have linked these factors to adverse obstetrical 
outcomes, such as low birth weight (Love et al. 2010). A large population-based study 
in the United Kingdom concluded that the risk of perinatal anxiety, depression, and se-
rious mental illness was higher in socioeconomically deprived groups and increased 
with increased maternal age (Ban et al. 2012). 
Treatment 
A comprehensive algorithm for a treatment approach to perinatal anxiety does not yet 
exist. Generally, the approach for perinatal anxiety disorders, such as perinatal GAD, 
is similar to that for GAD in the general adult population. For mild GAD, nonmedica-
tion strategies such as cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) are effective; for moderate 
to severe anxiety, pharmacotherapy is indicated, typically in combination with psycho-
therapy (Misri et al. 2015). Generally, sele ctive serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) 
and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are the first-line agents for 
addressing perinatal anxiety, and benzodiazepines are effective for short-term use for 
acute anxiety symptoms; although data on atypical antipsychotics for anxiety are lim-
ited, use of these agents may be appropriate for severe manifestations if antidepressants 
or benzodiazepines have been insufficient to address symptoms (Misri et al. 2015).
