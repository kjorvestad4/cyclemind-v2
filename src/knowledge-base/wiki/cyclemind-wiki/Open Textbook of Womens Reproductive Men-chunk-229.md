Of note, there are sex differences in the use of HBRAs based on variable pharma -
cokinetics. In 2013, the FDA required the manufacturers of zolpidem to lower the rec-
ommended dosage for women from 10 mg to 5 mg (and from 12.5 mg to 6.25 mg for 
extended release) due to a concern for motor vehicle accidents, excessive daytime se-
dation, and next-morning functional impairment; women appear to be at higher risk 
of these adverse effects due to slower rate s of elimination of zolpidem as compared 
with men (Nowakowski and Meers 2019). This is the first and only drug in the United 
States to have different recommended dosages for women versus men (Greenblatt et 
al. 2014; Nowakowski and Meers 2019). 
Given the limited information for HBRAs, women who require pharmacological in-
tervention for severe sleep disturbance in pregnancy may wish to consider other op-
tions. If a sedative-hypnotic is required for sleep during pregnancy, a benzodiazepine 
may be preferred over an HBRA, especially if anxiety is associated with the sleep dis-
ruption. Both trazodone and sedating antihistamines, as discussed earlier (see “Anxiety 
Disorders in the Perinatal Period”), are options for insomnia as well. Another medica-
tion class to consider is sedating tricyclic antidepressants, such as amitriptyline or nor-
triptyline, especially if depressive symptoms are contributing to the sleep disturbance.

Management Steps. Postpartum women prescribed sleep aids should be counseled 
to have another adult available to care for their infants overnight due to the potential 
for difficulty waking after taking sedating medication and so that they are able to ob-
tain uninterrupted sleep. Women taking sedating medications should also receive spe-
cific counseling regarding safe sleep practices with their infants (Task Force on Sudden 
Infant Death Syndrome 2016).
