“Risk-Risk” Analysis 
After arriving at a diagnosis, the clinician needs to help the patient arrive at a decision 
about risk. Clinicians are familiar with risk-benefit decision making when discussing 
treatment recommendations that balance the potential benefit of a treatment against 
the known risks and help patients make the most informed decision possible among

long been underestimated or misunderstood, a more helpful approach when discuss-
ing psychiatric treatment during pregnancy is a “risk-risk” analysis, which requires 
assessing the risks associated with psychiatric medication exposure to the fetus and 
pregnancy versus the risks of untreated psychiatric illness to the mother and the de -

Given all of these various dangers, clinicians from all disciplines who treat perina-
tal women with mental health conditions ha ve a responsibility to partner with their 
patients to develop treatment plans that aim to mitigate both the risks of untreated or 
undertreated illness and the potential risks of tr eatment, including medication. Al -
though most women would likely prefer not to take any medications during preg -
nancy, many face significant morbidity if psychiatric medications are discontinued, 
changed, or not initiated due to an imbalanced assessment of these risks. Also, many 
women may not have a real choice as to whether to continue or start medication if they 
are not presented with the full range of evidence-based treatment options available to 
treat their symptoms, including psychotherapy, lifestyle changes, and social supports. 
The balance of risks will be different for every patient. Different risks will be faced

unique moment of opportunity for patients and families to engage in positive changes

By age 44 years, 85% of U.S. women will be mothers (Livingston 2018). Unintended 
pregnancies, resulting from both failure to use contraception and failure of the con -

2014; Mosher and Bachrach 1996). The rate of unintended pregnancies has decreased 
slightly in recent years but remains highest among young women (ages 18–24 years), 
low-income women, cohabiting women, and women of color (Finer and Zolna 2016).

It is also important to discuss, when a medication is first prescribed, whether it can 
be safely used during pregnancy, even if the patient is not currently sexually active or 
planning pregnancy. Furthermore, clinicians must be careful of their own bias in con-
sidering whether patients with mental illness may become pregnant: although fertil-
ity rates among women with severe mental illness are lower than among the general 
population, most women with severe and pe rsistent mental illness become mothers 
and describe motherhood as central to their existence (Jones et al. 2014). Asking 
women regularly about their plans to become pregnant, discussing contraceptive op-
tions, and questioning their plans in the event of an unplanned pregnancy help in ra-
tional treatment planning prior to concepti on, reduce the likelihood of a crisis, and 
may also reduce the likelihood of an unplanned pregnancy. It is vitally important to 
remember that this issue applies equall y to transgender men and nonbinary people 
who may also plan future pregnancies.

The Biopsychosocial Model 
Most clinicians strive for diagnostic accuracy, but the course of psychiatric illness and 
responses to treatment are often complex and informed by a mix of biological factors 
(e.g., genetic vulnerability, medical illness, substance use), psychological factors (e.g., 
trauma, early attachments), and social determinants (e.g., partner or family support,

the most appropriate recommendations to mitigate these risks. In addition, psychiat-

to engage in a true risk-risk discussion and create a rational treatment plan, the clini-

with which the perinatal patient presents, her response to past treatments, and the ra-
tionale for her current treatment (if she is already in treatment). 
For example, a young woman with a history of childhood abuse who acts out and 
abuses substances may have been diagnosed in adolescence as “bipolar” and treated 
with mood stabilizers; however, a careful review of her history might reveal that she 
would be more appropriately diagnosed with PTSD and substance dependence. An-
other woman, diagnosed with depression and treated with antidepressants for years, 
might reveal in a careful review that she ha s had cycles of hypomania as well as de-
pression. Many women who began treatment during an acute episode of depression 
might have been continued indefinitely on antidepressants by primary care providers 
without review of whether their depression is likely to recur or is comorbid with an 
anxiety disorder that may be amenable to other treatment options. 
Each of these scenarios has profound implications for treatment recommendations. 
The young woman with trauma might bene fit most from intensive trauma-focused 
psychotherapy and possibly an antidepres sant medication and may not need to be 
maintained on mood stabilizers that confer more risk in pregnancy. The woman with 
mood cycling, in contrast, could be at hi gher risk for postpartum psychosis if her 
symptoms represent an undiagnosed bipola r disorder. A woman with a single past 
depressive episode could attempt to taper off medications safely prior to pregnancy 
but may benefit from cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) to address residual anxiety 
and to prevent relapse. Treatment planning in pregnancy necessitates this careful re-
view of diagnosis so that patients and clinicians can consider the full range of appro-
priate treatment options that maximize safety and mental health.

Somatic Treatments and Interventions 
Accurate assessment of diagnosis and illness severity, as well as psychological factors, 
supports, and stressors, can help guide re commendations not only for psychiatric 
medications but also for evidence-based psychosocial interventions. Numerous stud-
ies support evidence-based psychotherapie s such as interpersonal psychotherapy 
and CBT as effective interventions for mild to moderate depression in pregnancy and 
the postpartum period (Genovez et al. 2018 ). Although less studied in the perinatal 
population, evidence-based psychotherapies for diagnoses other than depression are 
likely to remain effective. For example, a patient with comorbid borderline personal-
ity disorder and anxiety who is engaging in self-injurious behaviors may benefit most 
from dialectical behavioral therapy. Psychotherapeutic interventions should be con -
sidered for every perinatal patient and included in a discussion of treatment options, 
not simply as alternatives to medications but as interventions that might help mitigate 
residual symptoms, reduce the need for adjunctive medications such as benzodiaze-
pines, or allow for a trial reduction of medications. 
In addition to traditional psychotherapy, some “complementary” treatments have 
evidence to suggest their benefit in mitigating the burden of psychiatric illness in peri-
natal women. Interventions such as exercise, yoga, acupuncture, or massage may 
have only modest evidence for benefit in cl inical trials but, barring obstetric compli-
cations, are generally considered safe an d may promote overall well-being (Reza et 
al. 2018). Bright light therapy is another example of an underutilized tool for depres-
sive symptoms, with evidence of benefit when used alone or as augmentation in peri-
natal women (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2014). Given the clear connection between 
poor social or partner su pports and perinatal depression, other interventions that 
shore up these supports might be appropriate, such as peer support, particularly for 
women with a history of childhood adversity (Racine et al. 2018). A comprehensive, 
individualized treatment plan for perinatal patients should include discussion of the 
breadth of nonpharmacological interventions that could offer benefit with minimal 
risk of harm. Unfortunately, given the wide health care disparities in our society, not 
all patients will have access to high-quali ty nonpharmacological interventions or to 
strong social supports, and this reality must also be factored into the treatment plan. 
Minimize the Number of Adverse Exposures 
An important goal is to minimize potential adverse exposures to medication(s) and to 
maternal psychiatric illness. At one time, psychiatric providers often recommended 
discontinuing all psychiatric medications during the first trimester, when major organs 
are formed. However, the risk of psychiatri c relapse is high with discontinuation of 
medications: up to 70% of women with major depression and 85% or more of women 
with bipolar disorder relapse when their psychiatric medications are discontinued for 
pregnancy (Cohen et al. 2006; Viguera et al. 2000, 2007). The developing infant is then 
potentially exposed to the adverse effects of psychiatric illness. In this situation, psy-
chiatric medications are often restarted—and sometimes more are added in the case 
of a significant psychiatric relapse, result ing in the fetus being exposed to maternal 
psychiatric illness, the originally prescr ibed psychiatric medication(s), and poten -
tially other psychiatric medications. Such ri sk of increased exposure to both illness

Use Medications With Data When Possible 
Although all new medications must undergo some animal testing to evaluate poten-
tial toxicity in gestation, pregnant and lactating women are generally excluded from 
clinical trials that test safety and efficacy of new drugs, so human data in pregnancy 
are collected after the drug is approved. Effectively, this means that the longer a med-
ication has been available, the more data have been accumulated on the safety and 
risks of that medication in pregnancy. Medications new to the market generally do not 
have any human reproductive safety data; when possible, prescribers should consider 
the availability of such data when treating women of childbearing age and make rec-
ommendations accordingly, including considering a switch to an older medication 
that has more data if the patient is planning to conceive. 
The FDA pregnancy categories A–D and X were misleading and caused confusion 
for many physicians when the FDA combined into Category B those medications that 
had 1) reassuring animal data but no human data and 2) demonstrated an adverse ef-
fect in animals but no risk in humans (U.S. Food and Drug Administration 2014). Thus, 
many providers concluded that if a medication was in Category B, it was safe to use 
during pregnancy or was preferable to a Category C or D medi cation. However, be-
cause medications new to the market usually had animal data but no human data in 
pregnancy, they were often placed in Category B. Switching to a Category B medication 
from a Category C or D medication is no t always advisable because researchers may 
have much more data for and understand exactly what the risks are with those Cate-
gory C or D medications than for the Category B medication. The FDA pregnancy cat-
egories have been phased out in favor of a new labeling plan that includes a summary 
of the known data in pregnancy for each me dication and data for the illness(es) for 
which it is indicated. We have included this description of them here, however, because 
they continue to be available on the internet, and many patients still ask about them. 
It is also important to understand that more data about a medication does not neces-
sarily mean it is safer in pregnancy than a newer medication, particularly for a patient 
who did not do well in the past when taking an older medication. Clinicians must con-
sider the person’s past response to treatment as part of the “data” that help determine

Plan Ahead: Make Medication Changes Prior to Pregnancy 
In most cases, if medication changes are going to be made to decrease the risk to the 
fetus, they should be made prior to conception. Once a woman becomes pregnant 
while taking medication, the fetus has already been exposed to the medication. Switch-
ing at this point increases the number of medication exposures to the fetus and may 
increase the risk of relapse in the mother, in which case the fetus would be potentially 
exposed to the original medication and th e new medication, as well as the mother’s 
psychiatric illness. Unless a medication is a known teratogen, it makes more sense to 
continue the newer medication that is working well and thereby have one exposure 
for the fetus rather than two or more. 
Two psychiatric medications—valproic acid and carbamazepine—should be avoided 
during pregnancy if at all po ssible due to their high risk of major organ malforma -
tions. Either of these medications should be switched to another mood stabilizer prior 
to pregnancy so as to prevent even brief exposure to a fetus. Their use in any females 
of reproductive age should be limited to severe cases that have failed other manage-
ment strategies, and patients should be advised about the medications’ risks in preg-
nancy and counseled on effective birth control. 
Consider Age and Other Risk Factors When 
Designing a Treatment Plan for Pregnancy 
Other risk factors that can potentially impact pregnancy outcomes include, among oth-
ers, maternal age, tobacco use, and substance use. When designing a treatment plan for 
pregnancy, these other risk factors should be taken into account. For example, a young 
woman who is taking a newer psychiatric medication with less safety data will have the 
luxury of time to attempt to switch to another medication with more data before becom-
ing pregnant. In contrast, a woman in her early 40s might choose to proceed with attempt-
ing pregnancy sooner rather than wait for a medication change. Similarly, if a woman is 
using or abusing tobacco, marijuana, or other substances, these behaviors ideally should 
be addressed prior to attempting pregnancy so that the fetus is not exposed. In this case, 
smoking cessation and substance abuse treatment should take precedence over switching 
to a medication with more data. Likewise, if a woman with comorbid substance use dis-
order and psychiatric illness is more likely to relapse to using substances when psychiat-
rically ill, this risk is an important factor in any risk-risk discussion.

blood volume changes in late pregnancy, and increasing the dose of the medication 
above the FDA maximum to restabilize her symptoms is reasonable. In most cases, the 
dosage can be reduced again postpartum if the patient remains in remission. 
Many women and their providers may be reluctant to increase psychiatric medica-
tions during pregnancy even in the setting of psychiatric relapse or worsening of symp-
toms. A better practice is to educate patients about these changes and risks in pregnancy 
proactively, monitor symptoms closely, and increase medications if needed, shortly af-
ter symptoms emerge, with the goal of a healthy mother who is more likely to have a 
healthy pregnancy and positive pregnancy and infant outcomes (DeVane et al. 2006; 
Tracy et al. 2005).

Be Calm: Approach to an Unplanned Pregnancy 
In the case of an unplanned pregnancy, the first rule of thumb is not to panic. Ideally, 
the clinician and the patient will have already discussed the medication plan for preg-
nancy as described earlier (see “Expect the Unexpected Pregnancy and Prepare Ahead 
of Time”). Regardless, it is important not to immediately stop all psychiatric medica-
tions. Sudden discontinuation of psychiat ric medications can cause great stress and 
anxiety for the patient, precipitate withdrawal, and may precipitate a relapse of mental 
illness (Einarson 2005). The best approach is to review the medication list based on 
the principles outlined previously for pre-pregnancy planning and, if the decision is 
to discontinue a medication, to taper its removal. The baby has been exposed already, 
and although stopping some medications may make sense to minimize the impact on 
the fetus, doing so in a controlled and logical fashion is generally ideal. 
Also, in the setting of an unplanned pregnancy, it might not make sense to switch 
from a newer medication to an older one with more reproductive safety data. Such a 
switch might have made sense prior to pregnancy, but as mentioned, switching during 
pregnancy increases the number of exposures for the fetus, who has already been ex-
posed to the newer medication. In addition, the likelihood that the patient would relapse 
while switching is high, and the exposure to the psychiatric illness would constitute a 
third exposure for the child. Unless a particular medication has been clearly associated 
with significant risk (i.e., valproic acid, carbamazepine), continuation of the current reg-
imen may be most appropriate.
