Stimulant Drugs of Abuse 
Prenatal cocaine exposure results in vasocons triction that can affect the fetus at any 
stage of pregnancy (McLafferty et al. 2016 ). Several large studies have consistently 
identified several adverse outcomes associ ated with cocaine use during pregnancy, 
including premature rupture of membranes, placental abruption, preterm birth, low 
birth weight, and small for gestational age infants (Forray 2016; Gouin et al. 2011). The 
long-term effects of prenatal cocaine exposure on cognitive, motor, and language de-
velopment have been inconsistent, with some studies reporting positive findings, and 
some studies finding small or no effects (Frank et al. 2001). This inconsistency is likely 
related to the confounding effects of the postnatal environment, including dysfunc -
tional parenting, unstable home environm ents, and frequent rates of polysubstance 
abuse (Forray 2016). Its high lipid solubility and bioavailability allow cocaine to cross 
readily into maternal plasma and into the breastmilk. Case reports and case series are 
available of infants developing cocaine intoxication through breastmilk exposure 
(D’Apolito 2013). 
Like cocaine use in pregnancy, methamphetamine use has been linked with shorter 
gestational ages, lower birth weight, fetal loss, developmental effects, preeclampsia, 
gestational hypertension, and intrauterine fetal death (Forray 2016). Infants who were 
exposed to methamphetamines in utero may display trouble feeding, sleep disruption, 
and abnormalities of muscle tone, which resolve spontaneously within several weeks 
(McLafferty et al. 2016). Amphetamines can inhibit prolactin release and may reduce 
breastmilk supply. Case reports show maternal serum to breastmilk ratios ranging from 
1:1 to 1:7.5, with no clinical correlation regarding effect (McLafferty et al. 2016). 
Opioids 
Although prescription and illicit opioids are not known to be directly teratogenic to the 
fetus (McLafferty et al. 2016), significant risks are associated with untreated maternal 
opioid use disorder. Obstetrical complications can include preeclampsia, intrauterine 
growth restriction, placental insufficiency and abruption, premature rupture of mem-
branes, postpartum hemorrhage, low Apgar scores, preterm labor, and fetal demise 
(McLafferty et al. 2016). The exact mechanism by which opioid use has these effects is 
unclear, but it is hypothesized that the poor outcomes are related to repeated peaks and 
troughs in serum levels, intermittent exposure to opioid withdrawal, and concurrent 
lifestyle factors (McLafferty et al. 2016). Neonatal risks associated with maternal opi-
oid use disorder include postnatal growth deficiency, microcephaly, neurobehavioral 
problems associated with neonatal abstin ence syndrome, and sudden infant death 
syndrome. Neonatal abstinence syndrome, also known as neonatal opioid withdrawal 
syndrome, is a postnatal withdrawal syndrome that can occur in about 70% of neonates 
exposed to opioids in utero and is characterized by irritability, feeding difficulties, 
tremors, hypertonia, emesis, loose stools, seizures, and respiratory distress (Kocherla-
kota 2014). Breastfeeding has been shown to reduce neonatal abstinence syndrome se-
verity in opioid-exposed newborns and sh ould be encouraged (Reece-Stremtan and 
Marinelli 2015). Some infants will require treatment with opioid agonist medications if 
symptoms cannot be managed through nonpharmacological means. 
Recent longitudinal studies comparing demographically matched groups of infants 
exposed and not exposed to methadone in utero showed some cognitive and motor de-
lays in the exposed infants, but this is likely influenced by environmental risk factors

Differential Diagnosis 
Not all substance use in pregnancy constitutes an SUD; therefore, clinicians must as-
sess carefully to determine whether DSM-5 criteria for SUDs are met, incorporating 
patient self-report, available laboratory da ta, and external information (e.g., legal 
charges related to substance use) when available. 
Sociodemographic Considerations 
Gender and Racial Disparities 
Significant and widespread gender and racial disparities exist in the legal treatment of 
SUDs. For example, women, especially women of color, are disproportionately incar-
cerated for nonviolent drug offenses compared with men (Malinowska-Sempruch and 
Rychkova 2015). The commonly held but erroneous assumption that people of color 
are more likely to use drugs affects how people of color are treated by medical provid-
ers. For example, in Pinellas County, Florida, one study found that Black women with 
a positive drug screen were reported to authorities at 10 times the rate of white women 
(Chasnoff et al. 1990). Another example is demonstrated in the legal consequences for 
use of crack cocaine—commonly associated  with Black Americans—as compared with 
powder cocaine, more commonly used by white individuals. In the 1980s, substance 
use in pregnancy gained widespread attention with the media reporting an explosion 
of “crack babies,” mostly in the Black community (Chira 1990; Winerip 2013). 
In the 1980s, President Reagan signed the Anti-Drug Abuse Act of 1986 into law, en-
acting harsh punishments for drug crimes, such as mandatory minimum sentences for 
the distribution of cocaine and severe punishment for the distribution of crack cocaine. 
Over time, attitudes in the United States have shifted over how harshly drug offenders 
should be punished, resulting in changes in the law. In 2010, the Fair Sentencing Act 
was passed, which tried to address the disparity in punishment between crack cocaine 
and powder cocaine offenders, and the First Step Act, signed by President Trump in 
2018, gave judges more leeway to cut down mandatory minimum sentences and made 
the Fair Sentencing Act retroactive to apply to people sentenced prior to its passage 
(Fair Sentencing Act 2010; First Step Act 2018). 
Legal Considerations 
Pregnant women with SUDs continue to face a range of legal consequences, from crim-
inal to civil, with the most extreme consequences resulting in incarceration and termi-
nation of parental rights (Angelotta et al. 2016). Although no state currently has a law 
that explicitly defines substance use in pregnancy as a criminal offense, women have 
been charged under a range of criminal offenses for using substances while pregnant, 
including criminal child endangerment (Reyes v. Superior Court of the State of California 
1977), delivery of a controlled substance to a minor via the umbilical cord (Johnson v. 
State of Florida 1992), and manslaughter (State of Hawaii v. Aiwohi 2005) and had con -
victions upheld of criminal child neglect and homicide (Whitner v. South Carolina 1997;

McKnight v. South Carolina 2008) and chemical endangerment of a child ( Ankrom v. 
State of Alabama 2011) (Angelotta et al. 2016). Twen ty-three states and the District of 
Columbia consider substance use during pregnancy to be child abuse, and three con-
sider it grounds for civil commitment. 
Under the federal Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act, health care provid-
ers are required to notify state agencies about substance-exposed infants under their 
care (Keeping Children and Families Safe Act 2003). Methods for detecting substance 
use and indications for reporting vary based on state interpretation: 25 states and the 
District of Columbia require health care professionals to report suspected prenatal 
drug use, and 8 states require them to test for prenatal drug exposure if there is high 
suspicion for use (Guttmacher Institute 2021). Although this legislation is intended to 
protect children, it can also deter women from engaging in care and can interfere with 
the relationship between health care providers and patients (Angelotta et al. 2016; Po-
land et al. 1993). One study found that women using cocaine or opiates were more 
than six times more likely to have received little to no prenatal care, with fear of re -
porting to police or child welfare authorities cited as one of the greatest barriers to en-
gagement (Schempf and Strobino 2009). 
We know that engagement in prenatal care can reduce harmful effects of substance 
use in pregnancy. Engagement in prenatal care alone reduces risk of low birth weight 
and prematurity, factors strongly associated with neonatal mortality (El-Mohandes et 
al. 2003). Multiple professional organizations such as the American Academy of Pedi-
atrics, American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, American Medical Asso-
ciation, and American Psychiatric Association have called for universal access, early 
intervention, and timely initiation of care for women with SUDs in the perinatal period 
and caution against discrimination and social or legal measures that may thwart these 
efforts (American Psychiatric Association 2021). Thus, we have an opportunity in 
pregnancy because women may be more motivated to seek treatment and abstain from 
substance use in this period (Forray et al. 2015). This contrasts with high postpartum 
relapse rates, when stressors potentially increase and support often diminishes (Forray 
et al. 2015). 
Evidence consistently demonstrates support for low-barrier access to comprehen-
sive prenatal care and addictions treatmen t (El-Mohandes et al. 2003; Lester et al. 
2004) in improving both neonatal and maternal outcomes. Conversely, the deleterious 
effects of punitive policies have been shown to deter women from seeking treatment 
and to interfere with the therapeutic relationship between doctor and patient (Ange-
lotta et al. 2016; Paltrow et al. 2000; Pola nd et al. 1993; Schempf and Strobino 2009).

listens to Ms. GG describe her goals and nonjudgmentally explores her ambivalence

Given Ms. GG’s anxiety about CPS, the doctor reviews the state’s reporting require-
ments, highlighting the shared goals between Ms. GG and her treatment team and the 
future support that will be provided regardless of whether a CPS investigation is opened. 
Together, they develop a treatment plan to help Ms. GG to work toward her goals while 
respecting her autonomy to choose which recommendations to accept at this time. 
Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
It is well established that estrogen’s effects on dopamine systems may contribute to sex 
differences in addiction. Estrogen facilitates release of dopamine (Bobzean et al. 2014). 
Studies of the menstrual cycle in rodents and humans have found that the follicular 
phase is associated with greater positive subjective effects of cocaine, relative to the lu-
teal phase. Administration of progesterone attenuates the subjective response to co -
caine (Evans and Foltin 2010). Among postpartum women, progesterone treatment 
has been associated with lower rates of cocaine relapse (McHugh et al. 2018). 
Neuroimaging research has identified sex differences in brain volume and function 
in response to several substances. For example, both females and males with cocaine 
use disorder exhibited lower gray matter volume relative to control subjects, but the 
regions exhibiting these volu metric differences varied (McHugh et al. 2018). Hypo-
thalamic-pituitary-adrenocortical dysregulation in women may play a role in vulner-
ability to substance use relapse in the contex t of negative affect, such as in times of 
emotional stress (Greenfield et al. 2010). The possible neurobiological effects of preg-
nancy on reward circuitry and substance us e are largely unknown, so it is unclear 
whether reductions in substance use during pregnancy are mediated by psychosocial 
factors or whether biology may also play a role. One study found that gray matter cor-
tical volume was reduced in postpartum mothers who had used substances during 
pregnancy compared with mothers who had not used substances, particularly in fron-
tal brain regions, and that gray matter vo lume was negatively correlated with levels 
of behavioral activation in substance-using mothers (Rutherford et al. 2015).

Psychological Factors 
Mood and anxiety disorders are common among women with SUDs. In a national 
epidemiological survey conducted in 2009,  12-month prevalence rates of mood and 
anxiety disorders in women with SUDs were 29.7% and 26.2%, respectively (Green -
field et al. 2010). A comprehensive psychiat ric assessment is crucial to differentiate 
substance-induced, transient symptoms from a separate, non-drug-related disorder. 
Many women who use substances in pregnancy have significant trauma histories, 
including physical and sexual abuse. Among treatment-seeking women with SUDs, 
rates of physical and sexual abuse are as high as 55%–99% (Greenfield et al. 2010). It 
has been reported that one-third to one-half of substance-abusing women had experi-
enced some kind of sexual abuse during childhood (Hans 1999). Women with SUDs 
are more likely to have been raised by parents with SUDs. Although the intergenera-
tional patterns of substance use may have some genetic basis, ample evidence also sug-
gests problematic relationships in families with a parent with SUD that raises concerns 
about intergenerational transmission of problematic parenting behavior (Hans 1999). 
Social Considerations 
Social risk factors for problematic substance use in pregnancy include unintended 
pregnancy, lower level of education, un employment, younger age, environmental 
stress, intimate partner violence, easy access to substances, and lack of knowledge of 
the impact of substances on fetal development (Louw 2018). Women with SUDs tend 
to live more chaotic lives. They are often involved with men who also use drugs (Hans 
1999). The common perception of substance abuse as a problem of poor women of 
color is inaccurate, and this perception may be acted on by medical providers in a prej-
udicial manner (Hans 1999). 
Treatment 
Overview 
Despite the high prevalence of use, women only account for one in five individuals in 
substance use treatment, indicating barriers to access unique to women (Meyer et al. 
2019). Pregnancy may present a window of opportunity for the treatment of SUDs. 
During this time, women are more likely to have access to health insurance and are of-
ten motivated toward positive health behaviors. Health care providers have multiple 
points of contact with pregnant women and can work with women to improve their 
health, potentially preventing t h e  g e n e r a t i o n a l  t r a n s m i s s ion of this chronic disease. 
However, during pregnancy, women may avoid substance use treatment due to fear of 
legal and social ramifications; fewer than 10% of pregnant patients with SUDs receive 
any treatment for those disorders (Ait-Daoud et al. 2019; Cook et al. 2017; Ryan 2019).

women with nicotine use disorders. A total of eight randomized controlled trials in -
cluding 2,199 women have been conducted with nicotine replacement therapy, and 
one trial including 11 women has been co nducted with bupropion as an adjunct to

Overall, nicotine replacement therapy appears to increase the rate of perinatal smok-
ing cessation by 40%, but when nonplacebo randomized controlled trials are excluded 
from analyses, it is no more effective than placebo for smoking cessation in pregnant 
women (Coleman et al. 2015). Compliance in the randomized controlled trials was 
low and may explain the lack of efficacy of nicotine replacement therapy for perinatal 
smoking cessation. The rates of poor obstetrical outcomes (e.g., miscarriage, stillbirth, 
premature birth, low birth weight, admission to neonatal intensive care, cesarean sec-
tion, congenital abnormalities, neonatal death) among women assigned to nicotine re-
placement therapy, compared with women assigned to placebo or a control group, 
appear to be similar, and the long-term impact of nicotine replacement therapy on 
child development is unknown. Due to its lack of efficacy for perinatal smoking ces-
sation, nicotine replacement therapy is not routinely recommended. However, it may 
be considered in a woman who has a history of successful smoking cessation with the 
use of this therapy in the past, after carefully weighing the risks of continued nicotine 
use compared with the use of nicotine replacement therapy. 
The primary treatment for perinatal nicotine use disorders includes behavioral in-
terventions, such as motivational interv iewing or contingency management, which 
have shown modest success in reducing smoking or relapse to nicotine use in pregnant 
women (Chamberlain et al. 2013; Heckma n et al. 2010). Co ntingency management 
with financial incentives appears to be the most effective for smoking cessation during 
pregnancy (Chamberlain et al. 2013; Lerfino et al. 2015); however, implementation of 
financial incentives can be challenging fo r treatment programs. Treatments aimed at 
relapse prevention in the postpartum period have included psychoeducation and mo-
tivational interviewing and have demonstrated modest success (Levitt et al. 2007). 
Alcohol Use Disorders 
No known studies have evaluated the efficacy of FDA-approved pharmacological in-
terventions for the treatment of alcohol use disorder (i.e., naltrexone, disulfiram, aca-
mprosate) among pregnant women in genera l or pregnant women with alcohol use 
disorder (Zagon et al. 1998). However, clinical data using naltrexone for the treatment 
of opioid use disorder during pregnancy are available, and, overall, naltrexone does 
not appear to be associated with poor birth outcomes (Wachman et al. 2019). Interpre-
tation of these data is limited by small sample sizes, lack of control groups, minimal 
control for confounding variables, and lack of long-term follow-up. Pharmacological 
treatments for perinatal alcohol use disorder are not routinely recommended due to 
the paucity of efficacy and safety data. However, patients and providers need to care-
fully weigh the well-known risks of alcoho l use in pregnancy against the potential 
benefits and both known and unknown risks of pharmacotherapy. 
No studies to our knowledge have systematically examined the effects of perinatal 
alcohol withdrawal or benzodiazepine tr eatment for perinatal alcohol withdrawal.

The use of benzodiazepines for perinatal alcohol withdrawal, however, would pose 
less risk than untreated alcohol withdrawal or continued use of alcohol during preg-
nancy (Thibaut et al. 2019). Historical studies examining the risks of benzodiazepine 
use during pregnancy have shown inconsistent associations with fetal malformations 
(DeVido et al. 2015). Recent data in this area, however, are more reassuring and show 
that prospective cohort studies are less likely than case control studies to show an as-
sociation between fetal malformations and benzodiazepine use (B ellantuono et al. 
2013). Recent studies have raised concerns  about possible associations between ben -
zodiazepine use and ectopic pregnancy and spontaneous abortion, but thus far these 
results have not been replicated (Sheehy et al. 2019; Wall-Wieler et al. 2020). Chronic 
use of benzodiazepines during pregnancy is associated with benzodiazepine with -
drawal in the newborn, particularly with higher doses (DeVido et al. 2015). Clinicians 
and their patients must weigh the risks of benzodiazepine exposure against the risks 
of alcohol withdrawal or co ntinued alcohol use during pregnancy. Although benzo-
diazepine use is justified for the treatment of alcohol withdrawal, ongoing benzodi -
azepine maintenance treatment for alcohol use disorder is not supported by evidence. 
Behavioral interventions evaluated for th e reduction of alcohol use in pregnancy 
include motivational enhancement therapy, brief psychodynamic psychotherapy, in-
terpersonal psychotherapy, educational interventions, family-focused therapy, pro -
fessional group education, self-help, and cognitive-behavioral therapy (DeVido et al. 
2015; Stade et al. 2009). Most treatments have shown some benefit in increasing absti-
nence or reducing alcohol consumption among pregnant women. Given the limited 
number of studies with small sample sizes and a high risk of bias, however, it is un -
clear if one intervention type is superior (Stade et al. 2009). 
Cannabis 
To date, no pharmacological treatments are available for cannabis use disorders. Mo-
tivational interviewing, cognitive-behavioral therapy, and contingency management 
demonstrate some success in the reduction of cannabis use among adult women, but 
these interventions have not specifically been evaluated among pregnant women (For-
ray 2016). Providers are encouraged to screen for cannabis use in pregnancy, inform 
women of the known risks associated with marijuana use, and provide evidence-based 
psychotherapy to support the reduction of perinatal cannabis use (American College 
of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2017b). 
Stimulants 
Pharmacological interventions are not available at this time for the treatment of cocaine 
or methamphetamine use disorders. Motivational interviewing, cognitive-behavioral 
therapy, and contingency management have shown benefit in reducing cocaine use, 
with contingency management having the greatest benefit for the reduction of perina-
tal cocaine use (Terplan et al. 2015). Fewer studies have examined interventions to re-
duce perinatal methamphetam ine use, but one small pilo t study demonstrated the 
feasibility of reinforcement-based treatment, which includes a combination of motiva-
tional interviewing, highly individualized treatment planning, and case management 
(Jones et al. 2014).

Opioids 
The standard of care for the treatment of perinatal opioid use disorder includes phar-
macotherapy with either methadone or buprenorphine (Substance Abuse and Mental 
Health Services Administration 2018) as part of a comprehensive treatment program 
including prenatal care, psychological an d pharmacological treatment interventions 
for relapse prevention, psychological and pharmacological treatment interventions to 
address comorbid trauma or other mental health problems, and social services to ad-
dress the myriad psychosocial stressors that commonly occur in women with opioid 
use disorder. Buprenorphine (including monotherapy and buprenorphine+naloxone 
formulations) and methadone are considered first-line treatment for opioid use disorder 
in pregnancy and are recommended by major organizations including the American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, American Society for Addiction Medicine, 
United Nations, and World Health Organization as the optimal approach to treatment 
of opioid use disorder during pregnancy (Jones and Kraft 2019). 
Although maternal and delivery outcomes are similar for women treated with bu-
prenorphine or methadone, infants exposed to buprenorphine are less likely to require 
medication for treatment of neonatal opioid withdrawal syndrome, require less medi-
cation for treatment of this syndrome if medication is required, and have shorter hospi-
tal stays postdelivery (Jones and Kraft 2019). Fetuses exposed to buprenorphine appear 
to have higher levels of heart rate variability, more heart rate accelerations, greater cou-
pling between heart rate and movement, le ss motor activity suppression, and lower 
likelihood of a nonreactive nonstress test than those exposed to methadone (Klaman et 
al. 2017). Some data suggest a greater incidence of intrauterine growth restriction in fe-
tuses exposed to methadone (Klaman et al. 2017). Current literature does not indicate 
an increased risk for birth defects in in fants exposed to maternal buprenorphine or 
methadone treatment (Klaman et al. 2017); thus, no compelling evidence suggests that 
methadone is either superior or inferior to buprenorphine for the treatment of opioid 
use disorder (Minozzi et al. 2013), and the decision to choose methadone or buprenor-
phine is largely driven by patient preference, feasibility, and prior treatment response. 
Pregnant women with opioid use disorder should be prescribed naloxone, and 
partners and family members should be educated on the administration of this drug 
in the context of a life-threatening overdose, as well as the risks associated with peri-
natal withdrawal after the administration of naloxone. Opioid taper or opioid detox-
ification, also known as medication-assisted withdrawal, is not recommended during 
pregnancy due to the high risk for relapse to substance use and the maternal, fetal, and 
newborn consequences associated with return to drug use (Guille et al. 2017; Terplan 
et al. 2018). Additionally, the risks of maternal, obstetrical, fetal, and newborn compli-
cations associated with opioid withdrawal during pregnancy are largely unknown 
(Terplan et al. 2018). 
Despite the recommendation against medi cation-assisted withdrawal and lack of 
evidence to support its use in reducing th e risk of newborn opioid withdrawal syn -
drome, women may want to pursue this treatment approach. It is important that pro-
viders and patients have a balanced, evidence-based discussion with respect to the 
risks of medications used to treat opioid use disorder in pregnancy and the risks of 
untreated illness. A shared decision-making tool is available to provide information
