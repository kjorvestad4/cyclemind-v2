Under the federal Child Abuse Prevention and Treatment Act, health care provid-
ers are required to notify state agencies about substance-exposed infants under their 
care (Keeping Children and Families Safe Act 2003). Methods for detecting substance 
use and indications for reporting vary based on state interpretation: 25 states and the 
District of Columbia require health care professionals to report suspected prenatal 
drug use, and 8 states require them to test for prenatal drug exposure if there is high 
suspicion for use (Guttmacher Institute 2021). Although this legislation is intended to 
protect children, it can also deter women from engaging in care and can interfere with 
the relationship between health care providers and patients (Angelotta et al. 2016; Po-
land et al. 1993). One study found that women using cocaine or opiates were more 
than six times more likely to have received little to no prenatal care, with fear of re -
porting to police or child welfare authorities cited as one of the greatest barriers to en-
gagement (Schempf and Strobino 2009). 
We know that engagement in prenatal care can reduce harmful effects of substance 
use in pregnancy. Engagement in prenatal care alone reduces risk of low birth weight 
and prematurity, factors strongly associated with neonatal mortality (El-Mohandes et 
al. 2003). Multiple professional organizations such as the American Academy of Pedi-
atrics, American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, American Medical Asso-
ciation, and American Psychiatric Association have called for universal access, early 
intervention, and timely initiation of care for women with SUDs in the perinatal period 
and caution against discrimination and social or legal measures that may thwart these 
efforts (American Psychiatric Association 2021). Thus, we have an opportunity in 
pregnancy because women may be more motivated to seek treatment and abstain from 
substance use in this period (Forray et al. 2015). This contrasts with high postpartum 
relapse rates, when stressors potentially increase and support often diminishes (Forray 
et al. 2015). 
Evidence consistently demonstrates support for low-barrier access to comprehen-
sive prenatal care and addictions treatmen t (El-Mohandes et al. 2003; Lester et al. 
2004) in improving both neonatal and maternal outcomes. Conversely, the deleterious 
effects of punitive policies have been shown to deter women from seeking treatment 
and to interfere with the therapeutic relationship between doctor and patient (Ange-
lotta et al. 2016; Paltrow et al. 2000; Pola nd et al. 1993; Schempf and Strobino 2009).
