Treatment 
The treatment of perinatal OCD has not been studied extensively; therefore, treat -
ment is conducted in the same manner as for general OCD, with adaptations appro-
priate to the perinatal context (Hudak and Wisner 2012). Treatment is indicated when 
OCD symptoms interfere with functioning or cause significant distress (Koran et al. 
2007). Given the negative impact on the entire family system, including the infant, 
perinatal OCD should be treated aggressively (Brandes et al. 2004). Psychiatric man-
agement consists of an array of therapeuti c modalities, most of which fall under the 
categories of pharmacotherapy (mainly serotonin reuptake inhibitors [SRIs]) and cog-
nitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) ( Figure 20–1). Presently, there does not seem to be 
any theoretical basis for predicting that perinatal OCD would respond differently to 
these interventions than does general OCD (Abramowitz et al. 2003). 
The selection of which intervention to use in the perinatal population depends on a 
host of factors, including severity of illness, co-occurring psychiatric and medical con-
ditions, past psychiatric and treatment history, current safety concerns, degree of im-
pairment in functioning, patient preferences, cultural and personal values, motivation 
for treatment, family/caregiver involvement in symptoms and role in support, and 
risk profile of both available treatments and no treatment (Koran et al. 2007). Given 
this complexity, allotting extra time and support in the decision-making process, to the 
extent that is reasonable and not compromising safety, is key when working with peri-
natal patients (see Chapter 8, “A Clinical Approach to Psychiatric Diagnosis and Treat-
ment During Pregnancy”). 
An important first step is to establish and maintain a strong therapeutic alliance, be-
cause this facilitates sharing of goals and eventual treatment adherence. Key features of 
alliance-building with patients with OCD include instilling hope, listening attentively, 
repeating explanations (given the excessive doubting characteristic of OCD), and ex-
ploring patients’ explanatory model of their symptoms (Koran et al. 2007; Sadock et al. 
2000). Regarding the latter, patients can often blend symptoms with aspects of their in-
ner selves (Nikodijevic et al. 2015), leading to confusion and overall marked distress. 
This is especially true in perinatal cases, when intrusive thoughts involving harm to the 
baby raise feelings of guilt, shame, and fear (Fairbrother and Woody 2008)—so much 
so that patients are often reluctant to share their symptoms with others, including pro-
viders. Providing reassurance and making use of psychoeducation are therefore critical. 
Many patients are relieved to learn basic facts about OCD, including its prevalence, 
the shame that many patients experience, that it is not due to “bad mothering,” and that 
treatments can be effective (Koran et al. 2 007). For patients who are particularly dis-
tressed by intrusive thoughts of infant harm, an explanation that such symptoms are 
common in perinatal OCD and do not typically reflect true desires or inner character 
especially resonates. Of course, this should be accompanied by a thorough safety eval-
