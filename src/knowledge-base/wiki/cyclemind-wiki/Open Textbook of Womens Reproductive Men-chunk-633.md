Behavioral interventions evaluated for th e reduction of alcohol use in pregnancy 
include motivational enhancement therapy, brief psychodynamic psychotherapy, in-
terpersonal psychotherapy, educational interventions, family-focused therapy, pro -
fessional group education, self-help, and cognitive-behavioral therapy (DeVido et al. 
2015; Stade et al. 2009). Most treatments have shown some benefit in increasing absti-
nence or reducing alcohol consumption among pregnant women. Given the limited 
number of studies with small sample sizes and a high risk of bias, however, it is un -
clear if one intervention type is superior (Stade et al. 2009). 
Cannabis 
To date, no pharmacological treatments are available for cannabis use disorders. Mo-
tivational interviewing, cognitive-behavioral therapy, and contingency management 
demonstrate some success in the reduction of cannabis use among adult women, but 
these interventions have not specifically been evaluated among pregnant women (For-
ray 2016). Providers are encouraged to screen for cannabis use in pregnancy, inform 
women of the known risks associated with marijuana use, and provide evidence-based 
psychotherapy to support the reduction of perinatal cannabis use (American College 
of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2017b). 
Stimulants 
Pharmacological interventions are not available at this time for the treatment of cocaine 
or methamphetamine use disorders. Motivational interviewing, cognitive-behavioral 
therapy, and contingency management have shown benefit in reducing cocaine use, 
with contingency management having the greatest benefit for the reduction of perina-
tal cocaine use (Terplan et al. 2015). Fewer studies have examined interventions to re-
duce perinatal methamphetam ine use, but one small pilo t study demonstrated the 
feasibility of reinforcement-based treatment, which includes a combination of motiva-
tional interviewing, highly individualized treatment planning, and case management 
(Jones et al. 2014).
