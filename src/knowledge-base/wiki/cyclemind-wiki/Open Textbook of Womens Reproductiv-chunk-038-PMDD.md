Clinical Presentation 
The clinical presentation of perimenopausal depression has many of the features of de-
pressive episodes outside the perimenopausal period: low mood, anhedonia, poor en-
ergy and low motivation, feelings of low self-worth, and sometimes suicidal ideation. 
Changes in sleep, appetite, concentration, and libido can also occur as part of MDD and 
can be challenging to distinguish from the symptoms of perimenopause. Sleep disrup-
tion is a common feature of depression in this period and can be mistaken for new-onset 
or previously undiagnosed bipolar disorder, which remains rare despite a possibly in-
creased prevalence during perimenopause (see earlier subsection “Other Mental Ill -
nesses in Perimenopause”). Perimenopause may trigger exacerbations of previously 
identified bipolar disorder, including manic episodes, although depressions are more 
common. The following case represents a typical constellation of symptoms that a pro-

Social challenges are common in perimenopausal women and may trigger or exac-
erbate mood disturbances. Physical complaints or medical comorbidities may distract 
from or mask depressive etiologies in this population. Finally, alcohol and substance 
use often complicate the picture of depression at midlife. The following case illustrates 
the challenge of delineating social stressors, medical issues, and the use of medications, 
substances, or drugs in order to develop a differential diagnosis and appropriate treat-
ment recommendations. 
Ms. N is a 50-year-old woman who presents with difficulty sleeping and low mood that 
started about 3 months prior in the setting of her mother’s death from breast cancer. She de-
scribes low energy, tearfulness, and trouble with both sleep initiation and maintenance. It 
takes up to an hour for her to fall asleep, and she then has several awakenings, each lasting 
about 30 minutes. Her sheets are sometimes damp when she wakes. She has had occasional, 
mild daytime hot flashes for several years without recent change in frequency or severity. 
Ms. N has no prior history of mental illness. She has gained about 15 lb over the past 
2 years and was diagnosed with type 2 diabet es 1 year ago; she has been taking met -

roid problems.” Ms. N smokes half a pack of cigarettes a day and does not use any 
drugs, including marijuana. She has been drinking one or two glasses of wine 4–5 nights 
a week for the past 2 months. She lives wi th her husband of 20 years and their two 
daughters, ages 15 and 18. Her aging father moved in with them recently after the death 
of her mother. She works as a trial attorney.

meet criteria for an adjustment disorder. General screening laboratory tests are used as

Levels of sex hormones are not typically assessed because levels of FSH, estradiol, 
progesterone, and luteinizing hormone can vary significantly day to day (Gordon et

linked to depressive symptom burden during perimenopause, clinicians are not able 
to identify when this occurs (Joffe et al. 2020). The exception to this guidance against 
checking sex hormone levels is when a woman is experiencing premature meno -
pause; in this case, referring the patient to a gynecologist or a primary care provider 
for a comprehensive evaluation, typically including sex hormone levels and a pelvic 
examination, is indicated. 
Course and Prognosis 
Perimenopausal depression has a similar co urse and prognosis as depression diag -
nosed at other times of life and responds si milarly to treatments used at other times 
of life, with some caveats. Its diagnosis may be complicated or delayed due to symp-
toms related to perimenopause, such as VMS and sleep disturbance (resolution and 
treatment of these are discussed later). Additionally, other symptoms (e.g., migraines, 
which can worsen in perimenopause) may exacerbate depression or make its treat -
ment more challenging (Pavlovic 2018). Some studies have suggested that perimeno-
pausal women with depression may have lower rates of remission compared with 
premenopausal women or may respond better  to combined treatment with antide -
pressants and hormone therapy (see “Treatment” subsection). However, other studies 
have yielded contradictory outcomes (Kornstein et al. 2013), and this is an area of on-
going research. Finally, women who experience recurrence of MDD in perimenopause 
are likely to continue to have a higher risk of depression after their final menstrual 
period, and some evidence indicates that women whose first episode of depression 
occurs during perimenopause are less likely  to have further episodes after they stop 
menstruating (Freeman et al. 2014).

role in the etiology of perimenopausal depression. Multiple studies indicate that the

etiology of perimenopausal mood disturbances (Bromberger and Epperson 2018). In 
a study of women with no prior history of depression, fluctuations in estradiol, FSH,

when she was premenopausal were associated with greater depressive symptoms 
during perimenopause (Freeman et al. 2006). Furthermore, a subset of women with a 
history of either premenstrual dysphoric disorder or postpartum depression appears 
to be especially vulnerable to the dynamics of estrogen and progesterone withdrawal 
because these are associated with an incr eased risk of perime nopausal depression 
(Freeman et al. 2006; Woods and Mitchell 2005). 
Strong evidence for the estrogen withdr awal hypothesis comes from a small ran -
domized controlled trial (RCT) that in cluded 56 asymptomatic postmenopausal 
women with a history of perimenopausal depression. The subjects were given 3 weeks 
of transdermal estradiol 100 µg/day and then randomized to either placebo or contin-
ued estradiol for another 3 weeks. When depressive symptomatology in the two groups 
was compared, the women who continued estradiol remained asymptomatic, but the 
women who were switched to placebo, and therefore were in estrogen withdrawal, had 
an increase in depressive symptoms (Schmidt et al. 2015). Polymorphisms in estrogen 
receptor genes may play a role in the etio logy of perimenopausal depression (Brom-
berger and Epperson 2018). Studies such as SMWHS and SWAN have reported that 
polymorphisms in genes transcribing enzymes related to estrogen metabolism are as-
sociated with increased perimenopausal depression and hot flash risk in some women 
(Bromberger and Epperson 2018; Kravitz et al. 2006; Woods et al. 2018).
