Suicide 
Multiple studies show an increased risk of suicide following perinatal loss, particu -
larly immediately after the loss. The mean annual suicide rate in the first year follow-
ing pregnancy loss is 18.1 of 100,000, compared with 5.9 of 100,000 following live birth 
(Bhat and Byatt 2016; Hvidtjørn et al. 2016). Perinatal loss or neonatal death can in -
crease the rate of psychiatric hospitalization for suicide attempts up to threefold in the 
year following loss (Schiff and Grossman 2006). Mothers who lose a child often de -
scribe ambivalence about their own mortalit y. Suicide can serve a dual purpose: re -
lease from the pain of the loss and reunio n with the deceased child. Obligations to 
other living children may serve as a protective factor (Gissler et al. 1996).

The burden of depression and anxiety does not remit following the birth of a 
healthy child. For instance, Gravensteen et al. (2018) showed higher prevalence of anx-
iety (22.5%) and depression (19.7%) in pregnant women with a history of stillbirth 
compared with nulliparous women or women with a history of live birth; moreover, 
these symptoms could persist up to 6–18 months after the birth of a live infant. Per -
ceived low levels of support can increase the risk of depression and anxiety in a subse-
quent pregnancy, as can pregnancy within the first year following the loss (Armstrong 
et al. 2009). Parents also report decreased attachment to the infant in pregnancies sub-
sequent to perinatal loss (Armstrong 2002; Armstrong and Hutti 1998). 
The timing of assessment of mood and anxiety in a subsequent pregnancy is im -
portant, because anxiety varies depending on the gestational age and time of assess-
ment in relation to timing and circumstances of the previous loss. Anxiety may abate 
after the mother gets reassurance through routine testing and screenings (Hunter et 
al. 2017). The anxiety often increases when th e mother gets closer to the gestational 
age at which the previous perinatal loss occu rred (Mills et al. 2014). In a subsequent 
pregnancy, parents often experience isolatio n related to societal expectations to re-
cover from the previous loss (Mills et al. 2014). It is common for parents not to share 
news of their pregnancy after a previous perinatal loss. Some might be emotionally 
detached from the pregnancy, whereas others might experience intrusive levels of 
anxiety and mental preoccupation with the pregnancy (Armstrong 2002). 
Although many studies show an increased rate of stress, anxiety, and depression 
symptoms in a subsequent pregnancy, no t all results are similar (Armstrong 2004; 
Bergner et al. 2008; Blackmore et al. 2011; Hughes et al. 1999). These variations might 
be due to a broader definition of perinatal loss, differing uses of the term across stud-
ies, and the impact of cultural beliefs and practices in emotional outcomes for women 
and their partners. 
Treatment of Perinatal Loss and Grief 
Psychopharmacology 
To date, no psychopharmacology treatment studies of psychiatric conditions associ -
ated with perinatal loss have been publis hed, despite the finding in one study that 
37.5% of parents were prescribed a medi cation—usually an antidepressant—in the 
year following perinatal or neonatal loss  (Lacasse and Cacciatore 2014). Medication 
management of MDD and PTSD related to perinatal loss should follow the same prin-
ciples and practices as treatment of these conditions at other times of a woman’s life 
(see Chapters 13 and 18 for more information). Because many women do attempt an-
other pregnancy after perinatal loss, clinicians should follow guidelines for prescrib-
ing medication in pregnancy and the postpartum period.

Psychotherapy 
General approach. Perinatal loss, especially sponta neous abortion, is a very per -
sonal loss that is often not shared with others, so grieving patients often feel alone in 
their suffering (Markin and Zilcha-Mano 2018). Reproductive loss is a type of “disen-
franchised grief”—a grief that occurs when a loss is not generally recognized by soci-
ety (Doka 1989)—so a major part of the corr esponding treatment is the establishment 
of a strong therapeutic alliance with other individuals, couples, or families navigating 
through such loss. Alliance can be fostered by assisting those experiencing the loss 
through validation of their seeking treatment (Jaffe and Diamond 2011). 
By setting therapeutic goals, providing psychoeducation on reproductive loss, and 
helping those experiencing reproductive loss to identify their strengths, clinicians can 
facilitate patient empowerment to navigate through the grief and mourning process. 
Worden (2009) provided a framework for facilitating adjustment to grief. He listed 
four tasks of grieving: 1) accepting the reality of the loss, 2) processing the pain of grief, 
3) adjusting to a world without the deceased, 4) and finding an enduring connection 
with the deceased in the midst of embracing a new life. It is important to stress to those 
obtaining mental health services pertaining to reproductive loss that these tasks are not 
linear but rather more like “messy circles.” Exploring what is known as one’s “repro-
ductive story” may help with the grieving process. These narratives are internalized 
images of what parents hope for their chil dren, and most are not fully realized until 
the parents experience a reproductive loss (Jaffe 2017). Psychoeducation should be 
provided to patients to reinforce individualized methods and timelines for grief. 
Engagement in mental health treatment a ppears to be influenced by racial, cul -
tural, and economic factors. For instance, studies of Black women in the United States 
have found that many grieving women ch ose not to utilize perinatal loss support 
groups due to the groups’ lack of racial diversity (Boyden et al. 2014; Van and Meleis 
2003). Outreach to high-risk groups, research regarding barriers to care, and culturally 
sensitive treatment approaches are needed. 
Specific psychotherapy interventions. Despite the well-established association of 
perinatal loss with psychiatric sequelae, a systematic review of psychotherapy treat -
ment studies published in 2012 did not find  demonstrated superiority of psycholog-
ical treatments over no treatment (Murphy et al. 2012). Since that publication, more 
studies using evidence-based treatments have been completed, and a new systematic 
review is in process (Charrois et al. 2020). Still, very few randomized controlled trials 
for specific psychiatric symptoms and disorders associated with specific forms of 
perinatal loss have been conducted to date. IPT, which is evidence based for the treat-
ment of perinatal depression, has been adapted to perinatal loss (Sockol 2018). Early 
trials that used a manualized phone approach reported significantly decreased de -
pressive symptoms in treated subjects over the six to nine sessions (Neugebauer et al. 
2006, 2007). In a pilot randomized controlled trial of women with MDD after miscar-
riage, stillbirth, or early neonatal death, in-person IPT was found to be feasible, accept-
able to patients, and associated with redu ced depressive symptoms (Johnson et al. 
2016). For women after miscarriage, both in-person and internet-based CBT have been 
found to reduce depression and anxiety symptoms from pretreatment to posttreat -
ment in small studies (Kersting et al. 2011; Séjourné et al. 2010).

Perinatal Loss in LGBTQ+ Families 
Most studies on the psychological and psychiatric aspects of perinatal loss include cis-
gender women, typically in heterosexual relationships. Very few studies exist examin-
ing the experience of perinatal loss in LGBTQ+ families. For these families, pregnancy 
is often achieved through ART, and these pregnancies often require a high degree of 
effort and planning. Consequently, loss of these pregnancies is experienced as highly 
distressing (Peel 2010; Wojnar 2007). A concept of double disenfranchisement has been 
proposed (Cacciatore and Raffo 2011): be cause historically many LGBTQ+ families 
have been devalued by the wider society,  t h e i r  p e r i n a t a l  l o s s  r e p r e s e n t s  a  “ d o u b l e  
loss,” and their isolation in their grief represents a “double disenfranchisement.” 
Gender Differences 
To date, all studies of men’s responses to perinatal loss have focused on cisgender het-
erosexual men, and studies are needed that focus on the unique experience of miscar-
riage, IUFD, or neonatal death for men who have chosen to become parents through 
surrogacy or adoption (Obst et al. 2020). In heterosexual couples, perinatal loss has 
been found to have varied effects on men, and these effects are important to clarify 
because they may affect couples’ relationships, which can have downstream effects 
on a woman’s functioning. In their systematic review of 19 quantitative and 26 qual-
itative studies, Obst et al. (2020) found that men are likely to engage in active coping 
styles and invest in both emotional and in strumental supportive behaviors for their 
partners. In turn, this may leave the men feeling less able to express their grief or to 
seek help from family and other supports for their own psychological distress, lead -
ing them to also feel a sense of disenfra nchisement (Obst et al . 2020). They are also 
more likely to engage in avoidance behaviors during grief, including excessive atten-
tion to work outside the home and substance abuse (Obst et al. 2020).
