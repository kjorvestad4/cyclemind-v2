Screen for Primary Care (Cotton et al. 2003) are brief screening measures ideally suited 
for most settings, including primary care. Both include five clinician-administered 
questions, and two or more positive responses on either measure is strongly associated

Each of these instruments is available in many languages and can greatly increase the 
odds of detecting an ED in women. Screening should be targeted to high-risk times 
over the course of a woman’s lifespan, beginning after puberty, into young adulthood, 
during peripartum transitions, and through the perimenopausal period. When a pos-
itive screen is identified, a referral or care coordination with specialists in the ED field 
is recommended. Hospitalization may need to be initiated when suicidality or serious 
medical complications are identified during the screening process (Andersen and 
Ryan 2009). 
Special Considerations in the Peripartum Period 
Weight and eating habits are closely monitored during pregnancy, yet recognizing ED 
symptoms can still be difficult during the peripartum period. In addition to the typi-
cal risk factors listed in the previous section, warning signs of an ED during the peri-
partum period may include a lack of weight gain over two consecutive prenatal visits 
in the second trimester, unexplained hyperkalemia or other electrolyte abnormalities, 
hyperemesis gravidarum, gastrointestinal problems, abnormally low BMI, low bone 
density, sexual dysfunction, subfertility, miscarriage, preterm delivery , or low birth 
weight in offspring (Andersen and Ryan 2009). 
Diagnostic Criteria 
The ED diagnoses are characterized by disordered eating behaviors accompanied by 
psychological reactions to these behaviors, body image, or weight. The DSM-5 diag -
nostic criteria for AN, BN, and BED are outlined in Boxes 22–1, 22–2, and 22–3 (Amer-
ican Psychiatric Association 2013). Although  weight changes can be seen in all EDs, 
AN specifically has low weight as a component of the diagnostic criteria. Low weight 
can be obtained via restricting behaviors or compensatory behaviors, including binge-
ing and purging. BED is distinguished from BN by its lack of compensatory behaviors 
after a binge, although many patients with BED may have some restricting behaviors 
between binge episodes (not part of diagnostic criteria).
