Benzodiazepines, a class of antianxiety medications that includes clonazepam, lora-
zepam, and alprazolam, can be useful in the short-term as either anxiolytics or hypnot-
ics. Benzodiazepines are agonists at the GABA receptor, with inhibitory postsynaptic 
signaling (D’Hulst et al. 2009). They are commonly prescribed in pregnancy, estimated 
at 1%–4% of prescriptions, with the highest rate of prescribing in the third trimester (Bais 
et al. 2020; Hanley and Mintzes 2014; Lacroix et al. 2009; Riska et al. 2014). Lorazepam 
is the most commonly prescribed benzodiazepine in pregnancy (Bais et al. 2020). Both 
benzodiazepines and hypnotics cross the placenta, are present in the amniotic fluid, and 
cross the fetal blood-brain barrier (McElhatton 1994; Vigod and Dennis 2019). 
There has been lengthy debate in the literature regarding the reproductive safety of 
benzodiazepines, especially the risk of co ngenital malformations. Early studies had 
suggested an association between benzodiazepines and cleft lip or palate (Dolovich et 
al. 1998); however, recent data have been reassuring (Ban et al. 2014; Bellantuono et al. 
2013; Wikner et al. 2007). A multisite, population-based case-control study raised con-
cerns about malformations associated with  b e n z o d i a z e p i n e  u s e  i n  p r e g n a n c y  ( e . g . ,  
Dandy-Walker malformation, esophageal atresia), although study sizes were small and 
confidence intervals were wide, emphasizing the need for further data to confirm these 
results (Tinker et al. 2019). Notably, in this cohort, neither cleft lip/palate nor cardiac 
malformations were found to be associated with exposure (Tinker et al. 2019). 
A large population-based cohort study in the United Kingdom demonstrated no 
increase in the overall risk of malforma tions among offspring exposed to diazepam 
during pregnancy (Ban et al. 2014). However, a study using population-based health 
data noted that the use of SSRIs and a benzodiazepine in combination was associated 
with an increase in relative risk of congenital heart defects, even after controlling for 
confounding variables (Oberlander et al. 2008). A systematic review and meta-analysis 
appeared to confirm these findings: prenatal benzodiazepine use alone was not asso-
ciated with an increased risk of congenit al malformations with first-trimester expo -
sure, but concurrent use of benzodiazepines and an antidepressant in pregnancy was 
associated with an increased risk of malformations (Grigoriadis et al. 2019). 
Several studies have suggested an association between benzodiazepine use and ad-
verse outcomes early in pregnancy, such as miscarriage (Ban et al. 2012; Sheehy et al. 
2019). For example, a nested case-control study that included more than 400,000 preg-
nancies noted a statistically significant association between the miscarriage rate and 
benzodiazepine use in the first trimester (Sheehy et al. 2019). However, in this study, 
the miscarriage rate remained below the baseline rate of 15%–20% of identified preg-
nancies, and the study could not control for numerous confounding variables, such as 
the severity of the anxiety, whether the patient actually took the medication, or whether 
the patient abruptly discontinued the medication when the pregnancy was identified 
(Sheehy et al. 2019). In addition, a recent cohort study of more than 1.6 million women 
noted an association between benzodiazepine exposure prior to conception and an in-
creased risk of ectopic pregnancy (Wall-Wieler et al. 2020).
