found to reduce binge-purge episodes in individuals with BN, even after controlling 
for comorbid depression (Davis and Attia 2017). They have similarly been associated 
with a decrease in binge eating with BED, although they have not been associated with 
weight loss. Fluoxetine is the only FDA-approved medication for BN, although ser -
traline, fluvoxamine, and citalopram have also been studied in both BN and BED and 
can be utilized (Davis and Attia 2017). Because a key study demonstrated superior re-
sponse to higher-dose fluoxetine (60 mg) compared with both lower-dose fluoxetine 
(20 mg) and placebo (Levine et al. 1992), higher doses of antidepressants are often used 
when treating BN (Davis and Attia 2017). 
In AN, antidepressants have not been sh own to improve either weight gain or 
weight maintenance in weight-restored individuals (Davis and Attia 2017). Theories 
posit that the lack of response may be related to underlying malnutrition or neurotrans-
mitter deficits. Despite this, antidepressants are often prescribed for individuals with 
AN as part of their treatment plan. This is likely related to the high rates of comorbidity 
with depression and anxiety, whose symptoms can affect and potentially interfere with 
ED treatment. Because these comorbid diag noses are treated primarily with antide -
pressants, addition of these agents could provide additional benefit for those with EDs. 
SSRIs are often considered first-line due to their favorable side effect profile, and 
their lower risk of weight gain (compared with other classes of antidepressants) may 
make them more palatable to patients. Other classes of antidepressants have also been 
studied, including tricyclic antidepressants (TCAs; e.g., imipramine, desipramine, 
and amitriptyline) and monoamine oxidase inhibitors (MAOIs; e.g., phenelzine) in 
BN and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (duloxetine) in BED (Davis and 
Attia 2017). TCAs and MAOIs tend to be used less frequently due to their side effect 
profiles, with notable concerns for sedation and constipation, which are both common 
baseline symptoms of those with EDs. In contrast, bupropion is often avoided for in-
dividuals with EDs due to the potential for decreased appetite and the increased risk 
of seizures in those with BN, whose pur ging behavior and attendant electrolyte ab -
normalities lower the seizure threshold (Horne et al. 1988).
