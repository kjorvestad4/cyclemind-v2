531 Anxiety Disorders and Insomnia in the Perinatal Period 
Many anxiety disorders are at least in part heritable, with a moderate level of familial 
aggregation; heritability estimates are about 30%–50% (Hettema et al. 2005; Shimada-
Sugimoto et al. 2015). Twin studies have noted that genetic architecture is shared 
among anxiety disorders—consistent with the high clinical comorbidity among them 
(Hettema et al. 2005). Overall, based on twin studies, both genetic and environmental 
factors are thought to contribute to anxiety disorder prevalence (Hettema et al. 2005). 
However, genome-wide association studies have not yet identified genes associated 
with the diagnosis, leaving open the possibility of a significant role for an environ -
mental impact, potentially mediated through epigenetics (Bartlett et al. 2017). 
There is a close—albeit not well understood—relationship between stress and 
mental health conditions, such as anxiety. For example, from a clinical perspective, a 
recent study noted that external stress—as measured by number of adverse childhood 
events—is associated with an increased risk of postpartum psychiatric illness (Meltzer-
Brody et al. 2018). Animal models have suggested that early life stress is associated 
with anxiety behavior, brain volume, neuronal structure, and the plasticity of inhibitory 
networks (Castillo-Gómez et al. 2017). There has been intense interest in epigenetics to 
explain the relationship between stress and anxiety; for example, epigenetic control of 
the HPA axis in stress-responsive brain regions is thought to explain individual varia-
tions in anxious behavior (Bartlett et al. 2017). (See Chapters 11 and 18 for details of this 
relationship.) 
Perinatal anxiety—along with perceived stress, regardless of diagnosis—is associ -
ated with changes in serum measures of inflammation (e.g., cytokine levels) and im-
mune system function. For example, women with perinatal anxiety have been shown 
to have increased levels of interleukin (IL)-6, a proinflammatory cytokine (Furtado et 
al. 2019; Maes et al. 2000; Osborne et al. 2019b). Maes et al. (2000) further posited that 
the changes identified in immune activation related to the postpartum period, specif-
ically in women with postpartum anxiety, might reflect a lowered anti-inflammatory 
capacity of the pregnant woman’s immune system. A similar pattern (increased IL-6 
and reduced levels of IL-10) is seen in pregnant women exposed to psychosocial stress-
ors without perinatal depression or anxiety, suggesting that inflammatory cytokines 
are risk factors generally for unfavorable outcomes in late pregnancy (Coussons-Read 
et al. 2007). Additionally, specific changes in inflammatory cytokine profiles were seen 
in women with severe anxiety without depression as compared with women with co-
morbid anxiety and depression in late pregnancy, suggesting a specific correlation 
with anxiety (Leff Gelman et al. 2019). 
The well-documented hormone changes in pregnancy (specifically, the rise of estro-
gen and progesterone during pregnancy and rapid decline within hours of delivery) 
can predispose some (but not all) pregna nt women to the deve lopment of perinatal 
anxiety. Research has shown that levels of hormones measured at a given timepoint are 
no different between symptomatic women and asymptomatic women across the adult 
lifespan but that some women experience a differential sensitivity to the changes in 
hormones as they occur (Schmidt et al. 1998). Thus, it is possible that, in some women, 
perinatal anxiety is triggered by the hormonal events of pregnancy. For example, one 
study reported that maternal exposure to synthetic oxytocin conferred an increased
