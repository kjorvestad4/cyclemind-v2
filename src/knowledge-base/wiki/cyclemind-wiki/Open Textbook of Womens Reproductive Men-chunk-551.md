Pathophysiology 
Generally, clinically significant anxiety symptoms are thought to arise from a combi-
nation of an inherent biological vulnerab ility and an interaction with a stressful or 
traumatic environmental situation (Domhard t et al. 2019). Anxiety is not a unitary 
phenomenon, as evidenced by the multitude of DSM-5 anxiety disorders and the fact 
that anxiety can be a core feature of ot her syndromes and disorders, ranging from 
OCD to alcohol withdrawal to dementia  (Robinson et al. 2019). In addition, fear—a 
rapid “flight or fight” behavioral response  to an imminent threat—is thought to be 
distinct from anxiety—a lasting state of apprehension about a perceived future threat 
(Robinson et al. 2019). This suggests that, for example, the pathological fear in specific 
phobia and the diffuse worry of GAD may have at least somewhat distinct etiologies 
(Robinson et al. 2019). 
In addition, a cogent, concise formulation of the etiology of perinatal anxiety does 
not yet exist; the pathophysiology of perinatal anxiety disorders is likely multidimen-
sional, with biological, pregnancy-related,  a n d  p s y c h o s o c i a l  factors a l l  i m p l i c a t e d .  
Also, there is a complex relationship among anxiety and stress, mediated by alter -
ations in the maternal HPA axis, epigenetics, mitochondrial dysfunction, and immune 
system activation (see Chapter 11 for details). A detailed understanding of the etiol -
ogy of perinatal anxiety, both together with and separate from stress, is evolving. This 
section highlights several of the most well-described aspects of the pathophysiology 
of perinatal anxiety as a distinct entity. 
Biological Factors 
Neuroanatomical structures that play a role in anxiety include the amygdala, which 
has efferent projections to the stria terminalis, hippocampus, ventral striatum, orbitof-
rontal cortex, periaqueductal gray, and hypothalamus (LeDoux 2000). In particular, the 
bed nucleus of the stria terminalis, an area referred to as the “extended amygdala,” ap-
pears to regulate anxiety via its GABAergic projections to multiple limbic, hindbrain, 
and cortical structures (Davis 1998). Other areas thought to be important in the gener-
ation or regulation of anxiety include the medial prefrontal cortex and the hippocam-
pus; connectivity between these areas may comprise an anxiety “network” that may 
be regulated by the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (Robinson et al. 2019). Most anxiety 
symptoms are also mediated by the autonomic—particularly the sympathetic—ner -
vous system, and the neurotransmitters thought to be most involved are norepineph-
rine, serotonin, dopamine, and GABA (Chand and Marwaha 2020).
