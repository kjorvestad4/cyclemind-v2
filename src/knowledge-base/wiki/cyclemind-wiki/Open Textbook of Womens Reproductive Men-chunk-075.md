The DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for PMDD (see Box 6–1 earlier in chapter) emphasize 
mood symptoms that cause functional impairment. To meet criteria, the person must 
have five of these symptoms, at least one of which is a mood symptom. Symptoms must 
be accompanied by either behavioral or physical symptoms that begin during the luteal 
phase of the menstrual cycle, start to decrease after menstrual flow, and are minimal or 
absent during the early follicular phase (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
An accurate diagnosis of PMDD requires prospective mood charting of at least one 
but preferably two menstrual cycles. A m ood chart consistent with PMS or PMDD 
will demonstrate symptoms that begin in the premenstrual period and resolve at the 
beginning or shortly after the onset of menses. Ongoing symptoms during the follic -
ular phase that worsen during the premenst rual phase are more consistent with the 
PME of another disorder, such as depression or anxiety. Although no consensus exists 
regarding the necessary percentage of change, a 30%–50% change in symptoms be -
tween luteal and follicular phases of the cycle is generally considered an important 
diagnostic guideline (Hall and Steiner 2015).
