Substance Use Disorders 
To date, few studies have examined the prevalence of substance use disorders in 
women undergoing infertility treatment. In  one lar ge, r etr ospective cohort study of 
98,320 Danish women who had been evaluated for infertility, women who did not go 
on to give birth had twice the risk of alcohol and intoxicant abuse as women who had 
given birth (Baldur-Felskov et al. 2013). Heavy alcohol use (binge drinking !5 days in 
past month) is associated with decreased fecundity in women, because heavy drinking 
is associated with decreased ovarian reserve and a rise in estrogens, leading to de -
creased FSH secretion and impaired ovulation (Van Heertum and Rossi 2017). Ciga -
rette smoking has a negative effect on follicular development, embryo transport, and 
endometrial receptivity (Dechanet et al. 2011). In some studies, frequent marijuana 
consumption is associated with ovulatory dysfunction, although several large obser-
vational studies have not found that mariju ana use is associated with longer time to 
conceive (Dunne 2019; Kasman et al. 2018). 
Psychosocial Stress 
The role of psychosocial stress as a risk factor for infertility in women remains contro-
versial (Rooney and Domar 2018). Part of the complexity is due to the fact that, despite 
extensive study, psychosocial stress itself remains challenging to define. Stress is often 
defined as a process whereby a stimulus imposes demands on or overwhelms an or-
ganism’s ability to cope, causing physiological and psychological responses (Kool -
haas et al. 2011). However, the magnitude,  duration, and quality of these responses 
are contingent on individual interpretation of the stressor and how well the person 
adapts to or copes with it (Cohen et al. 2007; King and Hegadoren 2002; Lazarus and 
Folkman 1984; Wosu et al. 2013). 
Psychosocial stress can exert effects on many biological systems related to fertility. 
One example is its impact on follicular development and ovulation. During a physical 
or emotional stressor, the hypothalamus secretes corticotropin-releasing hormone 
and arginine vasopressin, which act on the pituitary gland to increase adrenocortico-
tropic hormone release, which in turn stimul ates the production of cortisol from the

Excessive stress may also affect fertil ization, implantation, and maintenance of 
pregnancy, in particular through the effects of cytokines (Haimovici et al. 2018). Cy -
tokines produced in the CNS and periphery in response to stress may influence go -
nadal function by changing hormone production and responsiveness within the HPA 
axis or directly through brain-gonadal input. CNS cytokines may also cause changes 
in peripheral leukocyte activation and fu nction and may provoke cytokine cascades 
in the periphery that can modulate specific functions related to reproduction (Altun 
et al. 2011; Capuron and Miller 2011; Ozka n et al. 2014). Proi nflammatory cytokines 
have been found to be elevated in both infertile men and women, and they may mod-

note, elevated levels of cytokines have also been found in women with repeat implan-
tation failures (Liang et al. 2015). However, cytokines are elevated in response to a va-
riety of conditions, and it can be challe nging to discern whether this response is 
related to stress or other factors that contribute to the presence of infertility. 
Although it is accepted that stress can increase rates of depression and anxiety 
symptoms, which can have an impact on fertility rates, it is less clear whether stress 
alone has a similar impact (Domar et al. 1993; Rooney and Domar 2018). From a pa-
tient perspective, this question can set the stage for a cycle of perceived “victim blam-
ing” and guilt, whereby a patient undergoing the stressful process of ART may blame 
her own stress if the ART is unsuccessful. Some studies report an impact of subjective 
distress on ART outcomes, whereas others do not (Rooney and Domar 2018). Reviews 
of the literature have reported that depr essive symptoms, anxiety, distress, and cer -
tain coping strategies are linked to reduced chances of pregnancy during ART (Eug-
ster and Vingerhoets 1999; Homan et al . 2007; Klonoff-Cohen 2005; Rooney and 
Domar 2018). However, data have been mixed. Some studies show that higher levels 
of distress during ART are correlated with  lower pregnancy rates (Matthiesen et al. 
2011; Purewal et al. 2018), but other studies report that stress levels have no effect on 
fertility treatment outcome (Boivin et al. 2011; Nicoloro-SantaBarbara et al. 2018). Two 
meta-analyses also reported conflicting resu lts (Boivin et al. 2011;  Matthiesen et al. 
2011). Thus, the relationship among stress, psychiatric illness, and success of infertil-
ity treatments remains unclear. What is clear is that infertility can create substantial 
distress in those affected, and interventions aimed at addressing this distress can be 
beneficial in overall treatment outcomes  (Rooney and Domar 2018). Future studies 
must control for important fertility variables such as age, ovarian reserve, sperm qual-
ity, lifestyle factors, and medication use (Williams et al. 2007). 
Lifestyle Factors 
Excessive exercise can lead to a reduction in the frequency of ovulation, poor endome-
trial development (luteal phase deficiency), and amenorrhea. Recreational or moderate 
levels of activity also can induce abnormalities of gonadotropin secretion and ovula-
tory disorders, even without inducing amenorrhea (Harrison et al. 2016; Hart 2016).

Impact of Treatment for 
Mental Health Disorders on Fertility 
Psychopharmacological treatment for MDD can increase the risk for infertility, primar-
ily through effects on hormonal processes, such as prolactin levels, thyroid function, 
and insulin resistance. For example, several categories of psychiatric medications, such 
as antipsychotics and selective serotonin re uptake inhibitors (SSRIs), are associated 
with increased prolactin levels (Ajmal et al. 2014). Prolactin has an important role in 
fertility. It directly decreases the sensitivity of the LH and FSH receptors in the gonads 
and indirectly reduces GnRH secretion, which suppresses LH and FSH secretion and 
thereby inhibits ovulation. Women with elevated prolactin levels generally present 
with oligomenorrhea or amenorrhea, anovulatory cycles, galactorrhea, and infertility 
(Patel and Bamigboye 2007). The risk of elevated prolactin levels has been reported to 
be highest with first-generation antipsychotics and the second-generation antipsy -
chotic risperidone (Smith 2003). Elevated prolactin levels with SSRIs are rare but can 
occur (Ajmal et al. 2014). 
Many psychiatric medications, such as antidepressants and antipsychotics, are as-
sociated with weight gain and insulin resi stance, both of which are associated with 
infertility (Arterburn et al. 2016; Barton et al. 2020; Blumenthal et al. 2014). These med-
ications also may be associated with decreased libido and sexual dysfunction, which 
may affect sexual frequency and thus the frequency of conception events (Elnazer et 
al. 2015; Montejo et al. 2015). Individual medications need to be considered as poten-
tially having an impact on fertility. For example, lithium is associated with hypothy-
roidism, which is a clear risk factor for infertility (Dosiou 2020; Gitlin 2016). Thus, all 
patients taking lithium should have thyroid levels carefully assessed when they at -
tempt to conceive.

Psychiatric Conditions 
The incidence rates of MDD in women undergoing infertility evaluation and treatment 
range from 20% to 40%, which are higher than rates in women of reproductive age in 
the general population (Williams et al. 2007). Previous history of MDD predicts its de-
velopment during ART; therefore, all patients should be screened for past history and 
current depressive symptoms (Holley et al. 2015). Higher rates of MDD are associated 
with lower levels of social support in women experiencing infertility (Lund et al. 2009). 
When women attending infertility clinics are screened for depression, high scores have 
been associated with both a decreased likelihood of initiating fertility treatment and an 
increased likelihood of stopping treatment (Crawford et al. 2017; Domar et al. 2018). 
Anxiety disorders are also more common in women undergoing infertility evalu -
ation and treatment than in women in the general population. Studies have suggested 
the prevalence of any anxiety disorder as ranging from 15% to 30% in women under-
going ART (Chen et al. 2004; Omani-Samani et al. 2018; Volgsten et al. 2008). Some 
anxiety disorders, such as needle or injection phobias, may interfere with the patient’s 
ability to complete infertility treatment. 
Although many studies demonstrate high rates of depressive and anxiety symp -
toms in women experiencing infertility, few studies have examined rates of completed 
suicide in this population. In a large study of more than 50,000 Danish women with no 
prior history of psychiatric hospitalizations, women with primary infertility who re -
mained childless had more than twice the rate of suicide compared with women who 
had at least one child after fertility evaluation (Kjaer et al. 2011). In a small study of 106 
women undergoing infertility treatment, 9.4% were identified as being at risk for sui-
cide based on the Suicidal Behaviors Questionnaire–Revised (Osman et al. 2001), and 
women at risk were found to have higher levels of depression symptoms, self-blame, 
social withdrawal, and the use of denial as a coping strategy (Shani et al. 2016). 
Psychological Reactions 
The experience of infertility is associated with significant personal distress (Rooney 
and Domar 2018). Childlessness is often perc eived as a life crisis, and the emotional 
strain is equal to that of traumatic ev ents (Cousineau and Domar 2007; Greil 1997; 
Mahlstedt 1985; Oddens et al. 1999; Schmidt et al. 2005). Grieving infertility often in-
volves shock, disbelief, anger, blame, shame, and guilt (Mahlstedt 1985; Rooney and 
Domar 2018). Loss of control is a central theme in women and their partners who are 
experiencing infertility (Greil et al. 2011). The affected woman’s sense of identity may 
be challenged, and infertility can lead to feelings of worthlessness and guilt (Greil et 
al. 2016). Achieving a pregnancy can become a singular focus, and professional goals 
and social connections are often placed on hold or dismissed altogether (Cousineau 
and Domar 2007; Thorn 2009).

Couples struggling with infertility often experience a wide range of emotional re -
actions, including guilt and a sense of isolation, due to perceived or real social unac-
ceptability (El Kissi et al. 2013; Péloquin et al. 2018). Given the invasive nature of the 
process of infertility workup and treatments, sexual dysfunction can be common (Tao 
et al. 2011). Marital discord may also occur (Tabong and Adongo 2013). Nevertheless, 
some studies indicate that couples who remain childless are as satisfied with their 
lives as those who have conceived, and ma ny couples report that their relationship 
has strengthened as a result of having managed the crisis together (Amiri et al. 2016; 
Masoumi et al. 2016; Schmidt et al. 2005). 
Notably, several facets of ART itself can be associated with distress (Boivin et al. 
2011). The process of decision making can be complicated and contribute to distress 
for couples. The diagnosis of “unexplained infertility” can be especially challenging 
because it may cause feelings of both hope and distress (Greil et al. 2011). The lack of 
a clear cause can set couples on what feels like an endless pursuit of additional exam-
inations and procedures. The option of third-party assisted reproduction (gamete do-
nation or surrogacy) may confront the couple with further complex decisions. ART is 
time consuming and logistically complicated, and the expense of infertility treatments 
can also contribute to stress and may strain the financial resources of the couple or in-
dividual (Cousineau and Domar 2007). Finally, there is a dearth of information on 
long-term effects of ART, either physical or emotional. 
Treatment of Psychiatric Conditions 
in the Context of Infertility 
Psychopharmacology 
Women struggling with infertility may be reluctant to take medications for depression 
and anxiety for fear that the medication mi ght present risks to fertility or pregnancy 
(Walton et al. 2014). Women already taking antidepressants at the time of ART should 
be cautioned that even with continued medica tion, they are at risk for relapse of de -
pression symptoms (Freeman et al. 2018). In the only existing systematic review of the 
effects of SSRIs on pregnancy and live birth rates in women undergoing ART, no SSRI 
effect was found on any parameters studied in IVF success, including mean number 
of oocytes retrieved, number of embryos tr ansferred, implantation rates, live births, 
and spontaneous abortions (Akioyamen et al. 2016). A nationwide cohort study in 
Sweden evaluated the odds of success of three IVF cycle outcomes (i.e., pregnancy, live 
birth, and miscarriage) (Cesta et al. 2016). The study found slightly reduced odds of 
pregnancy and live birth for women taking non-SSRI antidepressants compared with 
healthy women, but no difference between women taking SSRIs and healthy women. 
However, women with untreated anxiety or depression also exhibited reduced odds of 
pregnancy and live birth, indicating perhaps that the effect found for non-SSRI antide-
pressants may be a marker for the underlying illness. 
Psychotherapy 
Psychotherapy remains the first-line treatment for infertile women with mild to mod-
erate depressive symptoms and anxiety. Individual studies have demonstrated signif-
icant decreases in both anxiety and depression symptom scores for infertility patients

(social, sexual, and marital concerns), and both interventions were superior to the 
control (Faramarzi et al. 2013 ). Mindfulness-based interventions are associated with 
an increase in self-compassion, meaning-based coping strategies, and higher preg -
nancy rates (Li et al. 2016). IPT and brief su pportive therapy are also effective in the 
treatment of MDD among infertile women (Koszycki et al. 2012).

Third-Party Assisted Reproduction With Gamete Donation 
(Ovum and Sperm Donation) 
ART with donor sperm is common. Betw een 2015 and 2017, an estimated 440,986 
women used donor sperm for either IUI or IVF procedures (Arocho et al. 2019). Com-
mon reasons for conception through sperm do nation include male factor infertility, 
single motherhood, and family building for lesbian couples. Although some of these 
individuals suffer the stresses and psychological consequences associated with infer-
tility, others approach ART without infert ility and may have different experiences.

Ovum donation is much less common, because it requires time-intensive ovarian  
stimulation protocols; it is estimated that 10% of IVF cycles in the United States use 
ovum donation (American Society for Re productive Medicine 2018). Common rea -
sons for egg donation include advanced maternal age, premature menopause, infer -
tility due to previous treatments with toxic medications such as chemotherapy, or 
creation of an embryo in same-sex couples. Cumulative live birth rates in ART cycles 
with ovum donation decrease with increasing age of the donor: 33.6% when donors 
are between ages 33 and 35 years, 22.5% when they are between ages 38 and 40 years, 
and only 5.1% when they are older than 41 years (Hogan et al. 2019). Pregnancies from 
ovum donation are associated with an increased risk of obstetrical complications, re-
gardless of the recipient’s age (Storgaard et al. 2017). 
The American Society for Reproductive Medicine (ASRM) and the Society for Assisted 
Reproductive Technology recommend that all couples who choose to pursue pregnancy 
through gamete donation meet with an experienced mental health professional to discuss 
the psychological issues associated with donation (Practice Committee of the American 
Society for Reproductive Medicine and the Society for Assisted Reproductive Tech-
nology 2013). During this meeting, the mental health professional evaluates whether 
the couple is in agreement regarding several key issues, such as the choice of an anon-
ymous versus an identified donor. Psychological and financial coercion must be ruled 
out, including coercion within the couple’s relationship and within the relationship 
between the couple and the donor. The mental health professional should counsel the 
donor and the couple about the importance of mutually agreed upon boundaries, and 
all should agree regarding issues of disclosure with family members and friends. 
The ASRM publishes guidelines regarding disclosure with gamete donor–conceived 
children and acknowledges that although the ultimate decision to tell children of their 
origins is up to the couple, disclosure is recommended (Ethics Committee of the Amer-
ican Society for Reproductive Medicine 2018). No specific optimal age of disclosure has 
been identified, but studies suggest that telling the children prior to adolescence is pre-
ferred (Ethics Committee of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine 2018). 
Couples who choose to tell their children should be counseled that several preschool 
and early childhood books are available that describe the ways in which families are cre-
ated and may help in explaining their family building to children. Follow-up studies of 
heterosexual couples who chose gamete donation showed no significant differences in 
maternal–child relationships between donor-conceived and natural-conception families 
who disclosed origins to the child (Bracewell-Milnes et al. 2016; Imrie and Golombok 
2018). Children conceived through gamete donation, from both heterosexual and same-
sex couple families, do not appear to have increased rates of psychiatric disorders com-
pared with children not conceived via gamete donation (Imrie and Golombok 2018). 
The ASRM also publishes guidelines for th e evaluation of ovum donors. Relative 
contraindications to participating in an ovum donor program include active psychiat-
ric illness or substance abuse, high-risk sexu al practices, and a history of sexual or 
physical abuse without a history of professional counseling (Practice Committee of the 
American Society for Reproductive Medicine and the Society for Assisted Reproduc-
tive Technology 2013). Evaluation of ovum donors should include assessment of the 
donor’s ability to understand the potential medical risks associated with the ovarian 
stimulation procedure, as well as the potent ial psychological reactions. Most follow-
up studies report that ovum donors have favorable experiences, with few short- or

Ovum Cryopreservation 
Cryopreservation of oocytes, also known as  “egg freezing,” has become an increas -
ingly viable option for fertility preservation (Milman et al. 2017; Practice Committee 
of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine and the Society for Assisted Re -
productive Technology 2013). In this procedure, patients undergo ovarian stimula -
tion procedures followed by egg retrieval and vitrification. 
Cryopreservation of oocytes can offer some advantages to several populations. First, 
women may choose to undergo elective egg freezing to preserve oocytes from a chrono-
logically younger age for use in a future pregnancy. Second, premenopausal women who 
are diagnosed with cancer and planning to undergo treatments should be counseled re-
garding fertility preservation options and offered psychological counseling in order to de-
cide about these options (Lawson et al. 2015; Loren et al. 2013). Third, transgender men 
may choose to cryopreserve mature ooctyes prior to gender affirmation surgery, whereas 
transgender women may choose to freeze their sperm (Armuand et al. 2020; Ethics Com-
mittee of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine 2015; Hembree et al. 2009). 
Evidence indicates that ovarian stimulation and oocyte retrieval have excellent rates 
of success in transgender men, even if undertaken after testosterone therapy has been 
initiated, and this path to fertility preserva tion is increasingly undertaken in adoles -
cence prior to gender affirmation surgery (Adeleye et al. 2019; Amir et al. 2020). 
Surrogacy 
Surrogacy is used when there is a need for a person with a healthy uterus to act as a 
gestational carrier of a pregnancy—for exam ple, a woman unable to carry a child in 
her own uterus, a male same-sex couple, or some transgender individuals. Gestational 
surrogacy refers to implantation of an embryo created from the fertility patient’s or do-
nor’s eggs into the gestational carrier, whereas “traditional” surrogacy uses the car -
rier’s own eggs. Gestational surrogacy ART cycles are uncommon, but increasing, in 
the United States, comprising 2.5% of all ART cycles in 2013 (Perkins et al. 2016). 
ART with surrogacy requires careful legal, medical, and psychosocial planning. The 
ASRM has extensive guidelines for mental health counseling for both intended parents 
and potential gestational carriers. Unlike gamete donation, in which the third party 
may be either anonymous or identified, surrogacy requires some form of relationship 
between the parties. The ASRM recommends that couples participating in a gesta -
tional surrogacy program recognize the importance of the intended parents’ ability to 
manage the interpersonal complexity of the surrogate relationship. Contraindications 
include not only current untreated psychiatric illnesses and substance abuse but also 
inability to maintain a “respectful and caring relationship with the surrogate” or to 
comply with the gestational carrier’s desire about number of embryos to transfer 
(Practice Committee of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine and Practice 
Committee of the Society for Assisted Reproductive Technology 2017). Studies of chil-
dren born from gestational surrogacy report no increased rates of medical or psycho-
logical disturbances compared with childre n born naturally or from other forms of
