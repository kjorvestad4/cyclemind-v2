Both mood and anxiety disorders can present with sleep disturbances and insomnia 
and should be carefully evaluated. Mood disorders such as MDD and bipolar disorder 
can both present with profound disruption s in sleep. In addition, anxiety disorders 
such as GAD, panic disorder, OCD, and PTSD can all present with perinatal insomnia. 
At the same time, sleep deprivation can be a profound trigger of psychiatric conditions, 
such as with mood disorders. Substance intoxication or withdrawal can also present 
with sleep disruption. 
Medical diagnoses specific to pregnancy and the postpartum period should be ruled 
out. Pregnancy may predispose women to sleep-disordered breathing, which is charac-
terized by snoring, gasping, and apneas. Obstructive sleep apnea-hypopnea due to ob-
struction of airflow in the upper airway is more common in pregnant women than in 
nonpregnant women, with a prevalence of roughly 10%–25% (Nodine and Matthews 
2013). Distinct physiological and hormonal changes in pregnancy, such as weight gain, 
edema of mucus membranes (from elevated levels of estrogen), and displacement of 
the diaphragm from an enlarging uterus, can all contribute to breathing-related sleep 
disorders (Hashmi et al. 2016). Significant predictors of sleep-disordered breathing in 
pregnancy are age, BMI, and frequent snoring; patients with these risk factors may rep-
resent a subpopulation in need of further evaluation (Louis et al. 2018). 
Another important diagnosis is restless legs syndrome, which has an increased in-
cidence in pregnancy (estimated to be up to 30%), with symptom severity highest in 
the third trimester (Manconi et al. 2004). To the relief of sufferers, symptoms typically 
remit after delivery. Restless legs syndrome can be associated with pregnancy-related 
iron deficiency, which can be detected by a ferritin level to assess iron stores. Un -
treated restless legs syndrome increases the risk of depressed mood, and related sleep 
deprivation is linked with prolonged labor, increased pain perception, higher discom-
fort during labor, and increased rates of operative delivery (Chang et al. 2010). 
Pathophysiology 
To consider the pathophysiology of insomnia, it is helpful to review basic mechanisms 
of sleep itself. Normal sleep consists of two fundamental neuronal mechanisms. The 
homeostatic mechanism of sleep induces a pressure to sleep after every hour awake, 
and the circadian rhythm mechanism drives alertness during the day and sleepiness 
at night, based on light intensity as the main external “timekeeper” (Anderson 2018).

Both total sleep time and circadian rhythms are subject to significant variations over 
the course of our lives, including greater fragmentation over time—one of the reasons 
the prevalence of insomnia tends to increase with age (Ohayon et al. 2004). Short-term 
insomnia generally arises in association with acute psychosocial stressors, and the 
sleep disruption tends to resolve when the stressor resolves (Ellis et al. 2012). However, 
the development of chronic insomnia requires not just the acute stressor but also mal-
adaptive behaviors that can worsen sleep quality and disrupt the homeostatic and cir-
cadian rhythm drivers for sleep (Anderso n 2018). Paradoxically, some of the most 
common exacerbating factors are hypervigilant efforts around sleep (e.g., increased at-
tention to and ruminations about sleep), wh ich can lead to hyperarousal, which is a 
physiological stress response, and to worsened insomnia (Espie et al. 2006). 
The relationship between sleep difficulties and mental health conditions overall is 
complex and may be bidirectional (Krystal 2012). For example, at least 40% of patients 
with insomnia will eventually be diagnosed with an anxiety disorder, and persistent 
insomnia is one of the strongest risk factors for depression in the adult population (An-
derson 2018; Baglioni et al. 2011). A recent review suggested that sleep disturbance 
may be a potentially transdiagnostic mechanism across psychiatric diagnoses, and that 
sleep disturbance and emotion regulation may be neurobiologically linked (Harvey et 
al. 2011). Emerging evidence implicates autonomic hyperarousal as a pathway to in-
creasing the risk of depression (Fernandez-Mendoza and Vgontzas 2013). 
The etiology of perinatal sleep disturbance is also multifactorial. Many dynamic as-
pects of pregnancy, such as changes in hormone levels, physiology, metabolism, and 
psychology, likely contribute to sleep disturbances, particularly as pregnancy pro -
gresses (Bei et al. 2015; Hashmi et al. 2016). There is also evidence of extensive comor-
bidity between mental health conditions and insomnia in pregnancy, including anxiety 
and depressive symptoms; insomnia in pregnancy can be either a sign of a mental 
health condition, a trigger for one, or both (Chaudhry and Susser 2018; Swanson et al. 
2011; Wo%ynczyk-Gmaj et al. 2017). In the first trimester, sleep changes are most likely 
related to rapid escalations in reproductive hormone levels, particularly progesterone, 
which can lead to daytime sleepiness and sl eep interruptions at night (Hashmi et al. 
2016). Progesterone and its metabolites interact with the GABA A receptors, which is 
thought to effect these changes in sleep (Lancel et al. 1996). Sleep in the first trimester 
is also affected by overall fatigue and the nausea and vomiting familiar to many 
women who have traversed the first trimester of pregnancy (Lee et al. 2000). By the sec-
ond trimester, the total amount of sleep time is reduced and subjective reports of inter-
rupted sleep increase, in part due to factors such as nocturnal gastroesophageal reflux 
(Driver and Shapiro 1992). By the third trimester, <2% of pregnant women report no 
nocturnal awakenings (Hedman et al. 2002).  Common contributing factors include

Treatment 
As with many aspects of perinatal insomn ia, the optimal approach to treatment is 
evolving. This section focuses primarily on CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) and pharmaco-
therapy for insomnia in pregnancy and postpartum, although other methods are cur-
rently under active investigation. 
Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia 
In the general adult population, high-quality evidence supports the use of behavioral 
interventions such as CBT-I as the first-line treatment of  insomnia (Benz et al. 2020; 
Morin et al. 2009; Schutte-Rodin et al. 2008; Trauer et al. 2015). National and interna-
tional clinical practice guidelines an d national organizations have recommended 
CBT-I as the first-line treatment for insomnia in adults (Baker et al. 2014; National In-
stitutes of Health 2005; Qaseem et al. 2016; Riemann et al. 2017). A recent systematic 
review found that CBT-I was effective for adults with comorbid psychiatric illnesses, in-
cluding patients with depression, anxiety, PTSD, and substance abuse disorders (Taylor 
and Pruiksma 2014). CBT-I is usually delivered over six weekly sessions, typically re-
quiring 4–8 hours of therapy along with weekly sleep diaries to track progress (An -
derson 2018). 
In addition, evidence supports CBT-I specifically for perinatal insomnia (Bei et al. 
2019; Lee et al. 2016; Tomfohr-Madsen et al. 2017). A recent randomized controlled 
clinical trial revealed greater reduction in insomnia severity and a greater and more 
rapid insomnia remission (Manber et al. 2019). There was also an improvement in self-
reported sleep time above and beyond wh at was noted with objective measures of 
sleep; the authors hypothesized that CBT-I may help women feel less distressed about 
sleep difficulties, including those caused by increased pregnancy-related physical dis-
comfort (Manber et al. 2019). This is important, because self-reported sleep has been 
shown to be a stronger predictor of maternal mental health than objective measures of 
sleep itself (Bei et al. 2010). CBT-I is also preferred by pregnant patients, as compared 
with pharmacotherapy and acupuncture (Sedov et al. 2017). A summary of the steps
