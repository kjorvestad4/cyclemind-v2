CHAPTER 4 
Contraception 
Implications for Mental Health 
Lulu Zhao, M.D. 
Few medical inventions have changed the lives of women as much as the advent of 
reliable, accessible contraception. In 1973, about a decade after the introduction of the 
birth control pill, 70% of married American  women (to whom the pill was available 
legally) were using some form of contra ception (Gibson 2015). Today, almost 90% of 
women at risk for unintended pregnancy use some form of contraception, with almost 
75% of these women choosing modern method s of high or moderate efficacy (Kava -
naugh and Jerman 2018). 
Reliable contraceptive methods such as hormonal birth control, long-acting revers-
ible contraceptives, or permanent sterilizat ion effectively uncouple the act of sexual 
intercourse from the consequence of childbearing. Women who use contraception cor-
rectly and consistently account for only 5% of all unintended pregnancies in the 
United States; 95% of all unintended pregnancies result from women using contracep-
tion inconsistently, incorrectly, or not at all (Sonfield et al. 2014). Unplanned birth neg-
atively affects women’s educational and economic opportunities, and these losses 
often have downstream adverse effects on th eir mental health (Johnston et al. 2017). 
Children born from unplanned pregnancies may be subject to greater environmental 
adversity and have increased risks for both physical and psychological health condi-
tions (Sonfield et al. 2011). 
Women with mental health disorders are at higher risk for unplanned pregnancy 
for various reasons, including disease-related sexual behaviors, environmental influ-
ences that decrease contrace ptive access or uptake, lowered contraceptive efficacy 
due to medication interactions, and diseas e-related medical comorbidities that limit 
contraceptive choice (Dietz et al. 1999; Hall et al. 2013a, 2014; Judge-Golden et al. 2018; 
Marengo et al. 2015). A broad understanding of the biological mechanism of modern 
contraceptive methods, and of the interpla y between psychiatric illness and contra -
ceptive efficacy, should be part of psychiatrists’ armamentarium in providing high -

Physiology 
Female Reproductive Cycle 
The female reproductive cycle is controlled by interwoven processes within the brain, 
ovaries, and uterus—a signaling pathway re ferred to as the hypothalamic-pituitary-
gonadal (HPG) axis. The ormation of a mature oocyte starts with pulsatile releases of 
gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) by  the hypothalamus. This stimulates the 
anterior pituitary gland to release two gonadotropins, follicle-stimulating hormone 
(FSH) and luteinizing hormone (LH). These gonadotropins stimulate the ovary to be-
gin the process of folliculogenesis. The ovary, under the influence of FSH, produces a 
mature oocyte each cycle and, under the influence of LH, releases it toward the uterus 
for fertilization and implantation in a process called ovulation. During folliculogene-
sis, the maturing oocyte produces the sex steroid estradiol, which builds up the lining 
of the uterus in preparation for implanta tion. After ovulation, a dynamic endocrine 
gland called the corpus luteum forms within the empty space left by the ovulated oo -
cyte. The corpus luteum produces the sex steroid progesterone, which stabilizes and 
supports the thickened uterine lining while awaiting implantation. If pregnancy does 
not occur, ovarian sex steroid production  ceases, and the now unsupported uterine 
lining sheds in a process called menstruation.
