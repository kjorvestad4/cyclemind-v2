In general populations, CBT that relies on behavioral techniques, such as ERP , is rec-
ommended because it has the most evidentiary support (Cordioli et al. 2003; Eddy et 
al. 2004; Fals-Stewart et al. 1993; Fisher and Wells 2005; Foa et al. 2005; Franklin et al. 
2000; Greist et al. 2002; Lindsay et al. 1997; McLean et al. 2001; Nakatani et al. 2005; 
Rothbaum and Shahar 2000; Warren and Thomas 2001). In this modality , patients are 
taught to confront feared situations and objects and to refrain from performing rituals. 
Exposures may include in-vivo and imaginal confrontations of feared consequences. 
Exposures are graded from mildly to more intensely anxiety provoking. Patients must 
face their fears for a prolonged period while refraining from indulging in their usual 
ritualizing. The goal is to weaken the connections between the feared stimuli causing 
distress and the performance of rituals bringing relief from distress (Koran et al. 2007). 
There is also some, although relatively less, data on use of CBT that, while incor -
porating behavioral techniques, focuses on  cognitive approaches aimed at identify -
ing, challenging, and modifying dysfunctional beliefs (Ellis 1962; Emmelkamp and 
Beens 1991; Emmelkamp et al. 1988; Jones and Menzies 1998; van Oppen et al. 1995). 
Such dysfunctional beliefs include magical thinking, an inflated sense of responsibil-
ity for unwanted events, over estimations of the probabilit y of feared events, the as -
sumption that thoughts are morally equivalent to actions or inevitably lead to action 
(thought-action fusion), perfectionism, the belief that anxiety/discomfort will persist 
forever, and the need for control (Koran et al. 2007; Wilhelm et al. 2005). 
Although some randomized trials have investigated the efficacy of combined treat-
ment (CBT consisting of ERP+SRI) compared with either SRI or CBT alone, limitations 
in their design prevent definitive conclusions (Cottraux et al. 1990; Foa et al. 2005; Ho-
hagen et al. 1998; Koran et al. 2007; Marks et al. 1980, 1988; van Balkom et al. 1998). In 
the absence of definitive data, combined treatment is reasonable in cases involving 
treatment of a co-occurring disorder that is SSRI responsive, to augment a partial re-
sponse to monotherapy (Tenneij et al. 2005), and to reduce the chance of relapse when 
medication is discontinued (Simpson et al. 2004). Combined treatment may also be 
considered in cases of severe OCD because the medication may diminish symptom se-
verity enough to allow the patient to engage in CBT (Koran et al. 2007). In general pop-
ulations, CBT alone (consisting of ERP) is recommended for patients who are not too 
depressed, anxious, or severely ill to coop erate with this treatment modality or who 
prefer not to take medications and are willing to do the work that CBT requires (Amer-
ican Psychiatric Association 2007). Although many perinatal patients may prefer this 
approach to minimize potential exposures in pregnancy, a thorough risk-risk discus-
sion is required first, with thoughtful consideration of medication options (see “Treat-
ment Challenges” section).
