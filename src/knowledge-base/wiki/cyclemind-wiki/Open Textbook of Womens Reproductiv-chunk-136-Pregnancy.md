Key Points 
• ADHD is a common neurodevelopmental disorder that has a lifelong impact. 
It is associated with major functional impairment in multiple arenas, such as 
work, school, and social life. 
• Adult ADHD is highly comorbid with other mental health conditions, including 
depression, anxiety, and substance use disorders.

• Optimal treatment of ADHD includes a combination of medication and psy -
chosocial support. 
• Stimulants are one of the most commonly prescribed medication in pregnan-
cy. An increasing number of women of reproductive age have been diag -
nosed with ADHD and prescribed stimulants for this condition. 
• Data regarding the risks of stimulant exposure in pregnancy and breastfeed-
ing historically have contained significant confounding variables, such as the 
impact of ADHD itself and substance misuse. 
• Data are still evolving regarding the reproductive safety profile of stimulants; 
much of their effect appears to be related to fetal growth and placental function.

Forensic Issues in 
Perinatal Psychiatry

Aimee Kaempf, M.D. 
Jacqueline Landess, M.D., J.D. 
Sarah M. Kauffman, M.D. 
Reproductive psychiatry has many intersection s with forensic psychiatry and 
the legal system. Issues of informed consent for treatment or voluntary termination of 
pregnancy (VTOP), mothering and risk, reproductive psychiatry in corrections, and 
infertility are areas for which an understanding of forensic and legal issues is invalu-
able for the reproductive psychiatrist. This chapter provides an overview of these inter-
sections for the practicing psychiatrist. 
When women obtain a VTOP , their capacity to provide informed consent must be 
considered. Additional hurdles include recent U.S. laws requiring state-generated “in-
formed consent” information, which is distinct from the process of obtaining informed 
consent. Informed consent is also critical in pregnancy and postpartum. Psychiatrists 
often have worries about malpractice risk from prescribing medications in pregnancy. 
However, it is critical for the psychiatrist not to fail to treat the pregnant woman, which 
can also precipitate bad outcomes and ma lpractice risk. Proact ive discussions with 
women of childbearing age, mothers, and families are paramount. Rarely, psychiatrists 
may need to assess pregnant women for involuntary treatment (i.e., treatment over ob-
jection) and testify regarding this assessment. 
Capacity and risk are similarly at issue in parenting capacity evaluations. As man-
dated reporters, reproductive psychiatrists must consider the risk of child maltreatment 
among their patients. They need to consider not only the risk of peripartum suicide but 
also the risk of infanticide. Another specific population is women who are incarcerated, 
who are often of childbearing age. Women may experience pregnancy and delivery 
while incarcerated, and most incarcerated women have children. Finally, gestational 
surrogacy may present legal and psychiatric issues for both the gestational carrier and

Voluntary Termination of Pregnancy 
and Informed Consent 
Approximately half of all pregnancies in the United States are unintended, with about 
40% of unintended pregnancies ending in VTOP (Finer and Zolna 2016). Rates of un-
intended pregnancies in America are highest among women who are younger in age 
(18–24 years), who are of low income, who have not completed high school, who are 
non-Hispanic Black, and who are cohabiting with a partner but never married (Finer 
and Zolna 2016). 
The Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC) has collected data regarding 
VTOPs in the United States since 1969. The latest available data (from 2015) indicated a 
rate of 11.8 terminations per 1,000 women ages 15–44 years, and a ratio of 188 termina-
tions per 1,000 live births. In the United States, women in their 20s account for most ter-
minations. Approximately 91% of terminations occurred in the first trimester, with only 
1.3% occurring after 21 weeks’ gestation. Approximately one-quarter were described as 
early medical terminations (nonsurgical termination of pregnancy at  8 weeks’ gesta-
tion). Since 2006, the number, rate, and ratio of terminations reported to the CDC have 
steadily declined, with a growing percentage classified as early medical terminations 
(Jatlaoui et al. 2018). 
In the landmark case Roe v. Wade (1973), the U.S. Supreme Court ruled that unduly 
restrictive state regulation of VTOP is unconstitutional, thereby effectively legalizing 
the procedure. Since Roe v. Wade, states have created various laws aimed at regulating 
the circumstances under which VTOPs can be obtained. Common legislative provi -
sions include hospital requirements for terminations, limits on public funding, restric-
tions on private insurance coverage, gestational age limits, bans on “partial birth” 
terminations, allowing health care providers or institutions to refuse termination ser-
vices, state-mandated counseling, waiting periods, and mandated parental involve -
ment in a minor’s decision to have a te rmination (Guttmacher Institute 2019a). The 
most common of such provisions are “informed consent” laws requiring that women 
seeking VTOPs be presented with state-crea ted informational materials before a ter -
mination can be performed (Daniels et al. 2016). 
Informed consent (separate from the provisions required by various states, which 
are described later) is the process whereby communication between a patient and phy-
sician results in a patient’s ability to make a knowledgeable decision to consent to or 
decline a specific medical intervention. Informed consent is an ethical and legal require-
ment and stems from an individual’s right to determine what happens to her body. In-
formed consent requires three fundamental elements: 1) competency of the patient (or 
surrogate) to make decisions, 2) disclosure to the patient of relevant information, and 
3) lack of coercion (or voluntariness) in decision-making (Gossman et al. 2019). Stan-
dards for what information needs to be disclosed by clinicians to meet informed con-
sent requirements vary by state but generally include 
1. The diagnosis or medical condition, 
2. The nature and purpose of the proposed intervention, 
3. The risks and benefits of the proposed intervention, and

As with all medical decisions requiring informed consent, there can be complexity 
in determining a patient’s capacity to ma ke decisions regarding VTOP . Historically, 
women with intellectual or mental disabilities have been denied reproductive freedoms. 
In 2006, the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities sought to change these 
attitudes and provide women with reproductive autonomy by tailoring decision mak-
ing to the needs of the specific individual (Ngwena 2018). In women with severe mental 
illness, this tailored approach can take the form of assisted or surrogate decision-making 
depending on the patient’s decision-making capacity (Coverdale et al. 2004). 
It is important to note that most women with mental illness are able to demonstrate 
the cognitive understanding and appreciation of the consequences of a VTOP for them-
selves and their fetus and to use this to communicate a voluntary decision. There are 
cases, however, in which this ability may be acutely or chronically impaired. The re-
stricted time window in which decisions about termination must be made can be par-
ticularly challenging. In women who have impaired capacity during this crucial period, 
various strategies can be implemented to assist in decision-making. The first step in this 
process involves determining whether the patient has a guardian and, if so, determin-
ing whether the guardian has the authority to make decisions concerning pregnancy 
(Zalpuri et al. 2015). If such a guardian does exist, a substituted judgment should take 
into consideration the patient’s long-standing values and preferences. If such a guard-
ian does not exist, assisted decision-making may be utilized. Assisted decision-making 
can involve strategies such as discussing with the woman her past preferences regard-
ing pregnancy and motherhood and then helping to guide her decision-making based 
on any previously stated preferences. 
Since 2010, more and more states have  adopted termination “informed consent” 
statutes that, as noted earlier, require women seeking terminations be presented with 
state-authored informational materials prior to the procedure (Daniels et al. 2016). 
These statutes have little to do with standard informed consent. Instead, they involve 
giving women seeking VTOPs state-authored information about the alternatives and 
risks to termination and about embryologica l and fetal development (Daniels et al. 
2016). Of the 37 states identified by 2013 as having these laws, 23 states had made their 
material available to the public. These were  included in an analysis that found that 
31% of the statements in the “informed co nsent” material were medically inaccurate 
(with inaccurate statements ranging from 15% to 46% of the material for individual 
states). Examples of medically inaccurate information included statements such as the 
head being formed by 2 weeks and the ability to record brain activity at 4 weeks. These 
inaccurate statements call into question the constitutionality of these laws. Although 
“informed consent” laws were upheld by the Supreme Court in Planned Parenthood of 
Southeastern Pennsylvania v. Casey (1992), it is noted that information should be “truth-
ful” and “nonmisleading” and help the woman to make a “thoughtful and informed” 
decision (Daniels et al. 2016). This mandatory dissemination of inaccurate information 
is particularly concerning given that two-thirds of women seeking VTOP live in states 
with these mandates (Daniels et al. 2016).

Judicial bypass of parental consent for VTOP, in which a pregnant minor can obtain 
a VTOP with judicial consent as opposed to parental consent, occurs in most states 
(Friedman et al. 2015). These teenagers may legally be referred to as Jane Does. A met-
ropolitan Ohio study considering the characteristics of Jane Does found that their mean 
age was 16.4 years and that they usually had long-term boyfriends. They had often 
shared that they were pregnant with trusted adults but not with their parents, out of 
concern of being kicked out of the house or of family violence. The vast majority (95%) 
were eventually granted the judicial bypass. Mental health professionals may be in -
volved in these evaluations and, depending on the state, may be asked to opine whether 
the teenager is sufficiently mature or whether notification of her parents is against her 
best interests. In completing such evaluations, one must consider general principles of 
informed consent and determine whether the decision is being made knowingly, vol-
untarily , and with appropriate decision-making capacity (Friedman et al. 2015).
