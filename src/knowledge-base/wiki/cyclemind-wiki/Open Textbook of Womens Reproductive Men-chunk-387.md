Sleep Interventions 
Both education about sleep hygiene and CBT for insomnia have some evidence of ef-
ficacy in the perinatal population, but research in this area is still young. Studies have 
focused largely on infant sleep regulation, and those that have examined maternal de-
pressive symptoms have found a positive effe ct (Hiscock et al. 2014; Johansen et al. 
2019; Phillips et al. 2010; Smart and Hiscock 2007). Two preliminary open pilot studies 
of CBT for insomnia in perinatal women have promising results (Swanson et al. 2013; 
Tomfohr-Madsen et al. 2017). 
Alternative and Complementary Treatments 
Psychotherapy and psychopharmacological agents at this point hold the greatest body 
of evidence for safety and efficacy in pregnancy and lactation, but evidence is building 
for some alternative and comp lementary therapies. Because patients often see these 
modalities as desirable, clinicians must understand the evidence base and know which 
modalities have evidence of safety and which may be helpful. Although many patients 
believe such approaches will be safe “because they are natural,” the reality is that such 
agents are outside of the FDA approval process and thus unregulated, so there is little 
control over them. Most vitamins, minerals, and nutraceuticals have little evidence of 
either safety or efficacy and should thus be avoided in pregnancy, with the possible ex-
ception of omega-3 fatty acids (Reza et al. 2018). Bright light therapy has been found to 
be both safe and acceptable and superior to a dim-light placebo for AD, but a random-
ized controlled trial in PPD failed to show a difference, and more research is clearly 
needed (Johansen et al. 2019). Physical activity in general has evidence of safety and ac-
ceptability in AD and PPD, although research is lacking on whether it is effective for ei-
ther treating or preventing perinatal depression (Reza et al. 2018). More evidence exists 
for yoga, which is also safe and acceptable and shows greater reduction of depressive 
symptoms when compared with waitlist, treatment as usual, massage, social support, 
and parenting education (Davis et al. 2015; Field et al. 2012, 2013a, 2013b). For further 
details on the evidence base for integrative approaches to the treatment of perinatal de-
pression, please see Chapter 14, “Integrative Approaches to Perinatal Depression.”
