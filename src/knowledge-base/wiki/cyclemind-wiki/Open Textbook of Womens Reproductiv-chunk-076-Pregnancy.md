Pregnancy and Postpartum. The onset of mood episodes in women with BD has 
been strongly associated with reproductive events. In one study of 887 mothers with 
BD, 28% experienced their first lifetime episode of BD in relation to childbirth (Di Florio 
et al. 2018). Current evidence suggests that pregnancy is not protective against mood 
episodes for women with BD, and the postpartum period has been identified as partic-
ularly high risk. Women with BD are seven times as likely to experience a first-time psy-
chiatric admission and twice as likely to experience a psychiatric readmission in the 
first postpartum month as women in the general population (Terp and Mortensen 
1998). Viguera et al. (2000) found that, among women with BD who discontinued lith-
ium maintenance, pregnant women had a rate of recurrence similar to that of nonpreg-
nant women over a 9-month period (52%  and 58%), and postpartum women had an 
increased risk of recurrence nearly three times that of nonpregnant women (70% vs. 
24%). Findings from Di Florio et al. (2014) indicate that mood episodes were more

Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a rare but serious condition typically considered to 
be a psychiatric emergency. Although PPP may occur outside of the setting of BD, there 
is a close link between BD and this distinct clinical syndrome. For a large percentage 
of women who experience PPP , the episode can be understood as either a first episode 
or a recurrence of an underlying BD (Wesseloo et al. 2016). A personal or family history 
of BD is one of the greatest risk factors for PPP (Bergink et al. 2016; Jones and Craddock
