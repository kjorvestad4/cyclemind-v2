“Postpartum Psychosis,” covers the syndrome in greater detail. 
Ms. P is a 36-year-old woman, gravida 1 para 1, who is now 2 weeks postpartum with 
her first child. She has no known personal psychiatric history, but her father has a history 
of bipolar I disorder. She is brought to an emergency department by her worried wife, 
who reports that Ms. P seemed confused and “off.” The patient states that immediately 
upon delivery, she had felt that “I just do n’t feel right in my body. Something is just 
wrong with me all over.” She could not describe the feeling any further than that, and all 
medical workup was unremarkable. She had been discharged to home 36 hours after giv-
ing birth and had tried to reassure herself that her severe free-floating anxiety was nor-
mal. Ms. P was exclusively breastfeeding, and over the next week she was up during the 
night at least five to six times feeding the infant, with no more than 1–2 hours of sleep at 
a time. She began to notice that her thoughts were “racing”; she felt “on edge, wired”; 
and she did not need to sleep much at all. She felt that she had “so much energy that I 
was going to burst.” She started to have intrusive thoughts that harm would come to the 
baby, although she denied homicidal ideat ion or command auditory hallucinations. 
Over the next couple of days, her symptom s progressed to the point that she believed 
she could not trust the intentions of other people and became convinced that they would 
harm her child. When a baby nurse came for her night shift and asked to hold the baby, 
Ms. P became convinced that the baby was going to be permanently harmed under the 
nurse’s care, despite no evidence of any behavior that would have been of concern. Ms. 
P’s wife noticed the patient starting to argue with the nurse and refusing to let her care 
for the baby, and she brought Ms. P to the emergency department for evaluation. 
Risk Factors 
PPP is a rare but serious disorder affect ing approximately 1–2 per 1,000 postpartum 
women (Osborne 2018; VanderKruik et al. 2017). Although the greatest risk factor is 
a prior history of PPP or bipolar disorder, most patients presenting with PPP have no 
previous mood episodes (Bergink et al. 2012; Blackmore et al. 2013; Munk-Olsen et al. 
2009). A family history of bipolar disorder, particularly in first-degree relatives, also 
confers risk (Robertson et al . 2005). Other risk factors include primiparity, advanced 
maternal age, and the occurrence of a m ood disorder during pregnancy (Bergink et 
al. 2012; Blackmore et al. 2006; Valdimarsdóttir et al. 2009). 
Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
PPP is not currently recognized as a distin ct clinical disorder in DSM-5 (American 
Psychiatric Association 2013).  The DSM-5 brief psychoti c disorder diagnosis has a 
“with postpartum onset” specifier that is added if onset is during pregnancy or within 
4 weeks postpartum. Because PP P is widely considered to be an affective disorder, 
this diagnosis often does not sufficiently describe the disorder.
