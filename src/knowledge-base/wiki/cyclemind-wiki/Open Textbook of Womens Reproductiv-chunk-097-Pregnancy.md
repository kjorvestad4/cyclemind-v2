Reproductive Loss 
The course of trauma-related disorders rela ted to reproductive loss can be compli -
cated by a history of previous losses, the circumstances of the pregnancy, and the de-
gree of support from the partner/father of the baby. Evidence indicates that the length 
of gestation does not determine the course  or intensity of trauma symptoms, and an 
early gestational loss can be just as dist ressing as a later one (Leis-Newman 2012). 
Similarly, losing an unwanted pregnancy can be as distressing as losing a wanted one 
because the degree of attachment to a pregnancy can change over time, according to 
circumstances, and across developmental ph ases within the reproductive lifespan 
(Leis-Newman 2012). 
Traumatic Delivery 
Although longitudinal trajectories for PTSD in the population at large have been well es-
tablished, data on the clinical course of childbirth-related trauma are limited due to high 
attrition rates and multiple confounders. Aprospective study in the Netherlands enrolled 
348 participants, of whom 21% reported delivery as traumatic; however, at 10 months, 
only 0.35% of the entire sample met criteria for PTSD. Notable limitations include attrition 
of one-third of the sample and a lack of control for prior trauma and PTSD (Haagen et al. 
2015). The largest study to date examined 950 women from a community sample over 
a 6-month period, 226 of whom identified delivery as traumatic. Four trajectories 
were identified: resilient (61.9%), recovere d (18.5%), chronic PTSD (13.7%), and de -
layed PTSD (5.8%) (Dikmen-Yildiz et al. 2018). The high rates of recovery implore ex-
amination of resilience in addition to risk factors to develop more targeted treatments.

Sociodemographic Considerations 
Populations who may be predisposed to develop perinatal PTSD include women of 
younger age; women who lack support from the father of the baby, partner, or family; 
single parents; women with a preexistin g psychiatric disord er; Black women and 
women of other racial minorities; and LGBTQ+ individuals (Capaldi et al. 2012; Vig-
nato et al. 2018). Psychosocial stressors, such as homelessness, food and financial inse-
curity, and exposure to violence, can aggr avate symptoms of PTSD (Capaldi et al. 
2012). Conversely, factors favoring a good prognosis include a shorter duration of 
trauma symptoms, good premorbid functioning, and strong social supports (Capaldi 
et al. 2012). Having adequate time to be free from occupational demands during the

Economic Considerations 
Economic factors play a critical role in the course of perinatal trauma. Having finan -
cial security to both care for and provide for the baby and to allow for sufficient time 
off from work to adjust to the demands of  a new infant in the home plays a critical 
role in the early postpartum course (Meltzer-Brody et al. 2017). 
Pathophysiology 
Stress-diathesis models of PTSD include biological, psychological, and social vulnera-
bilities that are triggered by acute trauma, with the course of illness mediated by risk, 
vulnerability, and resilience factors (Bomyea et al. 2012). Biological, psychological, and 
social factors mediate the pathophysiology of trauma and stress-related disorders. No 
data indicate whether the biological pathways of perinatal PTSD differ from those of 
PTSD from other causes, so the following di scussion on biological factors is drawn 
from the general literature. 
Biological Factors 
The role of hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis suppression by stress hormones is a well-
established hallmark of the fear response (Dunlop and Wong 2019), but the relationship 
between an acute fear response and the prolonged hyperreactivity of PTSD is less well un-
derstood. Animal models of extreme chronic stress demonstrate neuronal remodeling, 
with the most notable changes in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus. In these areas, 
prolonged stress produces reductions in synaptic connectivity, disruption in glucocorti-
coid signaling, increased neuroinflammation, reduced levels of brain-derived neuro -
trophic factor (BDNF), and deficits in astrocytes (Abdallah et al. 2019; McEwen 2017). 
Reduced uptake of glutamate in the synapses leads to increased extracellular glutamate 
and excitotoxicity and ultimately to neuronal atrophy and synaptic loss (Abdallah et al. 
2019). In preclinical studies, these neuronal changes are associated with behavioral im-
pairment and anxiety dysregulation. Chronic stress in animal models also results in in-
creased synaptic connectivity and upregulated BDNF in the basolateral amygdala and 
nucleus accumbens, which is similarly associated with behavioral disturbances (Abdal-
lah et al. 2019; McEwen 2017). Human imaging studies looking at neurobiological find-
ings associated with PTSD have implicated reduced hippocampal volumes and structural 
differences in the dorsal and rostral anterior cingulate cortices, ventromedial prefrontal 
cortex, amygdala, and insula (Cirino and Knapp 2019; Fitzgerald et al. 2018). These 
pathways, and in particular the hypothalam ic-pituitary-adrenal axis, are altered in 
women with chronic PTSD and undergo changes during pregnancy and postpartum, 
further making women susceptible to peripartum PTSD (Cirino and Knapp 2019). 
Genetic studies investigating gene associations with PTSD have found gene variants 
(e.g., FKBP5) that mediate the volume of various brain regions, such as the putamen 
and amygdala (Kang et al. 2019; van der Merwe et al. 2019). Immune models of PTSD 
postulate an association of PTSD with proinflammatory states. Studies show that pa-
tients with PTSD have increased levels of proinflammatory markers such as interleu-

Psychological Factors 
Cognitive and affective theories suggest a predisposition to the development of PTSD 
based on prior cognitive styles and preexist ing mental health disorders (Ozer et al. 
2003). Other predisposing factors include female gender, family history of psychopa-
thology, and prior trauma exposure (Ozer et al. 2003). The psychological pathophys-
iology of PTSD is hypothesized to include disruptions in longstanding, firmly held 
cognitive schema around safety, trust, and self-confidence that lead to a contradictory, 
conflict-ridden dissonance. Th is contradictory state with high emotional valence is 
thought to result in symptoms such as reexperiencing, hyperarousal, avoidance, and 
dissociation as various methods to reconcile these conflicts (Ford et al. 2015). 
Sociodemographic Considerations 
Multiple studies have investigated racial differences in trauma exposure (Alegría et 
al. 2013; Huth-Bocks et al. 2013; Roberts et  al. 2011; Seng et al. 2011). These studies 
demonstrate that Black individuals have equivalent or higher trauma exposure and 
likely underreport traumatic events (Seng et al. 2011). They uniformly show a higher 
lifetime prevalence of PTSD in Black and indigenous women. Black women are at 
higher risk of being victims of violent crimes, have repeated exposure to racism, and 
are exposed to toxic stress, making them particularly vulnerable to developing PTSD 
before or during the perinatal period (Seng et al. 2011). 
Although PTSD risk has been shown to be elevated in Black women across socio -
demographic groups, race alone is unlikely to be the sole mediator in PTSD patho -
physiology. Concomitant risk factors, as determined by social and economic variables 
and determinants, are likely to contribute as well. Maternal mortality review commit-
tee data in recent years indicates higher rates of pregnancy complications for Black 
women, putting them at a theoretically higher risk of subsequent trauma and stressor-
related disorders. Factors related to systemic racism are a driving force in this disparity 
but are more difficult to identify and study, limiting published studies to data. Social 
supports, attachment/bonding, and social co gnition likely play a mediating role in 
PTSD pathophysiology, and racial disparities in economics, housing stability, and so-
cial constructs may have direct impact on the development of PTSD (Seng et al. 2011). 
For LGBTQ+ individuals, who have often faced chronic stress, structural stigma in 
health care systems have direct implicatio ns on both medical comorbidities and all-
cause morbidity and mortality (Hatzenbuehler 2009).

other medical interventions, and psychotherapy. Some guidelines, including those devel-

There are five core principles of TIC: safety, trustworthiness, choice, collaboration, 
and empowerment (Substance Abuse and Me ntal Health Services Administration 
2014a). Safety refers to ensuring both physical and emotional security. Physicians and 
other providers should cultivate a safe space for individuals to discuss trauma and its 
sequelae. Maintaining appropriate boundaries and making instructions and tasks clear 
is part of trustworthiness, as is following through on commitments. Given the lack of 
control individuals have had during trauma tic experiences, empowering patients to 
make active choices helps to restore their loci of control. Maximizing collaboration is also 
an integral part of TIC, both with patients and with other physicians and providers in 
their care (Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration 2014a). Em-
powerment also comes through skill building. 
Psychopharmacology 
Medications can be used to ameliorate the biological basis for PTSD symptoms along 
with co-occurring psychiatric diagnoses, and they may indirectly benefit psychological 
and social symptoms. Clinicians must consider the level of evidence available to sup-
port the use of a specific medication and tailor pharmacotherapy to the individual pa-
tient. Few studies have specifically investigated medication use for peripartum PTSD 
or PTSD specific to traumatic delivery, so management is often extrapolated from stud-
ies in nonpregnant/postpartum populations. 
First-Line Medications 
Evidence for PTSD pharmacology in the general population is strongest for three spe-
cific selective serotonin reuptake inhibito rs (SSRIs)—sertraline, paroxetine, and flu -
oxetine—and one serotonin norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor (SNRI)—venlafaxine 
(U.S. Department of Veterans Affairs an d Department of Defense 2017). Currently 
only sertraline and paroxetine are approved by the FDA for PTSD (Brady et al. 2000; 
Marshall et al. 2001). Citalopram is a first-line medication option along with sertraline 
and fluoxetine for child and adolescent patients, as recommended by the American 
Academy for Child and Adolescent Psychiatry. In PTSD, one mechanism of action of 
SSRIs might be to stimulate neuronal connections through BDNF, a hypothesis based 
on animal and clinical studies (Duman et al. 1997; Vermetten et al. 2003). Venlafaxine,
