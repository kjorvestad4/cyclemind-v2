Cognitive Therapy 
Several studies have investigated specific  cognitive therapies in the management of 
traumatic stress after childbirth. A randomized controlled trial of women after emer-
gent cesarean section found success in the use of an online visuospatial task aimed at 
reducing intrusive traumatic memories (Horsch et al. 2017). A four-session grief coun-
seling intervention in women who have experienced stillbirths found reduction in 
PTSD severity (Navidian et al. 2017). An internet-based cognitive-behavioral therapy 
intervention after childbirth was found to reduce PTSD symptoms in the women ran-
domized to receive the therapy (Nieminen et al. 2016). A recent systematic review and 
meta-analysis concluded that many trauma-focused psychological interventions can 
be helpful in reducing symptoms of postpartum PTSD in the short and medium term 
but that evidence is lacking on whether any particular intervention is more effective 
than others and on whether these interventions can help women with long-term re -
covery from clinically significant PTSD (Furuta et al. 2018). 
Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing 
EMDR was initially introduced in 1989 as an eight-phase manualized therapy with the 
objective of assisting patients in accessing and processing traumatic memories while 
bringing them to an adaptive resolution. Initial focus is on evaluation and planning fol-
lowed by desensitization and reprocessing, with end goals of installing positive cogni-
tions, replacing negative ones, and releasing associated somatic tension. The novel aspect 
of EMDR as compared with other trauma-focused therapies for PTSD is its inclusion of 
eye movements. Although experimental studies have shown that horizontal eye move-
ments are associated with a decrease in the vividness, emotionality, and arousal associ-
ated with memories, most of these studies are of healthy participants (van den Hout et 
al. 2001). Empirical evidence supports EMDR as superior to waitlist controls and com-
parable with PE and cognitive-based ps ychotherapies (Davidson and Parker 2001; 
Powers et al. 2010). Although one meta-analysis found no difference between EMDR 
and PE, another found PE to be superior to EMDR (Rothbaum et al. 2005; Taylor et al. 
2003). One small pilot study of four women that investigated EMDR after childbirth 
found it to be effective in the reduction of PTSD symptoms (Sandström et al. 2008). 
Treatment Challenges 
Treatment challenges fall into three broad categories: physician-/provider-specific, 
patient-specific, and system barriers. Additional challenges include the lack of studies 
specific to the management of perinatal PTSD.
