and metabolism of neurotransmitters and the regulation of homocysteine levels in the 
vasculature (Bottiglieri 1996). It is also crucial in supporting healthy fetal growth and

The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommends that all women who are ei -
ther planning or capable of pregnancy take a daily supplement containing 400–800 µg 
of folic acid daily. This dose of daily folate—started prior to conception and continued 
throughout pregnancy—has been associated with decreased risk of neural tube de -
fects (Freedman et al. 2018). It is difficult to obtain the recommended amount of folate 
consistently by diet, and in 1998, the FDA mandated fortification of foods. Since then, 
prevalence rates of infants born with neural tube defects have decreased significantly 
(Bibbins-Domingo et al. 2017). Maternal folate status also has other important effects 
on the fetus. A study found that low folate levels in early pregnancy may affect later 
cognitive functioning in children, even in the absence of neural tube defects. Low ma-
ternal folate levels have been associated with a smaller total brain volume and pre -
dicted poorer performance on language and visuospatial assessments in the children. 
Thus, folate insufficiency in early pregnancy may have long-lasting effects on fetal 
brain development (Ars et al. 2019). 
In terms of perinatal depression, folate deficiency has been associated with increased 
antenatal depression scores, and prolonged folate supplementation (>6 months) during 
pregnancy has been associated with a decreased risk of postpartum depression (PPD) 
in Chinese women (Chong et al. 2014; Yan et al. 2017). A small, open-label trial of a pre-
natal supplement in depressed versus heal thy women with a history of depression 
found that folate supplementation may have  some efficacy in both prevention and 
treatment of perinatal depression (Freedma n et al. 2018). In addition, evidence sup -
ports the use of folate as an antidepre ssant augmentation strategy for depression 
during pregnancy (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2010). 
Of note, folate is typically ingested in its natural form (dihydrofolate) in foods or 
in its synthetic form in vitamin supplements, which is then converted to the active 
form (L-methylfolate) by the enzyme methylene tetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR). 
The MTHFR gene has polymorphisms (seen in up to 50% of individuals in the United 
States) that affect the conversion of ingested folate (Naninck et al. 2019). Individuals

Vitamin D 
Vitamin D receptors are widespread throughout the human body, and it has many 
functions beyond its role in calcium homeostasis (Agarwal et al. 2018). Low vitamin D 
intake, or low 25(OH)D-levels, have been associated with preeclampsia, gestational di-
abetes, bacterial vaginosis, shorter length of gestation, and PPD (Christesen et al. 
2012). Conversely, vitamin D supplementation during pregnancy may be associated 
with reduced risk of preeclampsia and gestational diabetes. It also may reduce the risks 
of low birth weight and postpartum hemorrhage (Palacios et al. 2019). Vitamin D sup-
plementation has also been associated with positive fetal/infant outcomes, such as de-
creased risk of infant wheezing and of schizophrenia (Freedman et al. 2018). 
Low vitamin D levels have also been associated with both antenatal and postnatal 
depression and depressive symptoms (Aghajafari et al. 2018; Wang et al. 2018; Wil -
liams et al. 2016). There appears to be an inverse relationship between low serum vita-
min D levels and both pre- and postnatal depression (Trujillo et al. 2018). In one of the 
most robust meta-analyses (in nonpregnant women), an improvement was seen in de-
pression with supplementation of vitami n D !800 IU/day (and concomitant change 
in serum vitamin D levels) compared wi th antidepressant medication (Spedding 
2014). Vitamin D supplementation during pregnancy is low risk (at recommended 
doses, typically around 800–1000 IU daily), has benefits for general health, and may 
serve an adjunctive role in treatment of perinatal depression. 
S-Adenosyl Methionine 
S-adenosyl methionine (SAM-e) is a methyl donor in the brain. It is involved in the syn-
thesis of hormones, neurotransmitters, nucleic acids, proteins, and phospholipids, and 
it acts as an intermediate in the synthesis of norepinephrine, dopamine, and serotonin 
(Mischoulon 2009). SAM-e has been found to be efficacious in the treatment of major 
depressive disorder and superior to placeb o and equivalent to tricyclic antidepres -
sants, with fewer side effects (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2010; Sarris et al. 2016). To 
our knowledge, no studies have examined the efficacy or safety of SAM-e for depres-
sion specifically during pregnancy. One placebo-controlled study of SAM-e for post-
natal depressive symptoms found a significant decrease in depressive symptoms with 
SAM-e compared with placebo (Cerutti et al. 1993; Deligiannidis and Freeman 2014). 
Supplementation with SAM-e has been used to treat intrahepatic cholestasis during 
pregnancy, and it has generally been well tolerated with no adverse effects noted when 
used for this purpose. When used for depression in nonpregnant patients, side effects 
may include increased anxiety, mania, or hypomania in patients with bipolar depres-

Omega-3 Fatty Acids 
Omega-3 fatty acids (n-3s) are found primarily in oily, cold-water fish and certain nuts 
and seeds. The three main forms of n-3s are docosahexaenoic acid (DHA), eicosapen-
taenoic acid (EPA), and alpha linolenic acid (ALA). Both DHA and EPA are biologically 
active n-3 fatty acids and are critical to fetal growth and development. They are also 
important in visual and cognitive function in newborns, children, and adults. ALA can 
be obtained from plant sources such as flax seeds, walnuts, kale, and spinach. However, 
because only ~10% of ALA is converted to DHA, those who consume only these plant 
sources of n-3s (ALA) need to eat significantly larger quantities to obtain adequate daily 
amounts. 
Evidence of the effect of n-3s and their benefits in pregnancy are mixed. A Cochrane 
review of studies found a modest reduction in preterm birth (<37 weeks), early preterm 
birth (<34 weeks), and low birth weight babies in women consuming n-3s and a small 
increase in birth weight and length of gestation (Middleton et al. 2018; Newberry et al. 
2016). On the other hand, in a meta-analysis assessing the association of n-3s with mul-
tiple perinatal factors, supplementation was not associated with prevention of preterm 
birth, preeclampsia risk reduction, decreased fetal growth restriction/small for gesta-
tional age infants, decreased rates of PPD, or improved child neurodevelopment (Sac-
cone et al. 2016). In terms of fetal development, n-3s have been correlated with benefits, 
such as enhanced neonatal visual development, improved CNS functioning, and im-
proved childhood intelligence scores. DHA and EPA also appear to reduce the risk of 
childhood allergic diseases, such as asthma, autoimmune diseases, atopic dermatitis, 
and allergic rhinitis (Freedman et al. 2018). 
A review of n-3 supplementation and its effects on depression in nonpregnant pop-
ulations found that n-3s have been associated with a reduction in depressive symptoms 
(Qureshi and Al-Bedah 2013). In a review that examined studies looking at timing of 
intervention, type and dose of n-3 used, and existing maternal depressive symptoms, 
EPA supplementation was associated with reduced depressive symptoms both during 
pregnancy and postpartum (Hsu et al. 2018) . A randomized trial of higher-dose n-3 
supplementation in overweight and obese pregnant women using a combined dose of 
2 g/day of DHA+EPA found that the subjects had comparatively lower C-reactive pro-
tein levels (CRP) and decreased adipose and placental proinflammatory cytokines than 
control subjects (Haghiac et al. 2015). Maternal inflammation in the form of elevated 
CRP and proinflammatory cytokines has been associated with both depressive symp-
toms and adverse fetal/infant brain development (Hunter et al. 2019). 
In summary, n-3s may improve obstetrical outcomes and perinatal depressive symp-
toms and may reduce inflammatory cytokines. Variations in dose and methods across 
studies have made interpretation of the data complicated. However, based on tolerabil-
ity and possible benefits during pregnancy, low risk of use, and benefits for general 
health, n-3s may serve an adjunctive role for supplementation during pregnancy (De-
ligiannidis and Freeman 2010). Most expert s in perinatal mental health recommend

Probiotics 
Probiotics are living microorganisms that may play a beneficial role in health, and the 
use of probiotics during pregnancy to reduce rates of multiple adverse pregnancy out-
comes has become a common study interest. The proposed mechanisms of action of 
probiotics in the mental health literature focus on a bidirectional relationship between 
the brain and gastrointestinal tract (i.e., the “gut-brain axis”). Clear mechanisms of ac-
tion, however, remain the topic of research and are primarily theoretical at this time 
(Noonan et al. 2020). Probiotics are theorized to affect the genitourinary-gastrointesti-
nal tract relationship and to interfere with pathogens and inflammatory cytokines as-
sociated with preterm labor (Reid and Bocking 2003). 
Unfortunately, recent studies have been lackluster regarding probiotics and reduced 
risks of preterm birth, gestational diabetes, small or large for gestational age infants, 
or premature rupture of membranes. Study variables are considerably heterogeneous, 
including timing of intervention, type of probiotic supplemented, stated versus actual 
content of probiotic, and duration of the in tervention, which makes it difficult to in -
terpret the evidence (Jarde et al. 2018). The Probiotics in Pregnancy study sought to 
evaluate the impact of the probiotic Lactobacillus rhamnosus HN001 on pregnancy and 
postpartum outcomes through a large randomized controlled trial. Women who re -
ceived the probiotic had significantly lower Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale 
scores and lower rates of anxiety screening scores than those who received placebo 
(Slykerman et al. 2017). Although effects were modest, L. rhamnosus may be a reason-
able recommendation as an adjunct for mild depression and anxiety, along with stan-
dard-of-care screening and treatment recommendations during pregnancy. 
Melatonin 
Melatonin is a hormone secreted by the pineal gland (and the placenta during pregnancy) 
and is involved in regulating normal sleep-wake cycles by influencing circadian rhythm 
timing. It has also been shown to have antioxidant, anti-inflammatory, and antiapoptotic 
effects (Yang et al. 2018). Melatonin supplements are typically created synthetically (al-
though they can be derived from animals or microorganisms) and are sold over the 
counter as a supplement for insomnia and circadian rhythm problems, such as jet lag. 
From existing human and animal data, we understand that the interaction between 
normal circadian rhythms and melatonin pr oduction is important across human re -
production. During human pregnancies, melatonin serum concentrations increase 
markedly, and this is thought to stimulate luteal progesterone production and to de-
crease prostaglandin production (Brzezinsk i et al. 1992). Melatonin both crosses the 
placenta and is produced by the placenta in normal pregnancies (Reiter et al. 2014). 
Melatonin also plays several roles in normal pregnancy, including fetal maturation 
and the onset of labor, in association with oxytocin. Researchers are currently exam-
ining the use of exogenous melatonin in reducing adverse neurodevelopmental out -
comes in “at-risk” fetuses due to either grow th restriction or the risk of prematurity 
(Palmer et al. 2019; Wilkinson et al. 2016). In addition, there is currently research on 
melatonin’s capacity to mitigate placental oxidative stress during pregnancy; placen-

Tryptophan 
L-Tryptophan, an essential amino acid, serves as a precursor to the monoamine neu -
rotransmitter serotonin and the hormone melatonin. During the perinatal period, levels 
of total tryptophan are lower starting in the second trimester until delivery (van Lee et 
al. 2018). This decreased level of tryptophan may result in a decreased serotonin level 
producing symptoms of depression or anxiety, as well as a decreased melatonin level 
that may disrupt the sleep-wake cycle (Bell et al. 2001). Limited trials have suggested 
that in healthy, nonpregnant adults, supplementation of L-tryptophan and 5-hydroxy-
tryptophan (5-HTP , a metabolite of L-tryptophan) may improve symptoms of depres-
sion and anxiety (Birdsall 1998; Kikuchi et al. 2021). However, due to limited data 
evaluating efficacy and safety and to concerns about contamination, the routine use of 
L-tryptophan or 5-HTP supplements as an tidepressants is not recommended during 
pregnancy (Shaw et al. 2002). 
Specific Foods 
Delineating which parts of the diet are the most beneficial—the in dividual foods, the 
nutrients contained in them, or perhaps the entire diet itself—is complex. However, 
certain foods as described here may be particularly beneficial to perinatal outcomes, 
particularly in regard to perinatal depression and anxiety. 
Fish and Seafood 
A cohort study was conducted in 1966 in Finland based on the finding that n-3s—found 
in smaller cold-water fish—were related to mood. In this study, higher frequency of fish 
consumption was associated with a lower risk of depression, and lower frequency of 
fish consumption was associated with increased risk of depression in women but not 
in men (Timonen et al. 2004). Although these data do not appear to translate directly 
to pregnant women in many studies, the Pregnancy Diet and Associated Outcomes in 
the Avon Longitudinal Study of Parents and Children study data suggest that con -
sumption of fish and shellfish high in n- 3s and iodine was independently associated 
with lower frequency of maternal depressive and anxiety symptoms. Furthermore, 
there was a lower frequency of fetal growth restriction and improved measures of neu-
rocognitive development in the children of these women. These findings support the 
recommendation by multiple organizations that pregnant women eat at least two serv-
ings of low/no-mercury fish or shellfish weekly as part of a healthy pregnancy diet 
(Emmett et al. 2015). See the previous section, “Omega-3 Fatty Acids,” for detailed in-
formation on these nutrients as they relate to fish and seafood intake.

Dark Chocolate 
Cocoa and chocolate products are rich in flavonoids, a group of polyphenols that are 
potent antioxidants and have anti-inflammatory effects. A recent large cross-sectional 
survey in nonpregnant subjects found that dark chocolate was associated with signifi-
cantly lower odds of depressive symptoms (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2010; Jackson 
et al. 2019). In a systematic  review of the effects of ch ocolate consumption in preg -
nancy, chocolate intake was found to be beneficial, with both acute stimulatory effects 
on fetal reactivity and anti hypertensive effects in moth ers (Latif 2019). Other recent 
studies found that chocolate consumption of 30 g (~1 oz) of 70% cocoa daily reduced 
blood pressure without increasing weight gain or total cholesterol level in pregnant 
women (Di Renzo et al. 2012). Among pregnant Japanese women, those with the high-
est chocolate consumption (87.5 g/week) had a significantly lower risk of developing 
gestational diabetes than those with the lowest consumption (Dong et al. 2019). Dark 
chocolate consumption in pregnancy seems to reduce the negative effects of prenatal 
maternal stress on infant temperament and may be associated with a greater positive 
reactivity and activity in infants at 6 months (Brillo and Di Renzo 2015). Dark choco-
late should be consumed in moderation, especially during pregnancy, due to both the 
caffeine content (which should be limited during pregnancy) and the caloric intake. 
Botanicals 
The use of supplements and herbal products (botanicals) is popular among pregnant 
women (Dante et al. 2014). However, knowledge about both the efficacy and safety of 
these products in pregnancy and lactation is lacking (Dugoua et al. 2006). These prod-
ucts are not subject to the same stringent regulation by the FDA as pharmacological 
agents. Thus, there may be more variation in content and dosing compared with an 
FDA-approved medication. This section reviews a sample of commonly used supple-
ments and botanicals in pregnancy, with a focus on perinatal mood effects. 
St. John’s Wort (Hypericum Perforatum) 
Hypericum perforatum is derived from a naturally occurring plant, St. John’s wort (SJW). 
SJW has been used since ancient times for its medicinal properties (Deligiannidis and 
Freeman 2014). Several of its bioactive components, such as hypericin, hyperforin, and 
flavonoids, have affinity for neurotransmitter systems important in the pathophysiol-
ogy (and pharmacotherapy) of major depression, including activity at GABA, NMDA, 
and serotonin receptors (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2014). It may also inhibit interleu-
kin-6 and increase cortisol production, resulting in both anti-inflammatory and anti -
depressant effects (Butterweck 2003). One Cochrane review suggested that it is as 
effective as conventional antidepressants (and superior to placebo) for mild to mod -
erate depression, with fewer adverse side effects (Linde et al. 2008). A major safety 
concern about SJW is its ability to alter the pharmacokinetics or clinical response of a 
variety of clinically important drugs because it is a strong inducer of the cytochrome 
P450 3A4 enzyme. For example, SJW can lower the levels of hormonal contraceptives, 
increasing the risk of an unplanned pregnancy (Soleymani et al. 2017).

The data on the effects of SJW on human pregnancy are very limited. In a series of 58 
pregnancies exposed to SJW (49 of which included first-trimester exposure) compared 
with two control groups, no increase was reported in the rate of major malformations, 
preterm birth, or live birth (Moretti et al. 2009). Most experts do not recommend the use

Cannabis 
With the legalization of cannabis (marijuana) for either medical or recreational use in 
several states in the United States (and all of Canada), reports of use during pregnancy 
have increased markedly. Morning sickness , depression, stress, and anxiety are all 
commonly cited reasons for patients to ch oose to use cannabis during pregnancy 
(Brown et al. 2017; Roberson et al. 2014). As with other supplements, cannabis is often 
favored by women who want something “natural” to treat their symptoms, despite 
recommendations by the ACOG to avoid cannabis in pregnancy. Current data suggest 
a negative impact on fetal brain development that has been associated with problems 
with behavioral self-regulation, a precursor to later and more serious problems in 
adulthood. Children of mothers who used marijuana beyond 10 weeks’ gestation were 
found to be at increased risk for regulation problems at 3 months of age compared with 
infants who were not exposed to cannabis (or those whose mothers stopped using can-
nabis prior to 10 weeks’ gestation) (Hoffman et al. 2020). For these reasons, avoidance 
of all forms of cannabis is recommended during pregnancy. 
Mind-Body Practices 
Mind-body interventions are a broad category  of integrative practices that focus on 
the connections between the brain, mind, body, and behavior. A broad range of inter-
ventions can be included un der the category of mind-body, ranging from a focus on 
exercise and sleep to formal mind-body in terventions such as yoga and meditation. 
According to the 2012 National Health Interview Survey, the most common mind and 
body practices used by adults include yoga and meditation practices, including pro-
grams that incorporate mindfulness-based interventions (Clarke et al. 2018; National 
Center for Complementary and Integrative Health 2018). An analysis of data from the 
2007 National Health Interview Survey looking at the use of integrative medicine ap-
proaches found that mind-body practices were the most common modality used by 
women during pregnancy and postpartum (Birdee et al. 2014). 
Mind-body treatments have demonstrated effectiveness in helping lower anxiety 
and depression in pregnancy (Sheffield and Woods-Giscombé 2016). These practices 
may affect mood states by inducing relaxation and improving neuroplasticity, as mea-
sured by serum biomarkers including brain-derived neurotrophic factor, sirtuin 1, cor-
tisol, interleukin-6, dehydroepiandrosterone sulfate, and markers of oxidative stress 
(Tolahunase et al. 2018). A systematic review of nonpharmacological interventions for

Mindfulness Practice 
As mindfulness-based interventions (MBIs) have gained popularity over the past de-
cade, more focus has been dedicated to the potential benefits that these interventions 
may provide for alleviating symptoms of depression and anxiety for women during 
the perinatal period. Reviews evaluating MB Is, such as mindfulness-based stress re-
duction and mindfulness-based cognitive therapy, have sh own efficacy in reducing the 
severity of perinatal depression and anxiety and in alleviating perinatal anxiety and 
depression symptoms (Shi and MacBeth 2017) and stress symptoms (Lever Taylor et 
al. 2016). They have also been found beneficial in improving other aspects of maternal 
well-being, including negative affect and stress (Lever Taylor et al. 2016; Matvienko-
Sikar et al. 2016). One randomized controlled trial evaluating the effectiveness of MBIs 
for preventing a relapse of depression in pregnant women found that MBIs were su-
perior to treatment as usual (Dimidjian et al. 2016). 
Yoga 
The practice of yoga usually refers to its three main components: postures, breathing, 
and meditation. Yoga practices have been associated with reducing sympathetic activ-
ity, improving vagal tone, balancing heart rate variability, normalizing hypothalamic-
pituitary-adrenal axis activity, and infl uencing monoamine changes. These effects 
likely explain how yoga affects mood (Brown and Gerbarg 2005; Streeter et al. 2012). 
A systematic review and meta-analysis of randomized controlled trials to determine 
the effectiveness of yoga practice for prenatal depression found that women both with 
and without depression rated decreased depressive symptoms with yoga compared 
with standard prenatal care, antenatal exercises, or social support. Furthermore, this 
review found that an integrated yoga intervention (including breathing and medita-
tion as well as movement) significantly reduced the level of prenatal depression, 
whereas exercise-based yoga alone did not sh ow this same effect (Gong et al. 2015). 
However, another large systematic review found that depression and anxiety during 
the perinatal period were significantly reduced regardless of yoga intervention style. 
Yoga interventions were also found to have a beneficial effect on outcomes such as 
stress, pain, biomarkers (cortisol, progesterone, and estriol), anger, mindfulness, birth 
weight, gestational age, maternal–fetal at tachment, heart rate variability, optimism 
and power, and well-being (Jiang et al. 2015; Sheffield and Woods-Giscombé 2016). In 
another review of yoga interventions during pregnancy, women participating in yoga 
intervention groups had significantly improved relationships and reduced anxiety, de-
pression, anger, and stress. Even a one-time 20-minute yoga session led to significant 
improvements in anxiety and depression. Longer-term interventions showed signifi-
cant improvements in anxiety, depression, and stress; reduced anger; and lower corti-
sol levels (Jiang et al. 2015; Sheffield and Woods-Giscombé 2016). 
Yoga modified for pregnancy is an activity recommended as safe by the ACOG for 
women engaging in regular physical activity during pregnancy. Guidelines note that 
exposure to excess heat, as with “hot yoga,” should be avoided, as well as postures that 
may result in decreased venous return or hypotension (American College of Obstetri-
cians and Gynecologists 2020).

Exercise 
Regular physical activity is one of the mo st beneficial therapies available for numer-
ous physical and mental health conditions. Ex ercise is beneficial in the perinatal pe -
riod in nearly all pregnancies for the health of both the mother and the baby. Physical 
activity and exercise can also have a positive effect on mood. Exercise leads to an in-
crease in body and brain temperature, which leads to a feeling of general relaxation 
and tranquility. Exercise can have effect s on depression that are comparable with 
those of antidepressant medications. It is hypothesized that this is due at least in part 
to the secretion of endogenous opioid peptides in the brain that accompany exercise 
of sufficient intensity and duration (Dinas  et al. 2011). Exercise may improve auto -
nomic nervous system functioning by increa sing heart rate variability and reducing 
symptoms of anxiety (Stubbs et al. 2017). 
Exercise has been studied in relation to  both the prevention and the treatment of 
perinatal depression. When exercise was systematically reviewed for the prevention 
of antenatal depression, significant reductions were noted in all six of the included 
randomized controlled trials, with no difference in efficacy between aerobic and non-
aerobic exercise (Daley et al. 2015). In addition, exercise has a positive impact on both 
anxiety and stress reduction, and it improves the overall quality of life (Ko%omanska 
et al. 2019). When an exercise program was initiated in previously inactive pregnant 
women, significant reductions in depression, anger, tension, fatigue, and anxiety were 
noted after only 4 weeks of recorded episod es of moderate-to-vigorous exercise for 
30 minutes, four times per week (Gaston and Prapavessis 2013). Regular exercise (three 
times per week) has been shown to decrease the symptoms and incidence of depression 
in pregnant women (Perales et al. 2015). Even light-to-moderate physical activity has 
been associated with reduced PPD symptoms and, in cases of preexisting PPD, has also 
been shown to increase the odds of resolving depression by 54% (McCurdy et al. 2017). 
Physical exercise during pregnancy and th e postpartum period is a safe strategy 
for achieving better psychological well-being and reducing depressive symptoms in 
women (Poyatos-León et al. 2017). Healthy women should try to get at least 150 min-
utes of moderate-intensity aerobic activity over the course of each week. Women who 
regularly engage in vigorous-intensity aerobic activity may continue at this level pro-
vided that their condition and health remain unchanged or as recommended by their 
obstetrical care provider (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2020). 
In addition to mood benefits, regular physical activity has other benefits for preg -
nancy, including reducing the risk of excessive gestational weight gain, low back pain, 
gestational diabetes, and cesa rean delivery (Bauer et al. 2020; Domenjoz et al. 2014; 
Nascimento et al. 2012). 
Acupuncture 
Although acupuncture is a common complementary intervention used by women for 
both physical and mental health symptoms, there remain few studies and inconsistent 
findings regarding its utility for treatment of depression during pregnancy (van Ra -
vesteyn et al. 2017). A large review found that acupuncture may be an effective com-
plementary approach for depression in preg nancy (Smith et al. 2019). This is largely 
based on one study in which antenatal de pression outcomes were improved with

Other Approaches 
Bright Light Therapy 
As mentioned, women with depression during pregnancy may be hesitant to use med-
ications due to the fear of exposure to the fetus. Bright light therapy is a reasonable treat-
ment for perinatal depression because it is low cost, home-based, and has a much lower

commonly reported symptoms in women with perinatal depression, and bright light

Studies have suggested that disturbances in the regulation of melatonin and circa-
dian rhythms are associated with depression during pregnancy and postpartum. Timed 
bright light treatment can synchronize circ adian rhythms and has been used in the 
treatment of mood disorders (Parry et al. 2008). Small studies have found bright light 
therapy to be effective for perinatal depr ession. Significant decreases in depressive 
symptoms scored by depression rating scales were found with 5 weeks of treatment 
(Epperson et al. 2004). Larger randomized controlled studies are needed. Recommen-
dations are an initial dosing of 15–30 minutes of exposure within the first 10 minutes 
of awakening with a 10,000–15,000 lux commercial ultraviolet-blocked light box, and 
the light should be <12 inches from the face. Side effects are uncommon and usually 
mild; they may include nausea, sleep disturbance, or, in some cases, emergent symp-
toms of hypomania (Deligiannidis and Fr eeman 2014). Light therapy can be used 
alone or to augment pharmacotherapy (Lam et al. 2016). It can also be used in combi-
nation with “critically timed wake therapy” (Parry et al. 2019), which is discussed in 
the section that follows. 
Sleep 
Poor sleep, either in duration, continuity, or quality, is linked with increased risk for 
negative health outcomes such as cardiova scular disease, diabetes, depression, and 
anxiety (Khan and Aouad 2017; McHill and Wright 2017). Pregnant women have 
demonstrated changes in sleep patterns, su ch as longer sleep-onset latency, more 
nighttime awakenings, less total sleep time, less slow wave sleep, lower sleep effi -
ciency, and a lower percentage of rapid ey e movement (REM) sleep (Bei et al. 2015; 
Hedman et al. 2002). It has been hypothesized that these changes in sleep patterns may 
explain some of the reasons why the perinatal period may increase the risk of a mood 
disorder. Poor sleep quality in the perinatal period is associated with PPD and anxiety 
(Okun et al. 2018), increased pain perception, increased rates of cesarean delivery , and 
increased risk of preterm birth and low birth weight infants (Okun et al. 2018; Reichner
