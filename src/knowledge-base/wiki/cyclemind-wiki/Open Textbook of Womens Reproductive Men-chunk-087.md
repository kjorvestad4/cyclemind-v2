Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
Psychotherapy. Psychotherapy is the first-line treatment for mild to moderate de -
pression in perimenopausal women, as it is in other populations. It is helpful to nor-
malize the range of emotions that women experience about their changing bodies and 
social roles during perimenopause and to support healthy coping strategies, such as 
self-compassion and goal setting (Süss and Ehlert 2020). Cognitive-behavioral ther -
apy (CBT) has Level I evidence for treatment of VMS and proven efficacy for depres-
sion and therefore is a particularly relevant therapeutic modality to consider for 
treatment of depression during perimenopause (Ayers et al. 2011; Mann et al. 2012). 
Alternative treatments for perimenopausal depression. Currently, insufficient 
evidence exists to recommend alternative treatments for perimenopausal depression 
(Maki et al. 2018), although there are seve ral promising areas of research, including 
soy isoflavones (Stute et al. 2020). A meta-analysis of 11 studies found that low- to 
moderate-intensity exercise might be helpful for depressive symptoms in midlife and 
older women (Pérez-López et al. 2017). Pati ents with depression should be encour -
aged to exercise to augment other evidence-based treatments; this is likely to have 
multiple benefits and confers low risk. 
Treatment Challenges 
The largest challenge in treating perimenopausal depression often lies in distinguishing 
the symptoms of depression from the numerous symptoms of perimenopause, which 
overlap substantially. Perimenopausal symptoms can include VMS, sleep disturbances 
and fatigue, sexual problems and vaginal dryness, cognitive problems, urinary incon-
tinence, muscle aches and joint pain, weight gain, and depressed or irritable mood
