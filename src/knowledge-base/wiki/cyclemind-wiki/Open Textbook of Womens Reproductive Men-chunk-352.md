Neurobiological evidence exists for alterations of HPA axis activity, decreased hip-
pocampal size, increased amygdala reactivity, and stress response in infants exposed 
to stress in utero. Although genetics and stress exposure are important factors, research 
in both animal models and humans has shown that physical contact and proximity and 
parental nurturing behaviors can lead to epigenetic changes that can override these 
heritable traits. Mental health conditions may interfere with these nurturing behaviors, 
but early detection of any parent–infant rela tional difficulties and appropriate inter-
vention can lead to better self-regulation in the infant, encourage healthy attachment 
relationships, and result in improved psyc hoemotional well-being in infants as they 
grow and develop. 
Mental health clinicians can help parents by observing and understanding the nu-
ances of parent–infant interactions and by exploring parent perceptions of the infant 
and of themselves as parents. This process is especially salient in the setting of preg-
nancy and postpartum mood or anxiety disorders. Important areas to notice include 
the quality of physical contact, gaze, and vocalizations between the parent and infant, 
as well as the affective tone, mutual coregulation, and any mismatches and repairs 
within the back and forth of interactions. 
Several other populations may also requir e additional support. Parents who are 
members of racial minority groups are subject to health care disparities that are pro-
nounced in terms of perinatal care and peripartum outcomes and are raising children 
within the context of the additional stressor s associated with racist political and cul-
tural structures. Parents of infants who require care in the NICU are faced with addi-
tional pressures and anxieties associated with worries about the health of their child, 
as well as the logistical and emotional difficulties of having a child with a prolonged 
hospital stay and potentially continued medical complexities. Parents who have a his-
tory of trauma and poor attachment with their own primary caregivers are more likely 
to struggle. Parents may need support around having realistic expectations for them-
selves and their infants at different developmental stages. 
Other parental groups that may have un ique needs require additional study. We 
know that fathers are at increased risk for depression after the birth of a child, yet little 
is known regarding the best way to screen for these difficulties and to provide services 
successfully. LGBTQ+ couples who become parents have rarely been the focus of stud-
ies looking at attachment and early adaptation to the role of parent, although research 
has demonstrated that children raised in same-sex households are at no increased risk 
for emotional and psychiatric difficulties. There is little research on attachment in fam-
ilies with transgender or nonbinary parents. Adopted infants and children and their 
parents are another important population to keep in mind when screening for parental 
mental health concerns and addressing infa nt mental health and attachment in new 
parents. Ongoing research into the needs of these parents will enable us to ensure that 
the care we provide to families is data informed and culturally responsive.
