creases the clearance of lamotrigine via induction of hepatic glucuronidation (Reddy

should also be considered in the differential diagnosis of women with BD with premen-
strual mood exacerbations, although to date studies regarding the influence of the men-
strual cycle on lithium and valproate metabolism are limited (Carmassi et al. 2019). 
Menopause. Most women with BD will experience menopause, yet the clinical dis-
ease course during this transition remain s understudied. In the general population, 
mood symptoms are common but not universal during perimenopause and can include 
irritability, anxiety, depression, lability, and difficulty with concentration and sleep 
(Minuzzi et al. 2012). Marsh et al. (2008) monitored mood symptoms of women with 
BD ages 45–55 and found that the frequency of depressive episodes increased during 
perimenopause relative to the patients’ report of their disease course in reproductive 
years. In a prospective study of women with BD at various stages of menopause, greater 
depression and mood elevation symptoms were reported during late menopause and 
early postmenopause relative to reproductive age or early menopause, supporting the

Sleep disturbance is a well-known potential precipitant for mood destabilization, and 
many perimenopausal women experience sleep disturbances, so early intervention for 
sleep disturbances (and contributing factors, such as hot flashes) is recommended. 
Preconception. The potential for pregnancy introduces an additional level of com-
plexity in the psychiatric management of women with BD. Approximately 50% of preg-
nancies in the United States are unplanned, and women with BD are even more likely 
to have unplanned pregnancy than women in the general population (Finer and Zolna 
2016; Marengo et al. 2015). Many commonly used mood stabilizers carry a risk of tera-
togenicity at the earliest stages of fetal development; thus, it is critical that discussions 
about family planning occur early in the treatment of any woman of reproductive age 
as part of routine care. Psychiatrists should inquire about all women’s obstetrical his-
tory, plans for pregnancy, and contraceptive practices and preferences. Women with BD 
should be counseled about the risk of hypersexuality or high-risk sexual behaviors as 
a component of mania, as well as about the availability of long-acting reversible con-
traceptive options and methods to protect against sexually transmitted infections. 
When prescribing medication treatment, potential teratogenic effects should be in-
cluded in any discussion with women of reproductive age. Patients should be encour-
aged to engage proactively in preconception planning in collaboration with the treating
