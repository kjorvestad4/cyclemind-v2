therapist and a daily exercise routine. At 15 weeks’ gestation, however, she began to feel 
the return of her depressive symptoms, characterized by low self-worth, low motiva -

their son off at day care. She has been calling the daycare multiple times each day to 
check up on her son, and she calls her husband frequently at work to make sure he is 
all right. She knows rationally that this is not necessary, but she cannot help it. 
Differential Diagnosis 
Although diagnosis can be straightforward when classic depressive symptoms alone 
are present, several other disorders can be confused with depression in the perinatal pe-
riod (Table 13–5). “Baby blues,” which affects up to 75% of women (Nonacs and Cohen 
1998), refers to mild mood lability in the immediate postpartum period. Some women 
with perinatal mood and anxiety symptoms may mistakenly believe that their symp-
toms are consistent with the blues, but both patients and clinicians should know that 
this resolves by 2 weeks postpartum and is  never associated with severe symptoms, 
such as suicidality. Symptoms of anxiety disorders may overlap with those of depres-
sive disorders, with a high rate of comorbidity between the two (Jordan and Minikel 
2019). Medical disorders, such as hypothyroidism, anemia, or the rare Sheehan’s disor-
der, may present with symptoms such as lethargy and fatigue, which may be confused 
with symptoms of depression (Kang et al. 2020; Wilson et al. 2019). Either intoxication 
or withdrawal from substances can be confused with symptoms of depression, so sub-
stance use disorders must always be included in the differential diagnosis. Finally, any 
woman presenting with depressive symptoms in the perinatal period may in fact have 
bipolar disorder—in fact, some research in dicates that a high proportion of women
