Key Points 
• Eating disorders (EDs) are a group of diagnoses characterized by distur -
bance in eating that lead to functional impairment and are associated with 
numerous potentially severe medical comorbidities. 
• In addition to strong genetic correlation, risk factors for the development of 
EDs include cultural influences of the thin ideal, specific psychological traits, 
psychosocial factors, and comorbid psychiatric disorders. 
• The perinatal period can be a high-risk time for onset or relapse of disordered 
eating behaviors in some, whereas for others it can be a time of motivation 
for recovery. 
• Given the connection with reproductive hormones, EDs can be associated 
with effects on puberty, menstruation, fertility, and pregnancy. 
• Active ED symptoms during pregnancy carry risk for both mother and baby. 
• Initial treatment of EDs is focused on weight restoration and nutritional reha-
bilitation, with focus on medical monito ring given risk of negative sequelae 
from refeeding syndrome. 
• Treatment most often requires a multidisciplinar y team approach, including 
behavioral medicine, physical medicine, and nutrition, with the major focus on 
psychotherapy approaches to address ED cognitions and behaviors. 
• Data on use of psychopharmacology in EDs are somewhat limited, although 
antidepressants (particularly for bulimia nervosa), antipsychotics (for anorexia 
nervosa), and psychostimulants (for binge eating disorder) have been studied, 
with some FDA approvals. 
• Treatment of EDs often requires var ied levels of care, including inpatient, par-
tial hospitalization, intensive outpatient, and outpatient options.

ADHD is an early onset, highly prevalent neurobehavioral disorder characterized by 
significant deficits in two major domains: inattention and hyperactivity/impulsivity 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013; Kooij et al. 2010; Spencer et al. 2007). ADHD 
is typically diagnosed in childhood, but it often persists into adulthood, leading to 
functional impairment in school, work, and social settings throughout the person’s life 
(Agnew-Blais et al. 2016; Asherson 2010; Ba rbaresi et al. 2013; Faraone et al. 2015). 
ADHD in adults was recognized relatively recently (McCarthy et al. 2009), thus a com-
prehensive understanding is still evolving (Kooij et al. 2010); in turn, there are many 
more questions than answers regarding ADHD in women and in the perinatal period. 
Despite the lack of comprehensive information, ADHD is an important condition to 
address in the setting of reproductive mental health. An increasing number of women 
of reproductive age are diagnosed with ADHD (Leong et al. 2017; Louik et al. 2015), 
and they are at substantially increased risk of comorbidities such as depression, anxiety, 
self-injury, and substance use disorders (Owens et al. 2017). They are likely to face sig-
nificant difficulties with work, school, interpersonal relationships, and everyday func-
tioning (Corbisiero et al. 2017; Eddy et al. 2019). Although this hypothesis has yet to 
be examined rigorously, pregnancy and new motherhood may represent a time of ad-
ditional significant psychosocial stress related to the large number of tasks associated 
with the transition to motherhood (Freeman 2014). An increasing number of women

Ms. JJ is a 32-year-old woman who presents to a reproductive psyc hiatrist inquiring 
about whether she can take her stimulants during pregnancy. She has been taking stim-

sit still and was “daydreaming all the time” and recommended neuropsychological test-
ing, which was consistent with an ADHD diagnosis. She also had testing and deadline 
accommodations all throughout high school and college. Ms. JJ has used several differ-

been effective. She is now stable on a dose  of V yvanse, 40 mg/day orally , which is ef-

about completing her work effectively if she stops her stimulants. She is also worried 
about pregnancy-related fatigue and “brain  fog” and how to manage that along with 
her demanding work. Ms. JJ is “worried because no matter what I do, it’s all a risk, and 
I feel like I have to choose between a healthy pregnancy and being able to work at all.” 
She reports that she put herself through co llege and law school and is the primary 
breadwinner between herself and her partne r. She also financially supports her wid -
owed mother, who is disabled with a chronic medical condition. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
ADHD is common neurodevelopmental disorder with a high prevalence throughout 
the lifespan (Cho et al. 2017; Kessler et al . 2006; Kooij et al. 2010). The prevalence in 
children and adolescents is approximately 3%–7% (Thomas et al. 2015), and in adults 
it ranges from 2% to 5% (Fayyad et al. 2007; Kessler et al. 2005; Kooij et al. 2005; Simon 
et al. 2009). Adult ADHD is underdiagnosed and undertreated due to its relatively re-
cent recognition as a disorder (McCarthy et al. 2009), the presence of mental health co-
morbidities that may “mask” it (Kooij et  al. 2010), shifting symptomatology from 
childhood to adulthood (Biederman et al. 2000), and stigma about the condition itself, 
particularly around the use of stimulant medication (Buitelaar 2001; Biederman et al. 
1993). ADHD is commonly seen in women, with an estimated prevalence of 3.2% (Kes-
sler et al. 2006). In childhood, the male to female sex ratio is approximately 3:1, but in 
adults it is closer to 1:1 (Cortese et al. 2016; Rösler et al. 2006). It is unclear what drives 
the change in ratio over time; it has been hypothesized that at least one factor may be 
detection failure, leading to an underdiagnosis of female ADHD in childhood (Davies 
2014; Young et al. 2020).

The impact of ADHD on affected indivi duals and their loved ones is enormous. 
The direct health care expenditures for the treatment of ADHD, as well as the indirect 
economic deficits (e.g., loss of working hours), are also substantial. A recent study es-
timated that the total excess cost of ADHD in the United States in 2000 was $31.6 bil-
lion, including treatment of ADHD, other health care costs for patients and their family 
members, and loss of work time (Birnbau m et al. 2005). A recent study examining a 
claims database of more than 3,000 adult patients with ADHD noted that they were 
significantly more likely than the comparison cohort without ADHD to have psychi-
atric and medical comorbidities, higher outpatient and inpatient costs, and higher pre-
scription drug and total medical costs (Secnik et al. 2005). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors for perinatal ADHD have not yet been clearly delineated. Childhood pre-
dictors of adult ADHD include family hi story of ADHD, the combined subtype of 
ADHD in childhood, increased symptom severity, comorbid depression or other men-
tal health conditions, social adversity, and parental psychopathology (Table 23–1) (Bie-
derman et al. 1996; Lara et al. 2009). The prevalence does not appear to differ by race 
or ethnicity or in response to treatment, but Black and Hispanic children are diagnosed 
with ADHD at much lower rates than white children (Alvarado and Modesto-Lowe 
2017). Although less is known about these differences in adult ADHD, it is clear that 
rates of detection are substantially lowe r among racial and et hnic minority groups 
(Chung et al. 2019). 
Screening 
Screening for adult or perinatal ADHD is not in widespread practice, although there 
have been calls for increasing recognition and detection of ADHD given its significant 
impact on functioning and on physical and mental health across the lifespan (Ebert et 
al. 2003; National Institute for Health and Care Excellence 2008; Nutt et al. 2007). Sev-
eral validated rating scales are in clinical use for the assessment of ADHD (Rösler et al. 
2006); rating scales specific to the perinatal population have yet to be established. Rat-
ing scales applicable for adults include the Wender-Utah Rating Scale (Ward et al. 1993)

Numerous changes to the diagnostic criteria of ADHD were made with the publica-
tion of DSM-5, including an increase in age of onset from 7 to 12 years, allowance of the 
concurrent diagnosis of ADHD and autism spectrum disorder, and reduction of the cut 
point from six symptoms to five symptoms in adults (Matthews et al. 2014). Even with 
these changes, the diagnosis of ADHD as it applies to adults has been a matter of evo-
lution—and some controversy—over time because uncertainties remain about how 
well diagnostic criteria meet the complexity of the adult clinical phenotype (Matte et al. 
2012). A recent large cohort study of more than 3,500 adults aimed to describe the phe-
notype of adult ADHD and assess the psyc hometric validity of the DSM-5 diagnosis 
(Vitola et al. 2017). In this study , the adult ADHD prevalence with the use of DSM-5 cri-
teria was significantly higher when compared with that obtained using DSM-IV criteria 
(American Psychiatric Association 1994) and increased almost threefold when the age-
of-onset criterion was removed (Vitola et al. 2017). In addition, latent class analysis 
noted that adult ADHD comprises mainly inattentive and not hyperactive/impulsive 
symptoms, that inattention was the symptom cluster most highly correlated with func-
tional impairment, and that even the best cutoff for diagnosis (four or more symptoms) 
yielded low sensitivity and specificity (Vitola et al. 2017). This suggests that both con-
struct and discriminant validity challenges remain for the ADHD diagnostic criteria as 
applied to adults, despite recent modifications in DSM-5 (Vitola et al. 2017). 
Other models have been proposed for the diagnostic criteria for ADHD that focus 
on specific features of the disorder in adults. For example, the Utah criteria developed 
by Wender consist of the requirement of a long-standing and consistent history of 
symptoms (dating back to at least age 7) and adult symptoms co nsisting of hyperac-
tivity and poor concentration, with at least two of the following five associated symp-
toms: affective lability, “hot” temper, inability to complete tasks and disorganization, 
stress intolerance, and impulsivity (Wender 1995). These diagnostic criteria take into 
account the affective lability—most often, the brief, intense affective outbursts—com -
monly seen in adult ADHD; this affective liability in turn serves to increase cognitive

Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
The classic clinical presentation of ADHD includes two major symptom domains: in-
attention and hyperactivity/impulsivity. Examples of inattention symptoms include 
failing to pay close attention to details, making careless mistakes in tasks, appearing 
not to listen or hold attention, failing to finish tasks, not following through, and hav-
ing difficulty with organization. The doma in of hyperactivity/impulsivity includes 
symptoms such as fidgeting, squirming in seat, being “on the go,” talking excessively, 
blurting out an answer, having trouble waiting their turn, and interrupting others 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
Despite having the same DSM diagnostic criteria, adult ADHD symptoms can 
present differently than in childhood. The more overtly impairing symptoms (hyper-
activity and impulsivity) that may be particularly prominent in childhood seem to be-
come less obvious over time (Kooij et al . 2010). More subtle—yet still impairing— 
symptoms such as inattention, disorganizat ion, and inner restlessness tend to pre -
dominate in adulthood, as well as significant problems with executive functioning 
(Adler et al. 2017; Biederman et al. 2000; Fi scher et al. 2005; Hart et al. 1995; Kooij et 
al. 2001; Wender et al. 2001). This cluster of symptoms leaves a footprint in the prag-
matic aspects of adult life; adult patients often struggle with time management and 
procrastination, a feeling of internal restlessness, feeling overwhelmed or distracted, 
and starting but not finishing tasks (Fredriksen et al. 2014; Halmøy et al. 2009; Minde 
et al. 2003; Young et al. 2020). Anxiety, insomnia, and demoralization follow (Agarwal 
et al. 2012; Piñeiro-Dieguez et al. 2016; Seo et al. 2014), and the impulsivity inherent 
to ADHD often leads to comorbid substance misuse (Capusan et al. 2016; Piñeiro-
Dieguez et al. 2016) or binge eating (Brunault et al. 2019). 
Other domains also appear to play an important role in adult ADHD, including dif-
ficulties with cognitive impairment and emotional regulation. Most adults with ADHD 
have measurable cognitive dysfunction (Holst and Thorell 2017), although the degree 
of impairment can be highly variable; patients with significant executive dysfunction, 
working memory difficulties, or impulsivity tend to have the highest degree of func-
tional impairment in their daily lives (Mostert et al. 2015; Skodzik et al. 2017; Theiling 
and Petermann 2016). Cognitive impairment in ADHD is underappreciated yet im -
portant (Holst and Thorell 2017; Mostert et al. 2015; Skodzik et al. 2017; Theiling and

work mastery, and interpersonal functioning (Geffen and Forster 2018).

Gender-based differences are also apparent in ADHD symptomatology. Although 
girls and women can present with both inattention and hyperactive-impulsive symp-

and to become anxious, depressed, and socially withdrawn (Quinn 2005). The emo -

frustration tolerance, and mood alterations, may occur more commonly and severely 
in women (Cortese 2012; Hollingdale et al. 2014; Shaw et al. 2014). Whether there are

girls and women, however, ADHD is often a hidden disorder that is ignored or mis -

females who have ADHD experience psychological and academic impairments. In 
addition, most report low self-esteem, impaired social relationships, and a general de-
moralization. 
Delineating the symptoms of ADHD from the (putative) phys iological cognitive 
changes of pregnancy can be difficult, an d the two may overlap. Early observations 
suggested that certain cognitive functions, such as memory, were negatively impacted 
by pregnancy and that pregnant women were more likely than nonpregnant women to 
subjectively report memory changes (Poser et al. 1986; Sharp et al. 1993). The informal 
(and unfortunate) term pregnancy brain likely refers to this widely cited phenomenon 
of hormonal changes impacting cognition; however, objective studies of this hypothe-
sis have had mixed results (Freeman 2014). For example, one small study showed that 
pregnant women performed better on verbal cognitive tasks after delivery than before, 
and no associations were noted between sex hormone levels and cognitive perfor -
mance (Buckwalter et al. 1999). However, a subsequent study demonstrated that verbal 
memory declined and processing speed slowed in third-trimester pregnant women 
compared with nonpregnant women; similar results were seen in the postpartum pe-
riod (Henry and Sherwin 2012). In this study, cognitive changes were associated with 
hormonal changes; for example, estrogen and cortisol levels were negatively associated 
with attention scores postpartum (Henry and Sherwin 2012). 
A recent meta-analysis attempted to shed light on this issue; although the results 
were inconsistent, there were two major findings: 1) pregnant women were found to be 
impaired on some but not all selective measures of memory—specifically, memory 
functions that place relative ly high demands on execut ive cognitive control, and

Course/Prognosis 
It is now understood that ADHD persists throughout the lifespan (Young et al. 2020). 
Evidence indicates that ADHD persists into adulthood in approximately two-thirds of 
cases (Barkley et al. 2002; Gi ttelman et al. 1985; Lara et al. 2009; Lie 1992; Mannuzza 
et al. 1991, 1993; Weiss et al. 1985); more recent estimates have been even higher, esti-
mating the persistence of symptoms to be approximately 80% (Cheung et al. 2016; van 
Lieshout et al. 2016). A recent meta-analysis demonstrated that, of the cases that persist, 
most patients have residual symptomatology with functional impairment, whereas a 
minority continue to exhibit the full-blown disorder (Faraone et al. 2006). Symptoms 
tend to decline over time from childhood to adulthood (Faraone et al. 2006; Willcutt 
2012), particularly those in the hyperactive/impulsive domain; data regarding the tra-
jectory of the inattentive symptoms are more mixed (Larsson et al. 2011). This trajectory 
appears similar in both sexes (Hinshaw et al. 2006; Lapalme et al. 2018; Monuteaux et 
al. 2010). 
Some have speculated about the existence of an adult-onset variant of ADHD (Al-
exander and Farrelly 2018; Faraone and Biederman 2016; Moffitt et al. 2015). Evidence 
supporting this is offered by studies that have examined the continuity of ADHD in 
birth cohorts and found little overlap between those who met DSM-5 criteria in adult-
hood and those who had met it in childhood (Castellanos 2015). If adult-onset ADHD 
is a disorder with biology distinct from that of childhood-onset ADHD, perhaps a dis-
tinct biology also exists in the perinatal period, making this an intriguing area for fu-
ture research. 
Adult ADHD has profound negative consequences in everyday life. Studies of pa-
tients with untreated ADHD have found numerous long-term adverse consequences, 
including poor physical health, impaired quality of life (Agarwal et al. 2012; Seo et al. 
2014), impaired relationships and higher rates of divorce (Minde et al. 2003), difficulties 
with education and employment (Fredriksen et al. 2014; Halmøy et al. 2009), financial 
distress (Brook et al. 2013), impaired driving safety (Biederman et al. 2012; Fuermaier 
et al. 2017), premature death from accidents (Dalsgaard et al. 2015), and higher rates of 
imprisonment (Wilens et al. 2004). Evidence suggested an increased mortality in 
women with ADHD relative to men, which persists after controlling for comorbidities, 
such as substance use disorders, and is driven by accidents. It is hypothesized that un-
derdiagnosis and thus undertreatment in women leads to a greater risk of accidental 
death (Dalsgaard et al. 2015).

ADHD is highly comorbid with other mental health conditions. ADHD alone is un-
common in the adult population, and it may co-occur with other disorders as much as 
75% of the time (Faraone and Biederman 1998; Rösler et al. 2006). Common comorbid-
ities with adult ADHD include autism spectrum disorder, depressive disorders, bipo-
lar disorder, anxiety disorders, OCD, eating disorders (i.e., anorexia nervosa, bulimia 
nervosa, binge eating disorder), PTSD, learning disorders, personality disorders, and 
impulse-control disorders (Capusan et al. 2016; Huntley and Young 2014; Kessler et 
al. 2006; Nazar et al. 2016; Pliszka 1998; Sobanski 2006). 
Of particular importance is the relationship between ADHD and substance use dis-
orders. In adulthood, patients with ADHD are more likely to use tobacco (Brassett-
Grundy and Butler 2004), to engage in hi gher rates of substance use (Huntley and 
Young 2014), and to develop alcohol and substance use disorders (Charach et al. 2011). 
Notably, evidence has shown at least equal—if not higher—risk of alcohol and canna -
bis use disorders in women with ADHD compared with men (Ottosen et al. 2016). In 
addition, the presence of comorbid ADHD may portend a less favorable prognosis; for 
example, comorbid ADHD and bipolar disord er is associated with earlier onset of 
symptoms and a more chronic and disabling course than bipolar disorder alone (Brus 
et al. 2014). A recent systematic review also showed an association between ADHD 
and suicidality in all age groups and in both sexes that was mediated by the presence 
of psychiatric comorbidities (Furczyk and Thome 2014). 
There appear to be significant sex differences in the course and prognosis of ADHD 
throughout the lifespan (Young et al. 2020). Internalizing disorders, such as anxiety and 
depression, are more often reported in women than in men with ADHD (Cortese et al. 
2016; Edvinsson et al. 2013; Gershon 2002), as are borderline personality disorder traits 
(Stepp et al. 2012) and self-harming behaviors (Hollingdale et al. 2014), likely prompted 
by impulsivity. The impulsivity inherent in ADHD can lead to an increase in risk-taking 
behavior and undesired consequences in women (Quinn and Madhoo 2014). For exam-
ple, adolescent girls with undiagnosed ADHD may engage in at-risk behaviors such as 
early sexual activity, have an increased number of sexual partners, and experience a 
greater frequency of sexually transmitted infections (Barkley et al. 2006; Quinn 2005). 
Women with ADHD also have a higher risk of teenage, early, or unplanned pregnancies 
(Skoglund et al. 2019). They may be vulnerable to sexual harassment, exploitation, and 
abusive relationships; the Adult Psychiatric Morbidity survey in the United Kingdom 
found that 27% of women who experienced extensive physical or sexual abuse had 
ADHD traits (McManus et al. 2009). Along with the other mental health comorbidities, 
such as depression and PTSD, alcohol and other substance use disorders are highly co-
morbid with ADHD in women, often beginning at an early age (Wilens et al. 1995). 
No systematic studies have yet been published on the course of ADHD through the 
perinatal period (Baker and Freeman 2018). Women with ADHD who are pregnant 
have a higher risk of continued tobacco use and of being obese or underweight (Sko-
glund et al. 2019). Women with undiagnosed ADHD are hypothesized to struggle 
more with parenting, balancing work with household management, and maintaining 
partnerships and interpersonal relationsh ips (Nadeau and Quinn 2002; Young et al. 
2020), although empirical data on this topic are scarce. It has also been hypothesized 
that, in the perinatal period, women may experience significant psychosocial stress 
related to the large number of tasks associ ated with the transition to motherhood; 
however, this has not yet been examined rigorously (Freeman 2014; Young et al. 2020).

Differential Diagnosis and Assessment 
Assessment 
ADHD is a clinical diagnosis. In adults, that diagnosis requires a detailed clinical inter-
view and assessment, including retrospective assessment of childhood ADHD symp-
toms by patient recall or collateral information; evidence of ADHD prior to the age of 
12 (e.g., by school records, childhood health assessments, or family observations); an 
enduring pattern of difficulties that impair social functioning and limit school or occu-
pational attainment; and current adult ADHD symptomatology, including symptom 
severity and pervasiveness, functional impairment, quality of life issues, and psychiat-
ric comorbidity (Geffen and Forster 2018; Rösler et al. 2006). The assessment of ADHD 
should include a review of screening questionnaires, a review of physical and mental 
health history, and a physical examination (Geffen and Forster 2018). Weight and car-
diovascular fitness, particularly blood pressure, are particularly important data points 
because ADHD treatments may influence these parameters. A personal or family his-
tory of cardiac ischemia or cardiac arrhythmias should be elicited because this may pre-
clude treatment with stimulants (Geffen and Forster 2018). Additional measures, such 
as blood tests, electroencephalogram, CT scan, or MRI scan, should be considered as 
indicated (Rösler et al. 2006).
