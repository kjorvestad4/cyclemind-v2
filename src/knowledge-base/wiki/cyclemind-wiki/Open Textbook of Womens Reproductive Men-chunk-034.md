Universal screening for FSDs is recommended across subspecialties because it can 
reduce the burden placed on the patient to initiate disclosure of a sensitive concern 
and normalize the importance of this aspect  of health for wome n (Kingsberg et al. 
2019b; Parish et al. 2019). Assessment across subspecialties is essentially the same, but 
the timing may differ within the review of systems according to each subspecialty. For 
example, in obstetrics and gynecology settings, a review of systems and medical his-
tory would include sexual health and func tion, and in psychiatry settings, sexual 
function assessment could be included within a checklist of basic mental health and 
psychosocial history (e.g., when inquiring about mood, situational, and relationship 
factors). Psychiatrists prescribing psychotropic medications should assess for baseline 
sexual function and concerns prior to initiating treatment and then screen for sexual 
side effects of medications at follow-up appointments. When time is limited, providers
