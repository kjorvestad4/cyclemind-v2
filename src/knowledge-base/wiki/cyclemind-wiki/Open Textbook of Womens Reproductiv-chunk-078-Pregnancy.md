of symptoms should prompt the patient and physician to reevaluate her need for med-

maintenance pharmacotherapy with the agent that best balances efficacy with risk 
(Yonkers et al. 2004). Some women might elect to switch to a medication with a more 
favorable reproductive risk profile before or during pregnancy, but any plan to change 
a medication regimen should take into account the anticipated effectiveness of the 
treatment options, the inherent risk of relapse in the setting of medication changes, and, 
in the setting of switching during pregnancy, the risk of multiple medication exposures 
across fetal development. Figure 15–1 provides a useful algorithm to guide decision 
making around maintenance treatment prior to and during pregnancy. 
Once the decision is made to use a medication during pregnancy, awareness of preg-
nancy-related pharmacokinetic changes and the potential need for dosage adjustments 
is an important component of care that may further reduce risk of relapse and avoid 
unintentional supratherapeutic dosing. Therapeutic drug monitoring can complement 
close clinical monitoring. For medications without a standardized therapeutic range, a 
woman’s individual level at a point of euthymia will allow the physician to establish a 
target medication level throughout and after pregnancy (Deligiannidis et al. 2014).
