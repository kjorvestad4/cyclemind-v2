Perinatal anxiety has a significant and long-lasting adverse impact on children’s cog-
nition, social-emotional development, and mental health from infancy through adoles-
cence (Glover 2014; Kingston et al. 2012; Stein et al. 2014; Van den Bergh and Marcoen 
2004; Van den Bergh et al. 2005). It has been estimated that approximately 10%–15% of 
the attributable risk of child behavioral problems are related to antenatal anxiety and 
stress (Glover 2014). A prospective study in the United States noted that anxiety in 
pregnant women was associated with less robust maternal–infant bonding (Dubber et 
al. 2015); less responsive maternal–infant interactions (Stein et al. 2012); withdrawn be-
havior and blunted emotional tone in the infant (Stein et al. 2012); poor infant cognitive 
development (Keim et al. 2011); maternal reports of difficult infant temperament (Aus-
tin et al. 2005); and negative infant reactivity to novelty (Davis et al. 2004). 
A recent large-scale prospective study of more than 1,300 mothers noted that peri-
natal anxiety—whether antenatal or postnatal—was associated with impaired child so-
cial-emotional development at the age of 2 years, even after adjusting for confounding 
variables (Polte et al. 2019). Children of women with perinatal anxiety have a signifi-
cantly increased risk of cognitive issues, particularly attentional problems or ADHD, a 
risk seen independently of postnatal de pression (Brouwers et al. 2001; O’Connor et 
al. 2002; Van Batenburg-Eddes et al. 2013). A recent cohort study of more than 3,500 
mother–offspring pairs identified a maternal trajectory of severe depression, stress, 
and anxiety that later predicted internalizing behaviors in adolescent offspring, de -
spite controlling for important confoundin g variables such as concurrent maternal 
depressive and anxious symptoms (Betts et al. 2015). 
Differential Diagnosis and Assessment 
Assessment 
An anxiety disorder is a clinical diagnosis.  In addition to the use of screening ques -
tionnaires, the clinical interview should touch on the cognitive, physical, behavioral, 
and affective symptoms of anxiety. A basic laboratory workup should include a com-
plete blood count, blood chemistry, thyroid function tests, urinalysis, and urine drug 
screen (Cosci et al. 2015). If indicated, a more extensive workup may include electro-
encephalography, brain CT scan, evaluation for infection, or more extensive thyroid 
function tests (Cosci et al. 2015).
