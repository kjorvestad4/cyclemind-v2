Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
Psychotherapy. Psychotherapy is the first-line treatment for mild to moderate de -
pression in perimenopausal women, as it is in other populations. It is helpful to nor-
malize the range of emotions that women experience about their changing bodies and 
social roles during perimenopause and to support healthy coping strategies, such as 
self-compassion and goal setting (Süss and Ehlert 2020). Cognitive-behavioral ther -
apy (CBT) has Level I evidence for treatment of VMS and proven efficacy for depres-
sion and therefore is a particularly relevant therapeutic modality to consider for 
treatment of depression during perimenopause (Ayers et al. 2011; Mann et al. 2012). 
Alternative treatments for perimenopausal depression. Currently, insufficient 
evidence exists to recommend alternative treatments for perimenopausal depression 
(Maki et al. 2018), although there are seve ral promising areas of research, including 
soy isoflavones (Stute et al. 2020). A meta-analysis of 11 studies found that low- to 
moderate-intensity exercise might be helpful for depressive symptoms in midlife and 
older women (Pérez-López et al. 2017). Pati ents with depression should be encour -
aged to exercise to augment other evidence-based treatments; this is likely to have 
multiple benefits and confers low risk.

and fatigue, sexual problems and vaginal dryness, cognitive problems, urinary incon-
tinence, muscle aches and joint pain, weight gain, and depressed or irritable mood

Vasomotor Symptoms 
Epidemiology 
Rates 
VMS are the most common physical complaints among menopausal women (Sussman 
et al. 2015; Woods and Mitchell 2005). They  are experienced by up to 80% of women 
during perimenopause (Avis et al. 2018; Fr eeman and Sherif 2007; Williams et al. 
2008); perimenopausal and surgically menopausal women are more likely to experi -
ence VMS than premenopausal or postmenopausal women (Obermeyer et al. 2007). 
Risk Factors and Screening 
Risk factors for more frequent or more severe hot flashes include being Black or Hispanic, 
having a higher BMI, smoking, and having symptoms of anxiety or depression. Medical 
interventions such as a hysterectomy (even when the ovaries are conserved) or breast can-
cer treatment are also associated with more severe VMS (Avis et al. 2018) (Table 7–5). 
Recommended screening tools are the MRS (Heinemann et al. 2004) and GCS 
(Greene 1976), which include VMS among a diverse list of additional symptoms. The 
HFRDIS (Carpenter 2001) measures the degree to which VMS specifically affect daily 
functioning.

Course and Differential Diagnosis 
VMS vary greatly in severity, with stud ies suggesting that most women experience 
mild to moderate symptoms. VMS last a me an of 5 years but may last substantially 
longer for some women (Col et al. 2009; Freeman et al. 2011). In POAS, a 13-year lon-
gitudinal study involving assessment ev ery 9–12 months of women from premeno -
pause to postmenopause, the most common age at onset for moderate to severe VMS 
was 45–49 years, with an average duration of 10.2 years. Women with moderate to se-
vere VMS onset at a later stage of menopa use had a shorter dura tion of symptoms. 
The median durations were 11.6 years for women who experienced onset in the pre -
and late premenopause stages, 7.4 years for those with onset in the early transition, 
and 3.8 years for those with onset in la te perimenopause or early postmenopause. 
Moreover, Black women experienced a longer duration of VMS than white women in 
this study (Freeman et al. 2011) and in a study by Avis et al. (2015). 
In general, VMS peak around the late perimenopausal or early postmenopausal pe-
riod (Avis et al. 2015; Williams et al. 2008; Woods and Mitchell 2005). In one study, 24% 
of women ages 50–54 years had hot flashes, a percentage that decreased to 21% among 
women ages 55–59, to 10% among women ages 60–69, and to 5% among women ages 
70–79 (Brunner et al. 2010). Perimenopausal VMS must be differentiated from other 
causes of flushing reactions (Table 7–6). The most common systemic causes include 
thyroid disorders, especially hyperthyroidism and medullary thyroid carcinoma, as 
well as pheochromocytoma and carcinoid syndrome. Medications commonly associ-
ated with hot flashes include tamoxifen, nicotinic acid, calcium channel blockers, and 
opiates (Mohyi et al. 1997).

Serotonin also plays an important role in VMS. In animal and human models, es -
trogen appears to modulate serotonin synt hesis through multiple effects, including 
increased activity of tryptophan hydroxylase (the enzyme involved in serotonin bio-
synthesis), upregulation of serotonin 5-HT2A and 5-HTC receptors, and effects on the 
serotonin transporter (Krolick et al. 2018; Kugaya et al. 2003; Shanmugan and Epper-

as the kisspeptin, neurokinin B, and dynorphin (KND) signal system, have also been 
found in animal and human models to be associated with VMS (Bansal and Aggarwal 
2019). The finding that injection of neurokinin B in postmenopausal women leads to 
hot flashes (Jayasena et al. 2015) triggered the development of neurokinin 3 receptor 
antagonists as a treatment strategy. Initia l phase-2 double-blind RCTs have yielded 
promising results, and recent and ongoing studies include the investigation of opti -
mal dosing and safety (Depypere et al. 2019; Prague et al. 2018). 
Psychological Factors 
Psychosocial distress, including anxiety and depression, has been consistently associ-
ated with VMS (Avis et al. 2018). Cross-sectional studies do not allow the determina-
tion of whether VMS or psychological distress comes first, but longitudinal studies do 
allow this, and POAS found that anxiety and depression precede the onset of VMS 
(Freeman et al. 2005, 2009). In SWAN, the presence of symptoms of anxiety and depres-
sion along with VMS at subjects’ first visit was associated with longer duration of VMS; 
furthermore, higher stress levels and higher sensitivity to symptoms are related to lon-
ger-lasting VMS (Avis et al. 2015). However, the directionality of causation is not com-
pletely understood, and it may indeed be bidirectional and complex. Not all women 
experience the same pattern of perimenopausal symptoms, and the relative roles of en-
docrinological and psychosocial factors may vary by subgroup (Avis et al. 2018).

women (Gold et al. 2006). A survey of 745 women living primarily (91.6%) in the United

vegan diet and less bothersome VMS and other physical symptoms of menopause and a 
trend toward less troublesome symptoms with lower consumption of meat and higher 
consumption of vegetables (Beezhold et al. 2018). However, this study’s limitations are 
substantial, including self-report, recall bias, and the limited socioeconomic range of 
the participants, and replication with more rigorous methodology is needed. 
Treatment 
Psychopharmacology 
The only antidepressant with FDA approval (in 2013) for the treatment of VMS related 
to menopause is paroxetine, based on two large RCTs. Both trials described significant 
improvement in VMS in women who received the active medication, which was also 
well tolerated (see Carroll et al. 2015). Of note, the dosing of paroxetine in these stud-
ies, using the formulation marketed as Brisdelle, was 7.5 mg/day, significantly lower 
than the dose typically required to treat mood and anxiety disorders (20–60 mg/day). 
Similarly, as mentioned, ma ny first-line agents used to treat perimenopausal de -
pression, including citalopram, desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, escitalopram, fluoxetine, 
mirtazapine, venlafaxine, and vortioxetine , have also shown efficacy for treating 
VMS, often at lower doses than those used for clinical depression and anxiety, and are 
reasonable options (Joffe et al. 2003, 2007; Maki et al. 2018; Soares et al. 2006). 
Hormonal Treatment 
The use of estrogen to treat symptoms of perimenopause has a complicated and at times 
controversial history; knowing highlights of this history can help clinicians address the 
hesitation many patients and providers have regarding estrogen therapy. Estrogen first 
became commercially available in the United States in 1933 and was used to treat a range 
of perimenopausal complaints. The popularity of estrogen products grew, and by 1975, 
estrogen was the fifth most commonly prescribed drug in the United States (Kohn et al. 
2019). In the late 1970s, reports of an increased risk for endometrial cancer led to de -
creased use, but with the addition of progestogen or natural progesterone to decrease 
endometrial hypertrophy, use once again increased (Kohn et al. 2019).

Then, in the early 2000s, the results of one trial reshaped the history of MHT. The 
Women’s Health Initiative (WHI), a large multicenter primary prevention RCT for cor-
onary heart disease (CHD), was designed to assess the risks and benefits of MHT in 
healthy postmenopausal women. Because the prevention of CHD was the primary 
endpoint, the investigators intermittently examined the data to determine whether the 
benefits for CHD episodes outweighed the risks of MHT. The study had a planned du-
ration of 8.5 years, with two active arms, both compared with placebo: 1) women with 
a uterus receiving oral conjugated equine estrogen (CEE) plus an oral progestogen 
(medroxyprogesterone acetate [MPA]) versus placebo, and 2) women with prior hys-
terectomy receiving CEE alone. In May 2002, however, the trial was stopped ahead of 
schedule because in the CEE+MPA group (after a mean of 5.2 years of follow-up), the 
test statistic for invasive breast cancer exceeded the stopping boundary, and the global 
index statistic suggested that risks were exceeding benefits. The absolute excess risks 
per 10,000 person-years were seven more CHD events, eight more strokes, eight more 
pulmonary emboli, and eight more invasive breast cancers. The absolute risk reduc-
tions per 10,000 person-years were six fewer colorectal cancers and five fewer hip frac-
tures (Rossouw et al. 2002). After the publication of these initial results, which were 
widely covered in the press, prescriptions for MHT fell by as much as 80%, and provi-
sion of training in prescribing MHT dwindled rapidly (Chester et al. 2018). 
Despite this initial panic, further consid eration of the WHI results supports the 
provision of MHT to otherwise healthy women experiencing troublesome VMS. The 
average age of WHI participants at enrollme nt was 63 years, and many were not ex -
periencing VMS. In fact, the presence of moderate VMS prevented women from enroll-
ing in the study. The overall cohort thus differed significantly from the younger, 
symptomatic population of earlier observational studies and from the population that 
typically seeks treatment for symptoms of perimenopause. In fact, analysis of WHI data 
by age showed a reduction in myocardial infarction and all-cause mortality in women 
ages 50–59 treated with CEE alone; only women ages 70–79 had increased risks. For 
the group treated with CEE+MPA, the increased risk of myocardial infarction was 
minimal in women who were still within 10 years of their final menstrual period and 
younger than age 60. Risk of myocardial infarction increased with age and time since 
final menstrual period. Furthermore, when a particular woman develops VMS might 
provide information about her risk for cardiovascular disease: women in the WHI trial 
with VMS early in menopause had a lower risk of cardiovascular disease and all-cause 
mortality, whereas women still having hot flashes 14 years after menopause on average 
had a higher risk of CHD and all-cause mortality (Maclennan et al. 2004). 
Research since the WHI has sought to reconcile its results with those of the many 
prior studies that encouraged the prescripti on of MHT. Recent studies have investi -
gated younger women and lower doses of estrogen and have compared transdermal 
with oral estrogen preparations such as CEE or oral estradiol. The “timing hypothe -
sis” posits that age and time since menopause are key; risks of MHT are lower when 
it is prescribed closer to the menopaus al transition. Women who are <10 years from 
their final menstrual period at initiation  of MHT , or age 50–59 years when MHT is 
started, have lower hazard ratios for CHD than women starting MHT at an older age. 
Furthermore, transdermal estrogen appears to convey a lower risk of thromboembolic 
events than oral estrogen and is as effective in treating VMS (Canonico et al. 2007).

Psychiatric practitioners who choose hormonal strategies should consult with each 
patient’s gynecologist or primary care provid er to confirm that the patient is up to 
date on her annual examination and mammogram and has no contraindication to the 
use of MHT ( Table 7–8). Specific training is available to psychiatric providers inter-
ested in becoming competent to prescribe MHT themselves, but conferring with the 
patient’s gynecologist or primary physician is still recommended. These physicians 
are in a better position to surveille the endometrium and to manage any difficulties 
with bleeding patterns that might arise. If a patient’s doctor is opposed to MHT and 
the psychiatrist is convinced of its effectiveness and safety for a particular patient, the 
psychiatrist and patient should request a second opinion from another gynecologist 
or primary care physician. 
Estrogen formulation and dosage. Both oral and transdermal low-dose estrogen

Patients might ask about bioidentical hormone therapy , which refers to estrogen 
preparations prepared and dispensed by some compounding pharmacies. Claims that 
these preparations are “more natural” and “closer to the hormones produced by the 
body” and dispensed in formulations tail ored to a particular woman are unfortu -
nately unfounded. The FDA and Endocrine Society recognize bioidentical as a market-
ing term not based on scientific evidence. Some bioidentical hormones are made from 
components approved by th e FDA, but other compounded hormones are not. Bio-
identical hormone therapy is potentially inferior to FDA-approved treatments due to

therapy doses based on salivary, serum, or urine testing of hormone levels. The current 
lack of data regarding the safety and efficacy of so-called bioidentical products means 
they should not be recommended or prescribed unt il such data become available

Medicine Practice Committee 2012; Maki et al. 2018).

Other Pharmacological Agents 
Although MHT and antidepressants are the primary medications used in the manage-
ment of VMS, data exist supporting the off-label use of a handful of other medications, 
including gabapentin, pregabalin, and clonidine, all of which are commonly used off-
label by psychiatrists to treat symptoms such as pain, trouble falling asleep, and anx-
iety or impulsivity in the context of mood disorders. The most reliable evidence for 
using these agents during the perimenopausal transition is for the treatment of VMS. 
Gabapentin, which is FDA approved for peripheral neuropathy and postherpetic neu-
ralgia, has Level I evid ence for use in the treatment of VMS; RCTs using gabapentin 
300 mg three times a day showed significantly decreased frequency of VMS. A 300-mg 
dose at bedtime has also demonstrated some efficacy for night sweats. However, the 
extent of improvement seen with gabapentin at dosages < 2,400 mg/day is not compa-
rable with that of MHT, and at this dosage  gabapentin has poor tolerability . Because 
gabapentin is also used for the treatment of pain, restless legs syndrome, anxiety, and 
insomnia, it is an appealing option fo r some women during perimenopause, even 
though it does not have the same efficacy or tolerability as MHT (Guttuso et al. 2003; 
Hayes et al. 2011; Reddy et al. 2006). Pregabalin, a gabapentinoid, has Level II evidence 
for treatment of VMS, with one RCT showing significant improvement in symptoms 
and good tolerability at 7.5 mg twice daily (Loprinzi et al. 2010). Clonidine, an  2-ad-
renergic agonist, has Level II evidence for VMS, demonstrating moderate improve -
ment in symptoms. Due to its limited efficacy and side effects, however, it is not 
commonly used (Nelson et al. 2006). 
Alternative Treatments 
Up to 50%–80% of women turn to nonhormonal modalities for the treatment of VMS 
(Carpenter et al. 2015). Women elect nonho rmonal treatments of symptoms of peri -
menopause for reasons that include medical contraindications and fear of the risks of 
taking prescription medications in general, or fear of hormones and antidepressants 
in particular. A smaller group of women turn to nonhormonal treatment due to an in-
ability to tolerate the side effects of MHT. Due to the frequency of the use of alternative 
treatments, it is important that clinicians have a general understanding of the options 
available for the treatment of VMS beyond MHT and antidepressants, as well as the 
levels of existing evidence, to be prepared  to discuss the risks and benefits of these 
strategies. 
Behavioral strategies. Because VMS can be triggered by increases in ambient or 
body temperature, women often try cooling techniques with the goal of preventing 
and alleviating symptoms. They wear breathab le clothing materials, dress in layers, 
and keep ice packs and fans nearby. Some women avoid doing activities that raise 
body temperature, eating spicy foods, or drinking alcohol. Although these strategies 
are not known to have medical risks, no evidence supports them as either preventing 
or alleviating symptoms. 
Multiple RCTs have investigated the effects of exercise on VMS; none has shown a 
positive result. Given the known benefits of  exercise for mood, anxiety , and general 
health, exercise should be encouraged in  perimenopausal women, but not with any 
expectation that it will decrease VMS (Dal ey et al. 2014). Studies on yoga have been 
performed, but thus far RCTs have failed to show any significant difference in VMS

al. 2011). Interestingly, self-guided CBT was found to be as effective as group-based 
therapy. The therapeutic effect of this modality is thought to be due to developing a 
sense of control and increased understanding of and ability to tolerate symptoms. 
Along similar lines, there is Level II evidence for mindfulness-based stress reduction; 
one RCT showed a greater reduction in the intensity of VMS and the frequency of 
problematic symptoms in participants in a mindfulness-based stress reduction pro -
gram compared with control subjects, but th e result did not reach significance (Car -
mody et al. 2011). 
Level I evidence is available to support the benefits of hypnosis (induction of a deep 
relaxation state and an altered state of awareness using imagery techniques) in the 
treatment of VMS (Elkins et al. 2013). However, multiple trials of relaxation strategies 
have been performed, both in women undergoing natural menopause and women un-
dergoing treatment for cancer, with no significant benefits proven (Lindh-Åstrand and 
Nedstrand 2013; Saensak et al. 2014). Paced breathing (six to eight breaths per minute, 
inhaling through the nose and exhaling through the mouth) is sometimes taught as a 
means to manage VMS, but two RCTs showed  no difference in symptoms with this 
technique (Carpenter et al. 2013; Sood et al. 2013). 
Procedures. Acupuncture, among the most frequently used alternative therapies for 
VMS, has inconclusive evidence supporting its use. Although some studies show it re-
duces VMS compared with a waitlist control condition, others, in which acupuncture 
was compared with sham acupuncture (insertion of needles at nontherapeutic sites), 
found no significant difference in symptoms (Dodin et al. 2013; Ee et al. 2016). Four 
open-label studies have been performed to evaluate the efficacy of stellate ganglion 
block (using local anesthetic at the bilateral nerve group from vertebrae C6 to T2), with 
a reduction in VMS ranging from 34% to 90% (Walega et al. 2014). However, a random-
ized sham-controlled study showed no statistically significant overall change in VMS 
frequency between the two groups, although the group that received the actual proce-
dure for moderate to severe VMS did have a statistically significant reduction in VMS 
frequency (Walega et al. 2014). 
Dietary supplements and herbal therapies. The consumption of foods contain -
ing isoflavones (e.g., soybeans, soy products ) and other phytoestrogens (e.g., seeds, 
whole grains, legumes) has been an appealing strategy for limiting VMS, based on the 
theory that eating foods co ntaining compounds with estr ogenic properties will lead 
to replenishment of waning levels of estrogen. However, multiple studies, including 
large RCTs, have failed to show significant benefits. Study designs and interpretations 
are limited by manufacturing variability, leading to inconsistent levels of isoflavones 
and phytoestrogens across products (Chen et al. 2015; Kronenberg and Fugh-Berman 
2002; Lethaby et al. 2013).

Other Perimenopausal Symptoms 
Sleep Disturbance 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Sleep disturbance, the second most common perimenopausal complaint after VMS, is 
often related to but not necessarily the direct result of VMS. Sleep disturbance typically 
consists of both trouble falling asleep and multiple awakenings (Kravitz et al. 2003). 
The percentage of women experiencing sleep disturbances increases from around 30% 
in premenopause (already a significant pr oportion of women) to 39%–60% in peri -
menopause and postmenopause, with the prevalence of sleep problems at its highest 
during the late perimenopausal transition (Kravitz et al. 2003, 2008; National Insti -
tutes of Health 2005). Sleep disturbance can be triggered or exacerbated by hot flashes, 
and hot flashes are not helped by sleep disturbance; research has shown that their re-
lationship is complex (Baker et al. 2018; Kravitz et al. 2008). One study that objectively 
measured both hot flashes and night awak enings using skin conductance and actig -
raphy found that 78% of objectively measur ed hot flashes were associated with an 
awakening but that only 36% of awakenings were associated with a hot flash (Thur -
ston et al. 2019). 
Risk factors for sleep disturbance duri ng perimenopause include VMS, surgical 
menopause (Kravitz et al. 2003), and breast cancer treatments, particularly chemother-
apy but also radiotherapy and hormonal therapy in some studies (Costa et al. 2014). 
One study found that both surgical menopause and bilateral oophorectomy after natu-
ral menopause were associated with a higher risk of developing obstructive sleep apnea 
compared with natural menopause (Huang et al. 2018). Finally , one large study found

an association between lower-quality sleep and white ethnicity, increased perceived 
stress, cigarette smoking, and lower self-reported quality of life (Kravitz et al. 2003). 
Further Screening 
The PSQI, although not specific to perime nopause, can help identify the timing of 
sleep problems, their severity, and contributing factors (Carpenter and Andrykowski 
1998). Given the greater risk for obstructiv e sleep apnea with ag ing and higher BMI 
(Perger et al. 2019), the increase in BMI commonly observed at midlife and with meno-
pause (Montazeri et al. 2019), and the greater risk for obstructive sleep apnea among 
women with severe VMS (Gao et al. 2018) , all midlife women with sleep complaints 
should be screened for symptoms of obstructive sleep apnea, with a low threshold for 
referring patients to sleep medicine specialists for full evaluation if indicated. 
Course 
If due to perimenopause, symptoms such as sleep disturbance are generally most se-
vere during the perimenopausal transiti on and tend to improve after menopause 
(Santoro 2016). 
Pharmacological Treatment 
Sleep problems in perimenopause may be attributable to either VMS, perimenopausal 
depression, or both and may resolve with effective treatment of either. However, other 
sleep problems may not resolve, and other causes should be investigated. Evidence 
suggests that both sleep-di sordered breathing (includi ng obstructive sleep apnea) 
and restless legs syndrome may have an increased incidence in perimenopause and 
are important to diagnose and treat (Baker et al. 2018). 
One small, industry-funded RCT of both perimenopausal and postmenopausal 
women with sleep disturbance but without clinical depression found that eszopiclone 
increased total sleep time and improved other measures of insomnia in this popula -
tion (Joffe et al. 2010). 
Given the evidence that endogenous mela tonin decreases with age, particularly 
during perimenopause (Baker et al. 2018; Stute et al. 2020), an ongoing area of research 
is whether melatonin supplementation ca n help with perime nopausal symptoms. 
One small RCT found improvemen t in quality-of-life scores in the physical domain, 
including feeling worn out and tired, as well as in subjective reports of improved sleep 
in perimenopausal women randomized to melatonin 3 mg at bedtime (Kotlarczyk et 
al. 2012). 
Alternative Treatment 
CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) has compelling ev idence for efficacy in the population at 
large (see, e.g., Mitchell et al. 2012). In a study of 106 perimenopausal and postmeno-
pausal women also experiencing VMS, CBT-I was found to reduce insomnia (but not 
VMS); improvement in symptoms was maintained at 6 months (McCurry et al. 2016). 
Sexual Problems 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Sexual problems, including vaginal dryness and atrophy, which can lead to new-onset 
dyspareunia (pain with intercourse), are also commonly present in perimenopause.

There is evidence to suggest that overall sexual functioning declines around the time 
of the menopausal transition, with women experiencing increased pain and decreased 
sexual interest, arousal, ability to achieve orgasm, and emotional satisfaction. In one 
study, the steepest rate of decline in sexual functioning began around 20 months before 
the last menstrual period and continued for 1 year after the final menstrual period 
(Avis et al. 2017). Factors associated with th e decline in sexual function in this study 
included VMS, vaginal dryness, and depression or anxiety. Women undergoing a hys-
terectomy before their final menstrual period also had a decline in sexual functioning 
after their surgery (Avis et al. 2017). 
Vaginal dryness deserves particular atte ntion because it is a highly prevalent 
symptom that women often fail to report to physicians. This symptom increases with 
advancing menopausal stages. In a large longitudinal study, 19% of women ages 42–53 
reported vaginal dryness at baseline, and 34% of the same group reported the symptom 
at 17-year follow-up. Risk factors associat ed with vaginal dryness in this study in -
cluded age since menopause, surgically induced menopause, anxiety, and being mar-
ried (Waetjen et al. 2018). Vaginal dryness can be both the cause of dyspareunia and 
exacerbated by pain during intercourse. Among women who reported experiencing 
vaginal dryness in the prior 2 weeks in the study by Waetjen et al. (2018), 40% reported 
having pain sometimes or almost always with sexual intercourse, compared with 16% 
of women who reported no vaginal dryness in the prior 2 weeks. Both vaginal dryness 
and dyspareunia are underdiagnosed and therefore undertreated, although they can be 
improved by over-the-counter lubricants (Bygdeman and Swahn 1996), topical hor -
mones, and MHT. 
Hormonal Treatment 
If sexual problems are due to perimenopausal symptoms, vaginal estrogen cream or 
gel can be effective for dyspareunia and vaginal dryness (but not for hot flashes, 
which require systemic administration as described in the earlier section on treatment 
of VMS). Of note, evidence has shown that nonhormonal over-the-counter lubricants 
can be effective for the treatment of dyspareunia and vaginal dryness, and given their 
lower risk, they can be considered first-line treatment for women presenting with these 
symptoms alone or in women with contraindications to estrogen (Bygdeman and 
Swahn 1996). Women and their partners often have to try multiple lubricants to deter-
mine which is best for them. Water-based lubricants may require more frequent reap-
plication but are less irritating to sensitive skin. 
Cognitive Complaints 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Complaints of cognitive symptoms during the menopausal transition are common, but 
symptoms that may be attributed to perimenopause are typically subtle, have gradual 
onset, and are challenging to distinguish from the normal symptoms of aging. Abrupt 
and serious cognitive decline should always be investigated to rule out other causes. 
Typical findings of cognitive changes due to perimenopause are of self-reported prob-
lems with verbal memory, such as remembering words or names (reported by 72% of 
women in SWAN), or with forgetting things (reported by 50%) (Greendale et al. 2010; 
Woods and Mitchell 2016).

When objective measures of cognitive performance are used, identifiable changes as-
sociated with perimenopause are typically subtle and may be transient. The first report 
that menopause stage—independent of age at the time of testing, baseline cognitive per-
formance, race, and BMI—was associated with  significant declines in immediate and 
delayed verbal recall came from POAS (Epperson et al. 2013). Although declines oc -
curred across the transition, they became significant for immediate recall at the post-
menopause stage ( P=0.04) and for delayed recall in the early transition ( P=0.002), 
when women reported having had one skipped period in the previous year. 
One study examining processing speed, which is expected to improve over the 
course of repeated testing with the same in strument as subjects learn, found this ex -
pected improvement in women who were in premenopause, early perimenopause, or 
postmenopause but not in women at late perimenopause (Greendale et al. 2009). An-
other study, by Kuh et al. (2018), found that women who went through natural meno-
pause at a later age had higher verbal me mory scores but no changes in processing 
speed compared with women who completed menopause earlier. Although the au -
thors hypothesized that hormone therapy could boost cognitive function in women 
going through the menopausal transition, they found no significant benefit for those 
taking MHT on either verbal memory or processing speed except for those who had 
menopause due to bilateral oophorectomy, who did show some benefit. 
Not all cognitive domains are likely to be as negatively affected by the loss of estra-
diol. Given the impact of estradiol on neuronal morphology, receptors for various neu-
rotransmitters, and neurochemistry in the prefrontal cortex, it is not surprising that 
many menopausal women complain of difficulties within the executive function do-
mains of working memory, sustained attention, processing speed, and motivation for 
work (Epperson et al. 2015; Shanmugan and Epperson 2014; Shanmugan et al. 2020). 
Stimulant treatment may be beneficial for women with significant executive function 
complaints, but the potential adverse effects on blood pressure and heart rate should 
be considered, as well as the addictive potential of stimulant medications (Epperson 
et al. 2015). 
Risk factors for self-reported memory problems during pe rimenopause included 
higher perceived levels of stress and depressed mood (Woods et al. 2000). 
Further Screening 
The MoCA (Nasreddine et al. 2005) has high  sensitivity and specificity for detecting 
mild cognitive impairment. If this is id entified, causes other than perimenopause 
should be investigated. Women should be queried regarding undiagnosed ADHD if 
they present with significant symptoms of executive dysfunction. A recent study sug-
gests that childhood adversity and current depression and anxiety can increase risk 
for executive function complaints among women who have undergone risk-reducing 
salpingo-oophorectomy due to having had breast or ovarian cancer or being at genetic 
risk for such cancers (Shanmugan et al. 2020). Tools such as the Brown Attention-
Deficit Disorder Scale (Brown 1996; Sandra Kooij et al. 2008), Adult ADHD Self-Report 
Scale (Ustun et al. 2017), and Conners’ Adult ADHD Rating Scale–Long Version (Con-
ners et al. 1999; Sandra Kooij et al. 2008) ca n be used to assess severity of executive 
function difficulties both prior to and following stimulant treatment to assess the rel-
ative benefits and weigh these against the potential risks of long-term use.

Additional Strategies for Changes in Sleep, 
Sexual Function, and Cognition 
As discussed, successful treatment of mood disorders or VMS may result in improve-
ments in sleep, sexual functioning, and cognition. However, for changes that do not 
improve with successful treatment of anxiety, depression, or VMS or with the specific 
treatments described earlier in this chapter, further investigation, education, and re -
ferral may be warranted. Further specialized treatment options are beyond the scope 
of this chapter and vary depending on the cause of the problem. Thus, the first step 
is to further characterize the problem using a scale, a diary, or other measurement sys-
tem. Next, serious and treatable causes of the symptom should be explored and ruled 
out if possible so as to provide both reassurance and relief. Finally, the patient should 
be referred to specialists or additional sources of education. See Table 7–10 for specific 
steps to take in characterizing, investigating, and educating about these problems. 
Conclusion 
Perimenopause is a time of reproductive change that confers increased risk of mental 
illness, as well as complex physical change s such as VMS, difficulty sleeping, sexual 
dysfunction, and cognitive complaints. Distinguishing between depression, the symp-
toms of perimenopause, and other psycholo gical, behavioral, or medical causes of 
overlapping complaints can be challenging but will help guide decisions among the 
many treatment options, which include an tidepressants, hormones, and alternative 
treatments. A growing evidence base is available to guide our understanding and treat-
ment of patients at this time of life.
