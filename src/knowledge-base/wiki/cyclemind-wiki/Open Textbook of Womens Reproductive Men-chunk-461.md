Goals of pharmacological treatment include promoting sleep, improving psychosis, 
and stabilizing mood. Short-term use of benzodiazepines may be helpful to promote 
sleep. Case series literature has supported the use of electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), 
benzodiazepines, lithium monotherapy, adjunctive lithium treatment, and antipsy -
chotic monotherapy (Bergink et al. 2016). ECT has long been used to treat PPP , with a 
recent large naturalistic study from Sweden demonstrating that ECT in the postpartum 
period has a significantly higher response rate of 87% in comparison with ECT admin-
istered outside the postpartum period (Rundgren et al. 2018). A major expansion from 
case series literature was a naturalistic cohort study in 64 women who sequentially re-
ceived benzodiazepines, antipsychotics, and lithium to target each domain (Bergink et 
al. 2015). Twenty percent of women responded to antipsychotic monotherapy within 
2 weeks, with most responding to adjunctive lithium that was then continued as 
monotherapy after initial remission. In comparison with antipsychotic monotherapy, 
patients treated with lithium monotherapy had a significantly lower rate of relapse by 
9 months postpartum. This evidence supports the many specialists in PPP who argue 
that the robust response to lithium (as both monotherapy and adjunctive treatment) 
means that it should be considered the initial intervention unless contraindicated, and 
antipsychotics and benzodiazepines should be considered adjunctive treatment (Ber-
gink et al. 2016). This is especially applicable in the United States, where psychiatric 
hospitalizations tend to be far shorter than the several-month stay for the women suc-
cessfully treated in the study. For women with psychosis limited to the postpartum pe-
riod, maintenance treatment with lithium should be continued for 9 months after initial 
stabilization, after which time a gradual and monitored taper can be considered. 
In the event of a hospitalization for the acute presentation of PPP , discharge planning 
requires special consideration. Family involvement in the plan for discharge, including 
identifying specific supports around nighttime care of the infant, will be critical to con-
tinued improvement once outpatient treatment is initiated. Family members or other 
support persons should be counseled on the early signs of relapse, and close outpatient 
psychiatric follow-up should be solidified. The inpatient treatment team should care-
fully consider state requirements around reporting to child prot ective services. Al -
though most women with successfully treated PPP are unlikely to meet this threshold, 
child protective services should be notified when there is a reasonable concern for po-
tential child abuse or neglect (Hatters-Friedman et al. 2019).
