Approximately 12%–30% of women may ex perience PP-PTSD following a repro-
ductive loss or traumatic delivery (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecolo-
gists 2018). Diagnosing PP-PTSD is challe nging because of the overlap with grief, 
depression, and anxiety. Although no well-validated instrument is available to test 
specifically for PP-PTSD, commonly used instruments include 
• Perinatal Grief Scale (Toedter et al. 1988) 
• Munich Grief Scale (Beutel et al. 1995) 
• Perinatal Bereavement Grief Scale (Ritsh er and Neugebauer 2002) (validated in 
Spanish and English) 
• Perinatal Grief Intensity Scale (Hutti et al. 1998) 
Following a traumatic delivery, the physician or other provider should encourage 
and facilitate a comprehensive postpartum appointment, with help from family and 
other supports when possible, to ensure that appropriate screening occurs. This type 
of trauma may also result in profound grief, shame, or a sense of failure that can de -
crease the likelihood of the woman scheduling or attending follow-up care, so proac-
tive outreach to screen is imperative (Table 18–7).
