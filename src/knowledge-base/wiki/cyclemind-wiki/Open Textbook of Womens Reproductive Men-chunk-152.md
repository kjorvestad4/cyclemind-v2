Nutrition 
The foods that we eat—and the nutrients that are derived from them—play a critical 
role in both our survival and our overall state of health, wellness, and disease. Pregnant 
and lactating women have elevated nutrient needs, and these needs may be difficult to 
meet through normal diet or maternal stores (Bodnar and Wisner 2005). Because preg-
nancy and lactation are major nutritional stressors, childbearing-age women may be 
particularly vulnerable to the impact of nutritional deficiencies on mood, specifically 
depression (Bodnar and Wisner 2005). This section addresses dietary patterns, nutri-
ents, and specific foods that are thought to play important roles in healthy pregnancy 
outcomes and also have an impact on perinatal mood. 
Diet 
Evidence consistently suggests that certain overall dietary patterns during pregnancy 
may be protective for healthy outcomes. Specifically, diets that are higher in vegetables, 
fruits, fiber, nuts and seeds, legumes, and seafood—and lower in  processed meats and 
fried foods—appear to be the most protective (Raghavan et al. 2019) and may reduce the 
risk of preterm birth (Chia et al. 2019). A systematic review of dietary patterns in associ-
ation with perinatal depression and anxiety found an inverse relationship between diets 
consisting of whole foods, fruits, vegetables, fish, and seafood and perinatal depression 
and anxiety (Silva et al. 2019; Sparling et al. 2017). Conversely, poor dietary quality is 
being studied as a modifiable risk factor in depression (Bodnar and Wisner 2005). 
Nutrients and Dietary Supplements 
In addition to a generally healthy diet, specific nutrients are recommended for healthy 
pregnancy outcomes, and some of them may have an impact on perinatal mood.
