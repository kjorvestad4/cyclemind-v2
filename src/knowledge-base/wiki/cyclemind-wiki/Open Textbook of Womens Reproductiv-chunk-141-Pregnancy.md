Women who commit neonaticide often did not obtain prenatal care. The motive for neo-

woman consciously hides her pregnancy from others (Friedman and Resnick 2009).

Pregnancy and Mothering in Corrections 
Women are the fastest growing population in the U.S. correctional system, and most 
incarcerated women are of reproductive age, with 6%–10% of incarcerated women be-
ing pregnant (American Civil Liberties Un ion 2021; Clarke and Simon 2013). As in 
other areas of U.S. society, there are racial and ethnic disparities in the imprisoned pop-
ulation. Black women are more than twice as likely, and Hispanic women 1.3 times as 
likely, to be imprisoned as white women. The rate for Black women has been declining 
since 2000, whereas those for white and Hi spanic women have been increasing (The 
Sentencing Project 2020). 
Although the standards of perinatal care within the correctional system are the 
same as those in the community, providing an equivalent level of care during incarcer-
ation is difficult for a number of reasons, including higher rates of obstetrical risk fac-
tors among inmates and operational restrict ions inherent to institutions. Compared 
with community samples, incarcerated women are more likely to have unplanned 
pregnancies complicated by lack of prenatal  care, poor nutrition, trauma, substance 
abuse, mental illness, chronic medical prob lems, lack of social support, and low so -
cioeconomic status (Clarke and Adashi 2011). Institutional barriers to providing pre-
natal care include the process of transfer ring women to outsid e medical facilities, 
coordination of care among correctional and community providers, delays in access-
ing emergency obstetrical services, and inmates’ lack of control over living conditions, 
including sleep schedules, food choices, timing of meals, and activity levels (Clarke 
and Adashi 2011; Friedman et al. 2019b). 
Several organizations, including the American College of Obstetricians and Gyne-
cologists, the Federal Bureau of Prisons, and the National Commission on Correctional 
Health Care, have proposed standards for perinatal care in correctional settings, in -

Correctional systems are increasingly faced with challenges centered around pro -
viding care to pregnant inmates. Pregnancy testing is not routine in institutions, which 
often rely on inmate self-report, with confirmation testing performed as needed (Com-
mittee on Health Care for Underserved Women of American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists 2011; Kelsey et al. 2017). Although incarceration does not preclude 
a woman’s right to access VTOP , inconsistent practices have led to unreliable access to 
VTOP services for inmates (Roth 2011). The routine use of restraints among pregnant 
inmates is controversial (Hall et al. 2015) and should be limited to absolute necessity 
involving an imminent risk of escape or harm that cannot be managed with other in-
terventions (National Task Force on the Use of Restraints With Pregnant Women Un-
der Correctional Custody 2012). Pregnancy-related education is especially important 
for this population, with perinatal education and doula programs showing positive re-
sults (Ferszt and Erickson-Owens 2008). 
Despite the many challenges involved in providing care to pregnant inmates, stud-
ies show improved outcomes for pregnancies managed during incarceration than for 
women with similar risk factors whose pregnancies are managed in the community, 
with higher birth weights and lower rates of stillbirth (Knight and Plugge 2005; Sufrin 
et al. 2019). This suggests that certain aspects of confinement, such as access to prena-
tal care, stable housing, reduced exposure to violence, and restricted access to alcohol, 
tobacco, and illicit drugs, may promote perinatal outcomes among high-risk women 
(Friedman et al. 2020; Knight and Plugge 2005; Sufrin et al. 2019). Breastfeeding is not 
usually feasible in prison due to barriers su ch as physical separation from the infant 
and lack of access to breast pumping and storage equipment (Shlafer et al. 2018). 
Less than one-quarter of American states  have mother–baby units where infants 
can reside in an incarcerated setting with their mothers; most incarcerated women are 
separated from their infants shortly after birth (Friedman et al. 2020). These children 
are generally either placed in kinship care (i.e., care of children by relatives or, in some 
jurisdictions, close family friends) or in foster care. For children placed in kinship care, 
grandparents are the most common caregiver (Glaze and Maruschak 2010). Notably, 
children of incarcerated mothers are more likely to be placed in foster care than chil-
dren of incarcerated fathers, which highlights the particularly devastating impact on 
a family when a mother is incarcerated (Glaze and Maruschak 2010). 
In the United States, this separation can le ad to termination of parental rights for 
the incarcerated mother. For children placed in foster care for longer than 15 months 
(including because their parent is in pris on), the Adoption and Safe Families Act 
(1997) seeks to place them into permanent adoption, which necessitates termination 
of parental rights (Clarke and Simon 2013;  Warner 2015). When a child is placed in 
foster care, it can be particularly hard for a mother to reunify with her child, with one 
study finding that incarcerated mothers were half as likely to regain custody of chil -
dren in foster care compared with nonincarcerated women (Christian 2009).

Most female correctional facilities offer a parenting program, but most of these pro-
grams do not include visitation (Miller et al. 2014), despite contact during incarceration 
being an important predictor of reunification (Gilham 2012). However, most incarcer-
ated women keep in some form of regular contact with their children. Even for those 
mothers who are afforded in-person visits, many factors such as geographical distance, 
a physical environment that is not child-friendly, security procedures, restrictions on 
physical contact, and short length of visitation can be problematic (O’Malley and Dev-
aney 2016; Schubert et al. 2016). To address these challenges, some prisons have tried 
videoconferencing, with limited success (Cramer et al. 2017), and others have tried 
having the parent record bedtime stories and sending the recordings to their children 
(Poehlmann et al. 2010). Encouraging commu nication between incarcerated mothers 
and their children is important because prolonged separation has a significant impact 
on psychological outcomes for the children. Children of incarcerated mothers have 
been noted to be at increased risk for sleep disorders, depression, anger, and anxiety 
(Mignon and Ransford 2012).
