However, effective screening for perinatal anxiety remains limited, in part because 
many questions remain about the optimal screening tool, starting with which—of 
many—anxiety symptoms to address (Fairbrother et al. 2019; Matthey et al. 2013a). For 
example, the Generalized Anxiety Disorder seven-item subscale (Spitzer et al. 2006) 
identified GAD in perinatal populations with greater accuracy and specificity than the 
widely used Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS; Cox et al. 1987). However, 
the clinical presentation of perinatal anxiety can be highly variable, including symp -
toms from various DSM-5 disorders, such as panic or intrusive thoughts, making the 
selection of a single tool difficult (Matthey et al. 2013a; Meades and Ayers 2011). In ad-
dition, general anxiety scales may not delineate symptoms of anxiety from the common 
physical symptoms of pregnancy and postpa rtum, such as sleep disturbance, which 
may lead to false positives (Matthey et al. 2013a). Examples include “I feel rested” and 
“I feel comfortable” in the State-Trait Anxiety Index (STAI; Spielberger et al. 1983) and 
“I can sit at ease and feel relaxed” in the Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS; 
Zigmond and Snaith 1983). In contrast, the widely used EPDS has the advantage of ex-
cluding constitutional symptoms of pregnancy and postpartum (Cox et al. 1987). 
In addition, pregnancy and becoming a pare nt are major life transitions that can 
feel stressful or overwhelming and can lead to anxiety specific for this distinct period 
of time, yet general screening tools may not capture anxiety symptoms specific to this 
population (Fallon et al. 2016b). Many sy mptoms of perinatal anxiety may not meet 
DSM-5 criteria for an anxiety disorder but nevertheless may be significant in terms of 
symptom severity, functional impairment, and impact on maternal and infant out -
comes (Phillips et al. 2009). A recent sy stematic review analyzed the psychometric 
properties of several generalized anxiety screening tools designed for the general 
adult population, such as the STAI and the HADS, and concluded that there is a sub-
stantial need for further validation of these measures to enable accurate screening and 
detection in the perinatal population (Meades and Ayers 2011). 
On the other hand, several pregnancy-spec ific self-report questionnaires, such as 
the Pregnancy Anxiety Scale (Levin 1991), the Pregnancy-Related Anxiety Question -
naire (Van den Bergh 1990), and the Pregnancy-Related Anxiety Scale (Wadhwa et al. 
1993) focus on perinatal concerns but may have limited relevance for diagnosable anx-
iety disorders. Interestingly, although perinatal-specific scales focus on highly specific 
topics, they may predict adverse maternal–infant outcomes more effectively than gen-
eral measures of anxiety, and they are distinct from general indices of anxiety and de-
pression (Huizink et al. 2002). It was partly due to results such as these that the concept 
of pregnancy-specific anxiety—that is, anxi ety specific to concerns about pregnancy, 
delivery, postpartum, and parenthood, and distinct from anxiety experienced at other 
times of life—was developed (Huizink et al . 2004). For example, a confirmatory factor 
analysis and a later replication study noted that general anxiety and depression symp-
toms contributed little to the variance in pregnancy-specific anxiety and that topics 
specific to the pregnancy itself (e.g., concern about the health of the fetus) were more 
important factors (Brunton et al. 2019; Huizink et al. 2004).
