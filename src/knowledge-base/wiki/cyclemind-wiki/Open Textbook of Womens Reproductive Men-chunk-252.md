Tobacco 
Tobacco and nicotine exposure are associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes, in -
cluding damage to the umbilical cord structure, miscarriage, increased risk of ectopic 
pregnancy, placental abruption, preterm birth, low birth weight, small for gestational 
age infants, and increased infant mortalit y (Forray 2016; Horne et al. 2014). Prenatal 
nicotine exposure in humans is associated with greater risk of orofacial clefts and po-
tentially a range of other congenital anomalies (Holbrook 2016). Postnatal morbidities, 
such as sudden infant death syndrome, have been associated with both prenatal mater-
nal smoking and secondhand exposure. Both intrauterine and passive tobacco smoke 
exposure have also been associated with an increased risk of asthma, ear infections, 
and respiratory illnesses (Forray 2016). Nicoti ne transfers in relatively high quantity 
in breastmilk; higher amounts of nicotine are passed through breastmilk than through 
the placenta prenatally. Smoking more than 10 cigarettes per day may decrease milk 
production. Exposure to tobacco in breastmilk may also alter babies’ sleep patterns (Mc-
Lafferty et al. 2016). 
Alcohol 
Alcohol use during pregnancy is associated with the most well-established adverse fe-
tal and child health effects of all prenatal substance exposures (Pruett et al. 2013). Al-
cohol consumed during pregnancy is concentrated in the amniotic fluid, metabolized 
away at 3%–4% of maternal metabolic rate s, and readily absorbed into fetal blood 
(Burd et al. 2012). Consequently, the fetus is exposed to the same blood alcohol level 
as the mother, resulting in fetal toxicity and potential teratogenicity (McLafferty et al. 
2016). Fetal alcohol syndrome, first described in 1973, refers to a pattern of craniofacial, 
limb, and cardiovascular defects associated with prenatal-onset growth deficiency and 
developmental delay. The term fetal alcohol spectrum disorder (FASD) is now used to de-
scribe a broader range of pathologies, including low birth weight, small for gestational 
age infant, preterm birth, spontaneous abortions, behavioral problems, developmental 
delay, and cognitive deficits (DeVido et al. 2015). FASD is the most common and pre-
ventable cause of intellectual disability in the United States. No amount of alcohol use 
is recommended during pregnancy; evidence for the safety of even low to moderate 
alcohol use during pregnancy is inconclusive (Henderson et al. 2007). 
Literature examining the effects of alcohol use during lactation is limited. Alcohol 
readily diffuses in and out of breastmilk, and human milk levels generally parallel ma-
ternal blood alcohol levels. Milk levels typically peak at about 30–60 minutes after in-

Cannabis 
The endocannabinoid system is present early in fetal development, and it plays a key 
role in mediating neuronal maturation and development of key neurotransmitter sys-
tems in the CNS. Cannabis exposure in pregnancy during critical periods of brain de-
velopment can induce subtle and long-lasting alterations in neurodevelopment (Jaques 
et al. 2014). Current evidence does not support an association between marijuana ex-
posure and any specific congenital birth defects (Metz and Stickrath 2015). Cannabis 
exposure in pregnancy has been associated with stillbirth, preterm birth, fetal growth 
restriction, and lower birth weight, but these findings have been inconsistent and con-
founded by frequent co-use with tobacco and other substances and sociodemographic 
factors (Metz and Stickrath 2015). Also noteworthy is that the potency of marijuana has 
been steadily increasing, from about 3% in the 1980s to 12% in 2012, so data from pre-
vious studies may no longer be relevant (Volkow et al. 2014). Cannabis is lipophilic; 
therefore, it readily crosses the placental barrier and passes into breastmilk; breastmilk 
levels of delta-tetrahydrocannabinol can a ccumulate up to eightfold in comparison 
with plasma levels and can be found in breastmilk for up to 6 days following maternal 
use (Ordean and Kim 2020). 
Three long-term prospective longitudinal human studies followed offspring from 
the neonatal period into childhood and ad olescence. Subtle neurobehavioral distur -
bances have been demonstrated in cannabis-exposed neonates, such as exaggerated 
and prolonged startle responses and sleep-cycle problems (Jaques et al. 2014). Greater 
externalizing behaviors, including impulsiv ity and hyperactivity, were reported in 
children, as well as impairments in early executive function, abstract and visual rea -
soning, and visuoperceptual functioning in adolescents (Huizink 2014). It is not pos-
sible to determine the degree to which these effects were directly related to prenatal 
cannabis exposure or to potential confounding variables. In addition, a large retro -
spective analysis of all live births in Ontario, Canada, over a 5-year period recently 
found an increased rate of autism in the children of mothers who used cannabis in 
pregnancy (Corsi et al. 2020).
