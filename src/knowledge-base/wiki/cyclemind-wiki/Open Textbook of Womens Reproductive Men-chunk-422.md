Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Prevalence estimates suggest a cumulative 12-month prevalence of 2.4% for bipolar 
spectrum disorders and lower rates for indivi dual subtypes such as BD type I (BDI; 
0.6%) and BD type II (BDII; 0.4%) (Yatham et al. 2018). Age of onset is typically be -
tween 12 and 30 years of age, coinciding with the peak reproductive years (Epstein et 
al. 2014). 
Risk Factors 
General risk factors for BD include a family history of mood disorder, traumatic life 
events, and substance use (Roberts 2019). A previous perinatal mood episode is the 
strongest predictor for a perinatal recurrence of illness for women with BD (Di Florio 
et al. 2018). Primiparity has also been associated with a higher risk of a postpartum 
manic or psychotic episode in women with BD. In a study that included 934 women 
with BDI, 35% reported an episode of mani a or psychotic depression after their first 
pregnancy, compared with 20.5% after their second pregnancy and 14.6% after their 
third pregnancy (Di Florio et al. 2014). Other important risk factors for a postpartum 
mood episode in BDI include mood sympto ms during pregnancy, younger age, un -
planned pregnancy, and a family history of  a perinatal mood episode (Table 15–1) 
(Doyle et al. 2012). 
Screening Methods 
Identifying women with BD during pregnancy and postpartum may be aided by the 
use of screening tools. Although screening for depression during these periods is rec-
ommended by the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG), the 
U.S. Preventive Services Task Force, and the American Academy of Pediatrics, no for-
mal recommendations for BD screening as a part of routine obstetrical care have been 
established (American Colleg e of Obstetricians and Gyne cologists 2018; Earls et al. 
2019; Siu et al. 2016). Postpartum depression is more common among women with BD 
than among women with major depressive disorder, and most postpartum mood ep-
