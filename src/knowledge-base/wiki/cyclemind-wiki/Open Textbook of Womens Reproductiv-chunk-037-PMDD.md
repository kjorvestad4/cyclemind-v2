Key Points 
• Premenstrual syndrome (PMS) affects most women worldwide, with physical 
or emotional symptoms or both, but it is not impairing. 
• Premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD) affects a much smaller number (up 
to 8% of women) and impairs function. 
• PMDD only occurs in ovulatory menstrual cycles and can co-occur with other 
mood and anxiety disorders. 
• PMDD must be differentiated from premenstrual exacerbation of underlying 
mood and anxiety disorders, such as bipolar disorder and major depression. 
• Evidence-based treatments for PMDD include selective serotonin reuptake 
inhibitors (SSRIs), serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors, and sup -
pression of ovulation. 
• Bipolar disorder and PMDD comorbidity is especially important to diagnose 
and requires treatment with mood stab ilizers and contraceptives or a cau -
tious trial of SSRIs. 
• Nonpharmacological treatments are useful in the treatment of PMDD. These in-
clude calcium supplementation, cognitive-behavioral therapy, and acupuncture.

Likeother times of reproductive change in a woman’s life, perimenopause increases 
the risk for the onset of symptoms affectin g physical and mental  health, as well as 
exacerbations of previously diagnosed mental illnesses. The greatest body of evidence 
exists for the recurrence of depression (Cohen et al. 2006; Freeman et al. 2014; Maki et 
al. 2018) and new-onset depression (Freeman et al. 2006). During perimenopause, hor-
mones fluctuate significantly, and it is likely that these fluctuations, in addition to the 
overall waning amounts of estrogen and other hormones, are responsible for the 
increased risk of depression and the wide variety of symptoms that perimenopausal 
women may experience, including irritability, hot flashes, sleep disturbance, cognitive 
complaints, and sexual dysfunction. However, the role of these hormonal changes is 
complex and not yet fully understood, and additional factors, such as family history, 
adverse childhood experiences,  changes with aging, and later life experiences, also 
have roles (Epperson et al. 2017; Woods et al. 2008). 
In this chapter, we examine mental health at the end of the reproductive lifespan by 
focusing on the epidemiology, clinical features, presentation, pathophysiology, evalu-
ation, and treatment of both perimenopausal depression and vasomotor symptoms 
(VMS) that are common and frequently complicate depression. We also review other 
prevalent and potentially related perimenopausal symptoms, such as sleep distur -
bance, sexual problems, and cognitive complaints. Much of the information we have 
about this time of life is informed by three large prospective cohort studies performed 
in the United States: the Study of Women’s Health Across the Nation (SWAN), the 
Penn Ovarian Aging Study (POAS), and the Seattle Midlife Women’s Health Study 
(SMWHS). These studies focus on women during the perimenopausal transition and 
offer significant evidence about the natural course and treatment of perimenopausal 
symptoms.

Perimenopausal Depression 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Cross-sectional studies suggest that perimenopausal women experience depressive 
symptoms at higher rates than premen opausal women: 45%–68% of women during 
the menopausal transition versus 28%–31% of women prior to this transition (Maki et 
al. 2018). Fewer studies measure actual rates of major depressive disorder (MDD) as 
women progress from premenopause to postmenopause. The large prospective SWAN 
reported increasing MDD rates across this transition: 5.8% in premenopausal, 9.1% in 
perimenopausal, and 9.8% in postmenopausal women (Bromberger et al. 2011). Simi-
larly, POAS, which followed women over 8 years as many of them transitioned from 
premenopause to perimenopause, found that, after adjustments were made for risk fac-
tors such as smoking, BMI, history of pr emenstrual syndrome, estradiol levels, and 
VMS severity, perimenopausal women were more than five times as likely as premeno-
pausal women to experience a first episode of depression (Freeman et al. 2006). The 
smaller Harvard Study of Moods and Cycles reported MDD in 9.5% of premenopausal 
women versus 16.6% of perimenopausal women (Cohen et al. 2006). Finally, a large 
cross-sectional survey of European women found a sevenfold increased risk of suicidal 
ideation in women during perimenopause compared with women in premenopause or 
postmenopause, independent of mood and anxiety disorders (Usall et al. 2009). 
Risk Factors 
Women with a prior history of MDD are at increased risk of a recurrence during peri-
menopause (Maki et al. 2018). SWAN found that 58% of women with prior MDD expe-
rienced at least one major depressive episode over the study’s 13-year follow-up period, 
compared with 28% of women with no prior history of depression, a twofold increase in 
risk (Bromberger et al. 2015). In addition, a greater degree of hormonal change, particu-
larly as measured by blood levels of follicle-stimulating hormone (FSH) (Freeman et al. 
2004) and estradiol (Freeman et al. 2006), is associated with increased depressive symp-
toms during perimenopause versus premenopause. Longer time spent in the menopausal 
transition may further increase the risk for a perimenopausal depression (Avis et al. 1994; 
Freeman et al. 2006), as does surgically induced menopause (Obermeyer et al. 2007). 
Other risk factors for perimenopausal depression include a history of anxiety, premen-
strual syndrome, adverse experiences, a history of trauma during childhood, Black race 
or Hispanic ethnicity, financial stress, low social support, a history of violent or abusive 
relationships, VMS (hot flashes and night sweats) and other perimenopausal symptoms 
such as sleep problems, and health and lifes tyle factors, including elevated BMI and 
smoking cigarettes (Bromberger et al. 2015; Cohen et al. 2006; Epperson et al. 2017; 
Freeman et al. 2004, 2006, 2014; Maki et al. 2018; Schmidt et al. 2004). Risk differences 
by race and ethnicity may be attenuated or explained by psyc hosocial differences. 
SWAN, for example, found that psychosocial and health factors accounted for higher 
odds of depressive symptoms among Black and Hispanic participants (Bromberger 
and Kravitz 2011). Table 7–1 summarizes the risk factors for menopausal depression.

Other Mental Illnesses in Perimenopause 
Although research is limited, existing ev idence suggests that women are at an in -
creased risk of new onset or exacerbation of other mental illnesses during perimeno-
pause (Hu et al. 2016). A systematic review  that included seven cross-sectional and 
two longitudinal studies found that women with bipolar I or II disorder were at in -
creased risk of depressive episodes during  perimenopause (Perich et al. 2017). In a 
small longitudinal study, women previously diagnosed with bipolar illness scored 
higher on scales measuring depressive an d manic symptoms during late perimeno -
pause and early postmenopause (Marsh et al . 2015). Findings from several studies 
suggest that perimenopause is a time of increased risk for schizophrenia, with a sec-
ond wave being diagnosed between ages 45 and 54 (Crow et al. 2016; Gupta et al. 
2012). Studies showing that estrogen and selective estrogen receptor modulators im-
prove some symptoms of schizophrenia in women (Kulkarni et al. 2008; Searles et al. 
2018) and that antipsychotics become less effective for women the more time elapses 
after menopause (González-Rodríguez et al . 2016) all suggest that the menopausal 
transition plays an important role in schizophrenia. 
Anxiety disorders are less well studied during the menopausal transition, and cur-
rent evidence is insufficient to establis h an increased incidence during the meno -
pausal transition. However, there is evidence of increased anxiety symptoms during 
perimenopause among women who previously had low baseline anxiety before be -
ginning the menopausal transition, as well  as a relationship between VMS and both 
anxiety and panic attacks (Bromberger et al. 2013; Bryant et al. 2012; Claudia et al. 
2004; Freeman and Sammel 2016; Freeman et  al. 2005; Hantsoo and Epperson 2017). 
Additionally, somatic anxiety preceding the menopausal transition is a risk factor for 
worse VMS during the transition, suggesting that treatment addressing anxiety may 
also improve hot flashes (Freeman and Sammel 2016). 
The epidemiology of other disorders, such as substance misuse and eating disorders, 
requires further research. Perimenopause may be a time of increased vulnerability to a 
new onset or worsening of alcohol use disorder, and older women are more at risk from 
medical complications of alcohol use at lo wer levels of consumption than younger 
women or men (Milic et al. 2018). In addition, after controlling for other risk factors, a 
large study of women veterans with chro nic pain found that having menopausal 
symptoms was associated with the potential iatrogenic risk of long-term and higher-

Screening Methods for Perimenopausal Depression 
There is currently no widespread agreement on the timing of or specific scales to use in 
screening for perimenopausal depression (Willi and Ehlert 2019) and no validated peri-
menopausal depression scale (Maki et al. 2018). Gynecologists wishing to screen at an-
nual examinations may want to begin as early as late premenopause, or when one cycle 
has lengthened or shortened by at least 7 days in the past year, because studies show this 
to be when an increased risk of depressive symptoms begins (Freeman et al. 2006). The 
Center for Epidemiologic Studies Depression  Scale (Radloff 1977) is used in numer -
ous mood and menopause studies, but standard screening instruments used at other 
times of life, such as the Patient Health Questionnaire–9 (Spitzer et al. 1999), also can 
be used in the premenopausal population (Maki et al. 2018). The Patient Health Ques-
tionnaire–9 is a nine-item scale listing depressive symptoms based on DSM criteria, 
and a score  10 is considered a positive screen (Arroll et al. 2010). Before a patient 
starts taking antidepressants, the clinician should screen for bipolar disorder to pre -
vent antidepressant-induced mania. The Mood Disorder Questionnaire is a brief 
screening tool that is frequently used for bipolar illness presenting in the postpartum 
period when sleep disturbance and somati c symptoms are higher; these characteris -
tics are also found in the perimenopausal population (Clark and Wisner 2018).
