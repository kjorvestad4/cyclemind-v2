Compulsions in this population typically take the form of contamination-related 
rituals, such as cleaning or sterilizing baby-related equipment, or of checking and or-
dering rituals, such as repetitively checking on a sleeping infant (Hudak and Wisner 
2012; Speisman et al. 2011). Checking comp ulsions are the most frequently reported 
symptom in postpartum women with OCD (Hudak and Wisner 2012). Women fre -
quently check to ensure that they have not harmed the infant (in the case of intrusive 
thoughts of infant harm), to ensure the infant is still breathing, or to ensure that no 
harm has befallen the infant. At times, the obsessions can be so extreme as to lead to 
avoidance of the infant or of common situat ions or activities that provoke intrusive 
thoughts, as opposed to true ritualistic behavior (Hudak and Wisner 2012; Koran et 
al. 2007). Women may avoid such activities as bathing or diapering, avoid objects 
such as kitchen knives and stairs, or limit contact with the infant in general. 
Although acting on urges to harm an infant in perinatal OCD is rare, indirect harm 
is possible; for example, the patient may avoid or neglect the child, “clean” the child 
inappropriately with bleach or other harmfu l substances, or insist on inappropriate 
neatness (Koran et al. 2007). Rarely, patients can become aggressive when others inter-
fere with their compulsions. In such cases, or any other cases in which the clinician has 
significant safety concerns, it may be necessary to involve social agencies. Regardless 
of imminent involvement, a thorough discussion about the role of social agencies is im-
portant given the degree to which patients fear legal action and avoid help as a result. 
The symptoms and severity of perinata l-onset OCD are time-consuming and dis -
tressing. They can often detract from mother–infant bonding and can lead to long-term 
impairments in attachment patterns (Arnold 1999; Challacombe and Salkovskis 2009). 
Women with worsening OCD symptoms in the premenstrual or postpartum period 
frequently have comorbid mood symptoms (Uguz et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 1999) and 
more frequently report a history of premenstrual worsening of mood symptoms, ma-
jor depressive disorder, or postpartum depression (PPD) (Labad et al. 2005). 
Intrusive thoughts are not specific to perinatal OCD; they have also been reported 
under normal circumstances and in other perinatal mental health conditions, most 
specifically PPD (Abramowitz et al. 2003, 2010b; McGuinness et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 
1999). Many reports have suggested that such thoughts are common in new parents 
and do not represent psychopathology when they are experienced as fleeting and mo-
mentary. These symptoms can include subt hreshold intrusive, distressing, and un -
wanted thoughts that involve themes of safety, contamination, or harm coming to 
loved ones (particularly the infant) or even  thoughts of harming, shaking, or hitting 
the infant, especially at times when the infant is difficult to console (McGuinness et al. 
2011; Wisner et al. 1999). The thoughts are typically fleeting in nature and do not in-
terfere with normal daily functioning. They  have been reported in as many as 34%–

Course/Prognosis 
The onset of postpartum OCD tends to be variable; approximately half of women ex-
perience symptoms abruptly, and the other half have a more gradual onset (Maina et 
al. 1999; Speisman et al. 2011). OCD tends to have a chronic course over time. Few 
studies have looked at the course and prognosis of perinatal OCD specifically. A 2011 
study showed that women with preexisting OCD who experience worsening symp -
toms in the perinatal period have a significantly worse course of illness in the pres -
ence of comorbid major depressive disorder or an anxiety disorder (Uguz et al. 2011). 
Depression in particular is associated with a more chronic course of illness and poorer 
outcomes (Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015; Uguz et al. 2011). 
Perinatal OCD is associated with impaired mother–infant relationships and may be 
associated with impaired attachment patterns and neurodevelopment of infants in ex-
treme cases. Mother–infant interactions have been shown to be affected by early ma-
ternal anxiety in several studies (Feldman et al. 2009; Nicol-Harper et al. 2007; Warren 
et al. 2003). One study comparing postpa rtum mothers with OCD and their infants 
with control dyads showed that mothers with OCD were often less confident and re-
ported increased marital distress and less social support than peers (Challacombe et al. 
2016), but little research has been done in this area. Breastfeeding rates tend to be lower 
in postpartum women with OCD. In dyadic observation, mothers with OCD have been 
rated as less sensitive to infant needs, which is consistent with maternal anxiety disor-
ders in the postpartum period (Challacombe et al. 2016). Mothers with OCD also tend 
to perceive the quality of their mother–infant relationship as more negative and report 
finding it difficult to enjoy time with their infant (Challacombe and Salkovskis 2009). 
One case series showed impairment in mother–infant interactions in 71% of the sam-
ple, with the presence of either underinvolvement (avoidance of the infant) or overin-
volvement (overprotective care of the infant) (Arnold 1999). Some of these interactions 
can also be attributed to comorbid PPD.

Evaluation and Differential Diagnosis 
In addition to using the screening scales mentioned earlier, psychiatric practitioners 
must adequately rule out common medical comorbidities that might mimic or exacer-
bate obsessive and compulsive symptoms in the perinatal patient. In particular, prac-
titioners should conduct a thorough me dical history and physical examination, 
supplemented with laboratory studies when indicated. Referral to the patient’s obstet-
rical provider or other appropriate specialist is indicated with any abnormality in his-
tory, physical examination, or laboratory evaluation.
