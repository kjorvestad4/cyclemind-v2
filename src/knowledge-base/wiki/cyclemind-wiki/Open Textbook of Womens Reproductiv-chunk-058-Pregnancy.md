After birth, the parent–infant relationship continues to be a highly dynamic combi-
nation of nature and nurture. Two main neurobiological pathways are thought to be in-
volved in the parent–infant attachment bond: 1) the dopaminergic “reward” pathway 
and 2) the oxytocinergic system (Scatliffe et al. 2019; Strathearn et al. 2009). Oxytocin 
is a neuromodulatory hormone produced in the hypothalamus that plays a key role in 
physiological processes, such as labor and lactation. Oxytocin is also believed to be crit-
ical to the emergence and maintenance of the behavior that forms the basis of the par-
ent–infant bond (Kim et al. 2014). Oxytocin is released in response to stimuli such as 
infant suckling or the sight or sound of a nursing mother’s infant (McNeilly et al. 1983). 
Evidence suggests a relationship between oxytocin and prosocial effects, such as en -
hanced social memory, improved eye gaze when looking at faces, increased recognition 
of faces and facial expressions, and increased manifestations of trust (Baumgartner et 
al. 2008; Domes et al. 2007; Guastella et al. 2008a, 2008b; Kosfeld et al. 2005; Savaskan 
et al. 2008). Likewise, oxytocin is thought to mediate multiple behavioral components 
of the parent–infant relationship, including physical contact, synchrony, and respon-
siveness. These effects are notably seen in both mother–infant and father–infant pairs 
(Kim et al. 2014; Scatliffe et al. 2019; Vittner et al. 2018). Oxytocin receptors are located 
in the ventral striatum, an important dopaminergic brain region, and animal models 
have demonstrated that oxytocin receptor binding is associated with maternal prosocial 
behaviors (Olazábal and Young 2006). Thus, it is thought that oxytocin may link social 
cues to dopamine-associated reinforcement pathways (Strathearn et al. 2009).

The Parent–Infant Dyad 
The parent–infant relationship comprises the observable aspects of the interaction and 
the unobservable aspects (i.e., mental, emotional, representational) of the relationship. 
Observable Interactions 
The observable aspects of the parent–infant relationship consist of the nuances of their 
interactions with each other. Infants are born with unique capabilities at birth, includ-
ing having a preference for maternal smell (Macfarlane 1975; Sullivan and Toubas 
1998) and voice (DeCasper and Fifer 1980) and preferring the mother’s face over other 
shapes and objects or even a stranger’s face (Bushnell et al. 1989; Field et al. 1984; Pas-
calis et al. 1995). From the beginning, they are active participants in their environment 
and their interactions with their caregivers. The relationship between the parent and 
infant is very intimate and made up of physical closeness through touch, nonverbal 
interactions (e.g., gaze), mutual communication through vocalizations and protocon-
versations, and coregulation, such as the level of reciprocity between mother and in-
fant. Many facets of the parent–infant dyad  influence the quality of the interaction, 
including parental responsiveness, handling  of disruption and repair , and the plea-
sure experienced by both parent and infant. The microanalytic research, which looks 
at early mother–infant interactions, shows that infants and mothers engage in a rich 
and detailed back-and-forth exchange, which, if studied at 4 months of age, can pre -
dict the development of secure versus avoidant attachment patterns at 1 year (Kou -
lomzin et al. 2002). 
Unobservable Interactions 
Parents often evolve a rich set of imaginations and expectations regarding their infants 
and regarding themselves as parents, which are referred to as parental representational 
mind. These thoughts and fantasies are affected by the parents’ history with their own 
caregivers, culture, life events, hopes, dreams, fears, and history of trauma, among 
other factors (Stern 1985, 1995). Another important concept is parental reflective func-
tioning. Parental reflective functioning has been described as parents’ capacity to un-
derstand their own and their child’s ment al states, and the effects these have on 
behaviors and interactions between them (De Roo et al. 2019). Parental reflective func-
tioning is associated with better parent–child coregulation and is linked to the quality 
of the parent–child attachment (Moser et al. 2019).

In turn, through interactions with their primary caregivers, infants begin forming 
mental patterns of how they are perceived in each interaction, how they are expected 
to respond, and what they can expect from the other person. Over time, they might start 
internalizing these images and identifying with them. These inte rnalized images, or 
mental representations, of themselves and the world around them are also called internal 
working models (Crittenden 1990). Galinsky (1987) theorized that parent and child de-
velopment are intertwined, such that the child’s and parent’s developmental processes 
shape each other; the parental stages of development correspond with the baby’s own 
age and developmental trajectory. Just as children advance through several develop-
mental stages, adults also continue to move through developmental stages within the 
context of the transition to parenthood. 
The infant’s psychological life begins in the parents’ mind (Gilmore and Meersand 
2013). Galinsky (1987) proposed six progressive psychological stages of parenthood: 
Image-Making, Nurturing, Authority, Interpretive, Interdependent, and Departure. 
In Galinsky’s model, Stage 1 (Image-Making)—when parents form images about their 
infants and begin to imagine who and how they will be as parents—begins at concep -
tion and continues through delivery. Parent al mental representations have been hy -
pothesized to become more elaborate as the pregnancy progresses and to continue 
into the postnatal period, when parents en counter the real infant. These mental rep-
resentations can translate into feelings about the pregnancy, infant, and their evolving 
family and might be associated with parents projecting or attributing characteristics 
to the infant pre- or postnatally. These attributions might have a positive or negative 
valence. Even when positive, they may not be accurate and therefore not match the 
real infant. In effect, the imagined infant may or may not reflect the real infant. 
Of note, adults’ own experiences of being parented may affect how they parent their 
own children. Negative experiences of being parented as a child and their influence on 
how these adults parent their own children have been described as “ghosts in the nurs-
ery” (Fraiberg et al. 1975). These “ghosts” can be transmitted from one generation to 
the next and seem to influence parent–child interaction, silently and negatively, in the 
present moment, potentially leading to an intergenerational cycle of abuse and trauma. 
More recently, “angels in the nursery” have been proposed as a positive counterpart, 
representing benevolent parenting experiences (Lieberman et al. 2005b). Early positive 
experiences of parenting in childhood lead to security and self-confidence in adult -
hood. This, in turn, affects adults’ experience of and responses to their own children. 
It can also serve to interrupt negative patterns of interaction or abuse. 
Assessment 
Assessment of parent–infant relationships can start during pregnancy, before the infant 
is born. During the prenatal period, the clinician can get a sense of parental mental rep-
resentations by noticing how the parents talk about their infant and about themselves 
as parents. The clinician can also ask the parents what they think their baby will be like, 
how they imagine their relationship with their baby will be, if they have any worries 
about being a parent, and how the transition to parenthood has been affecting their un-
derstanding and feelings about each other. Later, when the infant is born, the clinician 
can explore whether the experience of having this infant and the transition to parent-
hood have been similar to what they imagined and, if not, how they have been differ-
ent. Within the psychotherap y context, there is space to evaluate and understand

the rates of internalizing and externalizing disorders in childhood (Tooten et al. 2014). 
The assessment phase continues after the child’s birth in terms of analyzing various 
components of the interactions between parents and their infant. Several formal meth-
ods of assessment and observation of parent–infant interactions have been developed. 
Some of the main factors identified and evaluated in these interactions consist of ma-
ternal sensitivity, responsiveness, contingency, dyadic reciprocity, disruption and re-
pair, level of intrusiveness or control, and the pleasure experienced by the parent and 
the infant. Several of the formalized assessment measures include the Crowell Parent– 
Child Interaction Procedure (Crowell et al. 1988); the Face-to-Face Still-Face Procedure 
(Tronick et al. 1978); the Marschak Interaction Method (Marschak 1960); and the Early 
Relational Assessment (Clark 1999). 
The perinatal mental health clinician who is seeing the mother or both parents in the 
postpartum period can also obtain rich information from clinically observing the inter-
actions between parents and their infant, including the quality of the gaze, the touch, 
the vocalization between the parents and th e infant, and the affective tone between 
them. Moreover, the clinician should assess the level of reciprocity within the dyad— 
how the infant responds to various verbal and nonverbal communications and contact 
from the parents, and vice versa. Does the dyad seem to reciprocate, engage, be soothed 
by, and enjoy the interactions? 
Parents and infants go through various cycles and can experience mismatches or dis-
ruptions regularly, even when all is going well between them. Mismatches are often fol-
lowed by repair and eventually get to a point of harmony (Tronick and Gianino 1986). 
Therefore, when observing parent–infant interactions, the goal is not to draw conclu-
sions based on each and every observable interaction but, rather, to consider them as a 
cumulative set of data that , when considered with the information the clinician has 
about the parent’s psychology, psychopathology, relationships, history of trauma, and 
representational mind, can help with better assessment of any relational problems be-
tween parents and infants. 
Touch 
Touch is one of the main ways in which parents and infants connect and communicate. 
The extensive research on the importance and necessity of physical closeness, touch, 
and SSC from the first moments after birth has translated into policies and practices 
that aim at supporting mothers, and increasingly fathers, in providing this experience 
for infants, including those born prematurely (Cleveland et al. 2017; Field et al. 2010). 
When assessing touch and the ways in whic h the infant is physically handled in the 
perinatal setting, one can pay attention to things such as 
• Is the parent able to be physically close to the infant and hold him or her? 
• How does the parent touch and handle the infant? 
• Do the parent and infant appear comfortable, and is the parent attuned to what the 
infant is expressing? 
• Is the quality of touch affectionate and gentle, or intrusive and rough? 
• Does the parent enjoy physical closeness to the infant?

Gaze 
So much of the communication between parents and infants is nonverbal, and gaze is 
one of the main ways that infants communicate with others (Negri 2007). In fact, gaze 
aversion in infants is thought to be one of their main ways of disengaging or signaling 
dislike, distress, or disconnect and at times a way to self-regulate before being able to 
reengage in more direct interactions. Parents can also have difficulty engaging in face-
to-face interaction, which includes periods of mutual gaze with the infant. When as -
sessing gaze in the dyad, some things to notice are 
• Is the parent able to engage in face-to-face interaction with the infant? 
• What is the quality of gaze (e.g., soft and affectionate vs. cold and distant)? 
• Are the parent and the infant able to engage in mutual gaze? 
• What happens when an infant averts his or her gaze? Is the parent able to wait for 
the infant to self-regulate and reengage, or is this too distressing for the parent, who 
must find a way to make eye contact with the infant again? 
• Is the infant able to look at the clinician and make eye contact? 
Of course, these interactions vary based on the infant’s age and developmental stage 
and the infant’s arousal state at the time of observation, such as whether the infant is 
interactive and playful or sleepy and restful. 
Vocalizations 
Parents and infants also communicate verbally and through vocalization. Infants and 
their caregivers engage in vocal turn-taking prior to the development of speech, called 
protoconversations. These protoconversations set the stage for bonding, language, and 
social development of the infant (Yoo et al. 2018). From a very early age, infants start 
vocalizing different sounds, to which parents respond in various ways (Bornstein et 
al. 2015). This sets the stage for contingent interactions within the pair. The rate of con-
versation turn-taking may vary within different communities and cultures; however, 
to date, our knowledge indicates that mothers universally respond to their infant’s vo-
calizations and that infant vocalization is also contingent on maternal verbal commu-
nication. Within these interactions, and more so in the beginning, mothers are more 
responsive to their infants than vice versa (Bornstein et al. 2015). By 6 months of age, 
infants have learned that their vocalization s have social value and that parental en -
gagement can be altered by the level and type of vocalization (Franklin et al. 2014). In 
perinatal mental health settings, the clinician can pay attention to the presence or ab-
sence of any conversational turn-taking, the affective tone of the vocalizations in both 
the parent and the infant, the match or mism atch in the tone and rhythmicity of this 
interaction, and the content (in the parent with a preverbal infant).

significantly affected by them. Parents seen in perinatal mental health settings receive 
treatment for their mental health issues, such as depression or anxiety. However, stud-
ies have shown that treatment of matern al psychopathology alone does not always

When there are concerns about the parent–infant relationship,the psychopathology and 
relational issues ideally should be addressed simultaneously whenever possible. 
Parent–infant psychotherapy focuses on th e relationship between the parent and 
the infant as the main target of treatment and intervention. It is a strength-based, cul-
turally informed model of care in which the therapist identifies and draws on the ex-
isting strengths in the relationship and family system. The treatment consists of a range 
of interventions, some practical (e.g., making sure the parent and infant have the basic 
necessities of a safe living environment), some behavioral (e.g., focusing on the observ-
able interactions between the parent and the infant), and some geared toward the men-
tal representations and emotional dynamics between the parent and the infant. 
Additionally, educating and informing parents about normal development and realis-
tic expectations can be a significant part of treatment. This is especially true for first-
time parents and those who have a child with medical or developmental problems.

Special Considerations 
Premature/Medically Ill Infants 
Each year, about 8%–10% of infants born in the United States need admission to a neo-
natal intensive care unit (NICU) due to prematurity or medical complications (Harri-
son and Goodman 2015). NICU admission brings on many challenges for infants and 
their parents and possibly within their relationship. NICU infants experience early 
separation, unnatural environments, painful and intrusive procedures, problems with 
physiological regulation, and long period s of isolation (Maroney 2003). They have 
been hypothesized to be neurologically more susceptible to both positive and negative 
environmental factors (Krugers et al. 2016). 
In turn, NICU parents have a significantly higher rate of depression, anxiety, and 
PTSD, as well as generally higher levels of psychological stress (Roque et al. 2017). The 
transition to parenthood often happens in an abrupt, unexpected, and traumatic man-
ner for these parents. They may struggle with parental self-efficacy because they are 
often dependent on NICU staff to care for their infant. They must adjust to caring for a 
medically vulnerable infant and communicate with multiple medical providers (Vance 
et al. 2020), and they may have fears of possibly hurting their infant themselves. NICU

infant attachment (Scala et al. 2021). Deve lopmental care practices are interventions 
that aim at decreasing infant pain and stress, providing appropriate sensory stimula-
tion, improving parental presence and self-efficacy, and familiarizing parents with pre-
mature infant communication and needs (Scala et al. 2021). As an example, promoting 
and facilitating SSC between parent and infant is part of developmental care practices. 
SSC has proved to be highly beneficial, with significant health benefits specifically for 
premature infants (Campbell-Yeo et al. 2015). Benefits of SSC for premature infants 
include reduced mortality, improved physiology (thermoregulation and cardiovascu-
lar stability), improved behaviors (breastfeeding initiation, sleep), less procedural dis-
comfort, and improved neurodevelopment (Campbell-Yeo et al. 2015). 
A number of formal programs are available, each of which utilizes a different com-
bination of interventions to improve infant and parental outcomes for NICU families. 
The Newborn Individualized Developmental Care and Assessment Program, Family 
Nurture Intervention, Mother-Infant Transaction Program, Creating Opportunities for 
Parent Empowerment, Auditory Tactile Visual Vestibular Interventions, NICU Family 
Support Core Curriculum, and Close Collaboration With Parents are among the more 
established and studied formal NICU programs (Scala et al. 2021). Despite advances 
in addressing infant and parental distress in the NICU, a significant challenge remains 
for identifying interventions that can support parental involvement in the NICU, sup-
port infant development, and optimize preterm neurodevelopmental outcomes (Au-
cott et al. 2002).

Fathers 
Fathers and partners play a crucial—yet less well understood—role in infants’ psy -
chosocial and neurobiological development. At the same time, there is increasing rec-
ognition that the perinatal period is a vulnerable time for fathers’ mental health. Two 
meta-analyses found an 8%–10% prevalence rate of paternal depression (with the 
highest incidence between 3 and 6 months postpartum) compared with the 4% prev-
alence rate of depression in men in the general population (Cameron et al. 2016; Paul-
son and Bazemore 2010). A meta-analysis of studies from 12 countries underscored the

paternal anxiety during this time (Lieferman et al. 2021). The rate of paternal anxiety 
during perinatal period was estimated to be 10.7%, which is a significant increase from

in the transition to parenthood, and there may be a correlation between paternal and 
maternal depression (de Magistris et al. 2013; Paulson and Bazemore 2010). 
Fathers can be key attachment figures, an d a positive perception of the parenting 
role is consistently associated with patern al caregiving activities and interpersonal 
warmth (Lucassen et al. 2011). As with maternal involvement, paternal involvement 
begins prior to birth; for example, internal parental representations during the mother’s 
pregnancy are positively associated with later child development (Maas et al. 2012; 
Vreeswijk et al. 2014). After birth, paternal caregiving is particularly important in in-

much to learn about the father–infant relationship, particularly in early infancy (Ram-

LGBTQ+ Parents 
A similar need for further study is in the ro le of other nonbirth parents, such as for 
LGBTQ+ families. A steadily increasing number of same-gender and nonbinary cou-
ples are building families and raising children, and this number is likely to continue to 
grow (American Academy of Pediatrics Committee on Psychosocial Aspects of Child 
and Family Health 2013). According to the U.S. Census, between 2 million and 3.7 mil-
lion children under age 18 have an LGBTQ+ parent, and approximately 200,000 of 
these children are being raised by a same-sex couple (Family Equality Council 2017). 
Extensive evidence supports the lack of relationship between parents’ sexual ori -
entation and children’s emotional, psychosocial, and behavioral development (Farr et 
al. 2010; Golombok and Badger 2010; Stacey and Biblarz 2001; Tasker 2005; Wainright 
et al. 2004). In contrast, many studies have attested to the normal development of chil-
dren in same-gender families when the ch ild is wanted, the pa rents have a commit-
ment to shared parenting, and the parents have strong economic and social support. 
This has led current researchers to conclude that the same set of factors determine 
healthy child development no matter what the sex or gender of their parents: 
In all, it is now well-established that the adjustment of children and adolescents is best 
accounted for by variations in the quality of the relationships with their parents, the qual-
ity of the relationship between the parents or significant adults in the children’s and ad-
olescents’ lives, and the availability of economic and socio-economic resources. (Lamb 
2012, p. 106)

these facts:

There remains a wide knowledge gap in the study of LGBTQ+ families and their ex-
periences through pregnancy, the transition to parenthood, the experiences of early par-

research is still centered on a heteronormative framework (Charter et al. 2018; Darwin 
and Greenfield 2019). Some research has focused on lesbian experiences of reproduc-
tive and maternity care but very little on the experience of pregnancy and parenting; 
research focusing on the experiences of gay male parents and transgender parents re-
mains scarce (Darwin and Greenfield 2019; Hafford-Letchfield et al. 2019; Tornello 
and Bos 2017; Van Rijn-van Gelderen et al. 2018). In addition, although there has been 
increasing focus on paternal mental health, a heteronormative approach to these data 
risks conflating gender (manhood/fatherhood ) with the role of coparenting or co -
caretaking (Darwin and Greenfield 2019). 
Much remains to be understood in terms of the complexity of family structures, such 
as a lesbian couple with a child who is biologically connected to a nonbirth parent or the 
existence of more than one birth parent within a particular family (Darwin and Green-
field 2019; Goldberg 2006). Despite these limitations, important concepts are emerging. 
For example, recent research has started to examine the experiences of preconception, 
pregnancy, and new motherhood from the perspective of lesbian nonbiological moth-
ers (McKelvey 2014; Wojnar and Katzenmeyer 2014). One descriptive phenomenol -
ogy study concluded that nonbirth lesbian parents experience an overall theme of 
“feeling different” (e.g., legal and biological concerns, losing relationships, feeling in-
complete, and the need to carve out a unique role) that permeate their preconception, 
pregnancy, birth, and early parenting experiences (Wojnar and Katzenmeyer 2014). 
Adoptive Parents 
Adoption is an important area of overlap with the discussion of LGBTQ+ parents and 
a topic of extensive study unto itself. Th ere are approximately 1.5 million adopted 
children living in the United States, or ab out 2% of all U.S. children (Kreider and 
Lofquist 2014). Recent trends in adoption include changes in the demographics of in-
fants and children over the past two decades. International adoptions into the United 
States peaked with nearly 23,000 per year in both 2004 and 2005. Due to various legal 
and political changes, by 2019, just under 3,000 children were adopted into the United 
States (U.S. Department of State Bureau of Consular Affairs 2020). Increasingly, these 
children tend to be older and may have had more placements in foster care or orphan-
ages prior to adoption. The number of children in foster care in the United States has 
increased slightly over the past decade, although it was decreasing from 2009 to 2012. 
In 2019, there were a record high 66,000 adop tions out of foster care. In 2018, of the

Like the many constellations of parents, adopted children comprise a heterogenous 
group. Preadoption experiences and degree of maltreatment or neglect, age at adop -
tion, length of time exposed to adversities prior to adoption, and in-utero exposures to 
substances all have a direct impact on child outcome (Harwood et al. 2013). Adoption 
type could be associated wi th some of these factors; for instance, children adopted 
through international or foster care pathwa ys show poorer school outcomes and in -
creased health care needs that are at least partially related to differences in preadoption 
placement settings (Harwood et al. 2013). However, most adopted children form se-
cure attachments with their adoptive pa rents and do well developmentally (van 
Londen et al. 2007). As with the general infant and young child population, the quality 
of parent–child relationships can at least partially mediate the effects of adverse pre-
and postnatal adversities and lead to better social and cognitive development (Stams 
et al. 2002). A study by Niemann and Weiss (2012) evaluated factors affecting attach-
ment 6 months after international adoption for children who were adopted late in the 
first or second year of life. Children wi th fewer placements prior to adoption and 
lower stress levels after adoption (as measured by basal salivary cortisol) were more 
likely to have attachment security with their adoptive mothers (Niemann and Weiss 
2012). It is hypothesized that a lower infant stress level may be a protective factor in 
promoting bonding with the adoptive moth er, and, alternatively, responsive care 
from the mother may buffer HPA axis dysregulation, leading to a lower stress level in 
the infant who is developing an attachment bond with the adoptive mother. 
As with all parent–infant dyads, the adoptee’s temperament can play a role in the 
dynamics of the relationship with the parent. Adopted children with a more difficult 
temperament have lower social and cognitive development and higher level of inter-
nalizing and externalizing behavior problems at age 7 (Stams et al. 2002). Multiple 
studies have evaluated short-  and long-term predictors of development in adoptive 
infants and children. Maternal sensitivity appears to be the strongest factor in promot-
ing secure attachment and positive social -emotional development, although respon-
sive and flexible parenting in general and adult attachme nt style are also factors 
(Roberson 2006). Maternal sensitivity is important both during early childhood and as 
children grow into adolescence and can help maintain a secure attachment. A large, 
longitudinal study of early infant international adoptees in the Netherlands also found

Conclusion 
As discussed, pregnancy and the postpartum period are critical times for both infant 
and parental development. Although this is  a time of increased risk for the onset or 
exacerbation of mental illness, it is also an important period for the developing fetus 
and infant in terms of stress response, emotional regulation, and developing a sense 
of security in relationships, setting the stage for subsequent emotional and neurocog-
nitive development. By observing and addr essing parent–infant relationship distur-
bances and challenges, medical and mental health providers caring for women during

this vulnerable time can have a long-term positive impact for the families in their care

Neurobiological evidence exists for alterations of HPA axis activity, decreased hip-
pocampal size, increased amygdala reactivity, and stress response in infants exposed 
to stress in utero. Although genetics and stress exposure are important factors, research 
in both animal models and humans has shown that physical contact and proximity and 
parental nurturing behaviors can lead to epigenetic changes that can override these 
heritable traits. Mental health conditions may interfere with these nurturing behaviors, 
but early detection of any parent–infant rela tional difficulties and appropriate inter-
vention can lead to better self-regulation in the infant, encourage healthy attachment 
relationships, and result in improved psyc hoemotional well-being in infants as they 
grow and develop. 
Mental health clinicians can help parents by observing and understanding the nu-
ances of parent–infant interactions and by exploring parent perceptions of the infant 
and of themselves as parents. This process is especially salient in the setting of preg-
nancy and postpartum mood or anxiety disorders. Important areas to notice include 
the quality of physical contact, gaze, and vocalizations between the parent and infant, 
as well as the affective tone, mutual coregulation, and any mismatches and repairs 
within the back and forth of interactions. 
Several other populations may also requir e additional support. Parents who are 
members of racial minority groups are subject to health care disparities that are pro-
nounced in terms of perinatal care and peripartum outcomes and are raising children 
within the context of the additional stressor s associated with racist political and cul-
tural structures. Parents of infants who require care in the NICU are faced with addi-
tional pressures and anxieties associated with worries about the health of their child, 
as well as the logistical and emotional difficulties of having a child with a prolonged 
hospital stay and potentially continued medical complexities. Parents who have a his-
tory of trauma and poor attachment with their own primary caregivers are more likely 
to struggle. Parents may need support around having realistic expectations for them-
selves and their infants at different developmental stages. 
Other parental groups that may have un ique needs require additional study. We 
know that fathers are at increased risk for depression after the birth of a child, yet little 
is known regarding the best way to screen for these difficulties and to provide services 
successfully. LGBTQ+ couples who become parents have rarely been the focus of stud-
ies looking at attachment and early adaptation to the role of parent, although research 
has demonstrated that children raised in same-sex households are at no increased risk 
for emotional and psychiatric difficulties. There is little research on attachment in fam-
ilies with transgender or nonbinary parents. Adopted infants and children and their 
parents are another important population to keep in mind when screening for parental 
mental health concerns and addressing infa nt mental health and attachment in new 
parents. Ongoing research into the needs of these parents will enable us to ensure that 
the care we provide to families is data informed and culturally responsive. 
Attachment and other aspects of the parent–infant relationship are rich fields with 
many nuances beyond what we have been able to cover in this chapter. Although the 
nature of clinical work focuses on identifying parents and families at increased need 
for support, we can be encouraged and hopeful about the opportunities to collaborate 
with families to remediate these difficulties and set infants on a healthier developmen-
