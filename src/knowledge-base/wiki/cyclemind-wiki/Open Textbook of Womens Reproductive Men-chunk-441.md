Management during pregnancy. Preconception valproate levels can help guide 
dosage adjustments in pregnancy, and the lowest effective dosage should be used. The 
metabolism of valproate is complex because it is metabolized by glucuronidation and 
oxidation. Valproate is also highly protein bound; its pharmacological activity is, how-
ever, dependent on the free or unbound drug, which is able to cross the blood-brain 
barrier. Importantly, although an increase in the clearance of valproate and an overall 
decrease in plasma concentration of the medication have been observed in late preg-
nancy, no significant changes have been found in the unbound (active) concentration 
of the medication. To monitor valproate levels accurately during pregnancy, both free 
and total plasma levels should be measured (Deligiannidis et al. 2014). 
Carbamazepine 
Carbamazepine is an antiepileptic drug that has traditionally been considered an al-
ternative agent in the treatment of BD rather than as a first-line mood stabilizer. Effi-
cacy has been established in acute mania, with some evidence to support its use in 
maintenance treatment. Carbamazepine has limited evidence to support its efficacy in 
acute depression (Yatham et al. 2018). Most experts agree that carbamazepine should 
be used in pregnancy only when clearly clinically indicated above other alternatives, 
given its teratogenicity, less established efficacy profile, significant drug-drug interac-
tions, and associated risk of neonatal hemorrhage (Yonkers et al. 2004). 
Early pregnancy. Carbamazepine exposure prenatal ly has been associated with 
5%–8% risk for major malformation, which is higher than the baseline risk of 3%–5% 
(Campbell et al. 2014; Tomson et al. 2018; Vajda et al. 2016). A recent report from an 
international antiepileptic drug registry showed that major malformation risk with 
carbamazepine exposure appears to be dose dependent, with more substantial risk at 
doses >700 mg/day (4.5% vs. 7.2%) (Tomson et al. 2018). Although overall malforma-
tion rates with first-trimester carbamazepine exposure are lower than corresponding 
rates with valproate, it is also considered a human teratogen (Yonkers et al. 2004). The 
most common malformations associated with exposure are neural tube defects, cra -
niofacial abnormalities, and cardiac and urinary tract defects. Combination of carba-
mazepine with another antiepileptic drug further increases the risk of malformations 
(Margulis et al. 2019; Yonkers et al. 2004). 
Obstetrical outcomes. Carbamazepine has been associated with small but signifi-
cant reductions in birth weight and head circumference (Yonkers et al. 2004). 
Neonatal outcomes. Carbamazepine use in late pregnancy has been associated 
with transient hepatic toxi city, coagulopathy, vitamin K deficiency, and low birth 
weight (Epstein et al. 2014; Yonkers et al. 2004). 
Long-term effects. Studies examining neurodevelopmental outcomes in infants 
exposed to carbamazepine in utero have yielded mixed results and have not consis -
tently shown a pattern of associated cognitive dysfunction (Epstein et al. 2014).
