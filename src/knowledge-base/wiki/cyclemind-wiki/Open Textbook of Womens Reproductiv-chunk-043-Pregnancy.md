General Treatment Principles 
Fourth Trimester 
Recognize the Enormity of the Transition 
The postpartum period, defined as the 12 weeks after delivery, is an important time for 
a new mother and her family and can be considered a fourth trimester (Paladine et al. 
2019). The weeks following birth are a critical period for a woman and her infant, set-
ting the stage for long-term health and well-being (American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists 2018a). The postpartum period is a time of significant challenge as 
the woman must adapt to hormonal and physical changes, recover from delivery, ex-
perience a change in family roles and responsibilities, and endure sleep deprivation— 
all while trying to care for and nourish a newborn (Spelke and Werner 2018). 
In recognition of the importance of th e postpartum period, in 2018 the American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) issued updated recommendations 
for postpartum care, emphasizing the need to establish a postpartum care plan during 
pregnancy (American College of Obstetrici ans and Gynecologists 2018a). According 
to these recommendations, postpartum care should become an ongoing process rather 
than a single encounter, with services and support tailored to each woman’s individual 
needs. The first postpartum visit should be within 3 weeks after birth, as opposed to 
the common practice of having the first follow-up visit at 6 weeks. The ACOG also rec-
ognizes the breadth and enormity of the transition into parenting and recommends that 
a comprehensive postpartum visit include a full assessment of ph ysical, social, and 
psychological well-being, including the domains of mood and emotional well-being; 
infant care and feeding; sexuality, contraception, and birth spacing; sleep and fatigue; 
physical recovery from birth; chronic disease management; and health maintenance. 
Recognize That This Is a Time of Mental Health Vulnerability 
The postpartum period can be a challengin g time for many new mothers (and their 
loved ones) in terms of their mental heal th, including the development or exacerba -
tion of psychiatric disorders. The etiology of postpartum psychiatric vulnerability is 
a complex interplay of psychological, biological, and social factors in addition to un -
derlying genetic and environmental contribu tions (Meltzer-Brody et al. 2018). Post -
partum disorders commonly include depression and anxiety but can, more rarely,

Infant Feeding and Lactation 
According to the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (2018), in the United 
States, 83% of mothers have ever breastfe d. More than half (57%) of mothers are 
breastfeeding at 6 months, and just over one-third of mothers (35.9%) are breastfeed-
ing at 12 months. Mothers younger than age 30 are somewhat less likely to breastfeed 
(80.0%) than older mothers (86.3%). Further disparities in breastfeeding rates include 
fewer non-Hispanic Black infants ever having been breastfed (74.0%) compared with 
Hispanic infants (82.9%) and non-Hispanic white infants (86.6%). Moreover, infants 
born into families with low socioeconomic status are less likely to be breastfed, with 
infants who receive the Special Supplemental Nutrition Program for Women, Infants, 
and Children (WIC) less likely to be breastfed (75.5%) than children who are eligible 
but do not receive WIC (89.0%) or infants who are ineligible for WIC (92.7%). 
Breastfeeding declined in the middle of the twentieth century, but by the 1970s there 
was renewed interest in high-income countries because of the natural foods movement 
and in low-income countries because of co ncerns about increased infant mortality 
from improper sterilization for formula (McIntyre et al. 2018). Despite substantial pub-
lic relations campaigns since then (such as “breast is best” and the Baby-Friendly Hos-
pital Initiative; McIntyre et al. 2018), only 37% of children younger than age 6 months 
are exclusively breastfed in low- and middle-income countries, with even shorter du-
ration in high-income countries.

Recognize That Decisions Around Infant Feeding Must 
Balance the Infant’s Needs With the Mother’s Well-Being 
Decisions regarding infant feeding, particularly breastfeeding, highlight the complex-
ities around optimizing the well-being of both the mother and the infant. Although 
breastfeeding is widely known to have clea r health benefits, the medical, emotional, 
and logistical reasons that women may choose not to breastfeed are less commonly dis-
cussed. Several factors may affect the mental health and well-being (not to mention the 
preferences) of the mother and should also be taken into account. Breastfeeding does 
have demonstrated benefits in improving mental health for some women, but for other 
women it may contribute to the incidence and severity of anxiety or depressive symp-
toms; these women may choose not to breast feed or terminate breastfeeding early

(McIntyre et al. 2018). As discussed later, women may choose not to breastfeed at all

Breastfeeding has been reported to be beneficial for both mother and baby. Studies 
have shown that breastfeeding has immediate benefits for the health and survival of 
the infant, particularly in low- and middle-income countries, where mortality rates for 
breastfed infants are lower; in meta-analyses of studies completed in these countries, 
three studies showed that infants younger than 6 months who were not breastfed had 
3.5–4.1 times higher rates of mortality than those who received any breastmilk. In high-
income countries, breastfeeding was associated with a 36% reduction in sudden infant 
deaths and a 58% decrease in necrotizing enterocolitis (Victora et al. 2016). 
Breastfeeding also improves morbidity sequelae from infections; this benefit is due 
to the molecular composition of breast milk itself, which has both immunological and 
antibacterial properties (Allen and Hector 2005). Studies have shown that breastfed 
babies have lower rates of diarrhea, respiratory tract infections, otitis media, and other 
infections and a decreased risk of exposure to pathogens from formula preparation, all 
significant risks to infants in low- and middle-income countries (Shamir 2016; Victora 
et al. 2016). Studies have also demonstrated  long-term benefits in children who are 
breastfed, with reductions in overweight and obesity (Shamir 2016; Victora et al. 2016). 
Some studies also show improved intellectual and motor development and increased 
intelligence in breastfed children (Shamir 2016; Victora et al. 2016), although demo -
graphic differences among families who choose to breastfeed may account for this. 
Breastfeeding has also been shown to ha ve both immediate and longer-term pro -
tective effects for the mother. The initiation of breastfeeding immediately after deliv-
ery stimulates the release of oxytocin, which helps contract the uterus, expel the 
placenta, and reduce postpartum bleeding (Victora et al. 2016). Furthermore, the com-
bined effects of oxytocin, prolactin, and cortisol, which are released during lactation, 
are reported to have both antidepressant and anxiolytic effects and may help decrease 
rates of postpartum depression and anxiet y (Godfrey and Lawrence 2010). Lastly, 
breastfeeding increases the period of amenorrhea, thus aiding in child spacing (Victora 
et al. 2016). Longer-term benefits include reduced risk of breast and ovarian cancers, 
type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, rheumatoid arthritis, and osteoarthritis (God-
frey and Lawrence 2010). A dose-dependent preventive association of breastfeeding 
with premenopausal and postmenopausal breast cancer has been reported; according 
to one meta-analysis, breastfeeding for 12 months decreased the incidence of breast 
cancer between 3% and 4% (Victora et al. 2016). Table 9–1 lists excess risks of not breast-
feeding for the health of both the child and the mother.

Support Choices Around Infant Feeding Nonjudgmentally 
Mothers who cannot or choose not to breastfeed may experience both external shame 
and internal guilt about this decision (Lakshman et al. 2009; Lee 2007; Thomson et al. 
2015). Women with depression, anxiety, and self-esteem issues, for example, may be 
more vulnerable to interpreting lactation and nursing difficulties as personal failures. 
Lack of breastfeeding can be experienced as not being a good mother or failing to live 
up to the ideals of womanhood (Taylor and Wallace 2012). Feelings of inadequacy and 
isolation may occur and can prevent a mother  from seeking help, especially because 
many mothers, if they start to use formula,  feel judged by health care professionals 
(Thomson et al. 2015; Schmied et al. 2011). Women who do not breastfeed, regardless 
of their reasons, are at higher risk of encountering shaming and stigma if their social 
network or their medical providers disagree with their decision (McIntyre et al. 2018). 
Clinicians can support women’s choices around lactation by taking a nonjudgmen-
tal approach that acknowledges these challenges while providing education about the 
risks and benefits, as well as helping to engage patients’ support systems and access 
to professional intervention when available. It is important to support each mother in 
her decision-making process regardless of the decision made. The clinician’s role is to 
enhance problem solving and flexibility around breastfeeding. Creating a space in 
which the mother can think about the risks and benefits of breastfeeding can help her 
come to her own decision. Often helpful are discussions around supplemental for -
mula use instead of complete cessation of breastfeeding, or the role of the mother’s 
partner in assisting with feedings. Directing a mother to obtain lactation support, 
whether through a group or a consultant, can be a helpful intervention as well. By di-
rectly addressing any negative feelings a mother has, clinicians can help mitigate the 
impact of these feelings. In addition, while discussing the benefits and potential dis -
advantages of breastfeeding with women, clinicians must be open to the psychosocial, 
cultural, and personal determinants affecting their decisions. 
Sleep 
Recognize That Postpartum Sleep Deprivation Is 
Common and Increases Risk of Psychiatric Illness 
Sleep is often the most critical self-care issue a new parent faces and arguably the most 
important modifiable risk factor for post partum psychiatric disorders. Postpartum 
sleep deficiency is common due to both postdelivery physiological changes and the 
mother’s need to care for the newborn (Wilkerson and Uhde 2018). Preexisting sleep 
problems, such as apnea and restless legs syndrome, are often exacerbated during this

of psychiatric illness, or both. 
Although it is universally recognized th at postpartum women get poor sleep, the 
etiology, specific sleep parameters affected, and numerous physical and emotional se-
quelae of sleep deficiency in  the postpartum period have  only recently begun to be 
comprehensively studied. Women in the first several weeks postpartum have been 
shown to have poorer sleep on several different subjective and objective measures, in-
cluding sleep quality, sleep latency, sleep efficiency, sleep medication usage, and day-
time dysfunction (Christian et al. 2019; Wilkerson and Uhde 2018). Sleep deficiencies 
in the general population have broad consequences, including increasing the risk for 
chronic illness and all-cause mortality (Carroll et al. 2019). In the postpartum popula-
tion in particular, sequelae include increa sed risk of PPD (McEvoy et al. 2019; Wilk -
erson and Uhde 2018), anxiety disorders (Okun et al. 2018), emotional dysregulation 
(Christian et al. 2019), interpersonal problems (Christian et al. 2019), and suicidal ide-
ation (Sit et al. 2015), all of which present the possibility of much greater impairment 
and distress when they occur during the postpartum period.
