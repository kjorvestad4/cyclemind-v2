Summary 
Infertility is a common problem in women, and mental health professionals serve in 
a wide variety of roles in the evaluation and treatment of psychiatric conditions in this 
population. They perform indi vidual psychotherapy in the private practice setting 
and take on consultation-liaison and embedd ed mental health roles within fertility 
clinics. Mental health professionals need to be aware of the unique relationships be -
tween common causes of infertility in wo men and comorbid psychiatric conditions. 
Unique aspects of these conditions need to be integrated into psychological and psy-
chiatric treatment approaches. Mental health professionals also need to be aware of 
the potential for fertility medications to destabilize mood in women. Future research 
should focus on the identification of patients at greatest risk for this mood destabili-
zation, as well as the adaptation and development of evidence-based psychotherapy 
and psychopharmacology treatments for mood and anxiety disorders in this popula-
tion. Psychopharmacology research should continue the investigation of effects of 
psychiatric medications on the menstrual cycle; fertility treatments, including egg and 
sperm quality; implantation physiology; and pregnancy outcomes. 
Perinatal Loss 
Perinatal loss is a common and distressing experience for many women, and it can be 
associated with significant psychological and psychiatric sequelae. The goals of this 
section are to review the epidemiology, clinical features, mental health impacts, and 
treatment of perinatal loss. 
Ms. J is a 38-year-old Black nurse who has experienced re current pregnancy loss. Ini-
tially, she was able to conceive without fertility treatments; however, at ages 32 and 34 
years she experienced spontaneous abortions at 10 and 12 weeks of pregnancy, respec-
tively. She says that she decided to “take a break” from trying to have a child after the 
second miscarriage because, although she had “coped well” after the first miscarriage, 
after the second she experienced her “first depression.” She had loss of interest in activ-
ities, experienced problems with memory and concentration, and found her work ex -
tremely challenging for several months be cause of a “foggy head.” Although she did 
not have suicidal ideation, she did feel hopelessness and helplessness. Ms. J did not seek 
treatment for her MDD, and she says that the symptoms slowly resolved over 2 years. 
When she turned 38, Ms. J and her husband pursued IVF, and she became pregnant 
after the first cycle. She wa s anxious throughout the firs t trimester, but when she 
reached week 14 of her pregnancy, she began “to have a little hope” that this pregnancy 
would go smoothly. However, at week 22, she experienced the sudden onset of intense 
back pain followed by the passing of bright red blood. She was rushed to the hospital, 
where an ultrasound revealed  placental abruption and intrauterine fetal demise, and 
she subsequently underwent an emergency ce sarean section. In the months that fol -
lowed, Ms. J experienced a return of her symptoms of depression. She says she “could 
have handled that because I had it before,” but this time the loss of energy and interest 
were accompanied by intense irritability. She had difficulty sleeping, with nightmares 
about the blood loss. During the day she experienced repeated flashbacks of the day of 
the placental abruption. Always known for her calm demeanor in the past, she reported 
having severe anxiety, with racing heart, shortness of breath, and sweating.
