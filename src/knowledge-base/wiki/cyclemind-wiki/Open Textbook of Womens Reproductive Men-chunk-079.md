In some studies, personality disorders have been found to be more common  in  
women with PMDD (Sassoon et al. 2011), but other studies have not found this asso-
ciation (Critchlow et al. 2001). Several studies, however, have found that a wide range 
of personality traits, especially neurotic ism, are more common in women with PMS 
and PMDD than in healthy control subjects (Fernández et al. 2019b); neuroticism traits 
have been found to be related to sympto m severity and seroto nin transporter poly-
morphisms in women with PMDD (Gingnell et al. 2010). Other commonly associated 
traits include obsessions (Critchlow et al. 2001), harm avoidance, novelty seeking, and 
reward dependence (Freeman et al. 1995). Higher levels of trait anger have been found 
in women with PMDD and PMS who attempted suicide (Ducasse et al. 2016). 
Social Considerations 
Compared with control subjects, women with PMDD often report higher levels of psy-
chosocial stress (Fontana and Palfai 1994),  including work stress (Namavar Jahromi 
et al. 2011) or lack of work and lower so cioeconomic status (de Carvalho et al. 2018). 
As previously mentioned (see “Epidemiology”), history of trauma has also been asso-
ciated with risk for PMDD and is considered an important potential social factor in its 
etiology (Eisenlohr-Moul et al. 2016; Perkonigg et al. 2004). Social stresses may lead to 
greater vulnerability to stress responses, and studies of women with PMDD report al-
terations in hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis function compared with control sub-
jects (Hantsoo and Epperson 2015).

Antidepressant Medications 
Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors. SSRIs are considered the first-line treat-
ment for PMDD. (When treating a woman of reproductive age, even if that patient is 
not planning a pregnancy, clinicians should always consider the safety of proposed 
medications for future pregnancies.) More than 30 well-designed double-blind ran -
domized controlled trials (RCTs) have demonstrated the efficacy and safety of SSRIs for 
the treatment of PMDD (Marjoribanks et al. 2013). Therefore, much of the treatment of 
PMDD centers around the use of SSRIs. Within this class of medications, sertraline, flu-
oxetine, paroxetine, escitalopram, and citalopram have been specifically studied and 
deemed equally effective as treatment options; other SSRIs, however, should also be 
considered viable options as well. 
Because of the decrease in serotonergic function during the luteal phase of the men-
strual cycle, some patients diagnosed with PMDD are treated with SSRIs only during 
the 2 weeks prior to menses. Several studies have shown that this type of luteal-phase 
dosing is as effective as continuous dosi ng in reducing the symptoms of PMDD and 
PMS (Shah et al. 2008). Another treatment st rategy for PMDD is to administer SSRIs 
only with symptom onset. Several small but well-designed RCTs have shown that 
symptom-onset dosing is an effective st rategy in reducing PMDD symptoms, with 
minimal treatment days (Steiner and Li 20 13; Yonkers et al. 2005b). Interestingly, in 
this research no symptom worsening was noted with discontinuation of treatment, 
and even with short-acting SSRIs such as paroxetine, withdrawal phenomena were 
not notable. Although there is more evidence, and therefore more support, for contin-
uous-dosing strategies for SSRIs, both luteal-phase dosing and symptom-onset dos -
ing represent reasonable strategies involving shorter treatment periods (with shorter 
exposure to side effects) for appropriate patients. 
Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors. Much like SSRIs, the serotonin -
norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are treatment options for PMDD due to 
their inhibitory effect on serotoninreuptake. Research has focused on two SNRIs, ven-
lafaxine and duloxetine. A few studies have demonstrated that with continuous dos-
ing, symptom remission rates for these two agents are superior to those of placebo 
(Freeman et al. 2001; Mazza et al. 2008; Ra mos et al. 2009). Limited data exist on the 
intermittent use of SNRIs for PMDD. One study found intermittent dosing of venla -
faxine to be efficacious in the treatment of PMDD, but some women experienced mild 
to moderate withdrawal-related symptoms (Cohen et al. 2004). Therefore, SNRIs rep-
resent an effective treatment strategy for PMDD but may be best used in patients who 
prefer continuous dosing. 
Tricyclic antidepressants. Limited data exist to support the use of tricyclic antide-
pressants (TCAs) in the treatment of PMDD. In two studies with small numbers of sub-
jects, clomipramine was shown to be supe rior to placebo in reducing premenstrual 
irritability and dysphoria; in one of those studies, both continuous and intermittent 
dosing appeared to be effective (Sundblad et al. 1992, 1993). Despite the limited data 
supporting its use, as well as the less desi rable side effect profile for TCAs, clomip -

Anxiolytics 
There has been some interest in benzodiazepines and the serotonin agonist buspirone 
in the treatment of PMDD. Although the benzodiazepine alprazolam did show im -
provement of symptoms compared with placebo, the response was not as robust as 
that from treatment with fluoxetine (Diegoli et al. 1998). Buspirone, however, did not 
show a statistically significant improvement in limited research (Landén et al. 2001). 
Both agents can increase sedation and somnolence, which is an undesired effect in a 
patient with PMDD who might be experiencing such symptoms as part of the under-
lying disorder. 
Hormonal Interventions 
Oral contraception. Oral contraceptive pills (OCPs) have been explored as a treat-
ment option based on the theory that cessation of ovulation would eliminate symp -
toms of PMS and PMDD. However, the evidence of any type of OCPs for treatment of 
PMDD is limited. In 2006, the FDA approved an OCP containing drospirenone for the 
treatment of PMDD. Drospirenone is a synt hetic progestin derived from spironolac-
tone rather than from the more commonly used 19-nortestosterone, which has been 
postulated to be associated with mood  symptoms (Rapkin 2003 ). One multicenter, 
double-blind, placebo-controlled study evaluated the use of monophasic drospirenone 
3 mg and ethinyl estradiol 20 µg administered for 24 days, followed by four inactive 
pills, in women with PMDD. They found that the treatment group’s response rate to 
this formulation was similar to that of women taking SSRIs. Of note, premenstrual 
physical symptoms improved with drospire none as well (Yonkers et al. 2005a). Al -
though these results are encouraging, it sh ould be noted that studies assessing dro -
spirenone have had large placebo effects and have often lost large numbers of patients 
to follow-up (Lopez et al. 2012). Additional high-quality research is desired in this area. 
In addition to the choice of agent, continuous versus cyclical dosing has also been 
studied. Some data have shown a benefit with continuous administration (without a 
hormone-free interval) of a formulation of the monophasic 19-nortestosterone deriv-
ative levonorgestrel and ethinyl estradiol in  treating both physical and mental pre -
menstrual symptoms, compared with placeb o (Halbreich et al. 2012). Other reviews 
have shown inconsistencies in symptom reduction with this type of treatment (Free-
man et al. 2012). 
OCPs are not without risks. Side effects that may impair quality of life, such as de-
creased sexual interest, can occur (Sanders et al. 2001). In 2012, the FDA made a safety 
announcement warning that drospirenone ma y be associated with an increased risk 
of thromboembolism (U.S. F ood and Drug Administrati on 2012). Vaginal bleeding 
has been reported in patients given continuous dosing of levonorgestrel. Therefore,

Gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists. The GnRH agonist class of medica -
tions includes agents such as leuprolide and goserelin. GnRH agonists downregulate 
GnRH receptors, which leads to a decrease in both luteinizing and follicle-stimulating 
hormones and eventual decreases in production of ovarian estrogen and progesterone. 
That these hormonal changes result in anovulation, amenorrhea, and a menopause-
type state is the basis of this type of treatment for PMS and PMDD. Studies evaluating 
GnRH agonists have shown them to be more effective than placebo in reducing PMDD 
symptoms (Wyatt et al. 2004). 
Clearly, one issue with GnRH agonists is that by creating a menopausal state, they 
cause women to experience signs and symp toms of hypoestrogenism, such as hot  
flashes and night sweats, and present a ri sk for decreased bone mineral density 
(Dawood 1994). Therefore, some research has investigated the use of “add-back” es -
trogen and progesterone for women receiving GnRH agonists. Although research on 
this topic is limited, a small study demonstrated that when estrogen and progesterone 
were added back to the treatment, some symptoms of PMDD did occur. Addition of 
estrogen alone appeared to be most effective for prevention of menopausal symptoms 
without PMDD symptoms; however, this is not considered a safe option due to the risk 
for endometrial hyperplasia (Segebladh et al. 2009). Due to the complications associ-
ated with use of GnRH agonists and the unclear implications of long-term treatment 
with them, they are not considered a first-line treatment for PMDD and may come into 
play only as a temporary treatment for severe treatment-resistant cases. 
Danazol. Danazol is a synthetic steroid with androgenic and antigonadotropic prop-
erties that suppresses ovulation. It has been shown to reduce premenstrual physical 
and mood symptoms in some cases (Hahn et al. 1995). However, this form of treatment 
is complicated by multiple side effects, such as decreased high-density lipoprotein cho-
lesterol, weight gain, hirsutism, and acne. Consequently, as with GnRH agonists, da-
nazol is not recommended as a first-line treatment for PMDD. 
Spironolactone. Spironolactone is an aldosteron e receptor antagonist and potas -
sium-sparing diuretic. The best evidence for spironolactone appears to be in the treat-
ment of premenstrual physical symptoms, such as swelling, breast tenderness, and food 
cravings. It has also been shown to reduce irritability and depression (Wang et al. 1995). 
Because this medication may increase potassium levels, careful monitoring for hyper-
kalemia is necessary. 
Bromocriptine and cabergoline. Both bromocriptine and cabergoline are dopa -
mine agonists that reduce prolactin levels. In doing so, they have been found to be ef-
fective in reducing premenstrual mastalgia. Additional symptom reduction has been 
inconsistent with bromocriptine. Cabergoline has been noted to have fewer side effects 
than bromocriptine, which has been associated with nausea, vomiting, and headaches

Nonpharmacological Somatic Treatments 
In all treatment efforts, a careful assessment with the patient about risk versus potential 
benefit is crucial. General health-oriented interventions with very low risk may in -
clude minimizing use of caffeine, salt, and alcohol, especially during the premenstrual 
week. Increasing other positive behaviors, such as sleep and exercise, is also recom -
mended. Depending on symptom severity, interventions such as scheduling changes 
that reduce stress during the premenstrual week and other nonpharmacological ap -
proaches may be useful. A great amount of interest has been shown in dietary modifi-
cations for the treatment of PMDD. Areas th at have been explored include intake of 
soy isoflavones, fat, protein, and carbohydrates. Some observational studies show as-
sociations between diet and PMS or PMDD, but the evidence is not consistent (Hashim 
et al. 2019; Houghton et al. 2017, 2018). Although some open-label studies have found 
improvements in symptoms with dietary modifications, others have not, and random-
ized evidence is lacking (Esmaeilpour et al. 2019; Houghton et al. 2019). 
Behavioral and Other Nonpharmacological Treatments 
Support for psychotherapeutic treatments for PMDD remains inconclusive but prom-
ising. CBT has the most support, but many studies have had limitations in study design, 
including use of retrospective versus pr ospective PMDD diagnosis and existence of 
mainly open trials (Lustyk et al. 2009). In a randomized comparison study, women with 
prospectively confirmed PMDD were randomly assigned to 10 sessions of CBT alone, 
fluoxetine 20 mg/day alone, or combined CBT and fluoxetine. All groups demonstrated 
improvement in PMDD. Fluoxetine was associated with earlier improvement, and CBT 
was associated with greater maintenance of improvement at 1 year (Hunter et al. 2002). 
Recently, internet-based CBT was shown to be effective in addressing the functional and 
psychological impairments of PMDD (Weise et al. 2019). Although, to date, published 
studies on the use of mindfulness-based stress reduction therapies for PMDD are lack-
ing, one small open study of women with  menstrual-related mood symptoms found 
that participation in a mindfulness-based stress reduction class was associated with sig-
nificant reduction in premenstrual symptoms, including decreased depressive symp-
toms, irritability, anxiety, and mood swings (Bluth et al. 2015). Thus, some data suggest 
efficacy of other low-risk interventions, such as mindfulness, to assist patients in stress 
reduction and coping during periods of illness. 
Acupuncture has also been evaluated in  the treatment of PMDD, and when com-
pared with sham procedures, acupuncture and acupressure are associated with de -
creased PMS and PMDD symptoms in many trials. No studies have yet compared this 
modality with SSRIs, SNRIs, or OCPs (Armour et al. 2018).

Vitamin E has been investigated in PMDD because it may have a role in prostaglan-

Other supplements, such as  magnesium and vitamin B 6, have been studied, with 
inconsistent findings. Some research has indicated a benefit for women with pre -
menstrual symptoms, and some has found no benefit (Whelan et al. 2009). Given the 
limited nature and inconsistencies in the results, it does not appear that these supple-
ments represent a robust management approach at this time. 
Herbal Treatments 
Historically, herbal treatment options have been used for the treatment of PMS and 
PMDD. Although some herbs such as St. John’s wort (Hypericum perforatum) have been 
shown to be somewhat effective for PMS (Canning et al. 2010), they have not shown 
improvements in mood symptoms of PMDD. Chaste tree extract or chasteberry (Vitex 
agnus-castus) has shown some benefit in treating physical and mood-related PMS 
symptoms in multiple double-blind RCTs and in uncontrolled studies (Loch et al. 2000; 
Schellenberg 2001; Verkaik et al. 2017). The postulated mechanism of action for chaste 
tree extract is that it may bind to dopamine receptors and inhibit prolactin (Jarry et al. 
1994) and also activate opioid receptors (Webster et al. 2011). Other promising herbal 
treatment strategies include saffron (Agha-Hosseini et al. 2008) and Ginkgo biloba 
(Ozgoli et al. 2009). Herbal remedies represent an interesting future direction for re -
search, but additional research is needed before treatment recommendations can be 
made (Jang et al. 2014). As with any herbal treatments, which are not under the same 
governmental quality controls as pharmaceuticals, caution must be advised in the pro-
curement and consumption of these products; actual formulations may differ widely 
even within the same brand due to the unregulated nature of the herbal treatment in-
dustry. Additionally, limited guidance is available about advising patients how best to 
administer and dose these supplements. 
Surgery 
In patients with severe PMS and PMDD, for whom no other treatments have been ef-
fective and who do not desire future fertility, surgical oophorectomy has been per -
formed as a last-line treatment. In small studies, oophorectomy with hysterectomy and 
estrogen replacement has been shown to be effective in patients with previously treat-
ment-refractory disease (Cronje et al. 2004). Because this is an irreversible procedure, 
all other treatment options should be exhausted first. Proper discussion—including in-
formed consent and a detailed understanding of the risks of hysterectomy and oopho-

Treatment Challenges: Partial Response, 
Treatment Resistance, and Comorbid Bipolar Disorder 
No studies have been published on treatment-resistant PMDD, despite reports that 40% 
of women do not respond fully to SSRIs or OCPs (Halbreich 2008). For patients with 
poor treatment response to SSRIs and SNRIs, clinicians need to first reevaluate the di-
agnosis and rule out another mood or anxi ety disorder with premenstrual exacerba-
tion as the primary diagnosis, because ma ny of the treatments for PMDD are either 
ineffective or potentially deleterious for pr imary mood or anxiety disorders. For in -
stance, drospirenone-containing OCPs (Peters et al. 2017) and GnRH agonists (Free -
man et al. 1997) are not effective for major depression with premenstrual exacerbation. 
Women with underlying bipolar disorder ar e at risk for antidepressant-induced hy -
pomania or mixed episodes if they are not taking a mood stabilizer (Gitlin 2018; Vik-
torin et al. 2014). 
To date, no RCTs are available to guide treatment of comorbid PMDD and bipolar 
disorder or of bipolar disorder with PME of mood symptoms. Current recommenda-
tions include first maximizing the mood stabilizer and then considering adding an 
OCP (Sepede et al. 2020). Support for this approach includes data from small studies 
of women with bipolar disorder who report  improvement in cyclical mood fluctua -
tions with OCPs (Frey and Minuzzi 2013) and with lamotrigine and OCPs compared 
with other mood stabilizers (Robakis et al. 2015). 
Furthermore, to date no sequenced treatment trials for PMDD have been published. 
Only one small, open-label study has examined the effectiveness of switching from one 
SSRI, if not effective, to citalopram 20–40 mg/day for either the luteal phase or the full 
cycle (Freeman et al. 2002). Clinical improvement, defined as Daily Symptom Rating 
Scale decreases of >50% from baseline, were  reported in 76% of the subjects, and no 
significant differences were found between the full- and half-cycle treatment groups. 
In a double-blind study, adjunctive quetiapine has been shown to reduce mood lability 
and depression in patients with PMDD who had only partial response to SSRIs (Jack-
son et al. 2015). Further studies are needed to guide treatment of partial response and 
treatment resistance.
