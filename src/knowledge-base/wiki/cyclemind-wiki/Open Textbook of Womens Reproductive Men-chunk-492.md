When treating a pregnant woman with a psychotic disorder, one should discuss 
both the risks of the medication treatment and the risks of not treating the mental ill-
ness. It is critical that psychiatrists not overlook the risks of failing to treat a psychotic 
disorder. The benefits of medication, which the woman has likely experienced in the 
past, also should be discussed. Consideration of obstetrical and fetal risk should be re-
framed in the context of the mother–infant pair because the overall health and well -
being of the mother is in the infant’s best interest. Finally, psychiatrists should enhance 
a woman’s decision-making capacity by describing and helping her understand the 
risks and probabilities of various outcomes , rather than relying on her perceptions, 
which may be influenced by the experiences of friends or what is reported in the media 
(Friedman et al. 2018; Miller 2009). 
Considerations of Involuntary Medications 
When a pregnant or postpartum woman is acutely psychotic and refuses treatment de-
spite a clear need for that treatment, the psychiatrist should evaluate that patient’s ca-
pacity for decision-making about psychiatric treatment. This would include whether 
she can appreciate her diagnosis and the consequences, whether she can understand 
relevant information about treatment, whether she is able rationally to manipulate this 
information, and whether she can communicate a clear choice (Friedman et al. 2018). 
The psychiatrist should also collaborate with obstetrical providers, even in situations in 
which the patient is on an inpatient psychiatric unit and requiring forced medications. 
When completing an evaluation for invo luntary treatment in pregnancy, psychia-
trists should be aware of the laws in their jurisdiction, the patient’s diagnosis, the ra-
tionale for treatment, and the reason behind the lack of capacity. It is critical that they 
collaborate with obstetrical providers and have a clear understanding of rational psy-
chopharmacology in pregnancy. The least restrictive alternatives for appropriate 
treatment should be determined. When describing the issues to the court, they should 
clearly identify the risks of no treatment, because courts may only focus on harm from 
medications rather than on the risks of not treating (Friedman et al. 2018). 
Parenting 
Most women with schizophrenia will have at least one pregnancy over a lifetime, and 
many mothers with schizophrenia parent their own children. Although women with 
schizophrenia are likely to care deeply about their children and their responsibilities 
as mothers, as a group they demonstrate significant parenting challenges (Montgom-
ery 2005). On average, women with schizophrenia display less maternal sensitivity to-
ward their infants and are more likely to have infants or toddlers with insecure or 
disorganized attachment patterns (Wan et al. 2008a) than are healthy control subjects.
