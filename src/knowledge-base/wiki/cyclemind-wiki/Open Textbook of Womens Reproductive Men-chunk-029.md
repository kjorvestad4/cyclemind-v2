Estimates of the prevalence of FSDs vary, in part due to variations in the diagnostic 
classification used, methodology used to assess conditions (e.g., some studies do not 
assess distress, which can result in overestimates of dysfunction), and sample charac-
teristics. Notably, comorbidity of FSDs is common (e.g., prolonged experience of sex-
ual pain can reduce sexual desire), and it is important to determine which dysfunction 
is primary when planning treatment (Hayes et al. 2008). Research indicates that dis -
orders of desire and sexual pain are the most common. To date, the largest prevalence 
survey on FSDs among U.S. women found that 10% experienced distressing low de -
sire, 5.5% reported distressing low arousal, and 4.7% reported distressing lack of or -
gasm (Shifren et al. 2008). Middle-aged women more commonly reported distressing 
sexual concerns (14.8% of women ages 45–65 years) compared with younger (10.8%) 
and older (8.9%) women. Sexual pain disorders were not assessed in that survey. 
Sexual Functioning Across the 
Reproductive Lifespan 
Specific biopsychosocial factors and chan ges associated with three reproductive 
stages—puberty, pregnancy and the post partum period, and menopause—may help 
determine women’s sexual functioning or contribute to FSDs. In this section, we dis-
cuss each stage and present rates of and risk factors for FSDs. 
Puberty 
Limited research is available on normal sexual functioning and rates of FSDs in girls 
during puberty, in part due to ethical concerns about assessing sexual function in mi-
nor populations. Also, the measures typically used to assess female sexual function -
ing are validated with adult populations. Much of the extant research focuses on 
sexual behavior and sexual health risk rather than function. However, understanding 
of normative sexual behaviors may help guide psychiatry providers in assessing and 
counseling their young patients.

In general, puberty is seen as the time of onset for sexual thoughts and behavior. Im-
portant developmental tasks of adolescence include acquiring a mature sexual iden-
tity, expressing responsible sexual behavior, and developing capacity for intimate 
relationships. Sexual experimentation, either through masturbation or partnered sex-
ual activity, can begin in early adolesce nce (ages 10–14 years). The physiological 
changes associated with puberty are typically experienced in middle adolescence (15– 
18 years) and accompanied by increased engagement in intimate relationships. Middle 
adolescence is associated with increased engagement in sexual risk-taking behaviors. 
The prefrontal cortex is still developing into age 22 among females, and decision mak-
ing and abstract thinking (which may affect sexual decision making and understand-
ing of risks) are not fully formed among adolescent girls (Tulloch and Kaufman 2013).

Research indicates that youth become aw are of their sexual orientation between 
ages 8 and 11 years. LGBTQ+ youth who experience victimization and lack of social 
support are at greatest risk for mental health concerns, and those who are early in their 
sexual identity development and who have not yet fully disclosed their orientation 
are at highest risk for suicide (Scott and Walsh 2014). For most individuals, gender 
identity will be consolidated by adolescence, although there is evidence of sexual flu-
idity (dynamic changes in one’s same-sex or other-sex desire) across time (Diamond 
2008). Thus, psychiatry practitioners should validate adolescents’ gender identities 
and normalize experiences with fluidity in sexual orientation and exploration. 
Rates 
As noted, research into FSDs during puberty is scarce. However, a Canadian study of 
144 sexually active female adolescents, ages 16–21 years, found that 49.3% reported a 
sexual problem, with difficulties in desire  and orgasm reported most often. Further -
more, 53.5% reported sexual distress associated with the sexual problem. Orgasm 
complaints appear negatively associated with age (perhaps as a result of increased un-
derstanding of sexual activity or increased sexual “skill”), whereas other complaints, 
including dyspareunia, may be positively associated with age (O’Sullivan et al. 2014). 
Risk Factors 
Specific biopsychosocial factors in the context of puberty may determine female sexual 
functioning (Fortenberry 2013; O’Sullivan et al. 2016; Tulloch and Kaufman 2013). For 
example, puberty can be experienced as a social role transition associated with the onset 
of sexual activity. Hormonal changes associated with puberty can initiate feelings of sex-
ual desire that may feel unfamiliar to girls. Physiological and bodily changes may increase 
vulnerability to body image or self-esteem concerns that can negatively affect sexual func-
tion. Young women may be vulnerable to social and peer pressures regarding engaging 
in sexual activity. Education about sexual activity and health may shape adolescents’ per-
ceptions and affect functioning. Further research is needed to examine risk factors for im-
paired sexual function among LGBTQ+ youth and specific racial or ethnic groups, given 
evidence in health disparities among minority populations. It may also be difficult to es-
tablish risk factors for FSDs in adolescents, especially those who have not yet had suffi-
cient sexual activity experiences to evaluate sexual problems. Assessment of risk factors 
that are established in adult populations is likely relevant to adolescents. For example, as-
sessment of abuse exposure (especially given that girls in late adolescence and early adult-
hood are at greatest risk for sexual victimization) and history of medical conditions (e.g., 
childhood cancer), including those that may affect body image, is warranted.

Rates 
Rates of FSDs in pregnant and postpartum women are not well documented and are 
likely overestimated, because researchers of ten do not consider sexual inactivity or 
distress. Most studies also do not assess pre-pregnancy sexual functioning, and mixed 
methodologies and sampling characteristics (e.g., mixed parities) make it difficult to 
draw generalizations from research. Motivations to abstain from sexual activity in late 
pregnancy or the early postpartum period may not be associated with lowered sexual 
function; thus, providers should take care to assess motivations and reasons for lack 
of sexual activity, when reported. 
Research indicates a decline in sexual functioning, including desire, arousal, and or-
gasmic functioning, across pregnancy but especially in the third trimester (Leeman and 
Rogers 2012; Serati et al. 2010). Research also indicates temporary decreases in sexual 
desire and orgasm functioning into 3–6 months postpartum. Typically, these decreases 
in sexual functioning resolve within 1 year postpartum (Barrett et al. 2000; Brubaker et 
al. 2008). Reports of postpartum sexual pain that reduces with time appear to have a 
similar trajectory, such that 42% of women experience perineal pain immediately after 
delivery, 22% at 8 weeks postpartum, and 10% at 12 weeks postpartum (Abdool et al. 
2009). Thus, women who are pregnant or intending to become pregnant may benefit 
from counseling about normative declines in sexual functioning in pregnancy and the 
early to middle postpartum period.
