Infanticide and Violence Risk Assessment 
In analyzing the world literature, Friedm an and Resnick (2007) found that although 
many cases of maternal infanticide occurred in the context of child abuse, some moth-
ers made suicide attempts at the same time. Studies of maternal infanticide found that 
the mothers were often unemployed caregivers of their children in their early 20s who 
experienced psychiatric disorders. Amon g mothers who committed filicide (killing 
one’s child who is younger than 18 years) in the general population, mothers were of-
ten socially isolated, of lower socioeconomic status, and full-time caregivers who had 
relationship problems or had been victimized by intimate partner violence. In some 
cases, crying or other child factors were identified as precipitants. Some mothers had 
previously maltreated their children, whereas others were devoted mothers with men-
tal illness. Substance abuse was also seen in this population, as were psychosis and de-
pression (Friedman and Resnick 2007). When specifically considering psychiatric 
samples of maternal filicide, these mothers had often experienced depression, psycho-
sis, and suicidal thinking. Some had personality disorders. Significant stressors in life 
were often found. It was recommended that psychiatrists assess for risk of filicide sys-
tematically, such as they do for suicide, first entertaining the possibility that such an 
act could occur (Friedman and Resnick 2007). 
Motives for maternal filicide initially described by Resnick (1969) and further de -
scribed by Friedman and Resnick (2007) include altruistic (murder out of love), which 
may be part of an extended suicide or may occur for psychotic or nonpsychotic reasons 
(e.g., a child’s debilitating illness); acutely psychotic (with no comprehensible motive, 
e.g., responding to command auditory hallucinations); fatal maltreatment (the final out-
come of chronic neglect or abuse); unwanted child; and partner revenge (e.g., in the midst 
of a custody battle). Among the motives for filicide, altruistic filicide is often related to 
postpartum psychosis (PPP) or postpartum depression (PPD), and acutely psychotic 
filicide is often related to PPP (Friedman et al. 2019a). However, partner revenge, un-
wanted child, and fatal maltreatment cases are rarely related to PPP , although a mother 
with this diagnosis may experience difficulty providing for the infant’s needs because 
of her psychosis. Specifically, in PPP , a mother may be at risk of child maltreatment due 
to delusional thinking, irritable mood state, or confusion. PPP itself elevates the risk of 
both infanticide and suicide (Friedman et al. 2019a). For a complete discussion of PPP , 
please see Chapter 16, “Postpartum Psychosis”; for suicide, please see Chapter 10.
