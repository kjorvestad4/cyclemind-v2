Key Points 
• Informed consent for voluntary termination of pregnancy (VTOP) may be com-
plicated by impaired decision-making capacity and mandated “informed con-
sent” laws in many states, which may include misinformation regarding VTOP . 
• Concerns about malpractice lawsuits in pregnancy should not prevent appro-
priate care. The discussion with the patient about the risks and benefits of 
medication, alternatives, and no treatment should be carefully documented. 
• Treatment over a patient’s objection may be necessar y in severe cases of ma-
nia or psychosis, and the psychiatrist should be able to explain the risks and 
benefits to the court. 
• Mental health professionals may become involved in cases relating to parent-
ing capacity. Serving as both treating clinician and forensic expert should be 
avoided in such cases due to issues related to dual agency. 
• Psychiatrists are mandated reporters of child abuse in U.S. jurisdictions. Phy-
sicians should be aware of the statutory standards for reporting child abuse, 
which in most states is suspicion or reason to suspect that abuse is occurring.

• When a mother experiences perinatal mental illness, not only should a suicide 
risk assessment be performed but infanticide and violence risk must also be 
considered. 
• Pregnancy and mothering during incarceration are becoming more common in 
the United States as more women enter the criminal justice system. Despite 
the many challenges involved in caring for incarcerated pregnant women, preg-
nancy outcomes overall are positive, likely due to certain beneficial aspects of 
confinement, such as access to prenatal care, stable housing, reduced expo-
sure to violence, and restricted access to alcohol, tobacco, and illicit drugs. 
• Although most children of incarcerated mothers are placed in either kinship 
care or foster care, most mothers are able to maintain some form of contact 
with their children while incarcerated. 
• Surrogacy is banned in many countries, and although it is allowed in the United 
States, laws vary by state regarding the legality of surrogacy, how the surrogate 
may be compensated, and the parental rights of the surrogate.
