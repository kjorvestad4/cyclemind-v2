Judicial bypass of parental consent for VTOP, in which a pregnant minor can obtain 
a VTOP with judicial consent as opposed to parental consent, occurs in most states 
(Friedman et al. 2015). These teenagers may legally be referred to as Jane Does. A met-
ropolitan Ohio study considering the characteristics of Jane Does found that their mean 
age was 16.4 years and that they usually had long-term boyfriends. They had often 
shared that they were pregnant with trusted adults but not with their parents, out of 
concern of being kicked out of the house or of family violence. The vast majority (95%) 
were eventually granted the judicial bypass. Mental health professionals may be in -
volved in these evaluations and, depending on the state, may be asked to opine whether 
the teenager is sufficiently mature or whether notification of her parents is against her 
best interests. In completing such evaluations, one must consider general principles of 
informed consent and determine whether the decision is being made knowingly, vol-
untarily , and with appropriate decision-making capacity (Friedman et al. 2015). 
Legal and Malpractice Concerns 
Regarding Medications in Pregnancy 
Regarding the use of psychotropic medications in pregnancy, studies have predomi-
nantly been retrospective and therefore are prone to bias and limitations. In evaluating 
studies, readers must consider the risks associated with the mental illness itself, asso-
ciated comorbidities such as substance use, and effects of poor prenatal care and of ad-
ditional medications (Friedman and Hall 2013). For a detailed discussion of risk-risk 
analysis in treatment decisions, please see Chapter 8, “A Clinical  Approach to Psychi-
atric Diagnosis and Treatment During Pregnancy.” Some studies are based on whether 
women filled prescriptions, without querying whether the medication was ingested.
