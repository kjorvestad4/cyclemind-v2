General Approach 
In any intervention to address agitation, clinicians should actively monitor the phys-
ical status of the pregnant patient and the developing fetus, with frequent assessment 
of vital signs and clinical status and evaluation for adverse effects. Psychiatry and ob-
stetrics teams should work together to mo nitor maternal and fetal health (including 
fetal heart monitoring and ultrasound) at all levels of agitation management. As with 
other clinical situations in reproductive psychiatry, performing a risk-risk analysis is 
important when considering the use of medications to treat agitation. Given the ad -
verse consequences associated with untreated agitation in pregnancy, the benefits of 
using psychotropic medications generally outweigh risks (Aftab and Shah 2017). If 
the patient has a history of good response to a particular medication, every effort 
should be made to use the same medication to minimize exposing the fetus to an ad-
ditional medication (Niforatos et al. 2019). 
Levels of Agitation Management 
The approach to management of acute agitation should progress from least restrictive 
to more restrictive based on the level of ag itation and response to each step of treat -
ment. Deescalation techniques are prioritized as a first-line treatment for the pregnant 
patient given the desire to minimize extensive use of psychotropic medication and to 
avoid physical restraint when possible (R odriguez-Cabezas and Clark 2018).  T able 
10–6 presents a stepwise approach to managing agitation in a pregnant patient. 
In cases of mild agitation, the initial response should include moving the patient to 
a low-stimulus environment and utilizing both verbal and nonverbal deescalation 
techniques (Aftab and Shah 2017; Richmond et al. 2012; Rodriguez-Cabezas and Clark 
2018). If a patient is moderately agitated or does not respond solely to deescalation, 
the clinician should progress to offering oral psychotropic medications. According to 
consensus guidelines, oral medications ar e clearly preferred over parenteral when 
possible (Allen et al. 2001). 
If in the general adult population, a pati ent’s severe agitation poses an acute dan-
ger to self or others, emergency interventions such as physical restraint, seclusion, or 
parenteral medication may be indicated. Expert consensus surveys have shown that 
most experts think emergency interventions are necessary in a minority of cases, from 
1% to 20% of patients (Allen et al. 2001). Particular precautions need to be taken, how-
ever, in the case of a severely agitated pregnant patient. The use of four-point restraint 
with a patient in the supine position increases the risk of inferior vena cava syndrome 
and may affect fetal blood flow, particularly in the second and third trimesters (Nifo-
ratos et al. 2019; Rodriguez-Cabezas and Clark 2018). Therefore, especially later in 
pregnancy, physical restraint should be used only as an intervention of last resort. The 
patient who requires physical restraint should  be placed in the left lateral decubitus 
position, 10–12 cm off the bed, with pillows and blankets for support. The patient 
should be monitored closely, with freque nt assessment of vital signs and physical 
checks to minimize risk (Niforatos et al. 2019).

Psychopharmacology for Agitation in Pregnancy 
The choice of medication to treat agitation should be based on a clinical assessment of 
the likely underlying cause, even if the diagnosis is provisional and subject to change 
when more data are available (Wilson et al. 2012). The best studied and most fre -
quently used medications for agitation are the first-generation antipsychotics (FGAs), 
second-generation antipsychotics (SGAs), and benzodiazepines (Wilson et al. 2012).
