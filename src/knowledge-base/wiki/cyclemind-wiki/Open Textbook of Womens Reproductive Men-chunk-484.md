Obstetrical outcomes. Although available data on the obstetrical course of women 
taking clozapine are limited, it has been reported that pregnant women taking cloza-
pine have elevated rates of obesity and gestational diabetes relative to the general preg-
nant population (Mehta and Van Lieshout 2017; Nguyen et al. 2020). Side effects of 
constipation, orthostatic hypotension, and cardiomyopathy have also been noted (Co-
hen 2017; Ronaldson 2017). 
Delivery effects. Because the expression of CYP1A2 is underdeveloped during fe-
tal development, clozapine can potentially accumulate in the fetal circulation (Fried -
man and Adityanjee 2003; Mehta and Van Lieshout 2017). High serum concentrations 
of clozapine at birth may place the infant at risk of seizures, agranulocytosis, or com-
plications such as floppy infant syndrome  (including decreased muscle tone, which 
is associated with motor developmental delay) (Mehta and Van Lieshout 2017). A 
case series by Nguyen et al. (2020) also noted fetal nonreactive cardiotocographic re-
cordings and poor neonatal adjustment. They found high rates of adverse neonatal 
outcomes including requirement for NICU admission and resuscitation at birth. 
Long-term effects. Data on long-term neurodevelop mental effects of fetal cloza -
pine exposure are lacking, but a recent study indicated that adaptive behavior scores 
on the Bayley Scales of Infant and Toddler Development were significantly lower in 
a clozapine-exposed group compared with infants of mothers who were exposed to 
other antipsychotic agents at both 2 months and 6 months, although they were not dif-
ferent at 12 months (Mehta and Van Lieshout 2017). 
Long-Acting Injections 
The literature on LAI antipsychotic use in pregnancy is extremely sparse and limited to 
a handful of case reports on individual long-acting agents, including haloperidol, 
fluphenazine, aripiprazole, olanzapine, risperidone, and paliperidone (Ballester-Gra-
cia et al. 2019; Clinebell et al. 2017; Kim et al. 2007; Manouilenko et al. 2018; Özdemir 
et al. 2015; Sabo et al. 2018; Zamora Rodríguez et al. 2017). Although published cases 
have not described any reports of major malformations among LAI-exposed infants, 
there have been reports of adverse obstetrical outcomes and neonatal effects. A case re-
port of a woman taking LAI risperidone in addition to oral risperidone, benztropine, 
and citalopram reported that her pregnancy was complicated by intrauterine growth re-
striction and suspected placental insufficiency. The case did not include information 
about other potential risk factors, such as tobacco or other substance use (Clinebell et 
al. 2017). Another case report of a patient taking long-acting risperidone reported a 
healthy outcome and attainment of infant developmental outcomes at 8 months of age 
(Kim et al. 2007). A report of a woman taking LAI haloperidol noted EPS in the infant 
that were treated with clonazepam (Collins and Comer 2003).
