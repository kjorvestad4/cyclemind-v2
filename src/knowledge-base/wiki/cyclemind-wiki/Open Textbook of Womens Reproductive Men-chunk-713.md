A systematic review of studies exploring the psychological outcomes for surrogate 
mothers did not reveal serious psychopathologies attributed to the surrogacy process. 
Several of the included studies noted that  some of the surrogate mothers endorsed 
moderate difficulties relinquishing the child (Söderström-Anttila et al. 2016). This 
same review also looked at psychological outcomes for mothers of children who were 
the product of surrogacies and found there were no major differences in psychological 
outcomes for these mothers compared with  those who conceived naturally . Impor-
tantly, it has been noted that the quality of evidence for both outcomes was low and 
more research is needed (Söderström-Anttila et al. 2016). 
Conclusion 
In summary, there is much overlap between legal and forensic issues and reproductive 
psychiatry with regard to informed consent and decision-making capacity for VTOP 
and for medication treatment in the peripa rtum, as well as involuntary treatment in 
the peripartum. Parenting capacity is a specific capacity that the expert witness may 
be asked to evaluate. Child maltreatment ri sk and infanticide risk should be consid -
ered by the treating psychiatrist, as well as risk of suicide. The reproductive psychia-
trist may also be involved in correctional mental health care of women, who often have 
children but limited interaction with them unless they are in a mother–baby unit. Fi-
nally, surrogate mothers and intended parents require special ethical and legal consid-
eration for the reproductive psychiatrist. 
Key Points 
• Informed consent for voluntary termination of pregnancy (VTOP) may be com-
plicated by impaired decision-making capacity and mandated “informed con-
sent” laws in many states, which may include misinformation regarding VTOP . 
• Concerns about malpractice lawsuits in pregnancy should not prevent appro-
priate care. The discussion with the patient about the risks and benefits of 
medication, alternatives, and no treatment should be carefully documented. 
• Treatment over a patient’s objection may be necessar y in severe cases of ma-
nia or psychosis, and the psychiatrist should be able to explain the risks and 
benefits to the court. 
• Mental health professionals may become involved in cases relating to parent-
ing capacity. Serving as both treating clinician and forensic expert should be 
avoided in such cases due to issues related to dual agency. 
• Psychiatrists are mandated reporters of child abuse in U.S. jurisdictions. Phy-
sicians should be aware of the statutory standards for reporting child abuse, 
which in most states is suspicion or reason to suspect that abuse is occurring.
