Valproate 
Valproate is an antiepileptic drug with a wide spectrum of efficacy in BD. Although it 
has the strongest evidence to support its use in acute mania, efficacy has been estab-
lished for its use in acute bipolar depression and in maintenance treatment of BD. Due 
to valproate’s substantial teratogenic and neurodevelopmental risks, the FDA and Eu-
ropean Medicine Agency recommend that it be avoided in women of reproductive age 
if alternative therapies are available and effective. If valproate must be used in women 
of reproductive age, contraception is advised, and adequate documentation of its clin-
ical justification and discussion of risks is recommended (Khan et al. 2016). 
Early pregnancy. Valproate is considered a human teratogen. Exposure to val -
proate in pregnancy is associated with a significant increase in the risk of congenital 
malformations and adverse neurodevelopmental  effects, a risk that exceeds that of 
other antiepileptics and mood stabilizers. The risk of major malformations has been 
reported to be 5%–11% with first-trimes ter valproate monotherapy (Campbell et al. 
2014; Diav-Citrin et al. 2008; Epstein et al. 2014; Tomson et al. 2018; Weston et al. 2016) 
and includes neural tube defects, craniofacial defects, cardiovascular anomalies, skel-
etal and limb malformations, and genitour inary defects. The risk has been found to 
be dose dependent, rising significantly (up to 20-fold) with daily doses over 1,000 mg 
(Diav-Citrin et al. 2008; Epstein et al. 2014; Morrow et al. 2006). 
Obstetrical outcomes. Literature on the reproductive risk of valproate has focused 
heavily on congenital malformations and neurodevelopmental outcomes. There are no 
consistent reports of increased risk of ob stetrical adverse outcomes, such as sponta -
neous abortion or preterm delivery. Although intrauterine growth restriction has been 
associated with valproate, this is typically associated with the “valproate syndrome,” 
which also includes skeletal and limb malformations (Epstein et al. 2014). 
Neonatal outcomes. Antenatal valproate exposure ha s been associated with in -
creased risk of neonatal heart rate decelerations, hypoglycemia, hepatotoxicity, low fi-
brinogen levels, abnormal tone, and feeding difficulties (Yonkers et al. 2004). 
Long-term effects. Higher rates of developmental delay, reduced IQ scores, learn-
ing difficulties, impaired verbal abilities, and autistic spectrum disorder (ASD) have 
been consistently associated with valproate exposure and appear to be dose dependent 
(Baker et al. 2015; Bromley et al. 2013; Meador et al. 2013).
