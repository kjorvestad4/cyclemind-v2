Systems of Perinatal Mental Health Care 
The experiences of pregnancy, birth, an d becoming a parent are transformational 
events in the lives of women and their families. Accompanying this set of enormous 
changes are physical and mental health chal lenges that have a clear impact on the 
mother and the infant; perinatal mental health and substance use disorders are asso-
ciated with poor obstetrical, infant, and child outcomes (Britton et al. 2001; Deave et 
al. 2008; Forman et al. 2007; Grote et al. 2020; Paulson et al. 2009). The perinatal period

represents an opportune time to identify and treat mental health disorders, in part be-
cause of frequent contact with health care providers (Smith et al. 2009). 
A fragmented and deeply unequal system of physical and mental health care fails 
women at one of the most vulnerable times in their lives. When they are most in need 
of a robust safety net of support, the vast  majority of pregnant women with mental 
health or substance abuse disorders go untr eated (Carter et al. 2005; Marcus et al. 
2003; Rowan et al. 2012). Moreover, health care disparities strongly affect perinatal 
mental health care. These disparities exist for a wide range of reasons, including lack 
of access to providers, geographical differences, differences in rates of private versus 
public insurance, differences in rates charged for people without insurance, commu-
nication differences, and cultural barriers  (American College of Physicians 2010). In 
addition, racial and ethnic disparities in women’s physical and mental health care 
serve as a barrier against quality and timely care. 
Screening represents an important first step in the identification of perinatal mental 
health disorders. For example, the ACOG now recommends that screening for peri -
natal depression and anxiety take place at  least once during the perinatal period 
(American College of Obstet ricians and Gynecologists 2018b). Currently, more than 
40 states have implemented guidelines and recommendations for screening for peri -
natal depression; however, it remains unclear if those steps have been successful in 
identifying—and treating—women with perina tal depression (Bauman et al. 2020). In 
a recent report, the Centers for Disease Co ntrol and Prevention noted that although 
screening captured a substantial prevalence of PPD, screening rates were highly vari-
able across states, ranging from 50% to 90% (Bauman et al. 2020). 
In addition, one of the most difficult dilemmas regarding access is how to pair iden-
tification and detection of perinatal mental health concerns with the resources needed 
to address positive screens. Several examples exist of innovative programs that aim to 
increase access and support for the mental health and well-being of mothers and their 
loved ones throughout pregnancy and the postpartum period. Examples include the 
integration of mental health care services into obstetrical (Goedde et al. 2020) and pri-
mary care settings (Lomonaco-Haycraft et al. 2018) and programs that increase access 
to specific populations, such as veterans (Katon et al. 2017). Another example is the 
Massachusetts Child Psychiatry Access Project for Moms, which aims to increase pro-
viders’ capacity to address perinatal depression (Byatt et al. 2016). This cost-effective 
approach, now replicated in many other st ates, implements 1) trainings and toolkits 
on depression screening, assessment, and treatment; 2) telephonic access to perinatal 
psychiatric consultation for providers serving pregnant and postpartum women; and 
3) care coordination to link women with individual psychotherapy and support 
groups (Byatt et al. 2016). 
The timing of interventions is also impo rtant. There has been increasing recogni -
tion that the postpartum period is a particularly vulnerable time for women’s mental 
health. In 2018, the ACOG issued updated recommendations regarding postpartum 
care, emphasizing the need for comprehens ive postpartum assessments at 3 weeks 
postdelivery (American College of Obstet ricians and Gynecologists 2018a). Services 
focused on the postpartum period have increased in number. One example is the 
Fourth Trimester Project, which is a woma n-centered engagement program of post -
partum support to meet the unmet needs of  new mothers and their families in this 
vulnerable period (Tully et al. 2017).

Despite increasing attention on a systems level to maternal health and well-being, 
painfully obvious racial and ethnic disparit ies in women’s health care remain. One 
needs only to look at the astonishingly high rates of maternal mortality among Black 
women in the United States to understa nd the breadth and depth of the problem 
(Holdt Somer et al. 2017). In addition, women of color in the United States, particularly 
in high-poverty neighborhoods, experience high rates of poor birth outcomes, includ-
ing cesarean delivery, preterm birth, low birth weight, and infant mortality (Thomas 
et al. 2017). Although some of the disparities affecting new mothers begin preconcep-
tion or in pregnancy (with higher rates of nearly all serious pregnancy morbidities af-
fecting nonwhite women; Admon et al. 2018 ), in many cases they become apparent 
only during the postpartum period. Pregnancy-related deaths, most of which occur at 
or after delivery, are three times higher in Black women than white women (Creanga 
et al. 2017). Women of racial and ethnic minorities tend to deliver in lower-quality hos-
pitals, with the 25% of hospitals that handle 75% of all Black women’s deliveries per-
forming lower on indicators of quality (Creanga et al. 2014; Howell et al. 2016). 
Hospital quality, however, explains only part of the story; Black women who de -
liver in higher-quality hospitals still have disproportionate rates of mortality and 
morbidity (Howell et al. 2016). Women in racial and ethnic minority groups face ad -
ditional challenges in the postpartum pe riod. Children born to nonwhite and non-
Asian women are far more likely to live in single-parent households (65% for Black, 
53% for Native American, 41% for Hispanic, 24% for white, and 15% for Asian fami -
lies) (Annie E. Casey Foundation 2020; O’Hare and Ritualo 2000). Although such rates 
do not factor in less formal partnership a rrangements, they do likely reflect the fact 
that more Black women will enter the postpa rtum period as single parents, with no 
partner to help with nighttime care or the care of other children. This situation, in turn, 
may lead to greater sleep disruption, which can have both psychological and biolog-
ical consequences. Poor sleep during pregnancy has been shown to be related to a more 
robust inflammatory response in African American women than in white women, for 
example, and to a higher risk for shortene d gestation (Carroll et al. 2019). African 
Americans in general (and not only in the perinatal period) have notably higher rates 
of short sleep duration and poorer subjective sleep quality than do white Americans 
(Christian et al. 2019). Also, African American women are more likely to retain excess 
weight after pregnancy than are white women, and that excess weight can contribute 
to excess inflammation (Christian et al. 2019). Poor sleep and obesity are in turn related 
to rates of PPD, which are known to be higher among racial and ethnic minority pop-
ulations (Bhati and Richards 2015; Cannon and Nasrallah 2019; Ertel et al. 2017). 
Thus, it is crucial that programs exist that focus specifically on addressing perinatal 
health while simultaneously addressing racial and ethnic health care disparities. For 
example, doula care has been linked to improvements in many perinatal outcomes; 
however, women of color and low-income women often face barriers in accessing 
doula support (Thomas et al. 2017). Recent  programs have implemented doula sup -
port for at-risk populations with the aim of reducing racial and income-based dispar-
ities in maternal and infant outcomes (Mottl-Santiago et al. 2020; Thomas et al. 2017).

improving systemic racism (Nature Medicine 2020; Obermeyer et al. 2019) and ineq-
uity within medical and mental health care start at the level of the doctor–patient re-
lationship. Multiple studies of focus groups of Black women have examined their

This textbook, with its focus on both biological and psychosocial aspects of mental 
health conditions related to reproductive tran sitions, is only one step in bringing at -
tention—and, more importantly, badly needed change—to address widespread dis -
parities in perinatal health care. In each chapter, we endeavor to present data about 
disparities where they are known, and we must acknowledge here the tremendous 
work yet to be done. Research studies are still overwhelmingly populated by white 
participants (with only 6% of participants being Black or Latinx) (Konkel 2015), so we 
are only in the beginning stages of understanding racial differences in etiology, patho-
physiology, and treatment (separate from but compounded by the disparities men -
tioned here). 
Conclusion 
The fourth trimester is a time of exquisite vulnerability for women due to the physical 
and emotional stress of delivering and caring for a newborn while establishing a new 
identity as a mother and managing a changing role in the family. Breastfeeding can be 
helpful or hurtful, depending on several factors, including how easily the process 
works, dietary problems, medical and medication issues, and managing sleep. Sleep 
deficiencies due to maternal hormonal ch anges and the baby’s nocturnal needs sig -
nificantly increase the possibility of depression and anxiety at a time when women 
are already at risk. The disparities that exist across health care systems are exacerbated 
during this time and further disadvantage minority women and their offspring, with 
long-lasting consequences for these individuals, their families, and their communities. 
We must do better. Women’s mental health presents clear examples of the need for 
a comprehensive health care system that op timizes quality, promotes access, and re-
duces the vast health care disparities of our society.
