Key Points 
• Ideally, the clinician and patient will make a treatment plan prior to pregnancy. 
Such treatment plans must be individualized and take into account not only 
the risks of illness versus the risks of medication but also the patient’s psy -
chosocial circumstances, access to health care, and health care disparities 
for racial and sexual minorities. 
• The relapse rate of maternal psychiatric illness with discontinuation of medi-
cations is extremely high. 
• An important treatment principle is to try to minimize the number of expo -
sures for the fetus to both medications and the mother’s psychiatric illness. 
• It is also important to avoid undertreating the patient during pregnancy.

Thefirst postpartum months are often referred to as the “fourth trimester” to under-
score the fact that the dependency of th e newborn and the physical and emotional 
demands on the mother are no less—and in many ways more—than during preg -
nancy. This period is a time of drastic biological, emotional, and social change and, as 
a result, a time of psychiatric vulnerabili ty. Great improvement in public awareness 
and understanding of postpartum depressi on (PPD) has occurred since the early 
2000s, but providers are still generally inadequately trained to recognize and treat the 
full spectrum of mood and anxiety disorders that women can experience during this 
time, despite the prevalence of such disorders. 
Two of the most critical considerations that must be at the center of good psychiatric 
care for postpartum women are 1) infant feeding and lactation and 2) sleep (or perhaps 
more accurately, sleep deprivation). The decision whether to breastfeed is difficult for 
many women, especially given the amount of social messaging around the topic. 
Women who do try to breastfeed have additional considerations: how to make it work, 
getting enough sleep, potential dietary restrictions, safety of medications in lactation, 
and involvement of the other parent or partner. In addition, regardless of whether the
