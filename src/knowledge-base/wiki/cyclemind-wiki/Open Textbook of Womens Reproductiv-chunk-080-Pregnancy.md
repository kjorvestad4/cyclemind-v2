Chapter 17. 
Pharmacology in Lactation 
The postpartum period is a time of substa ntially elevated risk for women with BD. 
Childbirth can also be a trigger for new onset of mood or psychotic symptoms in pre-
viously healthy women (Yonkers et al. 20 04). When acute symptoms present in the 
postpartum period, a thorough assessment of safety should be the priority; if needed, 
inpatient admission should be recommend ed. For women with known BD or a per -
sonal history of PPP , prophylactic treatment in the immediate postpartum period is 
considered the standard of care (Bergink et al. 2012). 
Because breastfeeding is associated with multiple well-established benefits for 
both mother and infant, many women with BD prefer to breastfeed their infants. 
Treating psychiatrists should discuss infant feeding preferences with a patient early 
in her pregnancy and regularly as the pregnancy progresses. Assuming the patient 
has capacity to make decisions about breastfeeding, the psychiatrist should provide 
available evidence about the risks and benefits of breastfeeding for the patient’s spe-
cific situation and the associ ated risks of formula feedin g versus indicated medica -
tions. If the patient has been treated with an efficacious medication during pregnancy, 
consideration should be given to continuing the effective medication, because switch-
ing medication during a high-risk period will not only introduce the infant to multiple 
exposures but also increase risk for symptom relapse for the patient. 
When a woman with BD does aim to breastfeed her infant, the treating psychiatrist 
should proactively discuss a plan for mate rnal and infant monitoring that includes 
collaboration with the treating pediatrician  and use of the lowest effective maternal 
medication dose. Information about available lactation services should be provided. 
Strategies to maximize sleep while breastfeeding should be discussed. These might in-
clude family members bringing the infant to feed at the breast at night and then assum-
ing all other night care of the infant (i.e., changing diapers, burping, soothing back to 
sleep). For single mothers who live alone, such conversations should include strategiz-
ing about friends or family who might be able to come for extended visits. Once breast-
feeding is established, the family member might bottle-feed the infant expressed 
breastmilk at night, allowing the patient uninterrupted sleep. Women with acute ma-
nia, psychosis, suicidality, or homicidality may not be capable of safely breastfeeding

In some cases, women with BD might choos e to formula-feed their infants, either 
due to personal preference or after a risk/b enefit discussion with their psychiatrist, 
obstetrician, or pediatric physician. Moth ers of infants with prematurity or other 
medical problems or women in need of polypharmacy treatment for BD may deter -
mine that the risks of medication exposure outweigh the benefits of breastfeeding. 
Women with a known sensitivity to sleep deprivation may determine that formula-
feeding by a support person overnight to allow prolonged uninterrupted sleep is a 
critical need in achieving or maintaining euthymia. Psychiatrists and other providers 
should be supportive patients who choose to formula-feed because this decision often 
induces feelings of shame and guilt that can be inadvertently reinforced by medical 
providers or family members. Validating the patient’s decision to make an informed 
decision and, when appropriate, to prioritize her own health in order to care for her 
baby can be a powerful intervention for a new mother during a vulnerable time. 
Regardless of how the patient chooses to f eed her infant, use of sedating medica -
tions (i.e., quetiapine, benzodiazepines) may present a safety concern. When such 
medications are prescribed, the patient should be instructed to exercise caution during 
feedings and to follow established safe sleep recommendations. Although the Amer-
ican Academy of Pediatrics universally recommends against parent–infant bed shar-
ing due to the associated increased risk of sudden infant death syndrome, sedating 
medications are specifically noted to further increase this risk (Task Force on Sudden 
Infant Death Syndrome 2016).
