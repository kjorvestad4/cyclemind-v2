Table 21–3 lists options for validated su bstance abuse screening tools developed 
specifically for the peripartum population. Other screens, such as the Drug Abuse 
Screening Test or the National Institute on Drug Abuse Quick Screen, may also be ap-
propriate because these are validated in general medical settings. They may all be ad-
ministered in person or remotely by a medi cal staff member or by the patient. Urine 
and blood drug testing may be used to confirm or monitor substance use but should 
not be used as a population-level screening method, due to methodological concerns. 
Many of the perinatal screening tools are highly sensitive but less specific because 
they are brief and ask broad questions about at-risk exposures or behaviors. In short, 
they are designed to facilitate conversati ons about substance use. A positive screen 
should prompt further evaluation, with the goal of defining factors such as the time 
period, frequency, and amount of use and severity of functional impairment. 
Providers should screen patients for substance use in an open-ended, empathetic, 
and nonjudgmental manner, provide education on the harmful effects of substance 
use, assess and support motivation to change, and refer to specialty or a higher level 
of care as indicated (Wright et al. 2016). This process is formally known as screening, 
brief intervention, and referral to treatment (SBIRT). SBIRT is recognized as the stan-
dard of care based on expert opinion and the recommendation of professional orga -
nizations (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention; U.S. Preventive Services Task

Diagnostic Criteria 
DSM-5 (American Psychi atric Association 2013) defines SUD as a pathological pat -
tern of behaviors related to the use of the substance. One key feature of SUDs is the 
underlying alteration in brain reward circuitry that persists beyond intoxication and 
detoxification. Ten separate classes of drug s are included in the SUDs: alcohol; caf -
feine; cannabis; hallucinogens; inhalants; opioids; sedatives, hypnotics, and anxiolyt-
ics; stimulants; tobacco; and other or unkn own substances. Each of these substances 
has the potential to activate the brain reward pathway, although the mechanism of ac-
tion may differ. See Boxes 21–1 through 21–4 for the DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for var-
ious SUDs. Specifiers may be applied to the diagnostic criteria to indicate severity, 
ranging from mild to moderate or severe. DSM-5 also includes the specifier “with 
peripartum onset” for mood disorders to indicate onset of mood symptoms during 
pregnancy or the first 4 weeks postpartum. However, this specifier should not be ap-
plied to SUDs (American Psychiatric Association 2013).

The American Society of Addiction Medicine defines addiction as a primary, chronic 
disease of brain reward, motivation, memory, and related circuitry, including dysfunc-
tion in these circuits leading to characteristic biological, psychosocial, and spiritual 
manifestations. This is reflected in an individual’s pathological pursuit of reward or re-
lief through substance use and other behaviors.The ICD-10 Classification of Mental and 
Behavioural Disorders (World Health Organization 1 992) includes a section on SUDs 
categorized by substance. Regardless of the diagnostic criteria used, the perinatal pe-
riod does not alter the diagnosis, nor is a specific specifier required. 
Clinical Features 
Sex and gender differences in psychological and physiological responses to drugs of 
abuse have been well documented and affect the clinical presentation of women with 
SUDs. Women progress through the stages of addiction differently than men; they 
transition from casual drug use to addiction faster (known as “telescoping”), are more 
reactive to stimuli that trigger relapse, and have higher rates of relapse than men (Bob-
