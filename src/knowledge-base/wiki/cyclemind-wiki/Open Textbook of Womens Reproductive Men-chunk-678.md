Given concerns regarding the risks of stimulant use in pregnancy, many women 
elect to discontinue stimulant treatment during pregnancy and breastfeeding; how -
ever, little is known about the impact of treatment (or lack thereof) on occupational 
functioning, quality of interpersonal relationships, impact on comorbid mental health 
conditions, and quality of life in women with ADHD in the perinatal period (Freeman 
2014). Untreated ADHD increases the risk of tobacco dependence (Kollins et al. 2005), 
yet there are clear recommendations regarding the importance of smoking cessation 
in pregnancy given the substantial evidence  of adverse outcomes (Siu and U.S. Pre -

the risk of maternal substance use disorders and tobacco dependence (Groenman et 
al. 2013), a factor that should be considered in the risk-risk discussion of stimulant ex-
posure in pregnancy. Of course, because prenatal factors are thought to be environ -
mental risk factors for ADHD in the offs pring, a robust understanding of ADHD of 
women in the perinatal period would serve to address the mental health needs of their 
infants and children in an intergenerational manner. 
Differential Diagnosis and Assessment 
Assessment 
ADHD is a clinical diagnosis. In adults, that diagnosis requires a detailed clinical inter-
view and assessment, including retrospective assessment of childhood ADHD symp-
toms by patient recall or collateral information; evidence of ADHD prior to the age of 
12 (e.g., by school records, childhood health assessments, or family observations); an 
enduring pattern of difficulties that impair social functioning and limit school or occu-
pational attainment; and current adult ADHD symptomatology, including symptom 
severity and pervasiveness, functional impairment, quality of life issues, and psychiat-
ric comorbidity (Geffen and Forster 2018; Rösler et al. 2006). The assessment of ADHD 
should include a review of screening questionnaires, a review of physical and mental 
health history, and a physical examination (Geffen and Forster 2018). Weight and car-
diovascular fitness, particularly blood pressure, are particularly important data points 
because ADHD treatments may influence these parameters. A personal or family his-
tory of cardiac ischemia or cardiac arrhythmias should be elicited because this may pre-
clude treatment with stimulants (Geffen and Forster 2018). Additional measures, such 
as blood tests, electroencephalogram, CT scan, or MRI scan, should be considered as 
indicated (Rösler et al. 2006).
