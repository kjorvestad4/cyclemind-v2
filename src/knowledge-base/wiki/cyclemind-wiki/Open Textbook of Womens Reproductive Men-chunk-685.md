Other Medications 
Scant reproductive safety information is available about other medications sometimes 
used for the treatment of ADHD, including atomoxetine and guanfacine. To our 
knowledge, no systematic studies in human pregnancy are available that have exam-
ined the safety of these medications (Freeman 2014). A few case reports and a registry 
analysis show no specific malformations or adverse pregnancy outcomes for either 
agent thus far (Alessi and Spalding 2003; Källén et al. 2013; Philipp 1980). 
Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
No known perinatal-specific psychotherap ies have been developed for the manage -
ment of ADHD. However, data are accumulating regarding nonpharmacological ap-
proaches in the general adult population, specifically cognitive-behavioral therapy 
(CBT) (Mongia and Hechtman 2012). CBT tailored for adult ADHD typically focuses 
on a combination of building organizational skills, such as prioritization and time man-
agement, and challenging self-defeating cognitions (Jensen et al. 2016; Morgensterns

Specific attention may be required to address the complex situations faced by 
women with ADHD in the perinatal period and early parenting, as they aim to bal -
ance multiple occupational demands, ho me management, and family/parenting re -
sponsibilities (Young et al. 2020), especially because household and parenting duties 
tend to be disproportionately borne by women (Treas and Tai 2016). Solutions have 
been cited in the literature, such as reducing the workload if possible and taking pub-
lic transportation instead of driving (Baker and Freeman 2018). Comprehensive pro-
grams of support have yet to be developed, but suggested interventions cited include 
the development of targeted support to manage feelings of stress and distress, manage 
and regulate emotions, support sexual health and safety, cope with rejection and isola-
tion, manage interpersonal conflict, learn assertiveness skills, and develop compromise 
and negotiation strategies in order to improve occupational and social outcomes and 
women’s ability to cope with everyday stressors (Young et al. 2020).
