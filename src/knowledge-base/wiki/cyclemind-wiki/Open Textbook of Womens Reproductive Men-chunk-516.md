495 Trauma and Reproductive Health 
PTSD from IPV and sexual trauma can present with cognitive appraisals, including 
self-blame, alienation, and fear (Hebenstre it et al. 2015). Dissociation and numbing 
are frequent symptoms and may serve as a mechanism to cope with recurrent trauma 
(Braun 1988). Avoidance of intimacy and, conversely, hypersexuality can present with 
a fluctuating course. Reexperiencing the traumatic event can occur with interpersonal 
conflict and can lead to hyperarousal in the form of difficulty trusting others. Shame 
and guilt can result in maladaptive relation ship behaviors, anger, and self-injurious 
behaviors in an attempt to control these cognitions (Foa et al. 1995). 
IPV/sexual trauma can also lead to myriad chronic health conditions, including mi-
graines, fibromyalgia, chronic pain, cognitive impairment from brain injury, irritable 
bowel syndrome, peptic ulcers, chronic abdo minal pain, decreased or absent libido, 
and chronic pelvic pain. Additional sequelae of IPV and sexual trauma include trans-
mission of sexually transmitted infections, unintended pregnancies, urinary tract in-
fections, and acute fractures. The severity of PTSD symptoms has been associated with 
greater burden of chronic medical conditions (Zoellner et al. 2000). Close collaboration 
with other medical specialties is essential, given the association of exacerbation of 
physical symptoms with stress and anxiety. 
Reproductive Loss 
Ms. DD is a 36-year-old gravida 4 para 0 who presents when 5 months pregnant, re -
ferred by her obstetrician for evaluation of anxiety. She has been trying to get pregnant 
for 3 years and has had three first-trimeste r miscarriages. Her medical workup for re-
current miscarriage has been negative. She re ports that, when trying to get pregnant, 
she experiences perseveration about her ovulation schedule, guilt and shame about not 
being able to keep a pregnancy, and sexual  dysfunction. When she became pregnant 
5 months ago, her anxiety significantly wors ened. She reports feeling terrified “every 
minute of every day” that she will miscarry, with frequent checking of her underwear 
for bleeding and constant attention to her body that interferes with normal functioning. 
In her prior pregnancy, Ms. DD experienced vaginal bleeding at 8 weeks, and her ob-
stetrician performed a vaginal ultrasound th at showed no identifiable abnormalities. 
She miscarried the following week. She states that she understands that the ultrasound 
did not cause the miscarriage, but she “can’t help feeling like it did,” and has avoided 
obstetrical visits in her current pregnancy. She reports she is no less anxious about mis-
carriage after completing the first trimester and feels unable to make any preparations 
for the baby coming “because I feel like it will jinx it.” She states that every time she tries 
to buy something for the baby, she imagines how she will feel holding that object after 
losing this pregnancy. 
The onset of trauma from reproductive loss can occur at any point within the peri-
natal period where there is an actual or potential threat to the health, life, or viability 
of the pregnancy or after the loss has occurred. In early pregnancy loss, women often 
simultaneously learn that they are pregnant  and that they are losing the pregnancy , 
resulting in delayed presentation of trauma symptoms. Data on the trauma of repro-
ductive loss are often limited to spontaneous abortions and stillbirth and often do not 
include VTOP . All types of reproductive loss or adverse birth outcomes can result in 
PTSD. Intrusive symptoms can manifest as dreams of babi es, flashbacks of abortion 
procedures, fetal images at varying ages, intense fear of pregnancy, fear of pelvic ex-
aminations, intense desire to become pregnant again, and the need or compulsion for 
a replacement baby (Daugirdaite et al. 2015).
