Anxiety Disorders in the Perinatal Period 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Anxiety disorders are among the most prevalent and consequential health disorders in 
the United States (Kessler et al. 2012). There are substantial sex differences in the preva-
lence of anxiety disorders: estimates of the lifetime prevalence of an anxiety disorder in 
women is 40%, twice that of men (Kessler et al. 2012). A recent meta-analysis estimated 
that the prevalence of having at least one anxiety disorder in pregnancy was 20.7%, with 
a trend toward a higher prevalence of anxiety in pregnancy as compared with postpartum 
(Fawcett et al. 2019). A systematic review found that the prevalence of perinatal anxiety 
was the same as or higher than that of anxiety in the general population, including gen-
eralized anxiety disorder (GAD, 9%) and panic disorder (1%–2%), as well as disorders 
with anxiety symptomatology, such as OCD (0.2%–4%) and PTSD (0.6%–8%) (Ross and 
McLean 2006). Many anxiety disorders run a chronic course; thus, many women enter 
the perinatal period already having an anxiety disorder (O’Hara and Wisner 2014). 
Anxiety has extensive comorbidity with other mental health conditions, particularly 
depression (O’Hara and Wisner 2014). The prevalence of anxiety disorders in adults 
with comorbid major depressive disorder (MDD) has been estimated to be as high as 
56% (Kessler et al. 2003). Anxiety symptoms are more prevalent in depression related 
to childbirth—specifically postpartum depres sion (PPD)—than depression that is un -
related to childbirth (Hendrick et al. 2000; Jolley and Betrus 2007). In a large-scale post-
partum screening study with diagnostic follow-up, 66% of the women diagnosed with 
MDD were also diagnosed with an anxiety disorder (Wisner et al. 2013). The converse 
is also true: Grigoriadis et al. (2011) found that in a cohort of perinatal women, nearly 
50% of the sample with GAD had a comorbid major depressive episode (MDE) or an-
other anxiety disorder, and 30% met criteria for both GAD and MDE. They also found 
significant comorbidity among anxiety-rela ted disorders themselves; in a cohort of 
perinatal women with GAD, 20% had comorb idity with a specific phobia, 9% with 
panic disorder, 9% with agoraphobia, and 4% with OCD (Grigoriadis et al. 2011).

Screening 
The increasing recognition of the signific ant impact of anxiety throughout women’s 
lives has resulted in a call for increased screening and resources allocated for diagnosis 
and treatment (Gregory et al. 2020). In 2020, the Women’s Preventive Services Initiative 
issued a recommendation for widespread screening for anxiety; the age group recom-
mended for screening was broad, from adolescent girls starting at age 13 years through 
adulthood (Gregory et al. 2020). Given the widespread prevalence and impact of peri-
natal anxiety, screening specifically in the perinatal period is also recommended. The 
American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) issued a recommenda-
tion that obstetricians-gynecologists and ot her obstetrical providers screen at least 
once during the perinatal period for depression and anxiety using a validated, stan -
dardized tool (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018). They also 
recommended that providers conduct a full assessment of emotional and mental well-
being in the postpartum period, even if such screening had already been completed 
during pregnancy (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018).

However, effective screening for perinatal anxiety remains limited, in part because 
many questions remain about the optimal screening tool, starting with which—of 
many—anxiety symptoms to address (Fairbrother et al. 2019; Matthey et al. 2013a). For 
example, the Generalized Anxiety Disorder seven-item subscale (Spitzer et al. 2006) 
identified GAD in perinatal populations with greater accuracy and specificity than the 
widely used Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS; Cox et al. 1987). However, 
the clinical presentation of perinatal anxiety can be highly variable, including symp -
toms from various DSM-5 disorders, such as panic or intrusive thoughts, making the 
selection of a single tool difficult (Matthey et al. 2013a; Meades and Ayers 2011). In ad-
dition, general anxiety scales may not delineate symptoms of anxiety from the common 
physical symptoms of pregnancy and postpa rtum, such as sleep disturbance, which 
may lead to false positives (Matthey et al. 2013a). Examples include “I feel rested” and 
“I feel comfortable” in the State-Trait Anxiety Index (STAI; Spielberger et al. 1983) and 
“I can sit at ease and feel relaxed” in the Hospital Anxiety and Depression Scale (HADS; 
Zigmond and Snaith 1983). In contrast, the widely used EPDS has the advantage of ex-
cluding constitutional symptoms of pregnancy and postpartum (Cox et al. 1987). 
In addition, pregnancy and becoming a pare nt are major life transitions that can 
feel stressful or overwhelming and can lead to anxiety specific for this distinct period 
of time, yet general screening tools may not capture anxiety symptoms specific to this 
population (Fallon et al. 2016b). Many sy mptoms of perinatal anxiety may not meet 
DSM-5 criteria for an anxiety disorder but nevertheless may be significant in terms of 
symptom severity, functional impairment, and impact on maternal and infant out -
comes (Phillips et al. 2009). A recent sy stematic review analyzed the psychometric 
properties of several generalized anxiety screening tools designed for the general 
adult population, such as the STAI and the HADS, and concluded that there is a sub-
stantial need for further validation of these measures to enable accurate screening and 
detection in the perinatal population (Meades and Ayers 2011). 
On the other hand, several pregnancy-spec ific self-report questionnaires, such as 
the Pregnancy Anxiety Scale (Levin 1991), the Pregnancy-Related Anxiety Question -
naire (Van den Bergh 1990), and the Pregnancy-Related Anxiety Scale (Wadhwa et al. 
1993) focus on perinatal concerns but may have limited relevance for diagnosable anx-
iety disorders. Interestingly, although perinatal-specific scales focus on highly specific 
topics, they may predict adverse maternal–infant outcomes more effectively than gen-
eral measures of anxiety, and they are distinct from general indices of anxiety and de-
pression (Huizink et al. 2002). It was partly due to results such as these that the concept 
of pregnancy-specific anxiety—that is, anxi ety specific to concerns about pregnancy, 
delivery, postpartum, and parenthood, and distinct from anxiety experienced at other 
times of life—was developed (Huizink et al . 2004). For example, a confirmatory factor 
analysis and a later replication study noted that general anxiety and depression symp-
toms contributed little to the variance in pregnancy-specific anxiety and that topics 
specific to the pregnancy itself (e.g., concern about the health of the fetus) were more 
important factors (Brunton et al. 2019; Huizink et al. 2004). 
Yet difficulties in screening also exist with perinatal-specific screening tools. The 
ACOG cited the EPDS in its recommendation as a screening scale for depression and 
anxiety, given that it is in widespread use, is a brief self-report scale, is health literacy 
appropriate, and has been translated into more than 50 languages (American College 
of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2018). However, the EPDS was originally devel -

Recent advancements have included the development of scales that can both map 
onto diagnosable anxiety disorders and capture anxiety specific to the perinatal period 
(Table 19–2); these include the Perinatal Anxiety Screening Scale (PASS; Somerville et 
al. 2014), the Postpartum Worry Scale–Revised (Moran et al. 2014), and the Postpartum

veloped to detect a broad range of anxiety symptoms, is specific to the perinatal period, 
and has a four-factor structure including 1) acute anxiety and adjustment, 2) general 
worry and specific fears, 3) perfectionism and control and trauma, and 4) social anxiety 
(Somerville et al. 2014, 2015). Preliminary validation of this scale was encouraging— 
the PASS identified 68% of women with a diagnosable anxiety disorder as compared 
with the EPDS anxiety subscale, which detected 36% (Somerville et al. 2014). 
Screening for perinatal anxiety has the potential to capture a broad population, to 
lead to earlier diagnosis, and to provide an opportunity for early intervention, which 
could have important consequences for obst etrical and mental health outcomes for 
women and their offspring. However, screening is just one aspect of caring for the 
mental health of women in the perinatal period. Comprehensive health care systems 
must be in place to pair consistent screening with rigorous assessment, early interven-
tion, and resources for follow-up for women with identified mental health disorders 
(Kendig et al. 2017). Additionally, future efforts might focus on identifying protective 
and resilience factors, such as self-efficacy and increased social support, to strengthen 
psychosocial resources as early as possibl e and optimize future outcomes (Dunkel 
Schetter and Tanner 2012). 
Diagnostic Criteria 
Anxiety disorders have in common the presence of excessive or uncontrollable worry. 
In DSM-5, the term anxiety disorder refers to a group of distinct but interrelated condi-
tions characterized by excessi ve fear—the emotional response to a real or perceived 
current threat—or anxiety—the response to a perceived future threat—and related be -
havioral disturbances (American Psychiatric Association 2013). Despite the high prev-
alence of perinatal anxiety, the DSM specifier “with peripartum onset” currently applies 
solely to mood disorders, not to anxiety disorders (American Psychiatric Association 
2013). Some researchers define perinatal GAD if symptoms meet other criteria for a 
minimum duration of 1 month (vs. 6 months, as per DSM-5 criteria), given the specific 
time course of excessive worry during pregnancy and postpartum (Buist et al. 2011). 
Defining anxiety in the context of reproductive mental health is complex (and con-
fusing). First, perinatal anxiety symptoms, even if they do not fit formal criteria for an 
anxiety disorder diagnosis (e.g., pregnancy-specific anxiety), are highly prevalent and 
may cause significant impairment in functioning (O’Hara and Wisner 2014). Second,

The following are core features of several of the more commonly described DSM-5 
anxiety disorders in the perinatal literature, as well as descriptions of other anxiety-
related terms discussed in the literature, including pregnancy-specific anxiety (which 
has substantial evidence as a distinct clinical entity) and trait anxiety, a little-studied 
but perhaps impactful subtype. 
• Generalized anxiety disorder : GAD is the most common perinatal anxiety disorder 
(Goldfinger et al. 2020). It is characterized by excessive anxiety and worry that oc-
curs for the majority of days and is associated with functional impairment. Worries 
include a number of events or activities and feel difficult to control (American Psy-
chiatric Association 2013). 
• Panic disorder: Panic disorder is diagnosed when a person experiences recurrent un-
expected panic attacks and persistent worry about additional panic attacks or their 
consequences (American Psychiatric Association 2013). A panic attack is defined by 
an abrupt surge of intense fear or discomfort, along with symptoms such as accel-
erated heart rate, feeling dizzy or lightheaded, fear of dying or losing control, and 
derealization or depersonalization (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
• Agoraphobia: Agoraphobia is defined as excessive fear or anxiety about being in a 
situation in which escape may be difficult or help not available in the event of de-
veloping panic-like or embarrassing symptoms. It is often comorbid with panic 
disorder (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
• Social anxiety disorder: Patients with social anxiety disorder experience intense fear 
or anxiety in social situations in which they may be exposed to possible scrutiny 
by others (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
• Specific phobia: Patients with a specific phobia experience marked fear or anxiety 
about a specific object or situation (e.g., flying, heights, injections, or seeing blood), 
and exposure to the object or situation immediately brings about severe fear or anx-
iety; as a result, these patients will try to avoid the anxiety-provoking stimulus to 
any extent possible (American Psychiatric Association 2013). Several specific pho-
bias discussed in the perinatal anxiety literature include emetophobia (fear of nausea 
and vomiting) and tokophobia (fear of childbirth); of note, fear of childbirth has been 
further described as fear of childbirth itself (primary) or fear after a prior negative 
birth experience (secondary) (Dencker et al. 2019). 
In addition to the DSM-5 disorders, other perinatal-specific anxiety terms are cited in 
the literature. Pregnancy-specific anxiety describes fears specific to the perinatal period, in-
cluding about the health of one’s baby, the hospital and health care experience (including 
concerns about the well-being of the pregnancy and oneself), the experience of delivery, 
and the transition into parenting (Dunkel Schetter 2011; Huizink et al. 2004). It appears

and meta-analysis of more than 100 studies and more than 200,000 women noted a 
prevalence of 29%–33% antenatally and 23% postnatally (Dennis et al. 2017). Lastly, 
distress is a term used in the pe rinatal literature to descri be negative affective states 
related to stress, such as anxiety or depression symptoms (Rallis et al. 2014b). 
Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
Generally, anxiety consists of symptoms in multiple domains, including cognitive (e.g., 
fear of losing control, frightening thoughts  or images), physiological (e.g., increased 
heart rate, shortness of breath, trembling or  shaking), behavioral (e.g., avoidance of 
threat cues, restlessness, agitation), and affective (e.g., feeling nervous, tense, wound 
up) (Chand and Marwaha 2020). Perinatal GAD is characterized by excessive, uncon-
trollable worry that can cause functional impairment (Misri et al. 2015). It is a clinical 
entity that is distinct from the anxiety associated with PPD, despite extensive comor-
bidity with it (Misri et al. 2015). The content of the anxiety in perinatal GAD typically 
revolves around fears about fetal well-being, maternal wellness, partner illness, and pa-
rental mortality (Misri et al. 2015). Worry is typically accompanied by physical symp-
toms of GAD, such as fatigue, irritability, difficulty with concentration, and insomnia; 
however, these may be mistaken for normal physical symptoms of pregnancy and the 
postpartum period, making diagnosis challenging (Misri et al. 2015). The symptoms of 
pregnancy-specific anxiety tend to be focused on core themes of the pregnancy itself, 
including losing control in or not coping with the pain of labor, fear of the baby being 
harmed or dying, concerns about changes in physical appearance, and concerns about 
coping well as a parent (Huizink et al. 2004). 
Course and Prognosis 
The severity of symptoms in perinatal anxiety can be variable, and its trajectory over 
time appears to be dynamic. For exampl e, Matthey and Ross-Hamid (2012) noted a 
possible difference between transient and enduring perinatal distress; approximately 
50% of the women in a cohort study who scored high on either the EPDS or the HADS 
anxiety subscale at their first prenatal appointment no longer scored “high” 2 weeks 
later; specific factors related to improvement were reduced morning sickness, reas -
suring tests from screening ultrasounds, reduced fear of miscarriage, and a reassuring 
outpatient visit (Matthey and Ross-Hamid 2012).

The risk factors associated with worsening anxiety were past depression, stress level, and

multiple distinct groups were noted, ranging from minimal to chronic (Bayrampour et

In addition, the trajectory of perinatal anxiety is interrelated with other factors, such 
as depression and stress. For example, a recent prospective cohort study noted that lev-
els of depression, anxiety, and stress symptoms all changed over time, with the nadir 
of distress during the middle of pregnancy (Rallis et al. 2014b). In this study, higher 
anxiety symptoms early in pregnancy predicted higher anxiety levels throughout the 
rest of pregnancy; higher depression and stress scores in early and midpregnancy, re-
spectively, predicted higher anxiety scores later in pregnancy (Rallis et al. 2014a). 
Perinatal anxiety is associated with a broad range of adverse maternal–infant out-
comes. Antenatal anxiety is associated with fewer prenatal visits (Murray et al. 2003), 
reduced pregnancy weight gain (Dayan et al. 2002), and delayed initiation—and ear -
lier termination—of breastfeeding (Fallon et al. 2016a). Multiple studies have demon-
strated an association between antenatal anxiety, particularly pregnancy-specific 
anxiety, and prematurity and low birth weight (Ding et al. 2014; Dole et al. 2003; Dun-
kel Schetter 2009; Ibanez et al. 2012; Kramer et al. 2009; Orr et al. 2007; Rose et al. 2016; 
Sanchez et al. 2013; Staneva et al. 2015) . A systematic review and meta-analysis 
demonstrated additional ad verse obstetrical outcomes, including infants who are 
small for gestational age and infants with reduced head circumference (Grigoriadis et 
al. 2018). Interestingly, such a robust association between depression and the breadth 
of these obstetrical outcomes has not been identified in similar meta-analyses, raising 
the question of whether antenatal anxiety may have a more significant impact on 
pregnancy outcomes than depression (Grigoriadis et al. 2013). 
Comorbid perinatal anxiety and depression have a particularly strong negative im-
pact on prognosis. Comorbid anxiety and depression are linked with more severe 
symptomatology and with poorer short-term and long-term outcomes, including an 
increased risk of suicide (Nepon et al. 2010). A recent prospective cohort study of peri-
natal women with comorbid MDD and GAD demonstrated more severe anxiety 
symptoms, lower quality of life, and slow er symptom remission than perinatal pa -
tients with depression who did not have GAD (Misri and Swift 2015). Antenatal anx-
iety is a strong independent predictor of PPD (Austin et al. 2007; Coelho et al. 2011; 
Goodman et al. 2014a; Robertson et al. 2004; Sutter-Dallay et al. 2004; Verreault et al. 
2014). In addition, there is evidence of a bidirectional relationship; for example, one 
study of more than 200 perinatal women no ted that depressive symptoms earlier in 
pregnancy predicted higher levels of anxiety in late pregnancy and that anxiety in late 
pregnancy predicted higher depressive symptomatology in the early postpartum pe-
riod (Skouteris et al. 2009) . Alarmingly, antenatal anxiety in early pregnancy is also 
associated with suicide risk (Farias et al. 2013).
