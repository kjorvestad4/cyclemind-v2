Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
A variety of nonpharmacological treatment approaches are used for FSDs. Pelvic floor 
physical therapy is a widely used approach for treatment of sexual pain, although re-
search examining the efficacy of specific protocols is limited. This approach includes 
manual therapy, psychoeducation, relaxation and breathing (re)training, local tissue 
sensitization, electromyographic biofeedback, and instructed, progressive use of vagi-

Research provides some limited support for sex therapy (for treatment of HSDD), 
cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT; for tr eatment of HSDD and sexual pain disor -
ders), mindfulness combined with CBT (f or treatment of HSDD, FSAD, and sexual 
pain disorders), therapist-aided exposure therapy (for treatment of sexual pain disor-
ders), and directed masturbation (for treatment of female orgasmic disorder). Psycho-
therapy to treat FSDs may be facilitated in individual, couples, or group format. 
Sex therapy typically includes psychoeducation on sexual functioning and sensate fo-
cus exercises. Sensate focus consists of a progressive series of exercises intended to in-
crease one’s attention to sensations and understanding of their physical response (Althof 
2011). Typically , couples are instructed to begin with nongenital touching, without the ex-
pectation of intercourse, to focus on a nonjudgmental and nonevaluative awareness of the 
sensations of touch with one partner touching the other. Exclusion of intercourse allows

Management of FSDs in the Context of 
Comorbid Psychiatric Illness or Treatment 
As noted earlier (see “Assessment of Female Sexual Dysfunctions”), diagnosis of FSDs 
requires consideration of other conditions, including any psychiatric conditions, that 
might be associated with the sexual proble ms. Due to the significant strength of the 
bidirectional relationship between depression and sexual dysfunctions (Atlantis and 
Sullivan 2012), screening for depression in women presenting with sexual problems, 
particularly low desire, is recommended (Cla yton et al. 2018). Psychotropic medica -
tions used to treat psychiatric conditio ns, most commonly antidepressants (Clayton 
and Montejo 2006; Montejo et al. 2019; Serretti and Chiesa 2009), often have sexual side 
effects. Unfortunately, these side effects can contribute to nonadherence and discontin-
uation of treatment and decreased recovery. However, it is difficult to clearly identify 
whether sexual problems are specifically due to depression, primary FSDs, or medica-
tion side effects. Moreover, for some individuals, sexual functioning improves with psy-
chotropic treatment, particularly for those whose depression is effectively improved. 
Specific rates of sexual problems due to psychotropic medications (i.e., treatment-
emergent sexual dysfunctions) vary, with rates ranging between 26% and 80% (Clay-
ton et al. 2016; Montejo et al. 2019; Serretti and Chiesa 2009). Identifying medications’ 
differential effects on sexual function is difficult, in part due to limited availability of 
randomized controlled trials and variatio ns in methodology (e.g., patient sponta -
neous report vs. assessment with a validated measure) and in part due to confound-
ing by indication (i.e., that depression also results in sexual dysfunctions). 
Evidence indicates that adverse sexual effects of antidepressants are most com -
monly in domains of desire and arousal, followed by orgasm, whereas evidence of ef-
fects on pain are mixed (Lorenz et al. 2016). Studies indicate that sexual side effects
