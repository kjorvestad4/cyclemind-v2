As with all medical decisions requiring informed consent, there can be complexity 
in determining a patient’s capacity to ma ke decisions regarding VTOP . Historically, 
women with intellectual or mental disabilities have been denied reproductive freedoms. 
In 2006, the Convention on the Rights of Persons with Disabilities sought to change these 
attitudes and provide women with reproductive autonomy by tailoring decision mak-
ing to the needs of the specific individual (Ngwena 2018). In women with severe mental 
illness, this tailored approach can take the form of assisted or surrogate decision-making 
depending on the patient’s decision-making capacity (Coverdale et al. 2004). 
It is important to note that most women with mental illness are able to demonstrate 
the cognitive understanding and appreciation of the consequences of a VTOP for them-
selves and their fetus and to use this to communicate a voluntary decision. There are 
cases, however, in which this ability may be acutely or chronically impaired. The re-
stricted time window in which decisions about termination must be made can be par-
ticularly challenging. In women who have impaired capacity during this crucial period, 
various strategies can be implemented to assist in decision-making. The first step in this 
process involves determining whether the patient has a guardian and, if so, determin-
ing whether the guardian has the authority to make decisions concerning pregnancy 
(Zalpuri et al. 2015). If such a guardian does exist, a substituted judgment should take 
into consideration the patient’s long-standing values and preferences. If such a guard-
ian does not exist, assisted decision-making may be utilized. Assisted decision-making 
can involve strategies such as discussing with the woman her past preferences regard-
ing pregnancy and motherhood and then helping to guide her decision-making based 
on any previously stated preferences. 
Since 2010, more and more states have  adopted termination “informed consent” 
statutes that, as noted earlier, require women seeking terminations be presented with 
state-authored informational materials prior to the procedure (Daniels et al. 2016). 
These statutes have little to do with standard informed consent. Instead, they involve 
giving women seeking VTOPs state-authored information about the alternatives and 
risks to termination and about embryologica l and fetal development (Daniels et al. 
2016). Of the 37 states identified by 2013 as having these laws, 23 states had made their 
material available to the public. These were  included in an analysis that found that 
31% of the statements in the “informed co nsent” material were medically inaccurate 
(with inaccurate statements ranging from 15% to 46% of the material for individual 
states). Examples of medically inaccurate information included statements such as the 
head being formed by 2 weeks and the ability to record brain activity at 4 weeks. These 
inaccurate statements call into question the constitutionality of these laws. Although 
“informed consent” laws were upheld by the Supreme Court in Planned Parenthood of 
Southeastern Pennsylvania v. Casey (1992), it is noted that information should be “truth-
ful” and “nonmisleading” and help the woman to make a “thoughtful and informed” 
decision (Daniels et al. 2016). This mandatory dissemination of inaccurate information 
is particularly concerning given that two-thirds of women seeking VTOP live in states 
with these mandates (Daniels et al. 2016).
