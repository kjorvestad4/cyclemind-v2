common in adult women than in adult men (Fontenelle et al. 2006). During times of 
reproductive transition, the risk of OCD rises above that of the general population. It 
is hypothesized that changes in gonadal hormone levels and their impact on the sero-
tonin system may play an etiological role in vulnerable populations (Abramowitz et 
al. 2010a). This, along with genetic factors and the psychological, social, and physical 
stressors of transitioning to motherhood, le ads to an increased risk of obsessive and 
compulsive symptoms in the perinatal period. Some women meet full criteria for the 
disorder, but other women have symptoms as part of mood or anxiety disorders. One 
study of women in a perinatal day hospital found that more than 30% had obsessions, 
but only 3.9% met criteria for OCD (Schofield et al. 2014). 
This chapter focuses on the epidemiology, clinical features, etiology, and treatment 
of OCD at times of reproductive transition, with particular emphasis on OCD in the 
perinatal period. Many features of OCD in general apply to the illness during these 
time periods; although touching on aspects of general OCD is necessary, the content in 
this chapter largely focuses on the unique aspects of OCD during times of reproductive 
transition.
