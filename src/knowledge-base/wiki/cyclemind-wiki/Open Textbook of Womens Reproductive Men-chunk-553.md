Other etiological factors related to perinatal anxiety are starting to be implicated in 
the pathogenesis of perinatal anxiety disorders, such as alterations in the gut-brain 
axis and microbiome, immune system deregulation, alterations in glucocorticoid sig-
naling, and insulin resistance (Glendining et al. 2018; Rackers et al. 2018; Sasaki et al. 
2013, 2014; Sullivan et al. 2014). However, much remains to be understood in terms of 
the relationship between these factors and anxiety in the perinatal period. 
Pregnancy-Related and Psychosocial Factors 
It would seem intuitively clear that preg nancy complications, independent of previ -
ous outcomes, are also associated with the development of perinatal anxiety. Unex -
pected birth outcomes and previous traumatic events have been shown to contribute 
to the onset of subsequent perinatal anxiety (Shapiro et al. 2017). For example, women 
with threatened miscarriage have a greater prevalence of depressive and anxiety 
symptoms compared with women with physiologically stable pregnancies (Zhu et al. 
2018). The Norwegian Mother and Child Cohort Study noted a high degree of anxiety 
and depressive symptoms in a subsequent pregnancy following a stillbirth, even if the 
subsequent pregnancy results in a healthy li ve birth (Gravensteen et al. 2018). Thus, 
women experiencing pregnancy complications should be appropriately monitored 
for the onset of anxiety in the perinatal period and referred for appropriate treatment 
if symptoms of perinatal anxiety are occurring in the context of a complicated preg -
nancy (Abrar et al. 2020; Fairbrother et al. 2017). (Please see Chapter 5, “Infertility and 
Perinatal Loss,” and Chapter 18 for details.) 
Components of the psychological transitions involved in becoming a parent and 
building a family also may lead to the de velopment of perinatal anxiety. The period 
surrounding childbirth is one of profound change and is often experienced as stressful 
and overwhelming (Harrison et al. 2020). For example, a recent study with focus 
groups identified several key themes of anxiety related to motherhood, including un-
realistic expectations of birth and motherhood, stigma, uncertainty, poor maternal con-
fidence, and a lack of mental health support and knowledge. Overall, perinatal women 
commonly felt undersupported and poorly prepared for motherhood (Harrison et al. 
2020). Partner conflict has also been identifi ed as a contributing factor for perinatal
