The Parent–Infant Dyad 
The parent–infant relationship comprises the observable aspects of the interaction and 
the unobservable aspects (i.e., mental, emotional, representational) of the relationship. 
Observable Interactions 
The observable aspects of the parent–infant relationship consist of the nuances of their 
interactions with each other. Infants are born with unique capabilities at birth, includ-
ing having a preference for maternal smell (Macfarlane 1975; Sullivan and Toubas 
1998) and voice (DeCasper and Fifer 1980) and preferring the mother’s face over other 
shapes and objects or even a stranger’s face (Bushnell et al. 1989; Field et al. 1984; Pas-
calis et al. 1995). From the beginning, they are active participants in their environment 
and their interactions with their caregivers. The relationship between the parent and 
infant is very intimate and made up of physical closeness through touch, nonverbal 
interactions (e.g., gaze), mutual communication through vocalizations and protocon-
versations, and coregulation, such as the level of reciprocity between mother and in-
fant. Many facets of the parent–infant dyad  influence the quality of the interaction, 
including parental responsiveness, handling  of disruption and repair , and the plea-
sure experienced by both parent and infant. The microanalytic research, which looks 
at early mother–infant interactions, shows that infants and mothers engage in a rich 
and detailed back-and-forth exchange, which, if studied at 4 months of age, can pre -
dict the development of secure versus avoidant attachment patterns at 1 year (Kou -
lomzin et al. 2002). 
Unobservable Interactions 
Parents often evolve a rich set of imaginations and expectations regarding their infants 
and regarding themselves as parents, which are referred to as parental representational 
mind. These thoughts and fantasies are affected by the parents’ history with their own 
caregivers, culture, life events, hopes, dreams, fears, and history of trauma, among 
other factors (Stern 1985, 1995). Another important concept is parental reflective func-
tioning. Parental reflective functioning has been described as parents’ capacity to un-
derstand their own and their child’s ment al states, and the effects these have on 
behaviors and interactions between them (De Roo et al. 2019). Parental reflective func-
tioning is associated with better parent–child coregulation and is linked to the quality 
of the parent–child attachment (Moser et al. 2019).

In turn, through interactions with their primary caregivers, infants begin forming 
mental patterns of how they are perceived in each interaction, how they are expected 
to respond, and what they can expect from the other person. Over time, they might start 
internalizing these images and identifying with them. These inte rnalized images, or 
mental representations, of themselves and the world around them are also called internal 
working models (Crittenden 1990). Galinsky (1987) theorized that parent and child de-
velopment are intertwined, such that the child’s and parent’s developmental processes 
shape each other; the parental stages of development correspond with the baby’s own 
age and developmental trajectory. Just as children advance through several develop-
mental stages, adults also continue to move through developmental stages within the 
context of the transition to parenthood. 
The infant’s psychological life begins in the parents’ mind (Gilmore and Meersand 
2013). Galinsky (1987) proposed six progressive psychological stages of parenthood: 
Image-Making, Nurturing, Authority, Interpretive, Interdependent, and Departure. 
In Galinsky’s model, Stage 1 (Image-Making)—when parents form images about their 
infants and begin to imagine who and how they will be as parents—begins at concep -
tion and continues through delivery. Parent al mental representations have been hy -
pothesized to become more elaborate as the pregnancy progresses and to continue 
into the postnatal period, when parents en counter the real infant. These mental rep-
resentations can translate into feelings about the pregnancy, infant, and their evolving 
family and might be associated with parents projecting or attributing characteristics 
to the infant pre- or postnatally. These attributions might have a positive or negative 
valence. Even when positive, they may not be accurate and therefore not match the 
real infant. In effect, the imagined infant may or may not reflect the real infant. 
Of note, adults’ own experiences of being parented may affect how they parent their 
own children. Negative experiences of being parented as a child and their influence on 
how these adults parent their own children have been described as “ghosts in the nurs-
ery” (Fraiberg et al. 1975). These “ghosts” can be transmitted from one generation to 
the next and seem to influence parent–child interaction, silently and negatively, in the 
present moment, potentially leading to an intergenerational cycle of abuse and trauma. 
More recently, “angels in the nursery” have been proposed as a positive counterpart, 
representing benevolent parenting experiences (Lieberman et al. 2005b). Early positive 
experiences of parenting in childhood lead to security and self-confidence in adult -
hood. This, in turn, affects adults’ experience of and responses to their own children. 
It can also serve to interrupt negative patterns of interaction or abuse. 
Assessment 
Assessment of parent–infant relationships can start during pregnancy, before the infant 
is born. During the prenatal period, the clinician can get a sense of parental mental rep-
resentations by noticing how the parents talk about their infant and about themselves 
as parents. The clinician can also ask the parents what they think their baby will be like, 
how they imagine their relationship with their baby will be, if they have any worries 
about being a parent, and how the transition to parenthood has been affecting their un-
derstanding and feelings about each other. Later, when the infant is born, the clinician 
can explore whether the experience of having this infant and the transition to parent-
hood have been similar to what they imagined and, if not, how they have been differ-
ent. Within the psychotherap y context, there is space to evaluate and understand

the rates of internalizing and externalizing disorders in childhood (Tooten et al. 2014). 
The assessment phase continues after the child’s birth in terms of analyzing various 
components of the interactions between parents and their infant. Several formal meth-
ods of assessment and observation of parent–infant interactions have been developed. 
Some of the main factors identified and evaluated in these interactions consist of ma-
ternal sensitivity, responsiveness, contingency, dyadic reciprocity, disruption and re-
pair, level of intrusiveness or control, and the pleasure experienced by the parent and 
the infant. Several of the formalized assessment measures include the Crowell Parent– 
Child Interaction Procedure (Crowell et al. 1988); the Face-to-Face Still-Face Procedure 
(Tronick et al. 1978); the Marschak Interaction Method (Marschak 1960); and the Early 
Relational Assessment (Clark 1999). 
The perinatal mental health clinician who is seeing the mother or both parents in the 
postpartum period can also obtain rich information from clinically observing the inter-
actions between parents and their infant, including the quality of the gaze, the touch, 
the vocalization between the parents and th e infant, and the affective tone between 
them. Moreover, the clinician should assess the level of reciprocity within the dyad— 
how the infant responds to various verbal and nonverbal communications and contact 
from the parents, and vice versa. Does the dyad seem to reciprocate, engage, be soothed 
by, and enjoy the interactions? 
Parents and infants go through various cycles and can experience mismatches or dis-
ruptions regularly, even when all is going well between them. Mismatches are often fol-
lowed by repair and eventually get to a point of harmony (Tronick and Gianino 1986). 
Therefore, when observing parent–infant interactions, the goal is not to draw conclu-
sions based on each and every observable interaction but, rather, to consider them as a 
cumulative set of data that , when considered with the information the clinician has 
about the parent’s psychology, psychopathology, relationships, history of trauma, and 
representational mind, can help with better assessment of any relational problems be-
tween parents and infants. 
Touch 
Touch is one of the main ways in which parents and infants connect and communicate. 
The extensive research on the importance and necessity of physical closeness, touch, 
and SSC from the first moments after birth has translated into policies and practices 
that aim at supporting mothers, and increasingly fathers, in providing this experience 
for infants, including those born prematurely (Cleveland et al. 2017; Field et al. 2010). 
When assessing touch and the ways in whic h the infant is physically handled in the 
perinatal setting, one can pay attention to things such as 
• Is the parent able to be physically close to the infant and hold him or her? 
• How does the parent touch and handle the infant? 
• Do the parent and infant appear comfortable, and is the parent attuned to what the 
infant is expressing? 
• Is the quality of touch affectionate and gentle, or intrusive and rough? 
• Does the parent enjoy physical closeness to the infant?

Gaze 
So much of the communication between parents and infants is nonverbal, and gaze is 
one of the main ways that infants communicate with others (Negri 2007). In fact, gaze 
aversion in infants is thought to be one of their main ways of disengaging or signaling 
dislike, distress, or disconnect and at times a way to self-regulate before being able to 
reengage in more direct interactions. Parents can also have difficulty engaging in face-
to-face interaction, which includes periods of mutual gaze with the infant. When as -
sessing gaze in the dyad, some things to notice are 
• Is the parent able to engage in face-to-face interaction with the infant? 
• What is the quality of gaze (e.g., soft and affectionate vs. cold and distant)? 
• Are the parent and the infant able to engage in mutual gaze? 
• What happens when an infant averts his or her gaze? Is the parent able to wait for 
the infant to self-regulate and reengage, or is this too distressing for the parent, who 
must find a way to make eye contact with the infant again? 
• Is the infant able to look at the clinician and make eye contact? 
Of course, these interactions vary based on the infant’s age and developmental stage 
and the infant’s arousal state at the time of observation, such as whether the infant is 
interactive and playful or sleepy and restful. 
Vocalizations 
Parents and infants also communicate verbally and through vocalization. Infants and 
their caregivers engage in vocal turn-taking prior to the development of speech, called 
protoconversations. These protoconversations set the stage for bonding, language, and 
social development of the infant (Yoo et al. 2018). From a very early age, infants start 
vocalizing different sounds, to which parents respond in various ways (Bornstein et 
al. 2015). This sets the stage for contingent interactions within the pair. The rate of con-
versation turn-taking may vary within different communities and cultures; however, 
to date, our knowledge indicates that mothers universally respond to their infant’s vo-
calizations and that infant vocalization is also contingent on maternal verbal commu-
nication. Within these interactions, and more so in the beginning, mothers are more 
responsive to their infants than vice versa (Bornstein et al. 2015). By 6 months of age, 
infants have learned that their vocalization s have social value and that parental en -
gagement can be altered by the level and type of vocalization (Franklin et al. 2014). In 
perinatal mental health settings, the clinician can pay attention to the presence or ab-
sence of any conversational turn-taking, the affective tone of the vocalizations in both 
the parent and the infant, the match or mism atch in the tone and rhythmicity of this 
interaction, and the content (in the parent with a preverbal infant).

sons, it is important to address these issu es because both parents and infants can be 
significantly affected by them. Parents seen in perinatal mental health settings receive 
treatment for their mental health issues, such as depression or anxiety. However, stud-
ies have shown that treatment of matern al psychopathology alone does not always 
translate into better connection and relationship with the infant (Forman et al. 2007). 
When there are concerns about the parent–infant relationship,the psychopathology and 
relational issues ideally should be addressed simultaneously whenever possible. 
Parent–infant psychotherapy focuses on th e relationship between the parent and 
the infant as the main target of treatment and intervention. It is a strength-based, cul-
turally informed model of care in which the therapist identifies and draws on the ex-
isting strengths in the relationship and family system. The treatment consists of a range 
of interventions, some practical (e.g., making sure the parent and infant have the basic 
necessities of a safe living environment), some behavioral (e.g., focusing on the observ-
able interactions between the parent and the infant), and some geared toward the men-
tal representations and emotional dynamics between the parent and the infant. 
Additionally, educating and informing parents about normal development and realis-
tic expectations can be a significant part of treatment. This is especially true for first-
time parents and those who have a child with medical or developmental problems.
