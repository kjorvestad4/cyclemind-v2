Key Points 
• Patients with a history of trauma may face significant challenges in the peri-
natal period. 
• Major health care organizations such as the American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists and the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force encourage 
universal or widespread screening of individuals for intimate partner violence. 
    
 
 
 
 
 
 
   
  
 
   
 
      
 
    
 
 
 
 
     
 
 
511 Trauma and Reproductive Health 
• Reproductive loss and traumatic birth experiences may lead to the develop-
ment of PTSD symptoms, and patients who experience these should be eval-
uated accordingly. 
• Populations who may be predisposed to develop perinatal PTSD include wom-
en with childhood adversity or trauma; women of younger age; women who 
lack support from the father of the baby, partner, or family; single parents; and 
women with comorbid or preexisting psychiatric disorders. 
• Trauma-infor med care is a holistic approach to treatment that considers the 
biological, psychological, neurological, and social impact of trauma and is 
recommended as a universal approach. 
• Management options include medications such as selective serotonin re-
uptake inhibitors and psychotherapy modalities such as cognitive-behavioral 
therapy, cognitive processing therapy, and eye movement desensitization and 
reprocessing.

When it comes to anxiety and reproductive mental health, as a field we still have 
more questions than answers. On the one hand, anxiety disorders are the most com -
mon mental health conditions in women ov erall (Gregory et al. 2020), and anxiety 
disorders complicate pregnancy and the po stpartum period at a rate higher than 
depressive disorders—affecting up to one-fifth of all pregnancies (Dennis et al. 2017; 
Matthey et al. 2003). In addition, perinata l anxiety has been shown to be associated 
with adverse maternal–infant outcomes, comparable with—and perhaps even exceed-
ing—the impact of perinatal depression (Grigoriadis et al. 2013). Nevertheless, perina-
tal anxiety is underdiagnosed and undertreated, and understanding of the clinical 
features, risk factors, pathophysiology, and treatment remains limited (Fallon et al. 
2016b; Goodman et al. 2014a; Harrison et al. 2020; Misri et al. 2015). However, clinical 
and research interest in this area has increased given both its high prevalence and its 
clear impact on maternal and infant outcomes (Grigoriadis et al. 2018).

Ms. EE is a 27-year-old gravida 1 para 0 woman at 14 weeks’ gestation, with no formal 
psychiatric history, referred by her obstetrician due to seeming anxious during a prena-
tal visit. She reports this was a planned pregnancy and that she has been happy when 
she conceived quickly because she worried that she would have difficulty getting preg-
nant. She believes she has always been a wo rrier, but it was not until college that she 
realized that not everybody “obsessed” about things to the same degree she did. She 
reports that her mind is constantly ruminating and never really shuts off. She will real-
ize on her drive home from work that she ha s not even turned the radio on, but it has 
not felt silent because of all the thoughts going through her mind. She will sometimes 
get stuck on one worry for an extended period but often is moving among many: situ-
ations at work, finances, managing the household, upcoming events. 
Since becoming pregnant. Ms. EE has been worried about the health of the baby and 
about potentially having a miscarriage or birth defect. She has not yet told anybody she 
is pregnant in case something happens. At the same time, she has wondered how she is 
going to be able to manage motherhood on top of all her other responsibilities. She has 
become a lot more nervous when her husband leaves for work or social events, request-
ing that he let her know once he has arrive d safely. She has difficulty falling asleep at 
night, often taking 2–3 hours due to review ing her worries. She feels constantly ex -
hausted and has had her doctor complete workups to try and identify the source of her 
fatigue, which have all come back normal. She frequently has teeth grinding, shoulder 
tension, and headaches. She is aware of being less efficient at work because her concen-
tration is impaired by her worries, and her boss recently commented on it. In addition, 
she and her husband have been having increased difficulty because she has been “snap-
ping” at him a lot more about little things, even while she recognizes these things are not 
important. 
It is important to understand the course and treatment of insomnia in pregnancy as 
a related clinical problem. Insomnia is a common perinatal symptom often assumed to 
be part of the normal physiology of pregnancy. However, recent evidence has pointed 
to the detrimental effects of perinatal insomnia itself, such as its relationship to preterm 
birth (Felder et al. 2017). In addition, perinatal insomnia plays an important role in ma-
ternal mental health because it can be both a symptom of an underlying mood or anx-
iety disorder and a trigger for these conditions. There is a complex interplay between 
insomnia, depression, and anxiety—making insomnia an important area to address for 
clinicians who treat women in the perinatal period.
