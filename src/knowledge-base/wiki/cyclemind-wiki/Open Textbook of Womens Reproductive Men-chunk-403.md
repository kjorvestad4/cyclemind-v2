The U.S. Preventive Services Task Force recommends that all women who are ei -
ther planning or capable of pregnancy take a daily supplement containing 400–800 µg 
of folic acid daily. This dose of daily folate—started prior to conception and continued 
throughout pregnancy—has been associated with decreased risk of neural tube de -
fects (Freedman et al. 2018). It is difficult to obtain the recommended amount of folate 
consistently by diet, and in 1998, the FDA mandated fortification of foods. Since then, 
prevalence rates of infants born with neural tube defects have decreased significantly 
(Bibbins-Domingo et al. 2017). Maternal folate status also has other important effects 
on the fetus. A study found that low folate levels in early pregnancy may affect later 
cognitive functioning in children, even in the absence of neural tube defects. Low ma-
ternal folate levels have been associated with a smaller total brain volume and pre -
dicted poorer performance on language and visuospatial assessments in the children. 
Thus, folate insufficiency in early pregnancy may have long-lasting effects on fetal 
brain development (Ars et al. 2019). 
In terms of perinatal depression, folate deficiency has been associated with increased 
antenatal depression scores, and prolonged folate supplementation (>6 months) during 
pregnancy has been associated with a decreased risk of postpartum depression (PPD) 
in Chinese women (Chong et al. 2014; Yan et al. 2017). A small, open-label trial of a pre-
natal supplement in depressed versus heal thy women with a history of depression 
found that folate supplementation may have  some efficacy in both prevention and 
treatment of perinatal depression (Freedma n et al. 2018). In addition, evidence sup -
ports the use of folate as an antidepre ssant augmentation strategy for depression 
during pregnancy (Deligiannidis and Freeman 2010). 
Of note, folate is typically ingested in its natural form (dihydrofolate) in foods or 
in its synthetic form in vitamin supplements, which is then converted to the active 
form (L-methylfolate) by the enzyme methylene tetrahydrofolate reductase (MTHFR).
