Once the decision is made to use a medication during pregnancy, awareness of preg-
nancy-related pharmacokinetic changes and the potential need for dosage adjustments 
is an important component of care that may further reduce risk of relapse and avoid 
unintentional supratherapeutic dosing. Therapeutic drug monitoring can complement 
close clinical monitoring. For medications without a standardized therapeutic range, a 
woman’s individual level at a point of euthymia will allow the physician to establish a 
target medication level throughout and after pregnancy (Deligiannidis et al. 2014). 
General tenets of prescribing during pregnancy are described further in Chapter 8. 
Ms. W is a 32-year-old woman with a history of BDII who was referred by her obstetri-
cian for a consultation at a reproductive psychiatry practice. She is currently pregnant, 
at 34 weeks’ gestation. Her current medications include a prenatal vitamin and lamo -
trigine 200 mg daily. Although her pregnancy has been medically uncomplicated, she 
reports increasing depressive symptoms over the past month. She describes feeling de-
pressed most of each day, takes hours to fall asleep at night, and is not feeling motivated 
to do her normal activities (i.e., cooking, nightly walks with her partner, preparing the 
baby’s room). Ms. W states that although she is not having suicidal thoughts, she does 
notice that her worldview has become more negative in recent weeks, and she spends a 
lot of her time doubting her ability to be a “good mom” to her baby. 
Ms. W reports that previous trials of antidepressants (i.e., selective serotonin reuptake 
inhibitors and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors) have not been effective 
and in many cases “made things worse.” Prior to pregnancy, she was treated effectively 
with lamotrigine 150 mg/day for 3 years. Her treating psychiatrist increased her lamo-
trigine dose to 200 mg/day after onset of these symptoms 3 weeks ago. However, be-
cause she is now on the maximum dose of lamotrigine, he has informed her that there is 
no more he can do pharmacologically to treat her depression. Ms.W tells the reproduc-
tive psychiatrist, “I am so worried that this will get worse. I don’t want to have postpar-
tum depression, have trouble bonding with my baby, or do something to hurt myself or 
my baby. I’m really freaked out and feel like I need help!” 
Lithium 
Lithium is a first-line medication for BD, with an evidence base for acute and main -
tenance treatment of manic and depressive symptoms (Munk-Olsen et al. 2018). It also 
has established antisuicidal properties an d emerging evidence to support its use in 
the treatment and prevention of PPP in wome n at elevated risk (Bergink et al. 2012; 
Poels et al. 2018).
