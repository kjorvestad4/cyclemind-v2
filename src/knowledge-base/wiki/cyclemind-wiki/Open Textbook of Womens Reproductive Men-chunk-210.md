Prolonged Exposure 
PE is based on emotional processing theory; patients learn that trauma-related mem-
ories are not perilous and experience these memories and feelings. The original inter-
vention protocol was described as 9–12 sessions, each 90–120 minutes in length, with 
use of both imaginal and in vivo exposure (Foa and Rothbaum 1998). The patient is 
challenged in a graduated fashion with the goal of achieving success in confronting 
feared stimuli and coping with evoked affect. PE has been found to be safe and effec-
tive in patients with co-o ccurring dissociation, borderline personality disorder, psy -
chosis, suicidal behavior, nonsuicidal self-injury, substance use disorders, and major 
depressive disorder (van Minnen et al. 2012). Patients randomly assigned to exposure 
therapy have significantly greater pre- to posttreatment reductions in PTSD symp -
toms compared with those who receive su pportive counseling, relaxation training, 
and treatment as usual, including pharmacotherapy (Asukai et al. 2010; Bryant et al. 
2003; Schnurr et al. 2007; Taylor et al. 2 003). Several meta-analyses have found that 
exposure therapy is more effective that non-trauma-focused therapies (Bradley et al. 
2005; Cusack et al. 2016; Powers et al. 2010; Watts et al. 2013).

Cognitive Therapy 
Several studies have investigated specific  cognitive therapies in the management of 
traumatic stress after childbirth. A randomized controlled trial of women after emer-
gent cesarean section found success in the use of an online visuospatial task aimed at 
reducing intrusive traumatic memories (Horsch et al. 2017). A four-session grief coun-
seling intervention in women who have experienced stillbirths found reduction in 
PTSD severity (Navidian et al. 2017). An internet-based cognitive-behavioral therapy 
intervention after childbirth was found to reduce PTSD symptoms in the women ran-
domized to receive the therapy (Nieminen et al. 2016). A recent systematic review and 
meta-analysis concluded that many trauma-focused psychological interventions can 
be helpful in reducing symptoms of postpartum PTSD in the short and medium term 
but that evidence is lacking on whether any particular intervention is more effective 
than others and on whether these interventions can help women with long-term re -
covery from clinically significant PTSD (Furuta et al. 2018). 
Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing 
EMDR was initially introduced in 1989 as an eight-phase manualized therapy with the 
objective of assisting patients in accessing and processing traumatic memories while 
bringing them to an adaptive resolution. Initial focus is on evaluation and planning fol-
lowed by desensitization and reprocessing, with end goals of installing positive cogni-
tions, replacing negative ones, and releasing associated somatic tension. The novel aspect 
of EMDR as compared with other trauma-focused therapies for PTSD is its inclusion of 
eye movements. Although experimental studies have shown that horizontal eye move-
ments are associated with a decrease in the vividness, emotionality, and arousal associ-
ated with memories, most of these studies are of healthy participants (van den Hout et 
al. 2001). Empirical evidence supports EMDR as superior to waitlist controls and com-
parable with PE and cognitive-based ps ychotherapies (Davidson and Parker 2001; 
Powers et al. 2010). Although one meta-analysis found no difference between EMDR 
and PE, another found PE to be superior to EMDR (Rothbaum et al. 2005; Taylor et al. 
2003). One small pilot study of four women that investigated EMDR after childbirth 
found it to be effective in the reduction of PTSD symptoms (Sandström et al. 2008). 
Treatment Challenges 
Treatment challenges fall into three broad categories: physician-/provider-specific, 
patient-specific, and system barriers. Additional challenges include the lack of studies 
specific to the management of perinatal PTSD.

Physician-/Provider-Specific 
A qualitative study specific to physicians’ perceived barriers for caring for sexual as-
sault survivors found internal barriers to ca re: fear of a patient disclosing, personal 
attitudes regarding sexual assault, and the emotional toll of caring for sexual assault 
survivors (Amin et al. 2017). Physicians and other providers may be unsure about 
what to do with a positive screen for PTSD. Therapists may lack comfort and compe-
tence in providing evidence-based therapies for PTSD. Similarly, physicians may lack 
knowledge about evidence-based treatment approaches for PTSD, particularly in the 
peripartum period. Myths continually propagate that trauma-focused therapies will 
lead to worsening of PTSD symptoms and dropout, a claim not supported by multiple 
studies (Feeny et al. 2003; Foa et al. 2002).

System Barriers 
Access to evidence-based treatments for PTSD is limited in many parts of the nation 
due to inadequate access to mental health services. In a sample of veterans with pos-
itive PTSD screens, most men (78.7%) and women (89.5%) would consider treatment 
for PTSD symptoms, with the largest proportion of both genders preferring counsel-
ing over a self-help computer program or phone application (Gutner et al. 2018). A 
small randomized controlled trial (N=20) evaluating the use of the PTSD Coach mo -
bile application with and without clinician support found that both the self-managed 
and clinician-managed versions led to PTSD symptom reduction based on the PTSD 
Checklist. The self-managed effect size was 0.41, which increased to 1.4 in the clinician-
supported PTSD Coach group, so although this intervention does not necessitate a cli-
nician, which improves access, involving a clinician improved the effectiveness of the 
intervention (Possemato et al. 2016). Indi viduals with PTSD st emming from prior 
medical treatment or discrimination by physicians or other providers may find com-
ing to the clinic or hospital for treatment of PTSD to be retraumatizing. Telemedicine 
may be a helpful tool for providing evidenced-based trauma-informed treatments to 
these individuals (Gilmore et al. 2016).

Special Considerations: 
Trauma and Psychological Distress Related to 
Voluntary Terminations of Pregnancy 
VTOPs occur when women desire to end pregnancies due to poor timing, lack of re -
sources, fetal anomalies, or fetal reductions. Although many women experience relief 
after VTOP , some may experience posttraumatic stress syndrome (PTSS) or PTSD. The 
etiology of PTSS/PTSD after VTOP remains inconclusive. Some studies have con -
cluded that stress after VTOP stems from premorbid or comorbid mental health dis -
orders and not from the termination itself, suggesting that mental health before VTOP 
predicts mental health afterward (Major et al. 2008; Robinson et al. 2009). Others have 
found that trauma stems from the unwant ed pregnancy and fact ors surrounding it, 
such as lack of partner or financial support (Fergusson et al. 2008; Mota et al. 2010). 
Still others conclude that stress emerges from the termination itself, as well as from a 
combination of variables (Broen et al. 2005; Coleman et al. 2010; Curley and Johnston 
2013). Alternatively, two recent studies found that being denied a VTOP contributed 
to more psychological distress (Biggs et al . 2017) and worse overall health than un -
dergoing one (Ralph et al. 2019). The etio logy of stress disorders after VTOP is con -
sidered multifactorial, stemming from any single to a combination of factors. 
Epidemiology 
In the United States, about 25% of wome n have experienced a VTOP . How many of 
these women go on to experience trauma or stress-related symptoms related to the 
VTOP is unclear. Some studies find no association between VTOP and PTSS/PTSD 
(Wallin Lundell et al. 2013). Others find rates of 12%–30% (Fergusson et al. 2008; Ham-
ama et al. 2010).
