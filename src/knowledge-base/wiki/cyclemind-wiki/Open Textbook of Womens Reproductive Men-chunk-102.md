Support Choices Around Infant Feeding Nonjudgmentally 
Mothers who cannot or choose not to breastfeed may experience both external shame 
and internal guilt about this decision (Lakshman et al. 2009; Lee 2007; Thomson et al. 
2015). Women with depression, anxiety, and self-esteem issues, for example, may be 
more vulnerable to interpreting lactation and nursing difficulties as personal failures. 
Lack of breastfeeding can be experienced as not being a good mother or failing to live 
up to the ideals of womanhood (Taylor and Wallace 2012). Feelings of inadequacy and 
isolation may occur and can prevent a mother  from seeking help, especially because 
many mothers, if they start to use formula,  feel judged by health care professionals 
(Thomson et al. 2015; Schmied et al. 2011). Women who do not breastfeed, regardless 
of their reasons, are at higher risk of encountering shaming and stigma if their social 
network or their medical providers disagree with their decision (McIntyre et al. 2018). 
Clinicians can support women’s choices around lactation by taking a nonjudgmen-
tal approach that acknowledges these challenges while providing education about the 
risks and benefits, as well as helping to engage patients’ support systems and access 
to professional intervention when available. It is important to support each mother in 
her decision-making process regardless of the decision made. The clinician’s role is to 
enhance problem solving and flexibility around breastfeeding. Creating a space in 
which the mother can think about the risks and benefits of breastfeeding can help her 
come to her own decision. Often helpful are discussions around supplemental for -
mula use instead of complete cessation of breastfeeding, or the role of the mother’s 
partner in assisting with feedings. Directing a mother to obtain lactation support, 
whether through a group or a consultant, can be a helpful intervention as well. By di-
rectly addressing any negative feelings a mother has, clinicians can help mitigate the 
impact of these feelings. In addition, while discussing the benefits and potential dis -
advantages of breastfeeding with women, clinicians must be open to the psychosocial, 
cultural, and personal determinants affecting their decisions.

Recognize That Postpartum Sleep Deprivation Is 
Common and Increases Risk of Psychiatric Illness 
Sleep is often the most critical self-care issue a new parent faces and arguably the most 
important modifiable risk factor for post partum psychiatric disorders. Postpartum 
sleep deficiency is common due to both postdelivery physiological changes and the 
mother’s need to care for the newborn (Wilkerson and Uhde 2018). Preexisting sleep 
problems, such as apnea and restless legs syndrome, are often exacerbated during this

Although it is universally recognized th at postpartum women get poor sleep, the 
etiology, specific sleep parameters affected, and numerous physical and emotional se-
quelae of sleep deficiency in  the postpartum period have  only recently begun to be 
comprehensively studied. Women in the first several weeks postpartum have been 
shown to have poorer sleep on several different subjective and objective measures, in-
cluding sleep quality, sleep latency, sleep efficiency, sleep medication usage, and day-
time dysfunction (Christian et al. 2019; Wilkerson and Uhde 2018). Sleep deficiencies 
in the general population have broad consequences, including increasing the risk for 
chronic illness and all-cause mortality (Carroll et al. 2019). In the postpartum popula-
tion in particular, sequelae include increa sed risk of PPD (McEvoy et al. 2019; Wilk -
erson and Uhde 2018), anxiety disorders (Okun et al. 2018), emotional dysregulation 
(Christian et al. 2019), interpersonal problems (Christian et al. 2019), and suicidal ide-
ation (Sit et al. 2015), all of which present the possibility of much greater impairment 
and distress when they occur during the postpartum period.
