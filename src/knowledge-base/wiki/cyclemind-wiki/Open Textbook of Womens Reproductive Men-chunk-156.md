Conclusion 
An integrative approach addresses the physiological, psychiatric, and emotional/spir-
itual aspects of human functioning and wellness. The use of integrative interventions 
is high among women, including during pregnancy and the postpartum period, in part 
due to relative limitations and concerns  regarding pharmacotherapy options during 
these periods. Although pharmacological treatment is effective and often necessary for 
treating perinatal depression and anxiety, many pregnant women may be concerned 
about exposure and potential adverse effects on the fetus. Although far less studied in 
the perinatal population, integrative appr oaches potentially offer women and their 
providers adjunctive or alternative options for treatment of perinatal depression and 
anxiety. Many women, even after discontinuing breastfeeding, elect to continue these 
integrative modalities when they are effective. 
Despite the high prevalence of integrat ive treatment use during pregnancy and 
breastfeeding periods, there remains a paucity of research focused on evaluating its ef-
ficacy and effectiveness. Therefore, empirical evidence is limited regarding the safety 
and benefit of integrative medicine to treat mental health symptoms during pregnancy 
and lactation, particularly approaches that use herbal supplements. Many encourag-
ing studies examining the use of integrative medicine as both a preventive strategy and 
an adjunct to treatment during the perinatal period were discussed in this chapter, with 
recommendations made where adequate data to support efficacy and safety exist.
