Chapter 2,  “Gynecological Conditions and Mental Health.” Endometriosis is another 
common disorder associated with both in fertility and mood symptoms (Macer and 
Taylor 2012). Endometriosis is associated with an increased risk of developing anxiety 
and depressive disorders, and higher levels of pelvic pain are associated with higher 
levels of depressive symptoms (Chen et al . 2016; Gambadauro et al. 2019). Similarly, 
autoimmune thyroid disorders, such as Hashimoto’s thyroiditis, Graves’ disease, and 
autoimmune thyroiditis, are associated with difficulty conceiving, increased rates of 
miscarriage, and mood, energy, and anxiety symptoms (Demartini et al. 2014; Fukao 
et al. 2019; Khizroeva et al. 2019; Samuels 2014). 
Functional hypothalamic amenorrhea (FHA) is the failure of ovulation and men -
struation in women, often associated with extreme physical exercise, disordered eat-
ing, or emotional stress. The condition is associated with increased cortisol levels and 
hypoestrogenism, leading to medical complications such as bone loss and impaired 
cardiovascular function (Shufelt et al. 2017). Certain temperamental traits may be as-
sociated with FHA, including perfectionism, low self-esteem, high need for social ap-
proval, and preoccupation with weight and body image (Giles and Berga 1993; Pentz 
and Rados 2017). In recognition of this association, the Endocrine Society’s clinical 
practice guidelines recommend screening for these traits, as well as anxiety and de -
pressive symptoms, in all women with FHA (Gordon et al. 2017).

Evaluation 
The evaluation of infertility involves a history and physical examination and targeted 
laboratory testing (including but not limi ted to follicle-stimulating hormone [FSH], 
luteinizing hormone [LH], testosterone, estr adiol, and prolactin levels), as well as 
studies to assess for structural abnormalities and ovarian function (American College 
of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019). One commonly performed study is hystero-
salpingography, a procedure used to view the uterus and fallopian tubes by injecting 
radiopaque contrast through the cervix during fluoroscopy (American College of Ob-
stetricians and Gynecologists 2019; Graupera et al. 2015). Male factor infertility is 
most often assessed with semen analysis, which evaluates sperm volume, total sperm 
count, concentration, motility, and morpho logy. Other tests that may be performed 
under specific circumstances include genetic analysis, chromosomal analysis, and tes-
ticular biopsy (Pathak et al. 2020). 
Assisted Reproductive Technology 
The ART process is time intensive and involves exposure to a number of medications 
at specific points in the menstrual cycle. The most common ART procedures, intra -
uterine insemination (IUI) and IVF, are described in the following discussion. 
Intrauterine Insemination 
IUI cycles typically involve ovarian stimulation with oral medications (clomiphene ci-
trate, aromatase inhibitors, or metformin), serial ultrasounds to evaluate and follow 
follicular development, an injection of human chorionic gonadotropin (hCG) to in -
duce follicular maturation and ovulation, and timed insemination with sperm. Some 
women are then prescribed progesterone supplementation, either oral or vaginal, for 
endometrial support (van der Linden et al. 2015). 
In Vitro Fertilization 
IVF cycles are significantly more intensive than IUI protocols, requiring daily visits 
for monitoring, additional procedures and medications (often in jectable), and seda-
tion/anesthesia for the outpatient surgical egg retrieval procedure. The IVF cycle often 
begins with at least 1 month of hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis suppres-
sion with oral birth control pills, with or without gonadotropin-releasing hormone 
(GnRH) agonists. The stimulation phase foll ows, which is typically 5–7 days of con-
trolled ovarian stimulation with injected gonadotropins, with daily assessment of fol-
licle growth and laboratory testing. When several follicles are mature, an injection of 
hCG triggers ovulation. Retrie val of mature oocytes is then done within the next 
2 days under twilight anesthesia in a dedicated operating room. The oocytes are then 
fertilized with partner or donor sperm, and embryos are cultivated in the laboratory 
for several days, often until the blastocyst stage. Embryos may then be transferred into 
the uterus (a “fresh” transfer) or may be frozen and transferred later (Wang and Sauer 
2006). Some patients choose to test the embryos for gene tic abnormalities, a process 
that requires frozen embryos. In most cycles, luteal phase support is added with pro-
gesterone—typically an intramuscular inject ion—and/or estrogen (van der Linden et 
al. 2015).
