BD is known to have one of the largest genetic components of all psychiatric disor-
ders, with heritability as high as 90% (Craddock and Sklar 2013). Despite this, genetic 
studies, including genome-wide association studies, have failed to find reliably spe -
cific genes that are definitively linked to the disorder, suggesting that it is a polygenic 
condition (Craddock and Sklar 2013; McMahon et al. 2010). Repeated identification of 
polymorphisms in L-type calcium channel genes has garnered significant attention, 
and this is particularly relevant given that some calcium channel blockers (including 
lamotrigine) can be effective mood stabilizers. Furthermore, at higher levels, estradiol 
can block L-type calcium channels (Vega-Vela et al. 2017), suggesting a mechanism by 
which low levels might lead to mood instability. 
A number of studies have found increased levels of inflammatory cytokines in pa-
tients with BD, both in the periphery and postmortem in areas of the prefrontal cortex 
(Maletic and Raison 2014; Rosenblat and McIntyre 2016; Thomas et al. 2004). Such in-
flammatory changes are associated with suppressed neuroplasticity and with changes 
in monoaminergic transmission (Raison et al. 2006). Both estradiol and progesterone 
are known to have anti-inflammatory effects, decreasing cytokines in the CNS; how-
ever, this has yet to be studied directly in BD (Frey and Dias 2014; De Nicola et al. 2018). 
Like many psychiatric disorders, uncontrolled BD can have neurodegenerative ef-
fects that are likely mediated by increases in oxidative stress, excitotoxicity, and sup-
pression of neurotrophic factors (Maletic and Raison 2014; Muneer 2016; Newberg et 
al. 2008). This can be seen anatomically, with increases in lateral ventricle size that cor-
respond with the number of manic episod es (Strakowski et al. 2002). Estrogens and 
progestins have well-known neuroprotective effects, such as increasing brain-derived 
neurotrophic factor (Heberden 2017; Singh and Su 2013) and protecting against oxida-
tive stress and excitotoxicity (Frey and Dias 2014; Irwin et al. 2008; Numakawa et al. 
2011), which emphasizes a potential modulating role of these steroids in bipolar patho-
physiology. 
Overall, current research has yet to elucidate a definitive or unique pathophysiology 
of BD during the perinatal period. For BD as a whole, various interrelated mechanisms 
likely contribute to the episodic disturbances in mood that characterize this illness. Fur-
thermore, estrogens and progestins appear to have an overall protective effect on patho-
physiological processes associated with BD, such as inflammation, oxidative stress, and 
suppression of neuroplasticity. Thus, when these hormones are in flux during the peri-
natal period (and during other reproductive transitions), a pathophysiological disinhi-
bition may occur, placing women at greater risk for bipolar episodes.
