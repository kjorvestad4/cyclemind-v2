Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Ms. O, the patient in the case example, is affected by mental illness during the peri -
natal period. Psychiatric illness, most commonly depression, is a serious and treatable 
complication affecting up to 20% of women and has significant short- and long-term 
adverse effects on mother and child (O’H ara and Wisner 2014; Stuart-Parrigon and 
Stuart 2014). Although pregnancy and the postpartum period have historically been 
identified as protective time periods with respect to suicide risk, suicidality remains 
a significant public health issue. In fact , maternal suicide during pregnancy and the 
first year postpartum is a leading cause of death in developed countries (Austin et al. 
2007; Lewis 2007; Oates 2003b; Palladino et al. 2011; Thornton et al. 2013). According 
to North American studies, the reported perinatal suicide rate is 3 per 100,000 live 
births in the United States (Palladino et al. 2011) and 2.58 per 100,000 live births in On-
tario, Canada (Grigoriadis et al. 2017). The prevalence of suicidal ideation or thoughts 
of self-harm ranges from 5% to 14%, and completed suicide accounts for up to 20% of 
postpartum deaths (Lindahl et al. 2005). Although rates of attempted and completed 
suicide are lower in the peripartum period than at other times in a woman’s life, cer-
tain subgroups of women may be at elevated risk—namely those with serious mental 
illness. Furthermore, women acting on suicidality during pregnancy and the postpar-
tum period are more likely to use more lethal, violent means than do nonperipartum 
women, suggesting high intent and higher risk of completed suicide (Grigoriadis et 
al. 2017; Khalifeh et al. 2016; Lindahl et al. 2005; Oates 2003). In two studies that ex -
amined the timing of suicide, findings su ggested that maternal suicides often occur 
later in the postpartum period, most frequently after the sixth month postpartum 
(Grigoriadis et al. 2017; Thornton et al. 2013). 
Most women who have died by suicide in  the perinatal period have been diag -
nosed with affective or anxiety disorders ra ther than psychotic illness (Gold et al. 
2012; Grigoriadis et al. 2017). A large study in the United Kingdom found that almost 
half of those who died by suicide in the perinatal period had been diagnosed with de-
pression. Unfortunately, this study also found that, compared with nonperipartum 
women, women who completed suicide in the peripartum period were less likely to 
be receiving active pharmacological or nonpharmacological treatment at the time of 
their deaths (Khalifeh et al. 2016).

Risk Factors 
Risk factors associated with suicide have been documented in various contexts, but 
literature on risk factors associated with maternal suicide in the perinatal period spe-
cifically is still emerging. As such, it may be helpful to keep in mind the World Health 
Organization (WHO) summary of key risk factors for suicide, which provides a useful 
framework for considering risks from the individual to health systems levels (World 
Health Organization 2014): 
• Individual factors, such as previous suicide attempt, mental disorders, harmful 
use of alcohol, job or financial loss, ho pelessness, chronic pain, family history of 
suicide, and genetic and biological factors 
• Relationship factors, such as sense of isolation and lack of social support, conflict, 
discord, or loss 
• Community factors, such as disaster, wa r and conflict, stresses of acculturation 
and dislocation, discrimination, and trauma or abuse 
• Health systems factors, such as barriers to accessing health care, social factors such 
as access to means, inappropriate media reporting, and stigma associated with ac-
cessing care 
Of these, the strongest risk factor is history of previous suicide attempt(s) (Yoshimasu 
et al. 2008). Findings from studies that reported on risk factors associated with peripar-
tum suicide have some overlap with the risk factors identified in the WHO report but 
also indicate risk factors unique to this population and period of time. Table 10–1 lists 
the risk factors that have been associated with maternal suicide specifically , as synthe-
sized from summaries in two review articles (Mangla et al. 2019; Orsolini et al. 2016). 
Notably, women who are hospitalized for postpartum mental illness appear to be 
at higher risk of suicide, a fact consistent with the increased risk of suicide associated 
with inpatient admission and recent discha rge seen in general psychiatric popula -
tions (Bolton et al. 2015). According to data from a Danish psychiatric registry, the risk 
of suicide among postpartum women following psychiatric admission was 70 times 
higher in the first year postpartum than the rate in the general population (Appleby 
et al. 1998). Some of this risk may be attributable to the type of inpatient care received. 
The United Kingdom Confidential Enquiries Into Maternal Deaths found that recently 
hospitalized postpartum women who completed suicide had been admitted to general 
psychiatry units and separated from their infants; they found no deaths among women 
admitted to specialized mother–baby units (Oates 2003b).

tive Services Task Force (O’Connor et al. 2016), the American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists (2018), and the American Academy of Pediatrics (Earls et al. 2010);

Assessment 
If suicidal thoughts or behaviors are report ed during screening, or if risk factors or 
psychiatric symptoms are identified, thes e should prompt more thorough explora -
tion. A number of clinical practice guidelines exist regarding suicide risk assessment 
and intervention (Bolton et al. 2015), including from the American Psychiatric Asso-
ciation (Jacobs et al. 2003), and these guidel ines can be used in the perinatal period. 
Guidelines for suicide risk assessment consistently recommend gathering informa -
tion about the following (Bolton et al. 2015; Orsolini et al. 2016): 
• Current stressors and psychosocial environment 
• Presence of psychiatric symptoms and history of current illness 
• Current substance use 
• Current medications  
• Current suicidal thoughts, including nature, timing, intent, and persistence 
• Current suicidal plan, if applicable, including lethality, access to means, detail, and 
preparation 
• Current or previous suicide attempt, if applicable, including timing, intent, method,

• Other risk factors

Follow-up questions should include ones about preparation for death, such as the 
creation of a will, stockpiling pills, or securing childcare for children left behind after 
a suicide (Wisner et al. 2012). A complete maternal risk assessment should also in -
clude assessment of thoughts about harming the infant or infanticide (Orsolini et al. 
2016). Barriers to completing a psychiatric risk assessment, especially in a primary 
care setting, are numerous, including reliance on a patient’s subjective report; lack of 
practitioner training, comfor t, and confidence; and time (Bolton et al. 2015; Orsolini 
et al. 2016). When available, information fr om other sources, including previous re -
cords, other health care providers, and family and friends, can be valuable in complet-
ing a psychiatric risk assessment (Jacobs et al. 2003). 
Management 
If suicidality is elicited, then the assessing clinician should proceed to develop a safety 
plan and make a referral for specialized psychiatric assessment (Orsolini et al. 2016). 
Considerations around safety planning, treatment setting, and urgency of referral will 
depend on determination of level of risk; ideally, the treatment plan should be com-
posed of the least restrictive interventions that are likely to be safe and efficacious (Ja-
cobs et al. 2003). For patients who are at high risk and for whom acute safety concerns 
are present, hospitalization is likely to be required. Examples of patients who would 
be considered high risk include those who present immediately after a suicide attempt 
(especially with a history of prior attempts), have a suicide plan with immediate in -
tent, are unable or unwilling to cooperate with assessment or treatment, or present 
with symptoms of psychosis (Jacobs et al. 2003; Orsolini et al. 2016). Treatment plan-
ning should proceed collaboratively as much as possible, with consideration and dis-
cussion of patient preferences, as well as of anticipated benefits and potential risks 
(Jacobs et al. 2003). Clinicians must be aw are of local medical legislation and proce -
dures regarding certification in high-risk cases for which involuntary admission may 
need to be considered. 
Mental health care can reduce suicidality. Part of the risk assessment is to deter -
mine modifiable risk factors that can be mitigated and potential protective factors and 
strengths that can be optimized (Orsolini et al. 2016). Treatment of suicidality should 
also involve close attention to and evidence-based treatment of underlying or comor-
bid psychiatric illness. Evidence has shown that treatment of depression with antide-
pressants and of bipolar disorder with mood stabilizers reduces suicidal thoughts and 
behaviors (Bolton et al. 2015; Jacobs et al. 2003). Pharmacotherapy should also be con-
sidered for perinatal women with serious psychiatric disorders, with thoughtful risk-
risk discussion around the developing fetus’s and/or child’s exposure to untreated
