Clinicians treating pregnant women must  make rational decisions about medica -
tion, discuss this decision-m aking with the patient (who is the ultimate decision-
maker), and document the encounter carefully. The clinician should educate the patient 
about the risk of untreated illness and the risk of medications. It is critical that the pa-
tient understand that a lack of medication use does not ensure a safe pregnancy . Family 
or significant others should also be involved in these discussions whenever possible 
(Friedman and Hall 2013). Informed consent forms are appropriate for electroconvul-
sive therapy, as in the nonpregnant population; however, informed consent documents 
are not necessary for pregnant women regarding medications in general. Rather, it is 
the discussion and shared decision-making that are crucial. 
Psychiatrists should be aware that unplanned pregnancy is more common among 
women with mental illness, and many women become pregnant while being treated 
with psychotropic medications. Thus, it is important to discuss the potential for preg-
nancy and options for birth control with all women of childbearing age who enter 
psychiatric treatment, regardless of whether they report being sexually active. Medi-
cations known to be relatively safe in pregnancy should be considered when prescrib-
ing for women of childbearing age—even when women report no current plans for 
pregnancy. If a patient becomes pregnant un intentionally, she should know to be in 
contact with the psychiatrist , and the psychiatrist should not make knee-jerk deci -
sions in these cases. Consultation with colleagues is invaluable in lawsuit prevention. 
Lawsuits may occur related to negative birth outcomes. One must remember, how-
ever, that lawsuits can also occur because of wrongful death by suicide or infanticide 
because appropriate treatment was not given in the perinatal period (Friedman and 
Hall 2013). A bias toward failure to treat occurs when physicians are concerned only 
about acts of commission, in which the trea tment provided by the doctor leads to a 
negative outcome, rather than acts of omission—that is, the lack of treatment of the 
illness (Friedman 2015; Miller 2009). One should be aware of this possible bias and en-
sure that, in discussions with patients, one considers and carefully describes both the 
risks of treating and the risks of not treating.
