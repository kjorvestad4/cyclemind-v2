In the postpartum period, women with schizophrenia have an increased risk of re-
lapse, with potentially devastating effects for both the mother and the infant. This in-
creased risk is likely multifactorial in etiology, with multiple contributing factors such 
as hormonal shifts after delivery, sleep de privation, and stress (Rochon-Terry et al. 
2016). A large population-based cohort st udy by Munk-Olsen et al. (2006) reported 
that women with schizophrenia had a fivefold increased risk of first-time hospital ad-
mission in the first 30 postpartum days, and this increased risk continued to a lesser 
degree for the first 6 postpartum months. A follow-up study by the same group look-
ing specifically at readmission rates for women with known preexisting psychiatric 
diagnoses showed similar results, with a relative risk of approximately fivefold in the 
first 20 postpartum days and an elevated risk over 6 months (Munk-Olsen et al. 2009). 
Although both of these population studies reported that women with schizophrenia 
had a rate of hospitalization that was considerably lower than postpartum women di-
agnosed with bipolar affective disorders, these findings strongly support the asser -
tion that the postpartum period is a hi gh-risk time for women with schizophrenia. 
Risk factors for relapse in the postpartum period among women with schizophrenia 
include acute symptoms during pregnanc y, medical or psychiatric comorbidity, 
higher maternal age, poor social support , lower socioeconomic status, and reduced 
engagement in antenatal care (Rochon-Terry et al. 2016).
