Anxiety Disorders 
Most scholarly work on perinatal loss focuses more on bereavement, depression symp-
toms, and trauma-related disorders and less on the development of specific anxiety 
disorders in the aftermath of loss (Armstrong and Hutti 1998; Blackmore et al. 2011). 
In a large prospective cohort study of 537 women who miscarried and 111 who had an 
ectopic pregnancy, moderate or severe anxiety occurred in 25% of patients (Farren et 
al. 2016). Although most studies look at an xiety symptoms in general, a few studies 
have examined specific disorders. Most have included OCD among the anxiety disor-
ders, as it was classified prior to DSM-5. Geller et al. (2004) compared 227 women who 
miscarried with 230 matched community control subjects and found that 3% of those 
who miscarried met criteria for OCD, an eight times greater risk than for women in 
the community. In contrast, in a study comparing 399 women with perinatal loss and 
232 women with live births at 9 months post-loss or postpartum, women with peri -
natal loss had higher odds of screening positive for generalized anxiety disorder and 
social phobia, but not panic disorder and OCD, when adjusting for confounding vari-
ables (Gold et al. 2014). Rates of generalized anxiety disorder and social phobia were 
19% and 44%, respectively, in bereaved women compared with 7% and 22%, respec-
tively, in nonbereaved women (Gold et al. 2014).
