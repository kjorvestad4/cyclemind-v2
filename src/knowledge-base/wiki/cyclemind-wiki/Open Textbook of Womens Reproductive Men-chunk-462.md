she was certain the waiters were collaborating to harm her baby, her mother brought Ms. 
Y into the emergency department. She was admitted to psychiatry and was treated in-
patient for 7 days and then discharged into outpatient care. She improved acutely on a 
regimen of lithium 900 mg/day, quetiapine  300 mg twice daily, and lorazepam 1 mg 
nightly. Although she has never experienced a symptom recurrence, she has remained 
on this regimen since that time. She reports that she would very much like to have a sec-
ond child but is scared to discontinue this regimen that has been so helpful to her: “I 
know I need this medicine, but my doctor told me it isn’t safe during pregnancy. I just 
need to know what I can take so that I can have a healthy baby and not get sick again.” 
For women with a history of psychotic illness isolated to the postpartum period, 
current evidence suggests that the initiation of prophylactic medication immediately 
after delivery is a highly effective strategy. This treatment strategy has the added ben-
efit of avoiding fetal exposure to medication and should be done in combination with 
close monitoring throughout pregnancy an d anticipatory guidance regarding plans 
for infant feeding. Lithium has the most evidence to support its efficacy for use in this 
clinical setting (Bergink et al. 2012; Wesseloo et al. 2016). 
For all women at elevated risk for PPP , an individualized and comprehensive post-
partum prevention plan can be a useful tool. Components of this plan may include an 
obstetrical birth plan, as well as a plan fo r infant monitoring in collaboration with a 
pediatrician if indicated. Education ab out warning signs for PPP and appropriate 
emergency numbers should be provided to both patients and family members. If ap-
propriate, patients can be counseled about the option of providing a release of infor-
mation for ongoing contact with family members or a psychiatric advanced directive. 
Direct discussion of strategies to priori tize sleep and maintain a regular circadian 
rhythm is indicated (Wesseloo et al. 2016). 
Other Management Steps 
The patient’s breastfeeding status may influence the treatment strategy for PPP . Specif-
ically, while she is acutely psychotic or hospitalized, the mother’s contact with the in-
fant may be limited. If she desires to breastfeed, inpatient treatment providers should 
offer the use of a breast pump unless this is clearly contraindicated by safety concerns.
