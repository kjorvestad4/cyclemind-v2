Postpartum Concerns 
Studies show that mothers with EDs are at risk of early cessation of breastfeeding. 
These women are also at greater risk of depression and anxiety during pregnancy and 
in the postpartum period (Franko et al. 2001). For many women with ED, the postpar-
tum period is particularly challenging because they struggle to adjust to a new body 
and motherhood, and this often leads to an exacerbation of symptoms (Zerwas et al. 
2014). A neonatal complication seen in mothers with BN is “slimming,” in which they 
project their body image onto the baby and perceive the baby as “fat.” This mother 
then may restrict feedings for or induce vomiting in the baby (Cardwell 2013). 
Menopause and Eating Disorders 
Research shows that the prevalence of EDs in midlife has been increasing, and up to 
4.6% of midlife women ages 40–60 years may meet full criteria for diagnosis of an ED 
(Mangweth-Matzek et al. 2014). Perimenopause can be a window of vulnerability for 
the development or exacerbation of EDs due to reproductive hormone changes (Baker 
and Runfola 2016). The prevalence of BED is higher in the older population, whereas 
the incidence of AN seems to decrease (Baker and Runfola 2016). 
Differential Diagnosis 
When evaluating someone for a possible ED, consider other medical and psychiatric 
conditions that could be contributing to symptoms. Among pregnant women, peripar-
tum nausea and appetite changes, as well as hyperemesis gravidarum, should be con-
sidered. Other medical diagnoses can also mimic symptoms of EDs, as documented in 
Table 22–5.
