Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
OCD has a prevalence of 0.7%–2.3% in the general population, a lifetime prevalence 
of up to 3.5%, and is the 10th leading cause of disability worldwide (American Psy -
chiatric Association 2013; Kessler et al. 2005; McGuinness et al. 2011; Murray and Lo-
pez 1996; Stein et al. 1997). The illness is likely underdiagnosed, and in the general 
population, as many as 25% of people ex perience obsessive and compulsive symp -
toms that do not rise to the level of diagnosis but can still be clinically significant and 
impairing (Kessler et al. 2005; Stein et al . 1997). Furthermore, prevalence rates vary 
based on region, population, and study methodology (McGuinness et al. 2011). OCD 
is more prevalent in adult women than in adult men (Fontenelle et al. 2006). 
Times of reproductive transition, including menarche, perimenopause, and, most 
commonly, the perinatal period, represent high-risk periods for a subset of women for 
whom changes in gonadal ho rmone levels convey increa sed susceptibility to mood 
and anxiety symptoms (Abramowitz et al. 2010b). For example, 20%–49% of women 
with OCD report an exacerbation of symptoms in the premenstrual period (Uguz et al. 
2011). Other studies show that 9% of women report onset of OCD symptoms in the per-
imenopausal period, and 47% of patients with preexisting OCD report exacerbation of 
symptoms in perimenopause (Labad et al. 2005; Uguz et al. 2007; Vulink et al. 2006). 
Many women trace the onset of their OCD symptoms to these periods (Abramowitz et 
al. 2003; Buttolph and Holland 1990; Labad et al. 2005; McGuinness et al. 2011; Nezi-
roglu et al. 1992; Speisman et al. 2011), and the prevalence of OCD at these times is re-
ported in various studies as higher than the prevalence for OCD in the general adult 
population (Andersson et al. 2003; Labad et  al. 2005; McGuinness et al. 2011; Speis -
man et al. 2011; Sutter-Dallay et al. 2004; Uguz et al. 2007, 2011; Vulink et al. 2006). 
In addition to triggering exacerbations, times of major reproductive transition of -
ten mark the onset of OCD in female patien ts. Onset of OCD has classically been as -
sociated with puberty, but some studie s have shown the association to menarche 
specifically (Labad et al. 2005). Retrospective studies have shown that 13%–22% of fe-
male patients with OCD attribute onset of the illness to menarche (Russell et al. 2013). 
One study showed that patients with compulsive hoarding behaviors—which can be 
associated with OCD when the hoarding is a compulsive, compensatory behavior in 
response to obsessions that are typical of OCD (American Psychiatric Association 
2002; Labad et al. 2010; Pertusa et al. 2008)—w ere more likely to report onset of symp-
toms within 12 months of menarche (Labad et al. 2010). 
There is robust evidence of increased prevalence of both new-onset and exacerbated 
OCD in the postpartum period. Prevalence rates for pregnancy-related OCD have 
ranged from 0.2% to 3.5% (Andersson et al. 2003; McGuinness et al. 2011; Sutter-Dal-
lay et al. 2004; Uguz et al. 2007) in various studies, and for postpartum OCD these rates 
have ranged from 2.3% to 9% (McGuinness et al. 2011; Uguz et al. 2007; Zambaldi et 
al. 2009). A 2013 meta-analysis showed that  OCD pr evalence estimates wer e higher 
among pregnant and postpartum women compared with the general population, and 
in this study the prevalence increased linearly from pregnancy to the postpartum pe-
riod (Russell et al. 2013). Retrospective studies have shown that 2%–39% of female pa-

Risk Factors 
Female sex and times of major reproductive transition are two major risk factors for 
the development of OCD. Other risk factors have been identified for OCD in general 
and for perinatal OCD specifically. For general OCD, family and twin studies show 
increased risk of OCD in families, and thus a genetic component to the illness is sug-
gested (Vasconcelos et al. 2007). Environmen tal factors also appear  to play a role in 
the development of OCD and are discussed la ter in this chapter. Gestational factors 
in pregnancy and parturition can influence the offspring’s risk of developing OCD 
later in life. Obstetrical complications, in particular prolonged labor and edema 
during pregnancy, can be a risk factor for the development of OCD in the offspring 
(Vasconcelos et al. 2007). Delivery-related complications, such as the use of forceps,
