Treatment 
Before deciding on a particular mode of treatment for depression during the perinatal 
period, it is important to consider the severity of a woman’s illness. The great fear that 
many clinicians have about prescribing in pregnancy can often override considerations 
about the most appropriate treatment for the patient’s illness. As discussed in Chapter 
8, “A Clinical Approach to Psychiatric Diagnosis and Treatment in Pregnancy,” there 
is no completely risk-free decision when a woman with depression is pregnant. Some-
times the best way to balance her risks will be to prescribe medication, but sometimes 
it will not. A woman’s ability to access care is also an important part of this equation. 
In many communities, low-income women have little access to quality psychotherapy, 
for example, which may affect the decision of what treatment to use. One recent study 
showed that Black women have higher rate s of hospital treatment for PPD, which 
likely reflects the fact that Black women may have less access to the outpatient care that 
might prevent hospitalization (Chan et al. 2020). 
In cases in which issues of access do not force the clinician’s hand, the primary con-
sideration about whether to use medication has to do with the severity of illness and 
prior response. Table 13–7 offers clinical tips for how to determine whether medica -

Psychopharmacology 
As discussed in Chapters 8 and 9, considerable controversy remains about treating de-
pression in pregnancy and during breastfeeding. We have no randomized controlled 
trials about medication risks and very few studies about medication efficacy, alterna-
tive treatments, or dosage alterations that may be required due to metabolic changes 
in pregnancy. Decisions about treatment must therefore rely on observational evidence 
only. Although there is some evidence of risks associated with antidepressant usage, 
those risks appear to be few now that studies (primarily of selective serotonin reuptake 
inhibitors [SSRIs]) are being conducted wi th appropriate control groups and con -
trolling for appropriate confounders. The substantial body of evidence we have con-
cerning the risks of untreated psychiatric illness to both mother and child (Field et al. 
2006, 2010; Li et al. 2009; Orr et al. 2007; Zuckerman et al. 1989) must be weighed 
against any known risks of medication.
