Preterm birth. Several studies have found an association between antidepressant 
usage and preterm birth (i.e., birth prior to 37 weeks’ gestation). Recent research, in-
cluding large database studies and high-quality meta-analyses, has upheld this asso-
ciation, finding a small but significant risk of preterm birth with antidepressant usage 
(Huybrechts et al. 2014b; Ross et al. 2013) even when controlling for depression. One 
study that directly compared the effects on labor of depression with those of antide -
pressant exposure found them to be about equal (Wisner et al. 2009). In most studies, 
the average reduction in gestational age is about 3–4 days (Yonkers et al. 2012). In this 
case, then, there does appear to be a statistical association with risk, but when making 
treatment decisions, we must consider whether this is a clinically important associa-
tion and how it compares with the risks of no medication. Another recent study con-
firmed that untreated depression is associated with significantly increased risks of 
preterm birth and low birth weight (Jarde et al. 2016), meaning that women with de-
pression, regardless of whether they take medications, may be at risk for preterm birth, 
thus indicating that this is a risk we cannot change. Thus, the decision about medica-
tion use should be made using other bases. 
Neonatal Outcomes 
Hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis reactivity. Several studies have shown that 
babies born to mothers depressed in preg nancy have elevated cortisol and possibly 
other biochemical changes reflective of their mothers’ depression (Fernandes et al. 
2015; Field et al. 2006; Oberlander et al. 2008; Osborne et al. 2018; Talge et al. 2007). In 
some cases, studies have shown altered infant reactivity to stress associated with these 
changes. The elevations in cortisol have been shown to persist at least until adolescence 
(O’Donnell et al. 2013). Although some evidence indicates that SSRI exposure may al-
ter this biochemical balance (Pawluski et al. 2012), it is unclear whether antidepressant 
exposure in depressed women would alter this balance for better or worse, and con -
siderable additional research is needed before drawing conclusions. 
Neonatal adaptation. One newborn outcome thought to be associated with antide-
pressant use in pregnancy is poor neonatal adaptation syndrome. The first observa -
tion of “withdrawal” from antidepressants in infants was published in 1973 (Webster 
1973), but it is now uncertain whether the behavioral syndrome observed in about 30% 
of babies whose mothers have taken SSRIs in the third trimester is actually withdrawal 
(rather than toxicity), leading to the current descriptive term, “poor neonatal adapta-
tion syndrome.” There is no consistent definition of this syndrome nor any specific 
measurement tool or blinded rating scale,  but repeated observations led the FDA in 
2004 to require that labels for both SSRI and serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhib-
itor (SNRI) antidepressants contain a warnin g that third-trimester exposure to these 
drugs may be associated with “respiratory  distr ess, cyanosis, apnea, seizur es, tem-
perature instability, feeding difficulty, vomiting, hypoglycemia, hypotonia, hyperto-

Long-term effects. Cognitive and behavioral effects of antidepressant exposure on 
children have been well studied, and there is no evidence of associated IQ deficits or 
long-term behavioral problems (Nulman et al. 1997, 2002, 2012). Depression in preg-
nancy and the postpartum period is associ ated with both cognitive and behavioral 
problems in children, including both internalizing and externalizing disorders, that are 
both acute and long lasting (Capron et al. 2015; O’Connor et al. 2007; O’Donnell et al. 
2013; Smith-Nielsen et al. 2016). Alarming newspaper headlines have linked the use 
of antidepressants in pregnancy to autism based on initial studies that found an in -
crease in risk (Croen et al. 2011; Rai et al. 2013). Subsequent studies that considered 
confounds more carefully have found no increased risk. Women with depression have 
a higher risk of giving birth to a child with autism, but the medication itself does not 
appear to confer additional risk (Andrade 2017a, 2017b; Hviid et al. 2013; Sørensen et 
al. 2013). 
When making treatment decisions, clinicians must always weigh the risks discussed 
here against the risks to both mother and child of untreated illness in pregnancy (Table 
13–8). For the mother, relapse into a mood episode (including possible suicidality) is 
substantial, and a considerable body of evidence points to increased risks for the child 
from AD (Field et al. 2006; Grace et al. 2003). Moreover, untreated depression in preg-
nancy is the biggest risk factor for PPD, which has known and devastating effects on 
children and families (Grace et al. 2003). Impaired bonding can lead to insecure at -
tachment (Hipwell et al. 2000), and PPD has also been associated with increased colic, 
impaired parenting behavior, increases in harsh discipline, impaired emotional and 
language development, impaired attention and cognition, and long-term behavioral 
problems (Akman et al. 2006; Flynn et al. 2004; McLearn et al. 2006). 
Management During Pregnancy 
As discussed in Chapters 8 and 9, any decision about medication use in pregnancy and 
lactation must be individual; for each woman, the balance of risk of medications versus 
risks of untreated illness will be different. The FDA’s new pregnancy and lactation la-
beling rule offers extremely useful—altho ugh lengthy—information about the risks of 
individual drugs. The defunct FDA categories (A, B, C, D, X) are not useful for under-
standing the risks of antidepressants; clinic ians tend to assume that this is a graded 
scale, with B being better than C, but in reality drugs are assigned to category B when 
there is insufficient information about risk to determine whether they belong in group 
A (no risk to humans) or group C (evidence of risk to humans). Because so much about 
medication use in pregnancy is unknown, it  is wiser to choose an older drug about

In general, all SSRIs and SNRIs can be used in pregnancy and lactation, as can tri-
cyclic antidepressants. (Please see the Appendix for a detailed chart of dosage sugges-
tions and pregnancy and lactation considerations.) We have fewer data on bupropion 
and mirtazapine, although the growing amount of evidence on these two drugs is re-
assuring. (Mirtazapine has the added benefits of being helpful for both sleep and nau-
sea.) Newer SSRIs, such as vortioxetine and vilazodone, have no evidence for their use 
in pregnancy, so they should not be considered first-line in a patient with no prior drug 
trials. They may, however, be the best choice for patients who have failed trials with 
other SSRIs and for whom these medications are known to provide stability, or for pa-
tients who present already stable on one of these medications postconception. 
Because we have little evidence about polypharmacy and no evidence of increased 
risk with increased doses, it is wise to try to maximize the dose of a single medication 
rather than use low doses of several medica tions. In addition, because the risks are 
comparable, it is wisest to use a drug to which the woman has responded in the past 
rather than choosing a drug that may appear to have lower risk (Payne 2019). There is 
no “one safe drug” for pregnancy; the choice depends on the woman’s history. Although we 
have more evidence of safety for sertraline and fluoxetine than for many others, the 
risk for all older SSRIs is low. There is slightly more evidence of risk with paroxetine 
(although, as described earlier, recent studies do not support this risk). It therefore 
makes sense, if starting a drug in a patient with no prior antidepressant exposure, to 
avoid paroxetine as the first choice—but again,  it makes little sense to switch a patient 
away from paroxetine if that is the drug that has kept her stable in the past. 
Dosage considerations in pregnancy are also important. Many clinicians will be in-
clined to decrease doses in the third trimester to lower the risk for neonatal adaptation 
syndrome, but there is no evidence supporting this approach. In fact, because phar -
macokinetic changes and volume of distribution in pregnancy mean that serum con-
centrations of most drugs will drop by 40% –50% by the end of the third trimester, 
many women will need increased rather than decreased doses. There are currently no 
clinical guidelines on how to manage this, but many experienced clinicians will raise 
doses prophylactically or closely monitor symptoms in late pregnancy so that dosage 
adjustments may be made expediently if necessary.
