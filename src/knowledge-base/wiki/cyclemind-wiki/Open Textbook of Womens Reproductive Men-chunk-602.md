Conclusion 
In summary, the clinical guidelines for perinatal OCD are similar to those for nonperi-
natal populations. In cases of mild to moderate OCD, nonpharmacological treatments 
such as CBT (incorporating ERP) can be used. In more severe cases, or those cases in 
which the patient responded well to a medication in the past, pharmacological strate-
gies are recommended, with SSRIs as first-line options. Tricyclic (e.g., clomipramine) 
and other antidepressants are second-line strategies if SSRI trials are not effective. Rea-
sonable adjunctive agents include second -generation antipsychotics. Other somatic 
treatments could be considered for treatment-refractory cases, but the evidence base in 
perinatal populations to support these interventions is very limited. 
Key Points 
• Obsessions are common in the perinatal period. 
• Perinatal obsessions can include intrusive thoughts of harm coming to the 
child, but having such thoughts does not necessarily mean the mother is at 
risk of harming her child. 
• Distinguishing between ego-syntonic and ego-dystonic thoughts is the key to 
understanding the risk of harm.
