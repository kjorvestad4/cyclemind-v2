Key Points 
• Depression is common both in pregnancy and postpartum, with an elevated 
biological risk in the early postpartum period. 
• A personal or family history of depression is the strongest single risk factor of 
perinatal depression. 
• Suicide is a leading cause of peripartum mortality, and all decisions about 
treatment in this period must bear that in mind. 
• Although changes in reproductive hormones may be involved in triggering 
postpartum depressive symptoms, little evidence shows that levels of estrogen 
and progesterone differ between women with depression and healthy women; 
rather, some women appear to be vulnerable to shifting hormones. 
• Good screening tools, including the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale, 
are important but cannot take the place of a clinical diagnosis. 
• Women with mild or moderate symptoms can often be treated successfully with 
psychotherapy and other nonpharmacological approaches, but differences in 
access to care may affect women’s ability to engage in such treatment. 
• Women with moderate to severe symptoms usually require pharmacological 
treatment. 
• Although very few randomized controlled trials have been published, increas-
ingly sophisticated statistical techniques have resulted in rigorous data from 
observational trials over the past few years. 
• Most data indicate that the risks of untreated illness often outweigh the risks 
of medication, so clinicians should no t hesitate to prescribe medication for 
women who are ill, if indicated.

Integrative medicine is a medical subspecialty recognized by the American Board 
of Physician Subspecialties. It combines conventional medicine with evidence-based 
complementary medicine, which includes but is not limited to lifestyle approaches, 
nutrition, supplements, and mind-body approaches. This subspecialty recognizes the 
integration between ancient and conventional medicine in the approach to the treat -
ment of disease. Additionally, integrative medicine has a holistic health approach and 
emphasizes wellness and prevention of illn ess. Many integrative medicine interven-
tions have been studied for the treatment of women and are often used along with con-
ventional treatments (Clarke et al. 2015). 
Approximately one-third of American adults reported using at least one integrative 
health approach in the past year (Clarke et al. 2015). Data show that women use inte-
grative treatments more often than men, and use is common throughout the reproduc-
tive years (Adams et al. 2009; Steel et al . 2020). Although data exist on integrative 
treatments for depression in pregnancy, they are less robust than the literature on psy-
chopharmacology and psychotherapy for the treatment and prevention of perinatal de-
pression. Nonetheless, women are highly likely to use integrative strategies, and 37% 
of pregnant women and 28% of postpartum women report use in the past 12 months, 
compared with 40% of nonpregnant/nonpostpartum women. Mind-body practices 
were the most common modality reported (25% of pregnant women). Diets and di -
etary supplements (excluding vitamins and minerals) were used during the postpar-
tum period by 8% of women (Birdee et al. 2014).
