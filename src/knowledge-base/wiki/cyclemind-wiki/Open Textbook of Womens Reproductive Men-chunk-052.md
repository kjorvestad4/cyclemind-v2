• There is no specific therapeutic window for lamotrigine, so we cannot say what 
the level should be for effectiveness. However, the decrease from a level at 
which the patient was stable may mean that her new symptoms have something 
to do with that decrease. 
• Lamotrigine is metabolized by glucuronidation (uridine diphosphate glucu -
ronosyltransferase [UGT] 1A4) and is renally excreted; estrogen is a known in-
ducer of UGTs and is also glucuronidated by 1A4. The interaction has resulted 
in increased renal excretion of lamotrigine. Evidence supports increased clear-
ance of lamotrigine due to exogenous, but not endogenous, estrogens, but thus 
far there is little evidence about the clinical effect of this increased excretion. 
• If the patient wishes to continue the oral contraceptive, it will be important to 
increase the dose of lamotrigine to achieve remission. The patient may benefit 
from continuous dosing of the contraceptive to minimize fluctuations in lamo-
trigine level. 
• The patient may also choose to switch to a progestin-only method or a method

Mood Effects of Contraception 
Recent studies analyzing the relationship between hormonal contraception and mood 
disorders are overall reassuring, with adverse mood effects being possible but infre -
quent (Böttcher et al. 2012; Sc haffir et al. 2016). A few studies have found an associa-
tion between hormonal contraception use and lifetime diagnoses of depression, 
particularly among adolescents (Gregory et al. 2018; Skovlund et al. 2016; Zettermark 
et al. 2018). Some studies suggest that the mood effects of hormonal contraception are 
related to individual sensitivity to the pr ogestin component (Merki-Feld et al. 2017; 
Svendal et al. 2012). However, multiple ot her studies have not found such an effect 
(McKetta and Keyes 2019; Pagano et al. 2016; Worly et al. 2018). In fact, some studies 
have found that current use of combined hormonal contraception improves mood, a 
finding that may relate to the benefits of  contraception, which include reproductive 
and menstrual control, decrease in premenstrual symptoms, and improvement in 
conditions such as acne and gynecological pain (Svendal et al. 2012; Toffol et al. 2012). 
The risk of contraception producing adverse mood effects must be weighed against 
the possibility that unintended pregnancy itself may lead to psychological distress or 
clinical depression. A large percentage of pregnancies in the United States are un -
planned or mistimed. An anal ysis of more than 100,000 mothers between 2009 and 
2011 found that only 32% of the women in the sample were desiring pregnancy at the 
time of conception (Gauthreaux et al. 2 017). Among individuals with mental health 
disorders, up to 92% of pregnancies may be unplanned, because individuals with af-
fective disorders may be more prone to risky sexual behaviors with ineffective con -
traceptive use (Adan Sanchez et al. 2019; Cunningham et al. 2016; Hall et al. 2012, 
2013a, 2014). Several studies have found th at mothers with unplanned pregnancies, 
even if the pregnancy is ultimately accept ed, experience higher rates of postpartum 
depression (Abajobir et al. 2016; Barton et al. 2017; Gauthreaux et al. 2017). Most of 
these studies cite a two- or threefold increase in postpartum depression risk. Preexist-
ing affective disorders, social support, and race may all influence the strength of this 
association (Barton et al. 2017; Suh et al. 2016).

Psychiatrist’s Role in Contraceptive Access 
For many women with mental health disorders, the psychiatrist will be the most con-
sistent point of medical contact in their lives. For this reason, psychiatrists play a cru-
cial role in helping women understand th e interplay of reproductive health and 
mental health and are positioned to be an active force in enabling reproductive choice. 
Although the placement of IUDs and admi nistration of contraceptive injections 
may be out of the purview of the general psychiatrist, most psychiatrists should be able 
to prescribe combined hormonal contraception with some basic education.
