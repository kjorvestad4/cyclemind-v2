Insomnia 
Ms. FF is a 34-year-old woman, gravida 1 para 0, who is currently 36 weeks pregnant. 
She presents to her reproductive psychiatrist for a routine follow-up visit. She has a his-
tory of GAD that, in the past, prior to conception, was well controlled with oral escitalo-
pram 10 mg/day. Prior to conception, she tapered down and off of the escitalopram and 
initiated CBT to good effect. However, she reports that in the past couple of weeks she 
has been having a hard time with her sleep. She reports that “no matter what I do, it is 
impossible to find a comfortable position,” and this keeps her awake. Ms. FF also reports 
that even when she falls asleep, she has to get up several times during the night to go to 
the bathroom, feeling like “having to go to the bathroom all the time makes it impossible 
to sleep.” She denies snoring or apneic events and states that her wife has not reported 
anything unusual in her sleep. She feels tired during the day, but because it is so difficult 
to find a good position, it is hard to take a nap. She asks about whether any medication 
can help with insomnia and wonders if her sleep difficulties mean that her anxiety is 
returning. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Insomnia is a major public health problem that affects millions of individuals yearly. 
Insomnia affects between 10% and 30% of the general adult population (Roth 2007). 
A recent analysis demonstrated that 50%–80% of patients with a psychiatric diagnosis 
had difficulty falling or staying asleep in a given year (Smith et al. 2002). Sleep diffi -
culties have been associated with numerous categories of mental health conditions, 
including MDD, bipolar disorder, anxiety disorders, substance use disorders, PTSD, 
and primary psychotic disorders (e.g., schizo phrenia) (Seow et al. 2016). Overall, in -
somnia is more common in women than me n (Hilty et al. 2009). Women are particu-
larly vulnerable to insomnia during times of reproductive hormonal transitions, such 
as during pregnancy and perimenopause or menopause (Chaudhry and Susser 2018; 
Nowakowski and Meers 2019). Questionnaire- based estimates of insomnia in preg -
nancy range from 30% to 60% (Baker et al . 2009; Bei et al. 2012; Facco et al. 2010; 
Román-Gálvez et al. 2018). The third trimester in particular tends to be the time of the 
most marked sleep disturbance, with approximately 60% of women experiencing sig-
nificant difficulties initiating and maintaining sleep (Sivertsen et al. 2015). Addition -
ally, 25% of perimenopausal women and 30% of postmenopausal women reported 
getting a good night’s sleep only a few nights per month or less (Baker et al. 2009). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors include increasing age, female sex, and comorbid medical conditions (Rie-
mann et al. 2017). In terms of perinatal insomnia, a recent prospective cohort study of 
nearly 500 pregnant women identified several risk factors for insomnia in pregnancy, 
including pregestational insomnia, high BMI, and less frequent daily exercise (Román-
Gálvez et al. 2018).

Screening 
To our knowledge, screening for insomnia in the general or perinatal population is not 
common practice. However, several scales have been validated for the clinical assess-
ment of insomnia and are in widespread use. A sleep diary can provide a subjective 
assessment of bedtime, nighttime awaken ing, awake time, and sleep quality (Roth 
2007). The Pittsburgh Sleep Quality Index (B uysse et al. 1989), although primarily 
used for research purposes, is a seven-item self-report measure of quality and patterns 
of sleep that has been validated in pregnant women (Skouteris et al. 2009). The Insom-
nia Symptoms Questionnaire is a 13-item self-report questionnaire that was validated 
in more than 100 women at 12 weeks’ gestation (Okun et al. 2015). 
Diagnostic Criteria 
The diagnostic criteria for insomnia recently underwent major revision with the in -
troduction of DSM-5. In prior editions of  DSM, insomnia was characterized as pri -
mary or secondary to a mental health condition. However, given the complexity of the 
relationship, the potential for a bidirectional etiology, and the difficulty in establishing 
a clear cause and effect pattern, the pr imary/secondary nomenclature was removed 
in DSM-5, which recommends that “insomnia disorder” be coded whenever diagnos-
tic criteria for it are met, regardless of a coexisting psychiatric disorder. The diagnostic 
criteria for insomnia disorder are summarized in Box 19–1.

Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
Anyone who has experienced insomnia will immediately recognize the frustrating 
constellation of symptoms: inability to fall or stay asleep, a subjective sense of poor 
sleep quality or nonrestorative sleep, dayt ime sleepiness or functional impairment, 
and regular or sustained occurrence despite adequate opportunities for sleep (Roth 
2007; Schutte-Rodin et al. 2008). 
Course and Prognosis 
Insomnia can have profound consequences for physical health, mental health, and 
quality of life. In the general adult population, chronic insomnia is associated with an 
increased risk of cardiovascular disease (e.g., hypertension, myocardial infarction), 
metabolic disorders (e.g., obesity, diabetes mellitus), and all-cause mortality (Ferraro 
et al. 2014; LeBlanc et al. 2009; Taylor et al. 2003). It is not surprising that sleep distur-
bance, given its extensive comorbidity an d association with ps ychiatric diagnoses, 
also affects the severity of mental health conditions. For example, sleep disturbance— 
independent of the psychiatric syndrome itself—is associated wi th a negative impact 
on the course of the mental health condition, including more severe symptoms, higher 
disorder severity, lower level of functioning, lower quality of life, and less robust re -
sponse to psychiatric treatment (Kallestad et al. 2012). Quality of life consequences in-
clude risk of accidents, de creased work productivity and concentration, increased 
absenteeism, and impaired daily performance (Roth 2007; Walsh 2004). 
Perinatal insomnia is associated with increased rates of gestational diabetes (Facco 
et al. 2017), higher rates of prolonged labor and operative delivery (Lee and Gay 2004), 
and preterm birth (Felder et al. 2017; Okun et al. 2011) and has also been linked with 
near-miss motor vehicle accidents among ne w parents (Malish et al. 2016). Perinatal 
sleep disturbances are also associated wi th perinatal depression and anxiety (Swan -
son et al. 2011; Wilson et al . 2019), mood changes immediat ely after birth (Bei et al. 
2010), and postpartum psychosis (Sharma and Mazmanian 2003).

Differential Diagnosis and Assessment 
Insomnia is a clinical diagnosis. The assessment of insomnia should include a clinical 
interview with detailed sleep history, review of sleep questionnaires and any available 
sleep diaries, review of physical and mental health history, and a physical examination 
(Riemann et al. 2017). Additional measures such as blood tests, electroencephalogra-
phy, or electrocardiography should be done as indicated, and polysomnography 
should be done if another sleep disorder, such as a sleep-related breathing disorder, is 
suspected (Riemann et al. 2017). In most cases, changes in sleep during pregnancy are 
physiologically normal, and excessive daytim e sleepiness is the predictable result. 
However, a careful assessment is necessary because insomnia can be a physiologically 
normal part of pregnancy, a medical complication of pregnancy (e.g., sleep-disordered 
breathing), a symptom of a mental health condition (e.g., anxiety), a trigger of a mental 
health condition, or a combination of these (Table 19–4). 
Both mood and anxiety disorders can present with sleep disturbances and insomnia 
and should be carefully evaluated. Mood disorders such as MDD and bipolar disorder 
can both present with profound disruption s in sleep. In addition, anxiety disorders 
such as GAD, panic disorder, OCD, and PTSD can all present with perinatal insomnia. 
At the same time, sleep deprivation can be a profound trigger of psychiatric conditions, 
such as with mood disorders. Substance intoxication or withdrawal can also present 
with sleep disruption. 
Medical diagnoses specific to pregnancy and the postpartum period should be ruled 
out. Pregnancy may predispose women to sleep-disordered breathing, which is charac-
terized by snoring, gasping, and apneas. Obstructive sleep apnea-hypopnea due to ob-
struction of airflow in the upper airway is more common in pregnant women than in 
nonpregnant women, with a prevalence of roughly 10%–25% (Nodine and Matthews 
2013). Distinct physiological and hormonal changes in pregnancy, such as weight gain, 
edema of mucus membranes (from elevated levels of estrogen), and displacement of 
the diaphragm from an enlarging uterus, can all contribute to breathing-related sleep 
disorders (Hashmi et al. 2016). Significant predictors of sleep-disordered breathing in 
pregnancy are age, BMI, and frequent snoring; patients with these risk factors may rep-
resent a subpopulation in need of further evaluation (Louis et al. 2018). 
Another important diagnosis is restless legs syndrome, which has an increased in-
cidence in pregnancy (estimated to be up to 30%), with symptom severity highest in 
the third trimester (Manconi et al. 2004). To the relief of sufferers, symptoms typically 
remit after delivery. Restless legs syndrome can be associated with pregnancy-related 
iron deficiency, which can be detected by a ferritin level to assess iron stores. Un -
treated restless legs syndrome increases the risk of depressed mood, and related sleep 
deprivation is linked with prolonged labor, increased pain perception, higher discom-
fort during labor, and increased rates of operative delivery (Chang et al. 2010). 
Pathophysiology 
To consider the pathophysiology of insomnia, it is helpful to review basic mechanisms 
of sleep itself. Normal sleep consists of two fundamental neuronal mechanisms. The 
homeostatic mechanism of sleep induces a pressure to sleep after every hour awake, 
and the circadian rhythm mechanism drives alertness during the day and sleepiness 
at night, based on light intensity as the main external “timekeeper” (Anderson 2018).

Both total sleep time and circadian rhythms are subject to significant variations over 
the course of our lives, including greater fragmentation over time—one of the reasons 
the prevalence of insomnia tends to increase with age (Ohayon et al. 2004). Short-term 
insomnia generally arises in association with acute psychosocial stressors, and the 
sleep disruption tends to resolve when the stressor resolves (Ellis et al. 2012). However, 
the development of chronic insomnia requires not just the acute stressor but also mal-
adaptive behaviors that can worsen sleep quality and disrupt the homeostatic and cir-
cadian rhythm drivers for sleep (Anderso n 2018). Paradoxically, some of the most 
common exacerbating factors are hypervigilant efforts around sleep (e.g., increased at-
tention to and ruminations about sleep), wh ich can lead to hyperarousal, which is a 
physiological stress response, and to worsened insomnia (Espie et al. 2006). 
The relationship between sleep difficulties and mental health conditions overall is 
complex and may be bidirectional (Krystal 2012). For example, at least 40% of patients 
with insomnia will eventually be diagnosed with an anxiety disorder, and persistent 
insomnia is one of the strongest risk factors for depression in the adult population (An-
derson 2018; Baglioni et al. 2011). A recent review suggested that sleep disturbance 
may be a potentially transdiagnostic mechanism across psychiatric diagnoses, and that 
sleep disturbance and emotion regulation may be neurobiologically linked (Harvey et 
al. 2011). Emerging evidence implicates autonomic hyperarousal as a pathway to in-
creasing the risk of depression (Fernandez-Mendoza and Vgontzas 2013). 
The etiology of perinatal sleep disturbance is also multifactorial. Many dynamic as-
pects of pregnancy, such as changes in hormone levels, physiology, metabolism, and 
psychology, likely contribute to sleep disturbances, particularly as pregnancy pro -
gresses (Bei et al. 2015; Hashmi et al. 2016). There is also evidence of extensive comor-
bidity between mental health conditions and insomnia in pregnancy, including anxiety 
and depressive symptoms; insomnia in pregnancy can be either a sign of a mental 
health condition, a trigger for one, or both (Chaudhry and Susser 2018; Swanson et al. 
2011; Wo%ynczyk-Gmaj et al. 2017). In the first trimester, sleep changes are most likely 
related to rapid escalations in reproductive hormone levels, particularly progesterone, 
which can lead to daytime sleepiness and sl eep interruptions at night (Hashmi et al. 
2016). Progesterone and its metabolites interact with the GABA A receptors, which is 
thought to effect these changes in sleep (Lancel et al. 1996). Sleep in the first trimester 
is also affected by overall fatigue and the nausea and vomiting familiar to many 
women who have traversed the first trimester of pregnancy (Lee et al. 2000). By the sec-
ond trimester, the total amount of sleep time is reduced and subjective reports of inter-
rupted sleep increase, in part due to factors such as nocturnal gastroesophageal reflux 
(Driver and Shapiro 1992). By the third trimester, <2% of pregnant women report no 
nocturnal awakenings (Hedman et al. 2002).  Common contributing factors include

Treatment 
As with many aspects of perinatal insomn ia, the optimal approach to treatment is 
evolving. This section focuses primarily on CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) and pharmaco-
therapy for insomnia in pregnancy and postpartum, although other methods are cur-
rently under active investigation. 
Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia 
In the general adult population, high-quality evidence supports the use of behavioral 
interventions such as CBT-I as the first-line treatment of  insomnia (Benz et al. 2020; 
Morin et al. 2009; Schutte-Rodin et al. 2008; Trauer et al. 2015). National and interna-
tional clinical practice guidelines an d national organizations have recommended 
CBT-I as the first-line treatment for insomnia in adults (Baker et al. 2014; National In-
stitutes of Health 2005; Qaseem et al. 2016; Riemann et al. 2017). A recent systematic 
review found that CBT-I was effective for adults with comorbid psychiatric illnesses, in-
cluding patients with depression, anxiety, PTSD, and substance abuse disorders (Taylor 
and Pruiksma 2014). CBT-I is usually delivered over six weekly sessions, typically re-
quiring 4–8 hours of therapy along with weekly sleep diaries to track progress (An -
derson 2018). 
In addition, evidence supports CBT-I specifically for perinatal insomnia (Bei et al. 
2019; Lee et al. 2016; Tomfohr-Madsen et al. 2017). A recent randomized controlled 
clinical trial revealed greater reduction in insomnia severity and a greater and more 
rapid insomnia remission (Manber et al. 2019). There was also an improvement in self-
reported sleep time above and beyond wh at was noted with objective measures of 
sleep; the authors hypothesized that CBT-I may help women feel less distressed about 
sleep difficulties, including those caused by increased pregnancy-related physical dis-
comfort (Manber et al. 2019). This is important, because self-reported sleep has been 
shown to be a stronger predictor of maternal mental health than objective measures of 
sleep itself (Bei et al. 2010). CBT-I is also preferred by pregnant patients, as compared 
with pharmacotherapy and acupuncture (Sedov et al. 2017). A summary of the steps 
involved in CBT-I and suggested modifications in pregnancy is provided in Table 19–5.

Management Steps. Postpartum women prescribed sleep aids should be counseled 
to have another adult available to care for their infants overnight due to the potential 
for difficulty waking after taking sedating medication and so that they are able to ob-
tain uninterrupted sleep. Women taking sedating medications should also receive spe-
cific counseling regarding safe sleep practices with their infants (Task Force on Sudden 
Infant Death Syndrome 2016). 
Conclusion 
Perinatal anxiety represents a paradox in perinatal mental health. On the one hand, 
perinatal anxiety disorders are extremely common, affecting more than 20% of all preg-
nancies—a prevalence higher than that for anxiety in the general population and also 
higher than for perinatal depression (Dennis et al. 2017; Matthey et al. 2003); moreover, 
a much larger group of women may have subthreshold symptoms. In addition, perina-
tal anxiety is associated with known adverse maternal–infant outcomes that are com-
parable with and perhaps even exceed the impact of perinatal depression (Grigoriadis 
et al. 2013). On the other hand, perinatal anxiety remains underdiagnosed and under-
treated (Misri et al. 2015), and our understanding of its etiology, identification, and op-
timal treatment is still evolving (Fallon et al. 2016b; Goodman et al. 2014a; Harrison et 
al. 2020). Research that separates anxiety from depression or distress is minimal. 
Perinatal insomnia presents a similar paradox. Insomnia is so common as to be 
nearly ubiquitous, and sleep disturbance is often considered simply a result of preg-
nancy itself. However, perinatal insomnia has a significant impact as well, including 
adverse outcomes such as preterm birth (Felder et al. 2017). It can also be both a trigger 
for and a symptom of mental health conditions such as depression and anxiety. There-
fore, insomnia also represents an important but little-understood area to address for 
clinicians who treat women in the perinatal period. 
There are many unanswered questions and thus future directions for research in 
perinatal anxiety and insomnia. Basic questions remain, such as the best operative defi-
nition for perinatal anxiety, which screening tool best captures this population, under-
standing the comorbidity between anxiety and depression, and developing optimal 
treatment for perinatal anxiety specifically. A greater understanding of the physiology 
of sleep in pregnancy and the nature of sleep disturbances and insomnia will also be 
of benefit, given both their high prevalence and their clear impact on maternal and in-
fant outcomes (Grigoriadis et al. 2018).
