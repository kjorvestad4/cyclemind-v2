at one-tenth the standard male dose and applied to a skin surface, typically the back 
of the calf, buttock, or upper outer thigh. Pellets, intramuscular injections, oral formu-
lations, and compounded formulations are not recommended to treat postmenopausal 
HSDD. Systemic testosterone administered according to these recommendations car-
ries the risk of androgenic effects, such as mild increases in acne and body and facial 
hair growth in some women. 
Oral testosterone is associated with adverse effects on lipid profiles, whereas non-
oral testosterone therapies (in dosing just described) have not shown to have signifi-
cant adverse short-term effects on lipid profiles, although recommendations regarding 
cardiovascular health effects of physiological doses of testosterone are not generaliz-
able to longer-term therapy or higher-risk populations (Davis et al. 2019). The only rec-
ommended indication for testosterone therapy is HSDD in postmenopausal women; 
however, research also indicates positive effects of testosterone therapy on arousal, or-
gasm, and sexual distress in this population (Davis et al. 2019). 
The ISSWSH process-of-care guidance document provides a summary of additional 
off-label pharmacological (e.g., bupropion, buspirone) and nonpharmacological treat-
ments with evidence of efficacy for FSDs and GSM in postmenopausal women (Parish et 
al. 2019).
