Chapter 11, “Stress in Pregnancy,” for a detailed discussion 
of the mechanisms through which parental stress gets transmitted to the offspring. 
It has long been appreciated that maternal stress is associated with adverse infant 
outcomes. We now know that maternal stress is associated with changes in fetal brain 
development in ways that may alter the stress response of infants throughout their lifes-
pan. For example, stress during pregnancy is associated with long-lasting changes in in-
fants’ HPA axis; in humans, these alterations are detectable at 6 months, 5 years, and 
even 10 years after birth (Lupien et al. 2009). Perinatal stress tends to affect the HPA axis 
and how sensitive it is to stress in the child's environment. One mechanism is by having 
the perinatal environment modify the chromatin around the genes relevant to the stress 
response, known as epigenetic modification, which can lead to multigenerational transfer 
of altered stress response. In addition, prenatal exposure to maternal stress is also asso-
ciated with modifications of at least two brain areas critical for emotional regulation: the 
size of the hippocampus and the reactivity of the amygdala (Qiu et al. 2015; Rifkin-
Graboi et al. 2015). Exposure to significant prenatal stress may even affect infants’ later
