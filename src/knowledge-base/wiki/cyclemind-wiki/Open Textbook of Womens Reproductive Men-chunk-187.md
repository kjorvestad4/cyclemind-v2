In the postpartum period, women with schizophrenia have an increased risk of re-
lapse, with potentially devastating effects for both the mother and the infant. This in-
creased risk is likely multifactorial in etiology, with multiple contributing factors such 
as hormonal shifts after delivery, sleep de privation, and stress (Rochon-Terry et al. 
2016). A large population-based cohort st udy by Munk-Olsen et al. (2006) reported 
that women with schizophrenia had a fivefold increased risk of first-time hospital ad-
mission in the first 30 postpartum days, and this increased risk continued to a lesser 
degree for the first 6 postpartum months. A follow-up study by the same group look-
ing specifically at readmission rates for women with known preexisting psychiatric 
diagnoses showed similar results, with a relative risk of approximately fivefold in the 
first 20 postpartum days and an elevated risk over 6 months (Munk-Olsen et al. 2009). 
Although both of these population studies reported that women with schizophrenia 
had a rate of hospitalization that was considerably lower than postpartum women di-
agnosed with bipolar affective disorders, these findings strongly support the asser -
tion that the postpartum period is a hi gh-risk time for women with schizophrenia. 
Risk factors for relapse in the postpartum period among women with schizophrenia 
include acute symptoms during pregnanc y, medical or psychiatric comorbidity, 
higher maternal age, poor social support , lower socioeconomic status, and reduced 
engagement in antenatal care (Rochon-Terry et al. 2016).

Limited studies have investigated common delusions during pregnancy and post-
partum. Chandra et al. (2006) investigated 108 women with a severe mental illness in 
the postpartum period who were admitted to  an inpatient psychiatric unit in India. 
They noted that more than half (53%) of the women had delusions related to the infant. 
When characterizing these mothers further, the authors found that the mothers who 
had persecutory delusions related to the infant were more likely to demonstrate affec-
tionate behavior and appropriate competency in caring for the basic needs of the infant 
but were also more likely to be disturbed or agitated if they were separated from the 
baby. Mothers who had delusions that the baby was ill-fated, a devil, or not their own 
child were more likely to demonstrate abusive behavior toward the baby. Overall, 
mothers who had infant-related delusions were more likely to have abusive incidents 
and to be assessed by staff as unsafe in caring for their baby (Chandra et al. 2006). 
Because psychotic mothers with delusions or hallucinations about their infants may 
be reluctant to share these beliefs, a psychiatric evaluation should include sensitive in-
quiry into this content. One method is to start by asking the mother about any concerns 
for the safety of her children  and to follow-up with more  focused questions as indi -
cated. Careful consideration is required to discern delusional thoughts involving infant 
harm from obsessional thoughts common in perinatal OCD. Postpartum obsessional 
thoughts are more likely than delusions to be ego-dystonic in nature and experienced 
with intact reality testing and good insight. Although postpartum women with obses-
sional thoughts are at low risk for harming their infants (Abramowitz et al. 2003; Speis-
man et al. 2011), active psychotic symptoms focused on the fetus or infant may prompt 
a lower threshold for inpatient hospitalization due to elevated risk (Friedman and Res-
nick 2007). For further details on the distinction between delusions and obsessions in
