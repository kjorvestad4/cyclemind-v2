Conclusion 
Schizophrenia is an uncommon but serious psychiatric illness associated with pro -
found disability across the spheres of personal, social, and occupational functioning. 
Gender differences in the phenomenology, course, and prognosis of this illness have 
been described for several decades, and investigations stemming from the estrogen hy-
pothesis of schizophrenia have contributed to the understanding of estrogen’s ubiqui-
tous role in the brain and complex interaction with neurotransmitter systems important 
to cognition, mood, perception, and behavior. 
Sociocultural changes and advances in psychopharmacology have contributed to an 
increase in fertility rates among women with  schizophrenia, such that most of these 
women will have at least one pregnancy. Because they are likely to require pharmaco-
logical treatment throughout a pregnancy and may be especially vulnerable to decom-
pensation in the postpartum period, the “general tenet” in reproductive psychiatry of 
the risk-risk equation is especially pertinent in both preconception planning and treat-
ment planning during and after pregnancy. Finally, because most mothers with schizo-
phrenia do parent their child(ren), psychiatrists must incorporate consideration of the 
child into safety and risk assessments and proactively consider parenting support as a 
component of comprehensive care.
