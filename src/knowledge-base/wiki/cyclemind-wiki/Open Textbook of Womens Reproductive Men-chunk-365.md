Screening Methods 
The most important aspect of screening for perinatal depression as a routine compo-
nent of obstetrical care is that it be done. Implementing some method of screening is 
vastly preferable to not implementing it, so clinicians should not hesitate to put screen-
ing procedures into practice even if the ideal cannot be achieved. Screening can and 
should be carried out by any clinician wh o treats a perinatal woman, including not 
only obstetrical clinicians but also primary care and mental health clinicians. By far, the 
most commonly used tool is the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS; Figure 
13–1), which was created by a perinatal ps ychiatrist and has b een validated in both 
pregnancy and the postpartum period and translated into more than 50 languages 
(Cox et al. 1987).

enough to not be a burden; many patients can complete it in 5 minutes or less. (See Ta-

and ethnic groups. Although the factor structure of the scale appears to differ slightly 
among ethnic and racial groups (Chiu et al. 2017; Hartley et al. 2014; King 2012), no 
data have suggested that it is not valid as a screening tool in any of these populations. 
Although other screening tools lack the advantages described for perinatal patients, 
they can nonetheless be useful and are worth administering if the EPDS is not feasible 
(as in electronic medical records that have just one screening tool built in). Both the 
Beck Depression Inventory and the Zung Self-Rating Depression Scale are comprehen-
sive rating scales for depression, but at more than 20 questions each—including con -
stitutional symptoms—they may be burdensome to complete and have not been 
validated in this population (Schaefer et al. 1985; Wang and Gorenstein 2013). 
Screening in and of itself is not enough. A positive screening result on the EPDS or 
any other scale is not a diagnosis of depression; it must be followed by a clinical eval-
uation. Misuse of screening measures as diagnostic tools can lead to potential harm 
by leading the clinician either to underval ue a patient’s report of depressive symp -
toms or to misdiagnose a patient who does not meet criteria for a major depressive 
episode. In fact, studies on the EPDS show that nearly all patients with false-positive 
screening results, or patients who screened positive but did not have a diagnosis of 
depression on further evaluation, did ind eed have a diagnosable mental health con-
dition (Milgrom and Gemmill 2014).
