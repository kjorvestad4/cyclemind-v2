Key Points 
• Alcohol is a known teratogen. Fetal alcohol spectrum disorders are the most 
common and preventable cause of intellectual disability in the United States. 
• Aside from the use of benzodiazepines for management of alcohol withdraw-
al, medications for alcohol use disorder (disulfiram, acamprosate, naltrexone) 
are not commonly used in pregnancy due to lack of data regarding reproduc-
tive safety. 
• Tobacco use in pregnancy is associated with adverse pregnancy and bir th 
outcomes. Women with major depressive disorder are more likely to smoke. 
• Data supporting the efficacy of most smoking cessation treatments in pregnant 
women are as yet limited, but the harms of tobacco use are such that nicotine 
replacement therapy and bupropion are reasonable to consider in conjunction 
with behavioral interventions for smoking cessation. 
• Cannabis use in pregnancy has been associated with adverse pregnancy and 
birth outcomes and adverse neurobehavioral outcomes in children, although 
causality is difficult to determine due to risks of confounding in observational 
studies. 
• Women should be encouraged to avoid cannabis during pregnancy and while 
breastfeeding and should be offered other treatment options if they indicate 
using cannabis to address common pregnancy-related symptoms, such as 
nausea or insomnia. 
• The standard of care for opioid use disorder during pregnancy is medication-
assisted treatment with either buprenorphine (in buprenorphine or buprenor-
phine/naloxone formulations) or methadone; opioid detoxification during preg-
nancy is not recommended.

• Although neonatal opioid withdrawal can occur in infants exposed to medica-
tion-assisted treatment in utero, these neonatal symptoms can be managed 
both nonpharmacologically and through medication treatment. 
• Use of stimulants such as methamphetamine or cocaine in pregnancy is as-
sociated with adverse pregnancy and birth outcomes. Pharmacological treat-
ments are not yet available, so comprehensive behavioral treatment and 
psychosocial supports are recommended for pregnant women with cocaine 
or methamphetamine use disorders. 
• Negative stereotypes about women of color and their likelihood of substance 
use affect how clinicians assess and respond to substance use disorders in 
different racial/ethnic groups, with Black women referred to the criminal justice 
system at much higher rates. Clinicians must be vigilant about these biases.

CHAPTER 2 2 
Eating Disorders 
Robin Valpey, M.D. 
Jyoti Sachdeva, M.D. 
Joy E. Moel, Ph.D. 
Eatingdisorders (EDs) are a group of diagnoses characterized by disturbance in eat-
ing that leads to impairment in health and psychosocial functioning (American Psy -
chiatric Association 2013). Disordered eating can lead to numerous medical sequelae 
across many organ systems, including reproductive pathways. Although most of these 
pathophysiological sequelae are reversible with treatment of the disorder and accom-
panying decrease in disordered eating behavior and weight restoration, some can be 
life threatening (Treasure et al. 2010). Given the high risk of mortality in this popula-
tion, providers must be aware of the diagnostic criteria and potential medical compli-
cations associated with EDs. 
Overall, EDs are more common among women than men, with large sex differ -
ences seen in both anorexia nervosa (AN) and bulimia nervosa (BN). Because they 
most commonly present in the reproductive years, the interplay between EDs and the 
reproductive cycle is an important componen t of this diagnosis. In this chapter, we 
review the common presentations and complica tions of EDs, risk factors, etiologies, 
and treatment options, with particular focu s on presentation in the perinatal period 
and implications for reproductive function. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
The most common forms of ED during a woman’s reproductive years include AN, BN, 
and binge eating disorder (BED), and thes e disorders are associated with decreased 
quality of life, increased health care costs, and high mortality rates (Arcelus et al. 2011).

Although their onset is highest during adolescence and young adulthood, the preva-
lence of recurring ED later in life highlights the need for attention to symptoms at older 
ages as well (Ward et al. 2019). Binge eati ng, rather than restrictive patterns, and in -
creased medical morbidity characterize midlife or perimenopausal EDs (Mangweth-
Matzek and Hoek 2017). EDs at any age are highly comorbid with medical and other 
psychiatric conditions, most notably anxiety disorders (up to 80%), depressive disor-
ders (up to 80%), and substance use disorders (up to 40%) (Erskine and Whiteford 
2018; Grilo et al. 2009; Kaye et al. 2004). 
AN has a peak incidence for women between ages 15 and 19 years (Attia and Walsh 
2009). The lifetime prevalence is 0.4% for women, and AN occurs much more fre -
quently in women than men, at a ratio of 10:1 (Hoek 2006). Approximately 20% of 
women with AN have a chronic course, whereas 33% improve with partial or residual 
features, and 46% go on to fully recover (Steinhausen et al. 2009). Although relatively 
rare, AN must be taken seriously because weight loss and malnutrition can lead to 
medical comorbidities and life-threatening conditions (Westmoreland et al. 2016). In 
fact, the mortality rate is approximately 5% for AN, and the standardized mortality 
ratio (SMR; observed deaths in population) is 5.9, which is higher than most other 
psychiatric disorders and the general popula tion (Arcelus et al. 2011). Medical com -
plications from limited nutritional intake and suicide are the most frequent causes of 
death (Arcelus et al. 2011). 
BN is a more common diagnosis than AN, with a lifetime prevalence rate estimated 
between 1.5% and 3% (Hoek 2006). This di sorder also occurs much more commonly 
for women than men, again at a ratio of 10:1. Peak incidence in women occurs be -
tween the ages of 16 and 20 years, and although recovery rates for the short-term suc-
cess of treatment are good (50%–70%), relapse rates are high and have been shown to 
range from 30% to 85% at 6 months to 6 years posttreatment. The SMR is 1.9, with ap-
proximately 20% of these deaths due to suicide (Arcelus et al. 2011). 
BED is somewhat more common in women than AN or BN, with a lifetime preva-
lence of approximately 3%. Peak incidenc e for women occurs between ages 18 and 
20 years (Stice 2016). Sex differences in BE D appear smaller than those observed in 
AN and BN, and BED is more likely to be associated with obesity (Javaras et al. 2008). 
Racial, Cultural, and Socioeconomic Considerations 
A 2018 U.S. study using a nationally representative sample of more than 36,000 sub -
jects indicated race/ethnicity differences among those with an ED diagnosis (Coffino 
et al. 2019). Hispanic and non-Hispanic Bl ack respondents had a significantly lower 
lifetime risk of AN compared with non-Hispanic whites; lifetime risk of BN was sim-
ilar across racial/ethnicity groups. Lifetime risk of BED was also lower among non-
Hispanic Black respondents compared with  non-Hispanic white subjects, although 
this was not found in the 12-month data and was also not seen in Hispanic respon -
dents. In addition, higher income was associated with greater odds of having an ED 
diagnosis. However, an Australian study found that symptoms of EDs distributed 
equally across socioeconomic status (Mulders-Jones et al. 2017). 
Some of the reasons for these differences may be related to disparities in screening, 
diagnosis, and treatment among different populations. Studies indicate that individ-
uals of color are less likely than their white counterparts to be asked by a doctor about 
ED symptoms (Becker et al. 2003), receiv e an ED diagnosis (Sonneville and Lipson

eating than their sexual-majority peers (McClain and Peebles 2016). The data on trans-
gender individuals are much more limited, with some studies showing the presence 
of EDs in this population (one focusing in particular on male-to-female transgender 
individuals) but little data to show whether either prevalence or presentation differs 
from that of the cisgender population (Jones et al. 2016; Khoosal et al. 2009). 
Special Considerations in the Peripartum Period 
The peripartum can be a high-risk time for the onset or a relapse of eating-disordered 
behavior, but it can also be an opportunity for recovery because women are often mo-
tivated to make changes to benefit the health of the baby. In fact, remission rates during 
pregnancy have been shown to range anywhere from 29% to 78% (Micali et al. 2007). 
These women have decreased concern for weight and shape and decreased restricting 
or purging behaviors. However, pregnancy can also trigger a relapse of symptoms in a 
subset of patients, and at least 5% of women still meet criteria for an ED during preg-
nancy (Easter et al. 2013). Physiological changes during pregnancy, such as increased 
food cravings or morning sickness, can be a trigger for BN or BED. Hyperemesis grav-
idarum, a severe type of nausea and vomiting during pregnancy, has also been associ-
ated with a worsening of ED symptoms during the peripartum period (Lingam and 
McCluskey 1996). In the postpartum period, stress related to the transition to mother-
hood and body changes may contribute to increased EDs (Andersen and Ryan 2009). 
Pica is generally a rare ED, but it must also be considered during the peripartum 
period because it is the most common ED to occur during pregnancy. Pica is the crav-
ing and chewing of substances that have no nutritional value, such as ice, clay, soil, or 
paper. A recent meta-analysis estimated the prevalence of pica during pregnancy to 
be 27.8% (Fawcett et al. 2016). 
Like during other times of a woman’s life, EDs in the peripartum period are asso-
ciated with additional mental health concerns (Santos et al. 2017). Significant comor-
bidity exists between EDs and perinatal depression, generalized anxiety disorder, 
OCD, and PTSD during the peripartum period (Andersen and Ryan 2009).

Risk Factors 
Risk factors for EDs are multifactorial an d include various biological, psychological, 
environmental, and sociocultural factors, as outlined in Table 22–1. Biological risk fac-
tors include genetics and hormonal factors, type I diabetes mellitus, and a family his-
tory of ED or other mental health diagno ses (Culbert et al. 2015). Psychological risk 
factors include body image dissatisfaction, internalization of the “thin ideal,” and a 
personal history of depression, anxiety, and substance use disorder (Ulfvebrand et al. 
2015), as well as personality traits and disorders including neuroticism, perfection -
ism, impulsivity, obsessive-compulsive personality disorder, and borderline person-
ality disorder (Culbert et al. 2015; Mazzeo et al. 2006). Anxiety disorders have been 
found to have an onset in childhood prior to the onset of AN or BN (Kaye et al. 2004). 
Environmental risk factors include family an d social factors, such as family discord, 
family dieting, and parental criticism (Fairburn and Harrison 2003). Sexual, physical, 
or emotional abuse puts women at risk for an ED, and sexual abuse has been identified 
as a specific risk factor for BN (Fairburn and Harrison 2003; Sanci et al. 2008). Socio -
cultural risk factors include media exposure to the thin ideal, weight stigma, and soci-
etal endorsement of weight loss and dieting (Culbert et al. 2015; Martínez-González et 
al. 2003). Clearly, the etiology of ED is complex and involves a multitude of individual 
and environmental risk factors. 
Special Considerations in the Peripartum Period 
EDs may be triggered in at-risk women during times of profound body changes and 
significant life change, both of which occur during the peripartum period (Andersen 
and Ryan 2009). It is also important to note that the risk factors for pica appear to be 
somewhat different from those for AN, BN, and BED. Stress and nutritional deficien-
cies, such as iron deficiency, are primary considerations that may increase risk for pica 
during pregnancy (Fawcett et al. 2016). In addition, geographical region, socioeco -
nomic status, and education also appear to impact pica prevalence, because rates were 
higher in Africa and among those of lower education attainment and socioeconomic 
status (Fawcett et al. 2016).

Female Reproductive Lifespan 
Given the close association among weight, body fat, and the HPG axis, EDs can have 
a major impact on women throughout the reproductive cycle. As shown in Table 22–3,

Effect on Menstrual Cycle and Fertility 
Menstrual cycle irregularity, including primary and secondary amenorrhea, is a com-
mon symptom of major EDs (Boisseau 2016) . Although most women with AN have 
amenorrhea as a presenting symptom, it is not pathognomonic of the disorder and is 
no longer included in the diagnostic criteria (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
The strongest predictors of amenorrhea in women with EDs include low BMI, low ca-
loric intake, and higher level of exercise (Kimmel et al. 2016). Menstrual irregularity 
is also common in patients with BN and BED (Poyastro Pinheiro et al. 2007). There is 
a noteworthy association of BN and BED with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). 
More than 75% of patients with BN have PCOS, and one in four patients with PCOS 
meet criteria for BN (McCluskey et al. 1992). Menstrual and sexual dysfunction in EDs 
also contribute to fertility difficulties in this population (Linna et al. 2013).

Pregnancy in patients with EDs is often unplanned. Women with AN are at twofold 
risk of unplanned pregnancy, and those wi th BN are at 30-fold increased risk, com -
pared with women in the general population (Claydon et al. 2018; Micali et al. 2014). 
Unplanned pregnancy puts the woman and fetus at risk through delayed prenatal 
care, unintended substance exposures, and inadequate nourishment, because mal -

et al. 2016). Pregnancy in these women is often associated with negative effects as they 
struggle with changing body image in the face of pregnancy-related weight gain, poor 
sense of self-worth, worry about the baby’s health, and the dilemma of discussing their

Postpartum Concerns 
Studies show that mothers with EDs are at risk of early cessation of breastfeeding. 
These women are also at greater risk of depression and anxiety during pregnancy and 
in the postpartum period (Franko et al. 2001). For many women with ED, the postpar-

and motherhood, and this often leads to an exacerbation of symptoms (Zerwas et al. 
2014). A neonatal complication seen in mothers with BN is “slimming,” in which they 
project their body image onto the baby and perceive the baby as “fat.” This mother 
then may restrict feedings for or induce vomiting in the baby (Cardwell 2013). 
Menopause and Eating Disorders 
Research shows that the prevalence of EDs in midlife has been increasing, and up to 
4.6% of midlife women ages 40–60 years may meet full criteria for diagnosis of an ED 
(Mangweth-Matzek et al. 2014). Perimenopause can be a window of vulnerability for 
the development or exacerbation of EDs due to reproductive hormone changes (Baker 
and Runfola 2016). The prevalence of BED is higher in the older population, whereas 
the incidence of AN seems to decrease (Baker and Runfola 2016).

Biological, Psychological, and Social Contributions 
The genetic basis of EDs has been well do cumented, most notably in twin studies 
showing greater concordance in monozygotic twins compared with dizygotic twins 
(Mazzeo et al. 2009, 2010; Mitchell et al. 2010), and meta-analyses have shown moder-
ate to high heritability (Culbert et al. 2015). More recent studies into the specific heri-
tability of AN have identified genetic lo ci on chromosome 12 (Duncan et al. 2017). 
Neuroimaging also supports biological contribution to the development of EDs, with 
studies identifying structural changes in gray matter volume (particularly in the limbic 
system) and in ventricle and sulci size in AN (Phillipou et al. 2014) and changes in the

have also been identified in the corticolimbic, dorsostriatal, and corticostriatal circuits,

Sociocultural factors are not only risk factors but also likely at play in the develop-
ment of EDs. Although these disorders have been identified across time and cultures, 
increased adoption of the idealization of thinness in the 20th century and exposure to 
Western cultural values have both been associated with increased rates of EDs (Cul -
bert et al. 2015). Internalization of the social ideal is likely a key variable because not 
all individuals exposed to thin ideal me dia develop an ED (C ulbert et al. 2015; 
Martínez-González et al. 2003). Twin studies have identified individual differences in 
both genetic and environmental factors for th in ideal internalization, indicating the 
complexity of this vulnerability (Culbert et al. 2015). 
As mentioned, personality traits have also been purported as part of the etiological 
model, with specific association of EDs with negative emotionality/neuroticism, per-
fectionism, and impulsivity/negative urgency (Culbert et al. 2015). Individuals with 
AN and BN also have decreased cognitive fl exibility, as evidenced by impaired per-
formance on cognitive flexibility tasks (Wu et al. 2014); these deficits may even persist 
into recovery and weight restoration (Culbert et al. 2015).

Treatment 
Treatment of EDs often involves a multidisciplinary approach, including elements of di-
etary changes and weight restoration, psychotherapy, and pharmacotherapy. Manage-
ment of EDs during pregnancy offers different opportunities and challenges compared 
with that for women in other parts of the reproductive cycle. Treatment of pregnant 
women with an ED should focus on weight restoration and adequate nutrition, with 
early referral to a nutritionist and vitamin supplementation (Andersen and Ryan 2009). 
Weight gain should be monitored closely, with understanding that necessary weight  
gain may be greater for those at lower weights compared with the general population. 
Women should be educated about the risks for both mother and baby that are associated 
with disordered eating behaviors during pregnancy (Andersen and Ryan 2009). 
For women with EDs who are not yet pregnant, the time of trying to conceive can 
be another potential window for intervention. Education should be provided on the 
association between infertility and ED symptoms (discussed later), and women should 
be encouraged to seek ED treatment prior to conception (Andersen and Ryan 2009). 
Psychopharmacology 
Pharmacotherapy options for EDs vary by diagnosis. Medications are not considered 
first-line or primary treatment for AN, whereas they are more often indicated as a 
component of treatment in BN and BED. Nutritional rehabilitation (including weight 
restoration when necessary) and psychotherapy, with emphasis on an interdisciplin-
ary team approach, are the mainstays of treatment for all individuals with EDs.
