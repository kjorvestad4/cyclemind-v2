Course/Prognosis 
Left untreated, perinatal depression can have a devastating impact on mother, child, 
and the entire family (Beck 1998; Conroy et al. 2012; Goodman et al. 2011; Grote et al. 
2010; Murray and Cooper 1997). AD is associated with higher rates of obstetrical com-
plications, such as preeclampsia, preterm birth, low birth weight, and operative deliv-
ery (Hu et al. 2015; Jarde et al. 2016) and, independent of PPD, with negative sequelae 
for the child, including increased rates of internalizing disorders and altered cognitive 
development (Conroy et al. 2012; Zhu et al. 2014). In addition, AD is the biggest risk 
factor for PPD, which has the potential to lead to poor mother–infant bonding and to 
emotional and cognitive dysregulation for the child (Hoffman et al. 2017). 
In addition to its effects on the pregnancy and child, perinatal depression can have 
long-lasting effects on the woman herself. The natural course of the illness is not entirely 
clear because most studies have focused exclusively on the postpartum period, and few 
have followed women beyond 6 months po stpartum. A strong majority of women 
(70%–75%) will have persistent depressive symptoms at 6 months postpartum (Paulson 
et al. 2016; Torres et al. 2019), and some evidence shows that, although more than half 
of women will experience gradual remission, more than one-third will still have clini-
cally significant depressive symptoms at 12 months postpartum (Fisher et al. 2019). 
A 2014 review of longitudinal studies found decreasing levels of depression over 
time (with follow-up between 1 and 3.5 years) but great variability; at any time point 
between 4 months and 3 years, about 30% of mothers diagnosed with PPD in commu-
nity samples and about 50% in clinical samples remained symptomatic (Vliegen et al. 
2014). An examination of subgroups showed that, in most studies, there was a per -
sistently depressed group and that women who remitted prior to 6 months often then 
relapsed later, although sometimes only with subthreshold symptoms. Demographic 
factors such as age, socioeconomic status, ethnicity, and education have at times been 
linked to chronicity, but that evidence ha s been mixed, and no clear consensus has 
been found (Vliegen et al. 2014). However, some risk factors for PPD have been shown

Some studies have looked at differences in course and symptoms according to time 
of symptom onset. Women with onset during pregnancy have been found to have 
higher rates of prior depressive episodes and higher rates of ps ychosocial stressors 
than women with onset in the postpartum, who are more likely to have obsessive-com-
pulsive and psychotic symptoms (Altemus et al. 2012). Another group recently exam-
ined the trajectory of depressive symptoms  across time in the perinatal period and 
identified four groups: two that were stable over time, with persistent symptoms of ei-
ther low or moderate severity; one group with more severe symptoms during preg -
nancy that decreased in the postpartum; and another group that had few or moderate 
symptoms during pregnancy but develope d severe symptoms in the postpartum 
(Ahmed et al. 2018). Certain characteristics predicted which women would belong to 
which group. Those women who reported more stress were more likely to belong to 
the higher-severity groups. Women with a prior history of depression were most likely 
to belong to the antepartum or moderate -stable groups. Being nonwhite and having 
poor partner support were also associated with the antepartum group, and only young 
maternal age was associated with the postpartum group. Another group that looked at 
symptoms in pregnancy only found three classes—increasing, decreasing, and stable— 
and similarly found that poor partner suppo rt was the key distinguishing factor for 
women whose symptoms worsened (Boekhorst et al. 2019). 
Although perinatal depression is not known to have a different natural course than 
that of depression outside the perinatal peri od (although research is sparse), this ill -
ness may have lasting effects on a woman’s health long after the acute symptoms have 
passed. Women with perinatal depression may have a “reproductive subtype” of de-
pression and may have symptoms at other times of hormonal transition, including the 
premenstrual period and perimenopause (Payne et al. 2009). In addition, perinatal de-
pression is associated with other morbidities of pregnancy (e.g., gestational diabetes, 
preeclampsia, preterm birth) that have lasting effects on the woman’s or child’s 
health. Gestational diabetes is associated with an increased risk of subsequent Type II 
diabetes and preeclampsia with a subsequent increased risk of cardiovascular disease 
(Chasan-Taber 2016; Perry et al. 2018). 
Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
Considerable research is available on the genetics and epigenetics of PPD (McEvoy et 
al. 2017). (This work has, by and large, not extended yet to include AD, so this section 
focuses on PPD only.) Family and twin studies from the 1990s forward have indicated 
a genetic basis for PPD when it is defined as a mood episode beginning within 4 weeks

of parturition; some of these studies have also found familiality to PPD in families 
with MDD or bipolar affective disorder (Forty et al. 2006; Murphy-Eberenz et al. 2006; 
Payne et al. 2008; Treloar et al. 1999). Ge nome-wide linkage and association studies 
have found variations on areas of chromosomes 1 and 9, and a single nucleotide poly-
morphism study identified the potential involvement of two estrogen-responsive 
genes (Carroll et al. 2006; Green and Galea 2008; Mahon et al. 2009; Mehta et al. 2014). 
In addition, candidate gene studies have implicated polymorphisms in several prom-
ising genes, including the serotonin transpor ter gene (Hu et al. 2019; Pinheiro et al. 
2013; Zhang et al. 2015), the catechol- O-methyltransferase ( COMT) gene (Alvim-
Soares et al. 2013; Comasco et al. 2011; Doornbos et al. 2009; Klein et al. 2016), and the 
estrogen receptor alpha (ESR1) gene (Costas et al. 2010; Pinsonneault et al. 2013). Data 
are mixed on the involvement of several other genes, including brain-derived neuro-
trophic factor (BDNF) (Comasco et al. 2011; Figueira et al. 2010), the oxytocin receptor 
(OXTR) (Bell et al. 2015; Jonas et al. 2013; Mi leva-Seitz et al. 2013), hemicentin 1 
(HMCN1) (Alvim-Soares et al. 2014; Mahon et al. 2009), and monoamine oxidase A 
(MAOA) (Comasco et al. 2011; Doornbos et al. 2009; Ivorra et al. 2010). The one com-
monality across all of these genetic studies is the issue of timing, with results often pos-
itive when symptoms are measured close to delivery but negative for depressions that 
begin later. This may indicate that there is a genetic basis to PPD only when the timing 
is clearly linked to the hormonal trigger of childbirth. 
Genetics is only a part of the landscape of heritable risk factors for PPD, and recent 
work has also identified and replicated epigenetic biomarkers that consistently pre -
dict the development of PPD with an accuracy of more than 80%. Two loci have been 
identified at the HP1BP3 and TTC9B genes—identified origin ally by linking estrogen-
responsive loci in the mouse hippocampus to DNA methylation differences in blood 
samples from pregnant and postpartum women with mood disorders—and replicated 
subsequently in diverse populations both with and without preexisting mood disor -
ders (Guintivano et al. 2014; Osborne et al. 2016). Although the function of these two 
genes is not entirely clear, both are known to be responsive to gonadal hormones (Cao 
et al. 2006; Nassa et al. 2011). 
In addition to the genetic and epigenetic risk factors mentioned, we can also point 
to substantial evidence of biological risk factors that may not be driven by heritability. 
The most obviously involved biological system is the endocrine system, because all 
women undergo massive shifts in reproductive hormones across pregnancy, leading to 
a dramatic drop shortly after delivery, and perhaps related to the high rate of the baby 
blues. However, only some women develop perinatal depression, meaning that indi-
vidual women’s vulnerability is far more important than absolute levels of hormones. 
This was elegantly demonstrated two decades ago when women with prior PPD and 
healthy control subjects were subjected to an identical, pregnancy-mimicking hormone 
regimen, and only women with prior PPD became ill (Bloch et al. 2000). 
Initial research on estrogen and progesterone as risk factors for PPD was unillumi-
nating. Animal studies have suggested that estradiol withdrawal induces depressive 
symptoms (Bethea et al. 2002; Galea et al. 2001), and early studies indicated that estra-
diol affects levels of serotonin and that its administration could improve symptoms of 
PPD (Ahokas et al. 2001; Gregoire et al. 1996). However, numerous studies have also 
found no differences in either estradiol levels or rate of estradiol withdrawal in women 
with PPD compared with healthy women (C hatzicharalampous et al. 2011; Okun et

Other hormonal systems may also be involved in the pathophysiology of perinatal 
depression. The neurosteroid system works in close concert with the hypothalamic-
pituitary-adrenal axis, and a number of studies have exam ined dysregulation of the 
cortisol response in perinatal mood sy mptoms. Corticotropin-releasing hormone is 
produced by the placenta, and elevated levels have been tied to later PPD (Yim et al. 
2009). PPD has also been connected with al tered cortisol responsiveness and altered 
levels of adrenocorticotropic hormone (Cox et al. 2015; Groër et al. 2005; Iliadis et al. 
2015; Jolley et al. 2007; Scheyer and Urizar 2016), although data are mixed, and the di-
rectionality is not always consistent. Some studies have implicated dysregulation of 
the thyroid system (Kuijpens et al. 2001; Lambrinoudaki et al. 2010), but systematic 
reviews have failed to find consistent associations (Szpunar and Parry 2018; Yim et al. 
2009). Finally, oxytocin has been an area of interest, with studies showing that lower 
pregnancy oxytocin is associated with h igher levels of both AD and PPD symptoms 
(Skrundz et al. 2011), that women who went on to develop PPD have a drop in oxyto-
cin prior to parturition (whereas healthy women have an increase) (Jobst et al. 2016), 
and that oxytocin levels in the third trimester can predict the development of PPD only 
for women with a history of prior MDD (Massey et al. 2016). Prolactin has also been 
investigated and is thought to mediate normal  maternal behavior but with little evi-
dence thus far for a specifically different role in depression (Payne and Maguire 2019). 
Another system that may be implicated in perinatal depression is the immune sys-
tem. A connection between immune dysregulation and MDD is well established (Dow-
lati et al. 2010; Howren et al. 2009; Miller et al. 2009), but the directionality of this 
connection—and whether it app lies only to certain subsets of depressed people (Raison 
et al. 2013)—is still in question. It is logica l to wonder whether women in the perinatal 
period comprise one of those subsets because there is substantial immune dysregula-
tion in healthy pregnancy, allowing the body to adjust to the presence of a “foreign 
pathogen” (i.e., a fetus whose genetic material is at least half foreign). Early work in this 
area looked primarily at levels of proinflammatory cytokines, and both methods and 
outcomes were mixed (Osborne and Monk 2013). Numerous studies since then have 
found both elevations (Liu et al. 2016; Osborne et al. 2019; Simpson et al. 2016) and de-

associated with perinatal de pression, with the bulk of the evidence concerning in -

that is integral to the acute phase response and to B- and T-cell activation. More recently, 
some groups have begun to look at summative inflammatory scores (Bränn et al. 2017)
