639 Eating Disorders 
Recent studies have looked at higher initial caloric intake with more rapid increases 
that have not shown worsening in medical complications and refeeding syndrome 
when electrolytes have been appropriat ely monitored and abnormalities corrected 
(Garber et al. 2016). Current recommendations now opt for more aggressive caloric 
values for those with mild to moderate malnutrition in the setting of close electrolyte 
monitoring. 
Behavioral Treatments 
Patients with EDs can be treated at various levels of care, and many require more in-
tensive treatment beyond typical outpatient care given the medical risks and complex-
ities of the disorder. Recommendations remain to offer outpatient treatment to those 
with EDs, with inpatient intervention reserved for those at greatest medical (e.g., high 
risk of refeeding syndrome) or psychiatric (e.g., comorbid depression with suicidality) 
risk (Treasure et al. 2010). Descriptions of the varied levels of care are found in Table 
22–7. Universal across treatment modalities is the involvement of a multidisciplinary 
team, often including a psychiatrist, psychologist/therapist, dietician, nurses, and a 
general medical provider. 
Psychotherapy is the preferred evidence-based intervention for most EDs. CBT has 
been well studied in the treatment of EDs an d has been found to be a superior treat -
ment modality for BN and BED (Davis and Attia 2019; Treasure et al. 2010). Due to its 
efficacy, CBT techniques have been adapted for group environment, telemedicine ser-
vices, and self-help modalities, including an internet-based manualized CBT group 
therapy delivered via group chat (Davis and Attia 2019). Interpersonal psychotherapy 
is also an effective treatment for EDs, although treatment response a p p e a r s  t o  b e  
slower and potentially less robust compared with CBT (Davis and Attia 2019; Treasure 
et al. 2010). Given the high prevalence of emotional dysregulation in EDs, dialectical 
behavioral therapy has also been proposed as a treatment, and data support its use in 
BN and BED, although evidence in AN is limited (Davis and Attia 2019). Acceptance 
and commitment therapy has also been considered as a treatment to target the under-
lying maladaptive cognitions and behaviors in EDs, although data remain limited 
(Davis and Attia 2019). 
For children and adolescents with EDs, fi rst-line treatment involves family-based 
therapy, in which responsibility for refeeding the child is placed back on the parents. 
Recommendations are for early and aggressi ve treatment, given that early response 
to treatment and initial weight gain are likely the greatest predictors of treatment out-
comes, including posttreatment remission (Davis and Attia 2019). 
Treatment Challenges 
One challenge to the treatment of EDs is th eir changing presentation over time. Pa -
tients may initially present with certain disordered eating symptoms (e.g., predomi-
nant restricting behaviors) and later prog ress to other symptoms (e.g., binge/purge 
behaviors). This can lead to treatment ch allenges, particularly when certain treat -
ments are less effective or even contraindicated when behaviors change. Most import-
ant to consider are medications associated with appetite reduction and weight loss; 
although individuals with BN may be of normal to high weight initially, they may de-
velop more severe symptoms that result in low weight and a subsequent diagnosis of 
AN. Such factors should be considered in the initial selection of pharmacotherapy.
