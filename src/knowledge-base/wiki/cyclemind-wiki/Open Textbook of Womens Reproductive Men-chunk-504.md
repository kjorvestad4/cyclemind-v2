aspect of women’s mental health care. About 50% of women experience some type of 
trauma in their lifetime, and women are more likely than men to develop PTSD fol -
lowing a traumatic experience (Tolin and Foa 2006). Early life trauma and adversity 
have a significant impact on future mental , physical, and sexual health (Centers for 
Disease Control and Prevention 2020). The greater number of adverse childhood 
experiences (ACEs) a female child encounters , the more likely she is to grow up to 
engage in risky sexual behavior, to have a baby in adolescence, to have serious psy -
chiatric illness, and to develop major medical problems, including cancer (Alcalá et 
al. 2017; Carr et al. 2013; Williams and Finch 2019). With this high prevalence of 
trauma exposure and its broad clinical impact, it is critical for all providers who treat 
women to be aware of trauma and its consequences and create a therapeutic environ-
ment in which patients can share their trauma histories. 
Trauma-informed care (TIC) is a holistic, person-centered approach to treatment 
that understands and incorporates the biological, psychological, neurological, and so-
cial impacts of trauma on an individual and should be used when working with in -
dividuals with trauma histories. TIC is a recommended universal clinical approach
