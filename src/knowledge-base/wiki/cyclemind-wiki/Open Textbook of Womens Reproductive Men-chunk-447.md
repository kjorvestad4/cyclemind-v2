Lifestyle Considerations 
In addition to the dramatic fluctuations in female sex hormones across the perinatal pe-
riod, multiple biological, psychological, and social changes can contribute to a relapse 
in BD. Treatment discussions with the perinatal woman and family should include these 
factors in a manner that is mindful of the patient’s social and financial resources. The 
patient may consider planning to limit predictable stressors during pregnancy and the 
postpartum period by reducing work hours, deferring the start of new projects at home 
or work, or other means to simplify her schedule. Bolstering psychosocial supports 
helps to provide emotional and physical support. A family member or friend may be 
identified to watch for early warning signs of relapse, monitor medication compliance, 
or contribute to infant care postpartum. When financially feasible, instrumental support 
in newborn care, such as a postpartum doula or night nanny, can be recommended. 
The perinatal period is associated with normative alterations in sleep physiology 
and sleep disruptions. Nighttime infant feedings further contribute to poor sleep qual-
ity in the postpartum period and may contribute to postpartum depressive symptoms 
(Park et al. 2013). Sleep disruption is a commonly reported precursor to mania in pa-
tients with BD, and women wi th BD who have a personal  history of mania precipi -
tated by sleep loss have an increased risk for PPP (Lewis et al. 2018). Several practical 
strategies can be considered to minimize maternal sleep disruption. In the hospital set-
ting, approaches for women at elevated risk for postpartum mood disorders may in-
clude rooming them away from noise and traffic, assessing vital signs during waking 
hours only, clustering medication administ ration with lab draws to minimize room 
traffic, encouraging visitors to adhere to specific visiting hours, opening blinds during 
the day to increase exposure to natural light, and the option of rooming-out the infant 
at night. At home, patients and families may be given a “prescription” for prioritizing 
sleep and family, and trusted supports can be asked to provide overnight bottle feed-
ing of expressed breastmilk or  supplementation with form ula. When clinically indi -
cated, conventional sedative hypnotic medications may be considered, with the goal 
of reestablishing a normal sleep-wake rhythm (Ross et al. 2005). 
Conclusion 
Competent psychiatric care of women with BD across the reproductive lifespan re -
quires the psychiatrist to appreciate the potential influence of fluctuations in female re-
productive hormones. In women of reproductive age, the potential for pregnancy adds 
a layer of complexity to treatment planning. Preconception planning should be a rou-
tine component of psychiatric care for reproductive-age women with BD and should 
include inquiry about plans for pregnancy and contraceptive practices. When prescrib-
ing medications with teratogenic or other obstetrical risk, these risks should be part of 
a balanced risk-risk discussion with the patient or her proxy. When treating BD during 
pregnancy or the postpartum period, it is critical to take a comprehensive approach to 
treatment that includes psychological and pharmacological options and to prioritize
