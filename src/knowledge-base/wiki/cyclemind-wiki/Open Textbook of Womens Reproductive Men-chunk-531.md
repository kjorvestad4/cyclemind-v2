Research Limitations 
Research designs used to examine women’s responses to VTOP are limited by 
1. The inability to randomize 
2. High attrition rates among participants who are lost to follow up 
3. Withholding of accurate abortion data among participants who have had abortions, 
such as number of previous abortions and gestational age at the time of the abortion 
4. Use of nonequivalent comparison groups, such as comparing women who have 
wanted pregnancies (control group; deli very group) with women who have un -
wanted pregnancies (abortion group, obtained or denied) (Major et al. 2008) 
5. Misclassification problems, such as when participants who desire termination later 
change their minds in favor of parenting (Biggs et al. 2017; Fergusson et al. 2008) 
A highly debated diagnosis referred to as postabortion stress syndrome is not acknowl-
edged by the American Psychiatric Association or American Psychological Associa -
tion based on questions about the validity of the research describing this disorder. 
Treatment 
Treatment for PTSS/PTSD after VTOP follow s principles for treatment of trauma, 
such as using a phase-oriented approach th at includes stabilization, processing, and 
integration of the trauma narrative. Additionally, supportive readings for grief and 
loss tailored to the perinatal period can include On Death and Dying  by Kübler-Ross 
(1969) and Grief Counselling and Grief Therapy by Worden (2013). 
Conclusion 
Trauma and PTSD in the perinatal period require consideration of areas that include 
prior exposure to trauma (especially sexual trauma) and pregnancy factors that may 
cause trauma, including reproductive loss and traumatic birth experiences. Physicians 
and other clinicians must remain vigilant in screening carefully for trauma and using 
TIC principles in managing women with trauma in the perinatal period to ensure sen-
sitive, compassionate, patient-centered care. 
Key Points 
• Patients with a history of trauma may face significant challenges in the peri-
natal period. 
• Major health care organizations such as the American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists and the U.S. Preventive Services Task Force encourage 
universal or widespread screening of individuals for intimate partner violence.
