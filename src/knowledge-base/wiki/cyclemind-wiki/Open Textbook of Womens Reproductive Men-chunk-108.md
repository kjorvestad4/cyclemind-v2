Assessment 
If suicidal thoughts or behaviors are report ed during screening, or if risk factors or 
psychiatric symptoms are identified, thes e should prompt more thorough explora -
tion. A number of clinical practice guidelines exist regarding suicide risk assessment 
and intervention (Bolton et al. 2015), including from the American Psychiatric Asso-
ciation (Jacobs et al. 2003), and these guidel ines can be used in the perinatal period. 
Guidelines for suicide risk assessment consistently recommend gathering informa -
tion about the following (Bolton et al. 2015; Orsolini et al. 2016): 
• Current stressors and psychosocial environment 
• Presence of psychiatric symptoms and history of current illness 
• Current substance use 
• Current medications  
• Current suicidal thoughts, including nature, timing, intent, and persistence 
• Current suicidal plan, if applicable, including lethality, access to means, detail, and 
preparation 
• Current or previous suicide attempt, if applicable, including timing, intent, method, 
and consequences 
• Other risk factors  
• Protective factors

Management 
If suicidality is elicited, then the assessing clinician should proceed to develop a safety 
plan and make a referral for specialized psychiatric assessment (Orsolini et al. 2016). 
Considerations around safety planning, treatment setting, and urgency of referral will 
depend on determination of level of risk; ideally, the treatment plan should be com-
posed of the least restrictive interventions that are likely to be safe and efficacious (Ja-
cobs et al. 2003). For patients who are at high risk and for whom acute safety concerns 
are present, hospitalization is likely to be required. Examples of patients who would 
be considered high risk include those who present immediately after a suicide attempt 
(especially with a history of prior attempts), have a suicide plan with immediate in -
tent, are unable or unwilling to cooperate with assessment or treatment, or present 
with symptoms of psychosis (Jacobs et al. 2003; Orsolini et al. 2016). Treatment plan-
ning should proceed collaboratively as much as possible, with consideration and dis-
cussion of patient preferences, as well as of anticipated benefits and potential risks 
(Jacobs et al. 2003). Clinicians must be aw are of local medical legislation and proce -
dures regarding certification in high-risk cases for which involuntary admission may 
need to be considered. 
Mental health care can reduce suicidality. Part of the risk assessment is to deter -
mine modifiable risk factors that can be mitigated and potential protective factors and 
strengths that can be optimized (Orsolini et al. 2016). Treatment of suicidality should 
also involve close attention to and evidence-based treatment of underlying or comor-
bid psychiatric illness. Evidence has shown that treatment of depression with antide-
pressants and of bipolar disorder with mood stabilizers reduces suicidal thoughts and 
behaviors (Bolton et al. 2015; Jacobs et al. 2003). Pharmacotherapy should also be con-
sidered for perinatal women with serious psychiatric disorders, with thoughtful risk-
risk discussion around the developing fetus’s and/or child’s exposure to untreated
