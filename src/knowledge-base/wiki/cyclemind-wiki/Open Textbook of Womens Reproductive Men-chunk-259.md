Effect on Menstrual Cycle and Fertility 
Menstrual cycle irregularity, including primary and secondary amenorrhea, is a com-
mon symptom of major EDs (Boisseau 2016) . Although most women with AN have 
amenorrhea as a presenting symptom, it is not pathognomonic of the disorder and is 
no longer included in the diagnostic criteria (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
The strongest predictors of amenorrhea in women with EDs include low BMI, low ca-
loric intake, and higher level of exercise (Kimmel et al. 2016). Menstrual irregularity 
is also common in patients with BN and BED (Poyastro Pinheiro et al. 2007). There is 
a noteworthy association of BN and BED with polycystic ovary syndrome (PCOS). 
More than 75% of patients with BN have PCOS, and one in four patients with PCOS 
meet criteria for BN (McCluskey et al. 1992). Menstrual and sexual dysfunction in EDs 
also contribute to fertility difficulties in this population (Linna et al. 2013). 
Eating Disorders and Pregnancy 
Pregnancy in patients with EDs is often unplanned. Women with AN are at twofold 
risk of unplanned pregnancy, and those wi th BN are at 30-fold increased risk, com -
pared with women in the general population (Claydon et al. 2018; Micali et al. 2014). 
Unplanned pregnancy puts the woman and fetus at risk through delayed prenatal 
care, unintended substance exposures, and inadequate nourishment, because mal -
adaptive eating may continue while the patient is unaware of the pregnancy (Kimmel 
et al. 2016). Pregnancy in these women is often associated with negative effects as they 
struggle with changing body image in the face of pregnancy-related weight gain, poor 
sense of self-worth, worry about the baby’s health, and the dilemma of discussing their 
ED with a health care provider (Tierney et al. 2011). Many patients conceal their ED,
