Gestational trophoblastic disease (GTD), a group of conditions that raise the risk of 
malignancy and metastasis, includes such etiologies as complete and partial molar 
pregnancies, choriocarcinomas, and placental site trophoblastic tumors. GTD can de-
velop after pregnancy losses and, as such, may be diagnosed after what was initially 
expected to be a viable pregnancy. Few studies are available on the psychiatric impli-
cations of this kind of experience, but limited data suggest rates of anxiety and depres-
sion that exceeded communit y rates, with more than on e-fifth of the study sample 
reporting GTD-specific traumatic stress symptoms (e.g., avoidance, intrusive thoughts) 
(Stafford et al. 2011). 
Traumatic Delivery 
Manifestation of clinical symptoms following childbirth trauma presents diagnostic 
challenges (Table 18–8) because symptoms such as hyperarousal and poor sleep are 
common in early motherhood. Reexperiencing the delivery in the form of intrusive 
thoughts, images, and nightmares can occur. Typical avoidance of trauma-related stim-
uli may be centered on medical care and in cludes avoidance of hospitals, physicians 
and medical staff, outpatient clinics, and specific procedures, including pelvic exam-
inations (Ayers et al. 2016). Persistence of  avoidance can lead to not pursuing future 
pregnancies or requesting cesarean sections in future pregnancies. A systematic review 
found scheduled cesarean sections in women with childbirth trauma did not lower an-
tenatal anxiety (Olieman et al. 2017), highlighting the challenges in management of this 
population. Negative cognitions often carry themes of guilt and shame surrounding 
motherhood, inability to care for the baby, or fears about the potential for sickness and 
death in the baby (Ayers et al. 2016). More broadly, chronic PTSD secondary to child-
birth trauma has adverse ramifications for maternal health, including mother–infant 
bonding, relationship with significant other, poorer quality of life, and increased risk of 
comorbid psychiatric illness (Cook et al. 2018), as well as obstetrical complications in 
future pregnancies (Hernández-Martínez et al. 2019; Rogal et al. 2007; Seng et al. 2001).
