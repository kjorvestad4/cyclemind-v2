Biological Factors 
It is well established that estrogen’s effects on dopamine systems may contribute to sex 
differences in addiction. Estrogen facilitates release of dopamine (Bobzean et al. 2014). 
Studies of the menstrual cycle in rodents and humans have found that the follicular 
phase is associated with greater positive subjective effects of cocaine, relative to the lu-
teal phase. Administration of progesterone attenuates the subjective response to co -
caine (Evans and Foltin 2010). Among postpartum women, progesterone treatment 
has been associated with lower rates of cocaine relapse (McHugh et al. 2018). 
Neuroimaging research has identified sex differences in brain volume and function 
in response to several substances. For example, both females and males with cocaine 
use disorder exhibited lower gray matter volume relative to control subjects, but the 
regions exhibiting these volu metric differences varied (McHugh et al. 2018). Hypo-
thalamic-pituitary-adrenocortical dysregulation in women may play a role in vulner-
ability to substance use relapse in the contex t of negative affect, such as in times of 
emotional stress (Greenfield et al. 2010). The possible neurobiological effects of preg-
nancy on reward circuitry and substance us e are largely unknown, so it is unclear 
whether reductions in substance use during pregnancy are mediated by psychosocial 
factors or whether biology may also play a role. One study found that gray matter cor-
tical volume was reduced in postpartum mothers who had used substances during 
pregnancy compared with mothers who had not used substances, particularly in fron-
tal brain regions, and that gray matter vo lume was negatively correlated with levels 
of behavioral activation in substance-using mothers (Rutherford et al. 2015).
