Physical contact between mother and infant has important clinical benefits. For 
example, skin-to-skin contact (SSC)—also referred to as  Kangaroo Care—has clear ev -
idence of benefit for mother and newborn after birth, such as helping the newborn 
transition to extrauterine life, increasing rates of breastfeeding, and promoting early 
parent–infant bonding (United Nations Children’s Fund and World Health Organi -
zation 2009). These benefits have led to the implementation of SSC as part of the re -
quirements for the World Health Organization (WHO)’s Baby-Friendly Hospital 
Initiative (United Nations Children’s Fund and World Health Organization 2009). See 
Table 12–2 for a summary of the benefits of SSC. Given its efficacy, cost-effectiveness, 
and feasibility, SSC has been recommended as the standard of high-quality care for 
all newborn infants, no matter what the gestational age, geographical location, or so-
cioeconomic environment (Campbell-Yeo et al. 2015). 
Physical contact is also very  important in its absence. Physical separation from a 
caring adult increases stress and, if prolonged, results in long-term increases in stress 
reactivity. In rodents, even separations of 3 hours per day, if repeated, are associated 
with long-lasting changes in stress tone and in the neurobiology of the hippocampus, 
a brain area critical to learning and emotional response (Lupien et al. 2009). Humans 
deprived of physical contact in early infancy have myriad neurobiological changes 
that go well beyond the hippocampus and amygdala to include global brain size, at -
