cardiovascular health effects of physiological doses of testosterone are not generaliz-

however, research also indicates positive effects of testosterone therapy on arousal, or-
gasm, and sexual distress in this population (Davis et al. 2019).

Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
A variety of nonpharmacological treatment approaches are used for FSDs. Pelvic floor 
physical therapy is a widely used approach for treatment of sexual pain, although re-
search examining the efficacy of specific protocols is limited. This approach includes 
manual therapy, psychoeducation, relaxation and breathing (re)training, local tissue 
sensitization, electromyographic biofeedback, and instructed, progressive use of vagi-
nal dilators (Bergeron et al. 2010, 2015). Small pilot studies have demonstrated im -
provement in arousal and achieving or gasm by increasing b l o o d  f l o w  a n d  
engorgement with the use of a small, FDA-approved clitoral suction device (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019). 
Research provides some limited support for sex therapy (for treatment of HSDD), 
cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT; for tr eatment of HSDD and sexual pain disor -
ders), mindfulness combined with CBT (f or treatment of HSDD, FSAD, and sexual 
pain disorders), therapist-aided exposure therapy (for treatment of sexual pain disor-
ders), and directed masturbation (for treatment of female orgasmic disorder). Psycho-
therapy to treat FSDs may be facilitated in individual, couples, or group format. 
Sex therapy typically includes psychoeducation on sexual functioning and sensate fo-
cus exercises. Sensate focus consists of a progressive series of exercises intended to in-
crease one’s attention to sensations and understanding of their physical response (Althof 
2011). Typically , couples are instructed to begin with nongenital touching, without the ex-
pectation of intercourse, to focus on a nonjudgmental and nonevaluative awareness of the 
sensations of touch with one partner touching the other. Exclusion of intercourse allows

addition to techniques to modify maladaptive cognitions related to sexual activity and

judgmental awareness of sexual response and sensation and improving the ability to 
be aware of the present moment (Brotto and Goldmeier 2015). 
Exposure therapy for sexual pain includes psychoeducation and a graded series of 
in-vivo exposure exercises to sexual contact and penetration via a dilator (van Lankveld 
et al. 2006). Various dilator devices exist, including plastic dilator kits with a range of 
dilator sizes and expanding dilators that can increase or decrease in size. Collaboration 
with a pelvic floor physical therapist for such exposure treatment may be useful when 
available. Directed masturbation for orgasm dysfunction is a series of progressive exer-
cises that the patient engages in (at home) to build awareness of sexual stimulation and 
response, focusing first on self-stimulation to explore one’s body and genitals and pro-
gressing to partner-based activity (if desired) (Heiman and LoPiccolo 1988). 
Management of FSDs in the Context of 
Comorbid Psychiatric Illness or Treatment 
As noted earlier (see “Assessment of Female Sexual Dysfunctions”), diagnosis of FSDs 
requires consideration of other conditions, including any psychiatric conditions, that 
might be associated with the sexual proble ms. Due to the significant strength of the 
bidirectional relationship between depression and sexual dysfunctions (Atlantis and 
Sullivan 2012), screening for depression in women presenting with sexual problems, 
particularly low desire, is recommended (Cla yton et al. 2018). Psychotropic medica -
tions used to treat psychiatric conditio ns, most commonly antidepressants (Clayton 
and Montejo 2006; Montejo et al. 2019; Serretti and Chiesa 2009), often have sexual side 
effects. Unfortunately, these side effects can contribute to nonadherence and discontin-
uation of treatment and decreased recovery. However, it is difficult to clearly identify 
whether sexual problems are specifically due to depression, primary FSDs, or medica-
tion side effects. Moreover, for some individuals, sexual functioning improves with psy-
chotropic treatment, particularly for those whose depression is effectively improved. 
Specific rates of sexual problems due to psychotropic medications (i.e., treatment-
emergent sexual dysfunctions) vary, with rates ranging between 26% and 80% (Clay-
ton et al. 2016; Montejo et al. 2019; Serretti and Chiesa 2009). Identifying medications’ 
differential effects on sexual function is difficult, in part due to limited availability of 
randomized controlled trials and variatio ns in methodology (e.g., patient sponta -
neous report vs. assessment with a validated measure) and in part due to confound-
ing by indication (i.e., that depression also results in sexual dysfunctions). 
Evidence indicates that adverse sexual effects of antidepressants are most com -
monly in domains of desire and arousal, followed by orgasm, whereas evidence of ef-
fects on pain are mixed (Lorenz et al. 2016). Studies indicate that sexual side effects 
are more likely when the medication’s mechanism of action is on the blockade of se -

serotonin, which regulates satiety; opioids, which mediate sexual rewards; endocan-

tocin, which stimulate sexual arousal; melanocortins and dopamine, which stimulate 
desire and attention) can affect sexual function (Kingsberg et al. 2015). 
Consistent evidence suggests that bupropion, compared with SSRIs, is associated with 
lower rates of sexual side effects, perhaps due to its noradrenergic-dopaminergic effects, 
and that paroxetine tends to have higher rates of sexual side effects compared with other 
antidepressants. Emerging evidence also indicates that medications with multimodal se-
rotonin modulation, including mirtazapine, vilazodone, and vortioxetine, have less po-
tential than SSRIs for adversely affecting sexual function in women treated for depression 
(Clayton et al. 2016; Montejo et al. 2019; Serretti and Chiesa 2009). Research regarding 
differential sexual side effects of antipsychotics is limited and variable. Antipsychot-
ics, particularly first-generation antipsychotics and risperidone, followed by olanzap-
ine, appear to have higher rates of sexu al dysfunctions. Antipsychotics w i t h  l e s s e r  
sexual side effects appear to be ziprasidone, quetiapine, and aripiprazole (Basson and 
Gilks 2018; Clayton et al. 2016). Specifically , aripiprazole appears to have the strongest 
evidence of a favorable sexual side effect profile (Clayton et al. 2016). 
Research regarding treatment-emergent FSDs with lithium, mood stabilizers, and 
anxiolytics is limited and mixed (Montejo et al. 2019; Reisman 2017; Serretti and 
Chiesa 2009). Table 3–7 summarizes the relative frequency of sexual side effects that

Strategies to address antidepressant-emergent sexual dysfunctions have been sug-
gested, yet research evaluating the efficacy or comparative efficacy of each approach is 
limited. Prior to initiating treatment, providers should consider proactively assessing 
baseline sexual function in order to asse ss and monitor medication-related changes. 
Women should be counseled about the potential for sexual side effects when an anti-
depressant is initiated, and preventive strategies for women with concerns about sex-
ual side effects should be considered, including psychotherapy, physical activity prior 
to sexual activity to improve sexual desire, and selecting a medication with evidence of 
less impact on sexual function when indicated (Basson and Gilks 2018). 
Some research indicates limited success with dose reduction in the management of 
antidepressant-emergent FSDs (Balon 2006) , although other research suggests dose 
reduction may be effective (Clayton et al. 2016). Sexual side effects may be dose re -
lated for some antidepressants (Clayton et al. 2016); however, dose reduction should 
be considered only for individuals whose depression is in full remission, given the po-
tential for worsening of depressive symptoms with this approach (Balon 2006). “Drug 
holidays,” or temporary cessation of psyc hotropic treatment to allow improvement 
in sexual function, are relatively uncommon in clinical practice, in part due to con -
cerns related to discontinuation symptoms, treatment dropout, and worsening of de-
pressive symptoms (Balon 2006; Clayton et al. 2016).

Available evidence of the efficacy of adjunctive therapy is overall limited (Balon 2006; 
Taylor et al. 2005), although Table 3–9 presents an overview of options to address sexual 
side effects. Bupropion has the strongest evidence base to support its use in diminishing 
the sexual side effects of other psychiatric medications (Safarinejad 2011; Taylor et al. 
2005), likely due to its dopaminergic properties. Given that the cause of sexual dysfunc-
tions with SSRIs is thought to be related to stimulation of the serotonin system, strategies 
to counteract this effect include adding the 5-HT2 antagonist cyproheptadine on a stand-
ing or as-needed basis prior to sexual activity, a strategy with limited evidence from case 
studies (Aizenberg et al. 1995; Javanbakht 2015), or adding the partial 5-HT1A agonist 
buspirone (Landén et al. 1999), a strategy that is more commonly used in clinical practice. 
Side effects of the alternative medication or adjunctive therapy should be taken into con-
sideration when treatment planning. Weight gain (e.g., with mirtazapine) and anxiety 
(e.g., with bupropion) may subsequently negatively impair sexual function. Furthermore, 
potential antiserotonergic effects that might interfere with an antidepressant effect (e.g., 
daily administration of cyproheptadine) should be considered with adjunctive therapies. 
Finally, some evidence suggests efficacy of nonpharmacological approaches (e.g., adjunc-
tive sex therapy, complementary and integrative approaches such as acupuncture) to ad-
dress treatment-emergent sexual dysfunctions (Basson and Gilks 2018).

on this topic are more limited, although recommendations generally include dose re-
duction, switching to an antipsychotic with fewer side effects (e.g., aripiprazole), or 
concurrent psychotherapy and psychoeducation to target modifiable factors affecting 
sexual dysfunctions. In general, evidence indicates that the propensity of these drugs
