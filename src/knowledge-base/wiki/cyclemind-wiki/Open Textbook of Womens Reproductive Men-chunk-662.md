Another challenge is managing patients with severe and enduring EDs (SEEDs). For 
those who have persistent symptoms that are not responding to highly aggressive treat-
ment, referral to a higher level of care may not be indicated despite serious symptoms. 
Intensive treatment focuses on amelioration of ED symptoms, which might not be a re-
alistic goal for those with SEED symptoms. Individuals with SEEDs may also remain 
symptomatic at discharge, which could be a reflection of external limitations on length 
of treatment, and patient factors related to ambivalence about change and inability to 
fully address the complex functions of disordered eating symptoms and their associa-
tions with comorbid psychopathology. Va luing a patient’s autonomy by assessing 
readiness for change and identifying specific goals of care is an important component 
of treatment in SEED cases. 
Conclusion 
EDs are a group of psychiatric diagnoses associated with numerous medical comor-
bidities and risk of severe complications and mortality . Care must be taken to identify 
individuals with disordered eating symptoms, including application of screening meth-
ods across various medical settings. Treatment should be focused initially on weight 
restoration and safe nutritional rehabilitation, with particular focus on avoiding com-
plications of refeeding, most notably refeeding syndrome. Further treatment should 
address underlying cognitive distortions related to food and body image and under-
lying psychosocial contributors to the development of the disease.
