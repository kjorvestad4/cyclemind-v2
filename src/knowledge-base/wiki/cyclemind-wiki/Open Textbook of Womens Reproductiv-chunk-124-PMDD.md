Substance Use Disorders 
Julia Frew, M.D.

Substance use disorders (SUDs) generate special concerns for women and their 
health care providers, particularly in the perinatal period. Considerations include the 
teratogenicity of substances of abuse, the potential for neonatal substance withdrawal, 
the stigma related to substance use in pregnancy, and concerns about child protection 
reporting in cases of maternal substance use. Integrated care models that provide coor-
dinated SUD treatment and obstetrical care show promise for improving outcomes for 
women with SUDs and their children. This chapter reviews the epidemiology, clinical 
features, pathophysiology, and treatment of SUDs in women generally, with a partic-
ular focus on the perinatal period. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
The development and prognosis of SUDs are gendered. In the United States, 45% of 
people age 12 years or older who use illicit drugs are women. Women are most at risk 
of substance use between the ages of 18 and 29 years, coinciding with their reproduc-
tive years. In the United States, tobacco, alcohol, and cannabis are the three substances 
used most commonly during pregnancy. Notably, polysubstance use is common, with 
up to 50% of women endorsing use of more than one substance (Forray 2016).

Reported rates of substance use vary depending on the methodology of the study. 
The figures listed in Table 21–1 indicate use of each substance and do not distinguish 
between indicated substance use, substance misuse, or SUDs. Using opioids as an ex-
ample, a study of publicly insured women in Tennessee showed that 28% filled a pre-
scription for an opioid prenatally (Patrick et al. 2015). However, the estimated risk for 
opioid use disorder in pregnancy ranges from 0.4% to 0.8% (Patrick et al. 2012). More-
over, certain substances such as metham phetamine and benzodiazepines have ex -
tremely limited epidemiological data because they are frequently used in conjunction 
with other illicit substances and are rarely  studied alone. Finally , certain substances 
have much higher rates of polysubstance misuse compared with others. Data from the 
National Survey on Drug Use and Health, for example, found that 96% of heroin users 
also used at least one other substance in the past year, and 61% used at least three dif-
ferent drugs (Jones et al. 2015). Meanwhile, pregnant women reporting cannabis use 
were unlikely to use other substances unless they were also involved with the crimi -
nal justice system (Washio et al. 2018). 
In general, women endorse lower levels of substance use during pregnancy com -
pared with pre-pregnancy (Forray et al. 2015). This may result from a combination of 
advice from clinicians concerning the negative consequences of substance use in preg-
nancy, negative messages regarding substance use from society and health care pro -
fessionals, increased access to treatment services during pregnancy, and maternal 
concern about the impact of substances on the fetus. Postpartum, however, these same 
forces become less pronounced, and a sign ificant proportion of women resume sub -
stance use. One study found that by 3 months postpartum, 58% of women had re -
sumed smoking, 41% had resumed cannabis use, and 27% had resumed cocaine use 
(Forray et al. 2015). Because of the high ri sk of recurrent drug use postpartum, mor -
tality is also heightened during this period. A study of women in Massachusetts 
found that overdose deaths from opioids were particularly heightened 7–12 months 
postpartum, nearly four times the overdose rate in the third trimester (Schiff et al. 
2018). Meanwhile, a study of maternal deat hs in Colorado from the years 2004–2012 
found that most women who died from self-harm (either suicide or overdose) had co-
morbid substance use and affective psychiatric disorders (Metz et al. 2016). 
  
 
  
 
 
     
     
 
     
 
     
  
  
  
   
  
593 Substance Use Disorders 
Tobacco 
Tobacco is the most common substance used during pregnancy. Women who smoke 
cigarettes are less likely to abstain from all substances during pregnancy and have a 
higher prevalence of polysubstance use, with comorbid alcohol and cannabis use be-
ing the most common. Feelings of depression may contribute to continued smoking 
during pregnancy; in an observational st udy, women who continued to smoke ciga-
rettes during pregnancy reported significantly more depressive symptoms than recent 
ex-smokers and nonsmokers (Munafò et al. 2008). Within 1 year postpartum, 80% of 
women who quit smoking in pregnancy had resumed smoking (Forray and Foster 
2015). Women who smoke prior to pregnancy are more likely to cease breastfeeding to 
resume smoking (Forray and Foster 2015). Limited data are available regarding the use 
of alternative forms of tobacco during the perinatal period, although the rates of elec-
tronic cigarette use are increasing among women of reproductive age (Forray and Fos-
ter 2015). 
Alcohol 
Among pregnant women in the United States, the prevalence of any alcohol use ranges 
from 10% to 80% and is 3% for binge drinking (Dejong et al. 2019). Among women who 
reported drinking before pregnancy, 87% achieved remission during pregnancy, 6.6% 
reduced their intake, and 6.4% did not reduce their intake (Dejong et al. 2019). Accord-
ing to the 2013 Substance Abuse and Mental Health Services Administration National 
Survey on Drug Use and Health (NSDUH), risk factors for continued alcohol use 
during pregnancy include age between 35 and 44 years, having college degree, being 
employed, or being of American Indian, Alaskan Native, or non-Hispanic Black ethnic-
ity (Dejong et al. 2019). Of women drinking in late pregnancy, the highest prevalence 
of alcohol use was in non-Hispanic college graduates older than 35 years (Dejong et al. 
2019). Social stressors were associated with higher likelihood of continuing alcohol use 
during the third trimester. Limited data exist regarding alcohol use in breastfeeding, 
but one study found that 24%–80% of wome n consume alcohol wh ile breastfeeding 
(Dejong et al. 2019). 
Cannabis 
According to the NSDUH, the self-reported prevalence of cannabis use in pregnancy 
increased between 2002 and 2017. Women reporting any cannabis use during the first 
trimester of pregnancy rose from 5.7% in 2002–2003 to 12.1% in 2016–2017. Daily or near-
daily use during the first trimester was reported by 1.8% of women in 2002–2003 and 
by 5.3% in 2016–2017. Use of cannabis daily or near-daily in later trimesters increased 
from 0.6% to 2.5% (second trimester) and from 0.5% to 2.5% (third trimester) between 
2002–2003 and 2016–2017. These data suggest not only increased overall use of can -
nabis among reproductive-age women but also a possible perception of lower risk of 
cannabis in pregnancy in 2016–2017 compared with 2002–2003 (Volkow et al. 2019).

Prevalence rates of methamphetamine use during pregnancy range from 1% to 5%

Opioids 
The prevalence of maternal opioid use in pregnancy increased nearly fivefold from 1.19 
per 1,000 live hospital births in 2000 to 5.63 per 1,000 live births in 2009 (Patrick et al. 
2012). Studies of Medicaid-enrolled women have found that 18.5%–29% filled an opioid 
prescription during pregnancy (Yazdy et al. 2015). Pregnancy-related deaths due to 
opioid misuse more than doubled between 2007 and 2016, increasing from 4% to 10% 
(Gemmill et al. 2019). Psychiatric comorbidity is common among perinatal women 
with opioid use disorder, particularly mo od and anxiety disorders (Arnaudo et al. 
2017). Most women with opioid use disorder also use other substances: 77%–95% of 
women also smoke cigarettes (Forray and Foster 2015), about 26.6%report cocaine use, 
56.6% report regular alcohol use, and 73.5% report regular cannabis use in one study 
(Brogly et al. 2018).

<25 years, low education level (less than high school), lower socioeconomic status, co-
morbid psychiatric disorders, polysubstance use prior to pregnancy, social stressors,

Screening Methods 
All women should be screened for substance use. Screening allows providers to iden-
tify patients at risk, stratify severity, and intervene based on level of acuity. Universal 
screening is also recommended by professional organizations such as the American 
Academy of Pediatrics, American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists, Amer-
ican Medical Association, American Psychiatric Association, Centers for Disease Con-
trol and Prevention, and World Health Or ganization. In the peripartum period, 
screening is recommended at the initial prenatal visit and should be repeated every 
trimester for women who screened positive initially for past or current use (Wright et 
al. 2016). Reporting mandates vary widely per state, and physicians should be famil-
iar with the laws in their area of practice . To the extent allowed by law, physicians 
should strive to maintain patient confidentiality, autonomy, and dignity (American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2017a). The application of legal statutes 
by physicians to enforce substance use treatment disrupts the trust necessary to es -
tablish a therapeutic physician–patient rela tionship (American College of Obstetri -
cians and Gynecologists 2011).

Diagnostic Criteria 
DSM-5 (American Psychi atric Association 2013) defines SUD as a pathological pat -
tern of behaviors related to the use of the substance. One key feature of SUDs is the 
underlying alteration in brain reward circuitry that persists beyond intoxication and 
detoxification. Ten separate classes of drug s are included in the SUDs: alcohol; caf -

The American Society of Addiction Medicine defines addiction as a primary, chronic 
disease of brain reward, motivation, memory, and related circuitry, including dysfunc-
tion in these circuits leading to characteristic biological, psychosocial, and spiritual 
manifestations. This is reflected in an individual’s pathological pursuit of reward or re-
lief through substance use and other behaviors.The ICD-10 Classification of Mental and 
Behavioural Disorders (World Health Organization 1 992) includes a section on SUDs 
categorized by substance. Regardless of the diagnostic criteria used, the perinatal pe-
riod does not alter the diagnosis, nor is a specific specifier required.

Sex and gender differences in psychological and physiological responses to drugs of 
abuse have been well documented and affect the clinical presentation of women with 
SUDs. Women progress through the stages of addiction differently than men; they 
transition from casual drug use to addiction faster (known as “telescoping”), are more 
reactive to stimuli that trigger relapse, and have higher rates of relapse than men (Bob-
