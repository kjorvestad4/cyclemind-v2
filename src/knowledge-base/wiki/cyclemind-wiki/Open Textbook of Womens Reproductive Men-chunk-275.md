Although mood symptoms can sometimes be present with ADHD, mood episodes 
of adequate duration for diagnosis are th e hallmark of bipolar disorder. Psychotic 
symptoms are not a diagnostic component of ADHD (Brus et al. 2014). In addition, 
anxiety disorders, such as generalized anxiety disorder, panic disorder, and PTSD, can 
all present with difficulties with concentration and distractibility. Last, ruling out sub-
stance intoxication or withdrawal is crit ical; although many women reduce or stop 
substance use when a pregnancy is discovered, a significant number continue to use 
substances throughout pregnancy, leading to multiple negative maternal–infant out-
comes (Ordean et al. 2017). The presence of significant impulsivity and affective lability 
can raise the question of borderline personality disorder; however, the two can be dis-
tinguished by the presence of other symptoms specific to borderline personality disor-
der, including frantic efforts to avoid abandonment, identity disturbance, and recurrent 
suicidal behavior (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
Medical conditions that should be ruled out include thyroid disease, systemic dis-
ease such as hepatic failure, obstructive sleep apnea, and neurological disorders such 
as Tourette’s syndrome and epilepsy (Geffen and Forster 2018; Post and Kurlansik 
2012). Side effects of commonly prescribed medications and substances, such as ste -
roids, antihistamines, caffeine, and nicoti ne, can also mimic ADHD symptoms (Post 
and Kurlansik 2012). 
Pathophysiology 
ADHD is a clinically heterogeneous disorder that has a wide range of clinical presen-
tations. These include substantial variations in neurocognitive impact, structural and 
functional brain abnormalities, behavioral patterns, psychiatric comorbidities, and tra-
jectories over time (Faraone et al. 2015). Given this level of heterogeneity, it is perhaps 
not surprising that no single etiology nor core set of etiologies of ADHD has been iden-
tified, despite extensive analysis. Alternatively, it has been proposed that a combina-
tion of several genetic and environmental ri sk factors interact together to increase 
susceptibility to ADHD (Faraone et al. 2015) and that ADHD itself may comprise a het-
erogeneous cluster of distinct syndromes (Matthews et al. 2014).

Extensive analysis from neurocognitive , neurophysiological, and neuroimaging 
studies supports the idea that brain dysfunction plays a central role in ADHD in both 
children and adults (Bekker et al. 2005a, 2005b; Seidman et al. 2004a, 2004b, 2005). 
Functional MRI studies have demonstrated brain dysfunction with cognitive tasks spe-
cific to ADHD (Makris et al. 2008; Paloyelis et al. 2007; Pliszka et al. 2006; Rubia et al. 
2005; Suskauer et al. 2008). A consistent set of neural circuits involved in executive 
functioning, reward processing, and attention has been associated with ADHD, includ-
ing the frontoparietal, dorsal frontostriatal, and mesocorticolimbic circuits, as well as 
the default mode and cognitive control networks (Alexander and Farrelly 2018; Gallo 
and Posner 2016). 
In terms of brain structural findings, MRI studies have demonstrated smaller vol-
umes in the right frontal areas, cerebellum, corpus callosum, and caudate, as well as 
cortical thinning (Castellanos 2002; Ca stellanos et al. 1996, 2001; Durston 2008; 
Durston et al. 2004; Makris et al. 2007; Se idman et al. 2006; Semrud-Clikeman et al. 
1994; Valera et al. 2007). Recent studies have also demonstrated brain volume differ-
ences in the bilateral amygdala, nucleus accumbens, and hippocampus (Hoogman et 
al. 2017). PET scanning demonstrates abnormal glucose metabolism in the prefrontal 
and premotor areas of the frontal lobe in adults (Ernst et al. 1998; Zametkin et al. 1990, 
1993), and single photon emission comput ed tomography studies demonstrate hy -
poperfusion/hypofunctioning in the prefront al and striatal regions in both children 
and older adults (Amen et al. 2008; Lou et al. 1989). Alterations in neurotransmitter 
systems, including the dopaminergic, serotonergic, and glutamatergic systems, are ob-
served in ADHD populations (Dunn et al. 2019). 
Evidence from family, twin, and adoption studies strongly supports a genetic basis 
of ADHD (Alberts-Corush et al. 1986; Biederman et al. 1990a; Faraone et al. 2000; 
Sprich et al. 2000) and high heritability (Faraone et al. 2005; Larsson et al. 2014). Twin 
studies estimate its heritability to be 70%–80% (Burt 200 9) and have shown that this 
heritability extends from childhood through adulthood  (Chang et al. 2013). Family 
studies show that the parents and siblings of a child with ADHD have a twofold to 
eightfold increased risk of ADHD themselves (Biederman et al. 1990b, 1992). There is 
also evidence of genetic factors in the distinct dimensions of ADHD; for example, one 
twin study noted that heritability of inattention was 0.39, whereas the heritability of 
impulsivity-hyperactivity was 0.69 (Sherman et al. 1997). 
However, researchers have yet to find the needle in the haystack—that is, which (if 
any) genetic contribution is the most significant. Even though twin studies have esti-
mated that additive genetic effects are thought to explain up to 80% of the variance in 
the ADHD phenotype (Albayrak et al. 2008; Thapar et al. 1999), each gene marker 
identified thus far has had too small an effect to explain the totality of the heritability 
of ADHD (Durston 2008; Faraone et al. 2005). Genome-wide scans have identified ev-
idence of linkage in several chromosomal regions, including 5p12, 10q26, 12q23, and 
16p13 (Fisher et al. 2002; Smalley et al. 2002), and in one region proximal to the dopa-
mine transporter gene (Bakke r et al. 2003; Hebebrand et al. 2006; Ogdie et al. 2003); 
however, a recent meta-analysis found no significant genome-wide associations, sug-
gesting that the contribution of these markers is low (Neale et al. 2010).

ment with markers in or near the relevant receptor genes; examples include dopamine

Other biological factors are areas of active interest (Dunn et al. 2019). Evidence sup-
porting the role of inflammation includes the relationship between ADHD and auto-
immune disorders (Instanes et al. 2017; Liao et al. 2016; Lin et al. 2016), an association 
of ADHD with increased serum cytokines (Anand et al. 2017), and evidence of antibod-
ies against relevant targets in ADHD, su ch as the dopamine  transporter polymor-
phisms associated with in flammatory pathways and ADHD (Giana et al. 2015). In 
addition, early evidence suggests that prenatal exposure to inflammation is associated 
with changes in offspring brain development, such as reductions in cortical gray matter 
volume (Short et al. 2010) and alterations in neurotransmitter function (Luchicchi et al. 
2016). Other biological factors that have been proposed to play a role in the pathogen-
esis of ADHD include sex hormones, particularly in women. For example, a growing 
body of evidence demonstrates that sex hormones can regulate intracellular signaling 
systems thought to be abnormal in ADHD. This functional interaction between sex hor-
mones and neurotransmitter systems may be as sociated with the c linical features of 
ADHD in women (Haimov-Kochman and Berger 2014); however, little is known about 
the role of sex hormones in the pathophysiology of ADHD (Gross-Tsur et al. 2006). 
There has been extensive research into environmental factors that play a role in the 
pathogenesis of ADHD (Banerjee et al. 2007). However, recent studies have noted the 
complexity of determining causality and of delineating environmental effects from ge-
netic and familial confounding variables (Sciberras et al. 2017). Despite the complex-
ity, several prenatal exposures have been hypothesized to be related to ADHD in the 
offspring. Of the putative environmental factors, preterm birth and low birth weight 
appear to have the strongest relationship with the pathogenesis of ADHD in offspring 
(Botting et al. 1997; Milberger et al. 1997b; Sciberras et al. 2017; Stevens et al. 2008). 
Other cited exposures include prenatal exposure to alcohol, nicotine, illicit substances, 
hypertension, maternal stress, and severe early life deprivation such as prolonged 
childhood separation (Banerjee et al. 2007; Milberger et al. 1997a). Tobacco use in preg-
nancy has often been cited as a strong risk factor for ADHD in the offspring; however, 
genetic and familial factors may play a stronger role than previously thought (Sciber-
ras et al. 2017). In addition, prenatal exposure to nicotine may be related to genetic ef-
fects rather than the direct toxic effect s of nicotine or other tobacco components 
(Thapar et al. 2009). Other factors, including maternal medical conditions, such as au-
toimmune illness (Xiang et al. 2018); medi cation exposures, such as acetaminophen 
(Liew et al. 2014); and environmental toxins, such as lead (Nigg et al. 2010), have been 
proposed, but the limited evidence available is difficult to interpret, and further well-
designed studies are required (Sciberras et al. 2017).

The gold standard of ADHD treatment is psychopharmacology, typically with psycho-
stimulants such as methylphenidate or amphetamine, in combination with behavioral 
psychotherapy (Baker and Freeman 2018; Thapar and Cooper 2016). There is clear ev-
idence that maximum therapeutic efficacy is obtained when psychosocial support is 
delivered in addition to pharmacotherapy, rather than with pharmacotherapy alone 
(Cherkasova et al. 2020; Philipsen et al. 2015; Young et al. 2017). Stimulants are gener-
ally thought to have higher efficacy for adult ADHD than nonstimulants (Faraone and 
Glatt 2010; Peterson et al. 2008; Mészáros et  al. 2009). The use of stimulant medication 
to treat ADHD in women has risen dramatically in recent years, leading to more 
women becoming pregnant while taking stimulant medications. The Centers for Dis-
ease Control and Prevention recently used a commercial database to estimate the per-
centage of reproductive-aged women who filled prescriptions for ADHD medications 
each year; from 2003 to 2015, the percentage rose by an astounding 344% to an overall 
prevalence of 4% of women (Anderson et al. 2018). Current estimates are that approx-
imately 1% of pregnant women in the United States use medications for the treatment 
of ADHD, making these medications one of  the most commonly prescribed in preg -
nancy overall (Anderson et al. 2018; Louik et al. 2015). Although discontinuation of 
pharmacological treatment during pregnancy may be a viable option for women with 
mild symptoms, those with severe symptoms may require continued use in order to 
maintain psychological, social, and occupational functioning throughout pregnancy. 
Several systematic studies have examined the risks of perinatal exposure to stim -
ulants (Dideriksen et al. 2013; Pottegård et  al. 2014; Wajnberg et al. 2011); however, 
many of these studies are derived from da ta with significant confounding variables, 
such as illicit stimulant use/misuse, use of  stimulants as an anorectic drug, and co-
morbid substance abuse, tobacco use, or polypharmacy (Besag 2014; Bolea-Alamanac 
et al. 2014; Humphreys et al. 2007; Nörby et al. 2017). In addition, some studies have 
yielded outcomes that are either inconsistent or could also be attributed to untreated 
maternal ADHD (Bro et al. 2015; Diav-Citrin et al. 2016; Haervig et al. 2014; Källén et 
al. 2013; Newport et al. 2016; Wright et al . 2015). Clinical discussions regarding the 
risks of exposure to stimulants in pregnancy must be balanced against the substantial 
risks of untreated perinatal ADHD, including driving hazards and major functional 
impairment in both work and home life (Freeman 2014). 
Early pregnancy. Data are mostly reassuring regarding the risk of birth defects and 
stimulant exposure (Aktepe et al. 2009; De booy et al.1993; Diav-Citrin et al. 2016; 
Dideriksen et al. 2013; Källén et al. 2013 ; Milkovich and van der Berg 1977; Nörby et 
al. 2017; Pottegård et al. 2014), although a few human studies have noted an associa-
tion of stimulant exposure with cardiac (Jiang et al. 2019; Levin 1971; Nora et al. 1970), 
gastrointestinal (Anderson et al. 2020; Elliott et al. 2009), and limb malformations (An-
derson et al. 2020). Several studies have noted an association between stimulant expo-

Obstetrical outcomes. Adverse pregnancy outcomes associated with stimulant 
medications are likely to be related to their vasoconstrictive effects, which may impair 
placental perfusion. A number of studies have demonstrated an association between 
stimulant exposure and adverse pregnancy outcomes, such as low birth weight, small 
for gestational age infant, infant withdrawal, and premature birth; however, these stud-
ies were also complicated by confounders, including stimulant misuse and tobacco de-
pendence (Debooy et al. 1993; Ladhani et al. 2011; Smith et al. 2003; Wright et al. 2015). 
More recent studies have focused on women taking prescribed doses of stimulant med-
ications. Bro et al. (2015) utilized birth registry data to examine information from 
989,932 pregnancies, of which 186 of the pregnant women had used methylphenidate 
or atomoxetine. They found that, as compared with unexposed infants, those exposed 
to one of these medications during pregnancy were at increased risk for Apgar scores 
<10 (adjusted relative risk 2.06; 95% CI 1.11–3.82) (Bro et al. 2015). 
In a later cohort study, Nörby et al. (2017) analyzed birth registry data of 964,734 in-
fants, of whom 1,591 were exposed to ADHD medications during pregnancy. Analysis 
of these data concluded that infants exposed to ADHD medications were at elevated 
risk for admission to a neonatal intensive care unit (adjusted OR 1.5; 95% CI 1.3–1.7), 
CNS-related disorders (adjusted OR 1.9; 95% CI 1.1–3.1), and moderate preterm birth 
(adjusted OR 1.3; 95% CI 1.1–1.6) (Nörby et al. 2017). Both studies (Bro et al. 2015; 
Nörby et al. 2017) were limited by the fundamental differences between participants in 
the exposed versus unexposed groups. Cohe n et al. (2017) conducted a population-
based cohort study of more than 3,000 pregnancies exposed to prescribed psychostim-
ulants. After controlling for multiple confounders, they noted a small increased relative 
risk of preeclampsia (RR 1.29; 95% CI 1.11–1.49) in women exposed to stimulants versus 
unexposed women and a small increased relative risk of preterm birth for women who 
continued to use stimulants into the second half of pregnancy (RR 1.30; 95% CI 1.10– 
1.55) relative to those with only early pregnancy exposure (Cohen et al. 2017). Cumu-
latively, these data suggest a small increased risk for adverse obstetrical outcomes with 
psychostimulant use during pregnancy. 
Long-term effects. Data regarding fetal neurodevelopment and long-term offspring 
outcomes are limited. Data from animal models have demonstrated alterations in do-
pamine receptor expression and transmission in rodents (Flores et al. 2011), as well as 
decreased motor activity in postpubertal zebrafish (Levin et al. 2011), but it is unclear 
how that may relate to clinical relevance in humans. Human studies are limited, al -
though one large, population-based, prospective cohort study found that prescribed 
stimulant exposure was associated with an increased risk of neonatal CNS disorders, 
such as seizures (Nörby et al. 2017).
