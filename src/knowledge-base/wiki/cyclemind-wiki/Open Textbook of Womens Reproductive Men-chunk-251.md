Obstetrical Outcomes 
Women with SUDs are more likely to experience poor nutrition, inadequate prenatal 
care, poverty, chronic medical problems such as HIV and hepatitis C, and higher rates 
of intimate partner violence (Cook et al. 2017; Forray and Foster 2015). Medical com-
plications of drug use, such as bacteremia, cardiac disease, cellulitis, diabetes, sexually 
transmitted infections, urinary tract infections, and vitamin deficiencies are common. 
Use of alcohol and drugs impairs absorption of micronutrients, which can lead to fetal 
abnormalities such as intrauterine growth restriction and fetal alcohol spectrum disor-
der. Depending on the specific drug exposure, other obstetrical complications can oc-
cur, such as placental abruption, placenta previa, intrauterine demise, spontaneous 
abortion, preterm labor and delivery, and premature rupture of membranes (Forray 
and Foster 2015). 
Maternal Mental Health 
Women with SUDs commonly experience co-occurring mental health disorders and re-
port more severe functional impairment compared with men (McHugh et al. 2018). 
More than half (and in some studies nearly all) of treatment-seeking women with co-
occurring mental health and SUDs have a hi story of trauma (Greenfield et al. 2010). 
Women are more likely to be referred to substance use treatment by community agen-
cies, mental health providers, and physical health care providers rather than by the le-
gal system, which is a more common referral pathway for men (Greenfield et al. 2010). 
Women may seek treatment for SUDs earlier in the course of illness than men, but they 
may require accommodations to stay in treatment due to social factors, including fam-
ily obligations and childcare needs (McHugh et al. 2018). For individual substances, 
overlap with maternal mental health conditions are common. For example, smoking 
tobacco during pregnancy is more than four times more common among pregnant 
women with a major depressive episode (Goodwin et al. 2017).
