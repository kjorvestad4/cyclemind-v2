Pregnancy introduces special considerations around trauma and intimate partner 
violence (IPV); consequently, major health care organizations such as the American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (ACOG) and the U.S. Preventive Services 
Task Force (USPSTF) encourage universal or widespread screening of individuals for 
IPV (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2012; Curry et al. 2018). In-
teractions with women suspected of being victims of IPV should be approached with 
empathy; women should be given a forum to discuss their trauma without being in-
terrogated and should be supported throug h safety planning, a ppropriate referrals, 
and documentation. Untreated trauma can adversely affect subsequent pregnancies, 
intimate and family relationships, and qual ity of life. Early detection and interven -
tion, increased public awareness, and an environment that validates perinatal trauma 
can modify and alleviate symptoms. 
This chapter focuses on trauma and PTSD as they relate to reproductive health. Trau-
matic experiences in the context of IPV/sexual trauma, reproductive loss, traumatic de-
livery, and voluntary termination of pregnancy (VTOP) may lead to the development 
of PTSD, with sexual trauma leading to higher rates of PTSD than other forms of trauma 
(Capaldi et al. 2012). Furthermore, a wo man’s history of childhood adversity and 
trauma significantly affects her likelihood of developing perinatal PTSD (discussed 
later) and affective disorders (see Chapter 13, “Depressive Disorders,” and Chapter 19, 
“Anxiety Disorders and Insomnia in the Perinatal Period”) in the context of perinatal 
stress (Smith et al. 2006). This chapter covers evaluation and treatment of PTSD in the 
context of IPV/sexual trauma, reproductive loss, traumatic delivery, and VTOP .
