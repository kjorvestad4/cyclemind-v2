Chapter 3, “Sexual Health and Reproductive Psychiatry.” 
Given the fear associated with pelvic examinations and childbirth for women with 
sexual pain disorders, some practices can be  put in place to reduce anxiety for these 
women. Childbirth education and preexposure to the labor and delivery ward can be 
helpful. Doula support and trauma-sensiti ve obstetrical practices are also recom -
mended, when available. Women with sexual pain disorders may also benefit from 
mindfulness-based approaches to childbirth, such as letting go of rigid goals or birth 
plans and instead cultivating self-accepta nce and being in the moment (Rosenbaum 
and Padoa 2012). Table 2–6 summarizes recommended practices for helping patients 
with sexual pain disorders prepare for delivery. Important points regarding GPPPD 
are presented in Table 2–7.

to provide support for the patient who is navigating a complicated and often confusing 
medical system. It is important that patients  feel validated, because they have often 
heard, “It’s all in your head.” Moreover, given that women with many of these condi-

distrust toward the medical system. Mental health professionals are uniquely poised to 
support women who find themselves with uncertain diagnoses or who have experi -
enced treatment options that still leave them with residual symptoms.
