635 Eating Disorders 
Studies of other second-generation antipsychotics, including risperidone, queti -
apine, and aripiprazole, have shown mixed results, with olanzapine remaining the 
agent with the most consistent evidence for efficacy in AN (Davis and Attia 2017). Case 
reports have also described the use of aripiprazole in BN (Trunko et al. 2011), although 
other second-generation antipsychotics may be avoided by clinicians due to concerns 
that the side effect of appetite induction could contribute to binge episodes. 
Antiepileptics 
Topiramate has been shown to reduce both bingeing and purging in individuals with 
BN (Hoopes et al. 2003), although its associ ation with weight loss can inhibit its use 
in some individuals for whom weight loss may be contraindicated. Topiramate can 
also be used as treatment for BED, with demonstrated reductions in binge eating fre-
quency and weight that appear to sustain with longer-term use (McElroy et al. 2004). 
It has also been trialed in combination wi th phentermine in treatment of BED, with 
limited studies showing cessation of binge eating and significant weight loss (Davis 
and Attia 2017). However, initiation of these medications during pregnancy requires 
a risk-risk discussion given the potential im pact on both mother and fetus, particu -
larly given the association with malformations seen with topiramate and concerns for 
weight loss with phentermine. For a full discussion of the potential risks in the peri -
natal period, please see Table 22–6. 
Zonisamide has also been studied for BED, showing benefits in both weight loss and 
reducing binge episodes. In one study in which zonisamide was added to cognitive-
behavioral therapy (CBT), patients with zonisamide augmentation had reduced BMI 
and reduced binge eating frequency 1 year after treatment (Ricca et al. 2009). 
Other Medications 
Stimulants and other weight/appetite-impacting medications have also been studied 
in BED. A full discussion of risks associated with use of stimulants in the perinatal 
period can be found in Chapter 23, “Attention-Deficit/Hyperactivity Disorder.” Lis-
dexamfetamine is the first medication to receive an FDA indication for BED, with sup-
porting studies demonstrating significant reductions in binge eating frequency and 
improvement in Clinical Global Impressions scores (McElroy et al. 2015). Although it 
does not have specific indications, lisdexamfetamine is also associated with decreas-
ing weight, BMI, and triglyceride levels (Davis and Attia 2019). The lipase inhibitor 
orlistat has been studied in BED, showing benefit in weight loss and reducing Eating 
Disorder Inventory–2 scores, although not for reducing binge-eating frequency. Chro-
mium has also been studied in BED, with moderate doses demonstrating improved 
glycemic control (not seen with placebo or high-dose groups), although further study 
is needed (Davis and Attia 2017). The risks associated with these nonstimulant appe-
tite-impacting medications are described in Table 22–6. 
Used as an adjunct for some anxiety disorders, D-cycloserine has shown mixed re-
sults in the treatment of AN when combined with exposure therapy (Davis and Attia 
2017). The synthetic cannabinoid agonist dronabinol has shown some benefit in weight 
gain compared with placebo for women with AN, although it was also associated with 
increased energy expenditure (Davis and Attia 2017). Information for risk-risk discus-
sion in the perinatal period for these supplements is also found in Table 22–6.
