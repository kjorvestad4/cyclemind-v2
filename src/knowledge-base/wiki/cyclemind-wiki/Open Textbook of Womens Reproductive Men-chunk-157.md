Key Points 
• Diets consisting of whole foods, fruits, vegetables, fish, and seafood have been 
associated with decreased perinatal depression and anxiety. 
• Folate has multiple important effects on fetal growth and neurodevelopment. 
Deficiencies have been associated with neural tube defects, cleft lip, poor cog-
nitive functioning, and emotional problems in children and may also be asso-
ciated with maternal depression. 
• Omega-3 fatty acids may improve obstetrical outcomes, reduce inflammatory 
cytokines, and improve perinatal depressive symptoms. 
• There is limited evidence on the effectiveness of melatonin for insomnia during 
pregnancy. 
• Mind-body treatments have demonstrated effectiveness in helping lower anx-
iety and depression in pregnancy. 
• Exercise, even light-to-moderate physical activity, has been associated with 
reduced depression, stress, and anxiety in pregnancy; reduced postpartum 
depressive symptoms; and other health benefits in pregnancy. 
• Insomnia increases the risk of a mood disorder, and poor sleep quality is 
strongly associated with postpartum depression and anxiety. 
• Bright light therapy is a low-cost and home-based treatment that may alleviate 
sleep disturbance and depression.

Bipolar disorder (BD) is a common psychiatric illness often associated with signifi-
cant impairments in quality of life and psychosocial functioning (Yatham et al. 2018). 
BD frequently presents in adolescence or early adulthood, coinciding with peak repro-
ductive years. Although the sex ratio of BD is roughly equal, there are distinctions in 
the phenomenology of the illness between women and men. The typical age of onset is 
later in women than in men, and women with BD may experience symptom exacerba-
tion in accordance with menstrual cycle hormonal fluctuations, in the perinatal period, 
and in the menopausal transition (Freeman and Gelenberg 2005). BD is a chronic illness 
that typically requires ongoing management during both acute episodes and periods 
of maintenance.

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Prevalence estimates suggest a cumulative 12-month prevalence of 2.4% for bipolar 
spectrum disorders and lower rates for indivi dual subtypes such as BD type I (BDI; 
0.6%) and BD type II (BDII; 0.4%) (Yatham et al. 2018). Age of onset is typically be -
tween 12 and 30 years of age, coinciding with the peak reproductive years (Epstein et 
al. 2014). 
Risk Factors 
General risk factors for BD include a family history of mood disorder, traumatic life 
events, and substance use (Roberts 2019). A previous perinatal mood episode is the 
strongest predictor for a perinatal recurrence of illness for women with BD (Di Florio 
et al. 2018). Primiparity has also been associated with a higher risk of a postpartum 
manic or psychotic episode in women with BD. In a study that included 934 women 
with BDI, 35% reported an episode of mani a or psychotic depression after their first 
pregnancy, compared with 20.5% after their second pregnancy and 14.6% after their 
third pregnancy (Di Florio et al. 2014). Other important risk factors for a postpartum
