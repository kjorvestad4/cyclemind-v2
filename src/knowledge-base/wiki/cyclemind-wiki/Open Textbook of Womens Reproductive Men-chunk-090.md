Serotonin also plays an important role in VMS. In animal and human models, es -
trogen appears to modulate serotonin synt hesis through multiple effects, including 
increased activity of tryptophan hydroxylase (the enzyme involved in serotonin bio-
synthesis), upregulation of serotonin 5-HT2A and 5-HTC receptors, and effects on the 
serotonin transporter (Krolick et al. 2018; Kugaya et al. 2003; Shanmugan and Epper-
son 2014). Hypothalamic neuronal systems involved in gonadotropin secretion, such 
as the kisspeptin, neurokinin B, and dynorphin (KND) signal system, have also been 
found in animal and human models to be associated with VMS (Bansal and Aggarwal 
2019). The finding that injection of neurokinin B in postmenopausal women leads to 
hot flashes (Jayasena et al. 2015) triggered the development of neurokinin 3 receptor 
antagonists as a treatment strategy. Initia l phase-2 double-blind RCTs have yielded 
promising results, and recent and ongoing studies include the investigation of opti -
mal dosing and safety (Depypere et al. 2019; Prague et al. 2018). 
Psychological Factors 
Psychosocial distress, including anxiety and depression, has been consistently associ-
ated with VMS (Avis et al. 2018). Cross-sectional studies do not allow the determina-
tion of whether VMS or psychological distress comes first, but longitudinal studies do 
allow this, and POAS found that anxiety and depression precede the onset of VMS 
(Freeman et al. 2005, 2009). In SWAN, the presence of symptoms of anxiety and depres-
sion along with VMS at subjects’ first visit was associated with longer duration of VMS; 
furthermore, higher stress levels and higher sensitivity to symptoms are related to lon-
ger-lasting VMS (Avis et al. 2015). However, the directionality of causation is not com-
pletely understood, and it may indeed be bidirectional and complex. Not all women 
experience the same pattern of perimenopausal symptoms, and the relative roles of en-
docrinological and psychosocial factors may vary by subgroup (Avis et al. 2018).

Biological factors related to both VMS and culture include estrogen levels and varying 
genes involved in estrogen metabolism, lifestyle components (e.g., smoking, diet, body 
composition), and rates of gynecological surgeries (e.g., tubal ligation, hysterectomy, oo-
phorectomy) (Avis et al. 2018). Nonbiological or social factors include education; atti-
tudes toward menopause or VMS; sensitivity to VMS; language, which may affect the 
terminology used to describe or name VMS; and cultural norms around how the topic is 
regarded (Avis et al. 2018; Melby 2005; Melby et al. 2005, 2011; Pérez-Alcalá et al. 2013). 
The effects that specific diets, alcohol consumption, and level of physical activity have 
on symptoms have been studied by several investigators, with conflicting results (Avis 
et al. 2018). High consumption of dietary phytoestrogens had been thought to be useful 
in limiting VMS, but SWAN was unable to show that phytoestrogen intake is inversely 
associated with VMS or to account for the lower levels of symptoms reported by Asian 
women (Gold et al. 2006). A survey of 745 women living primarily (91.6%) in the United 
States and undergoing the transition to menopause found a significant link between a 
vegan diet and less bothersome VMS and other physical symptoms of menopause and a 
trend toward less troublesome symptoms with lower consumption of meat and higher 
consumption of vegetables (Beezhold et al. 2018). However, this study’s limitations are 
substantial, including self-report, recall bias, and the limited socioeconomic range of 
the participants, and replication with more rigorous methodology is needed. 
Treatment 
Psychopharmacology 
The only antidepressant with FDA approval (in 2013) for the treatment of VMS related 
to menopause is paroxetine, based on two large RCTs. Both trials described significant 
improvement in VMS in women who received the active medication, which was also 
well tolerated (see Carroll et al. 2015). Of note, the dosing of paroxetine in these stud-
ies, using the formulation marketed as Brisdelle, was 7.5 mg/day, significantly lower 
than the dose typically required to treat mood and anxiety disorders (20–60 mg/day). 
Similarly, as mentioned, ma ny first-line agents used to treat perimenopausal de -
pression, including citalopram, desvenlafaxine, duloxetine, escitalopram, fluoxetine, 
mirtazapine, venlafaxine, and vortioxetine , have also shown efficacy for treating 
VMS, often at lower doses than those used for clinical depression and anxiety, and are 
reasonable options (Joffe et al. 2003, 2007; Maki et al. 2018; Soares et al. 2006). 
Hormonal Treatment 
The use of estrogen to treat symptoms of perimenopause has a complicated and at times 
controversial history; knowing highlights of this history can help clinicians address the 
hesitation many patients and providers have regarding estrogen therapy. Estrogen first 
became commercially available in the United States in 1933 and was used to treat a range 
of perimenopausal complaints. The popularity of estrogen products grew, and by 1975, 
estrogen was the fifth most commonly prescribed drug in the United States (Kohn et al. 
2019). In the late 1970s, reports of an increased risk for endometrial cancer led to de -
creased use, but with the addition of progestogen or natural progesterone to decrease 
endometrial hypertrophy, use once again increased (Kohn et al. 2019).

Then, in the early 2000s, the results of one trial reshaped the history of MHT. The 
Women’s Health Initiative (WHI), a large multicenter primary prevention RCT for cor-
onary heart disease (CHD), was designed to assess the risks and benefits of MHT in 
healthy postmenopausal women. Because the prevention of CHD was the primary 
endpoint, the investigators intermittently examined the data to determine whether the 
benefits for CHD episodes outweighed the risks of MHT. The study had a planned du-
ration of 8.5 years, with two active arms, both compared with placebo: 1) women with 
a uterus receiving oral conjugated equine estrogen (CEE) plus an oral progestogen 
(medroxyprogesterone acetate [MPA]) versus placebo, and 2) women with prior hys-
terectomy receiving CEE alone. In May 2002, however, the trial was stopped ahead of 
schedule because in the CEE+MPA group (after a mean of 5.2 years of follow-up), the 
test statistic for invasive breast cancer exceeded the stopping boundary, and the global 
index statistic suggested that risks were exceeding benefits. The absolute excess risks 
per 10,000 person-years were seven more CHD events, eight more strokes, eight more 
pulmonary emboli, and eight more invasive breast cancers. The absolute risk reduc-
tions per 10,000 person-years were six fewer colorectal cancers and five fewer hip frac-
tures (Rossouw et al. 2002). After the publication of these initial results, which were 
widely covered in the press, prescriptions for MHT fell by as much as 80%, and provi-
sion of training in prescribing MHT dwindled rapidly (Chester et al. 2018). 
Despite this initial panic, further consid eration of the WHI results supports the 
provision of MHT to otherwise healthy women experiencing troublesome VMS. The 
average age of WHI participants at enrollme nt was 63 years, and many were not ex -
periencing VMS. In fact, the presence of moderate VMS prevented women from enroll-
ing in the study. The overall cohort thus differed significantly from the younger, 
symptomatic population of earlier observational studies and from the population that 
typically seeks treatment for symptoms of perimenopause. In fact, analysis of WHI data 
by age showed a reduction in myocardial infarction and all-cause mortality in women 
ages 50–59 treated with CEE alone; only women ages 70–79 had increased risks. For 
the group treated with CEE+MPA, the increased risk of myocardial infarction was 
minimal in women who were still within 10 years of their final menstrual period and 
younger than age 60. Risk of myocardial infarction increased with age and time since 
final menstrual period. Furthermore, when a particular woman develops VMS might 
provide information about her risk for cardiovascular disease: women in the WHI trial 
with VMS early in menopause had a lower risk of cardiovascular disease and all-cause 
mortality, whereas women still having hot flashes 14 years after menopause on average 
had a higher risk of CHD and all-cause mortality (Maclennan et al. 2004). 
Research since the WHI has sought to reconcile its results with those of the many 
prior studies that encouraged the prescripti on of MHT. Recent studies have investi -
gated younger women and lower doses of estrogen and have compared transdermal 
with oral estrogen preparations such as CEE or oral estradiol. The “timing hypothe -
sis” posits that age and time since menopause are key; risks of MHT are lower when 
it is prescribed closer to the menopaus al transition. Women who are <10 years from 
their final menstrual period at initiation  of MHT , or age 50–59 years when MHT is 
started, have lower hazard ratios for CHD than women starting MHT at an older age. 
Furthermore, transdermal estrogen appears to convey a lower risk of thromboembolic 
events than oral estrogen and is as effective in treating VMS (Canonico et al. 2007).

Thus, closer subanalyses of the WHI results, as well as studies conducted in its 
aftermath, yield adjusted recommendations for MHT in healthy women early in meno-
pause. Multiple expert organizations, including the North American Menopause So-
ciety, the Endocrine Society, and the Amer ican Society for Reproductive Medicine, 
concur. Recommendations are no longer “the smallest dose possible for the shortest pe-
riod of time” but rather the type, dose, and duration appropriate for each woman. The 
consensus of these multiple organizations is that for women younger than age 60 or 
within 10 years of the onset of menopause, and without contraindications, the benefit-
risk ratio is favorable for treating moderate to severe VMS, as well as for the prevention 
of bone loss or fracture in those at elevated risk (Pinkerton et al. 2017; Stuenkel et al. 
2015). Finally, nearly two decades after the abrupt cessation of the WHI study, analyses 
of all-cause mortality and mo rtality related to cancer or cardiovascular disease were 
not different for women in either of the hormone groups (CEE+MPA or CEE alone) 
compared with placebo (Manson et al. 2017).
