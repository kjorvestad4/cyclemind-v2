Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a rare but serious condition typically considered to 
be a psychiatric emergency. Although PPP may occur outside of the setting of BD, there 
is a close link between BD and this distinct clinical syndrome. For a large percentage 
of women who experience PPP , the episode can be understood as either a first episode 
or a recurrence of an underlying BD (Wesseloo et al. 2016). A personal or family history 
of BD is one of the greatest risk factors for PPP (Bergink et al. 2016; Jones and Craddock 
2001). For a thorough discussion of PPP , including its clinical presentation, epidemiol-
ogy, and treatment, please see Chapter 16,“Postpartum Psychosis.” 
Decisions regarding maintenance medications in pregnant women with BD strongly 
influence the risk of recurrence. One prospective cohort study examined the risk of 
mood episode recurrence in pregnancy among women with BD who were euthymic at 
conception. Women who discontinued mood stabilizer treatment experienced twice as 
many episodes of recurrence as those who continued maintenance treatment. Women 
who discontinued medication quickly (over a period of <2 weeks), experienced a more 
rapid relapse as opposed to those who tapered mood stabilizer medication more grad-
ually. Most mood episodes were characterized as major depression, followed by mixed 
state, hypomania, and mania (Viguera et al. 2007).
