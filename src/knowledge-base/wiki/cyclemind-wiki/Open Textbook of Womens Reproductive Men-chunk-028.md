Female Sexual Functioning 
Female sexual functioning incorporates multiple domains of sexual response, includ-
ing sexual desire (i.e., sexual “hunger,” interest, or drive), cognitive arousal (i.e., men-
tal excitement associated with sexual acti vity, feeling “turned on”), genital arousal 
(i.e., vulvovaginal lubrication, genital en gorgement, and sensitivity associated with 
sexual activity), and orgasm (i.e., transien t peak of pleasure sensation and release of 
sexual tension, usually accompanied by involuntary muscle contractions). Female 
sexual functioning is thought to be multifactorial, affected by biological/physiologi -
cal, psychological, interpersonal, and sociocultural factors. 
Biopsychosocial Model of 
Female Sexual Functioning 
Classic sexual response models, such as that by Masters and Johnson (1970), described 
a linear model of sexual response progressing from excitement, through plateau and 
orgasm, to resolution. Contemporary models of female sexual functioning describe a 
more dynamic, circular model of female sexual response that involves an interplay of 
emotional and physical intimacy, relationship satisfaction, sexual stimuli, and psy -
chological factors. These models indicate that some women are likely to begin from a 
place of “sexual neutrality” and seek out, or are receptive to, sexual stimuli for reasons 
other than spontaneous sexual desire (e.g., emotional intimacy with a partner) (Bas-
son 2000, 2001; Kaplan 1974). Thus, desire may be “responsive,” because sexual stim-
uli and arousal may precede sexual desire. These models also indicate that both the 
emotional and physical aspects of satisfaction are important outcomes of sexual activity. 
Disruptions in any part of the sexual response cycle may contribute to diminished 
sexual functioning and FSDs. Furthermore, it has been proposed that sexual response 
is determined by neurochemical and hormonal underpinnings, including excitatory 
factors (e.g., dopamine, norepinephrine, testosterone) and inhibitory factors (e.g., se-
rotonin, prolactin) (Kingsberg et al. 2015). According to dual-control biopsychosocial 
models (Bancroft et al. 2009; Perelman 2007), an imbalance in these factors (e.g., di -
minished excitatory or excessive inhibitory factors) can result in sexual dysfunction, 
including disruptions in desire and orgasm (Kingsberg et al. 2015; Parish et al. 2016; 
Portman et al. 2017). 
Studies have shown higher levels of the inhibitory neurotransmitters (e.g., sero -
tonin, endocannabinoids) versus excitatory  neurotransmitters (e.g., norepinephrine, 
oxytocin, dopamine, the melanocortins) in women diagnosed with hypoactive sexual 
desire disorder (HSDD) (Portman et al. 2017). Other neurochemical and hormonal fac-
tors, such as the estrogen deficiency as sociated with genitourinary syndrome of 
menopause (GSM) contributing to vulvovaginal atrophy and the sexual pain and vag-
inal dryness associated with lactation in postpartum women, may determine sexual 
response. The neurobiology of women’s pleasure has historically been understudied 
and poorly understood, but recent attempts have been made to elucidate the neuronal 
basis of sexual response in women. Functional MRI data show a gradual increase in 
brain activity leading up to and during orgasm in women. The brain regions activated

Female Sexual Dysfunctions 
FSDs are the experience of a persistent, distressing impairment in a domain of sexual 
response (i.e., desire, cognitive and genital arousal, orgasm) or the experience of sexual 
pain that is not otherwise accounted for by other medical or psychiatric conditions, the 
effects of medication or other substance use, other psychosocial stressors, or relation-
ship distress or problems (American Psychiatric Association 2013; Parish et al. 2019). 
Although specific definitions vary, dysfunction can be distinguished from normal vari-
ations in sexual functioning based on the persistence (e.g., minimum of 3–6 months) 
and frequency (e.g., impairment occurs in 75% or more of sexual experiences) of symp-
toms and the experience of distress (Parish et al. 2019; Simon and Lukas 2017). Sexual 
distress can refer to personal distress (e.g., concern, anger, frustration) or be related to 
the impact on the partner or relationship. 
DSM-IV-TR (American Psychiatric Association 2000) included diagnoses of HSDD 
(persistent lack of sexual desire or motiva tion), sexual aversion disorder (persistent 
aversive reaction to and avoidance of genital sexual contact with a sexual partner), fe-
male sexual arousal disorder (FSAD; persistent lack of genital lubrication-swelling re-
sponse of sexual excitement), female orga smic disorder (persistent lack of orgasm 
following excitement and adequate stimulation), vaginismus (persistent involuntary 
muscle spasm or contraction of muscles around the vagina with penetration or inter-
course that is often experienced as painful or impossible), and dyspareunia (persistent 
genital pain before, during, or after penetration or intercourse, often classified as su-
perficial or deep in terms of location of pain). (For further discussion of genito-pelvic 
pain/penetration disorder, specifically dyspareunia and vaginismus, please see Chap-
ter 2.) DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Associ ation 2013) includes diagnoses of female 
sexual interest/arousal disorder (integrating DSM-IV-TR’s HSDD and FSAD), female 
orgasmic disorder, and female genito-pelvi c pain/penetration di sorder (integrating
