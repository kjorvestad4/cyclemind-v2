Some studies have looked at differences in course and symptoms according to time 
of symptom onset. Women with onset during pregnancy have been found to have 
higher rates of prior depressive episodes and higher rates of ps ychosocial stressors 
than women with onset in the postpartum, who are more likely to have obsessive-com-
pulsive and psychotic symptoms (Altemus et al. 2012). Another group recently exam-
ined the trajectory of depressive symptoms  across time in the perinatal period and 
identified four groups: two that were stable over time, with persistent symptoms of ei-
ther low or moderate severity; one group with more severe symptoms during preg -
nancy that decreased in the postpartum; and another group that had few or moderate 
symptoms during pregnancy but develope d severe symptoms in the postpartum 
(Ahmed et al. 2018). Certain characteristics predicted which women would belong to 
which group. Those women who reported more stress were more likely to belong to 
the higher-severity groups. Women with a prior history of depression were most likely 
to belong to the antepartum or moderate -stable groups. Being nonwhite and having 
poor partner support were also associated with the antepartum group, and only young 
maternal age was associated with the postpartum group. Another group that looked at 
symptoms in pregnancy only found three classes—increasing, decreasing, and stable— 
and similarly found that poor partner suppo rt was the key distinguishing factor for 
women whose symptoms worsened (Boekhorst et al. 2019). 
Although perinatal depression is not known to have a different natural course than 
that of depression outside the perinatal peri od (although research is sparse), this ill -
ness may have lasting effects on a woman’s health long after the acute symptoms have 
passed. Women with perinatal depression may have a “reproductive subtype” of de-
pression and may have symptoms at other times of hormonal transition, including the 
premenstrual period and perimenopause (Payne et al. 2009). In addition, perinatal de-
pression is associated with other morbidities of pregnancy (e.g., gestational diabetes, 
preeclampsia, preterm birth) that have lasting effects on the woman’s or child’s 
health. Gestational diabetes is associated with an increased risk of subsequent Type II 
diabetes and preeclampsia with a subsequent increased risk of cardiovascular disease 
(Chasan-Taber 2016; Perry et al. 2018).
