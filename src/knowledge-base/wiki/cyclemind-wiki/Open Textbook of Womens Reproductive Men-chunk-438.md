417 Bipolar Disorder and Related Disorders 
Lamotrigine 
Lamotrigine is an antiepileptic drug approved by the FDA for treatment of bipolar de-
pression and maintenance treatment of BD. Lamotrigine does not have evidence to 
support its use in acute mania (Yatham et al. 2018). Lamotrigine’s relative reproduc-
tive safety and emerging evidence base in  the perinatal period make it an emerging 
first-line option for treatment of BD during pregnancy, particularly in women with a 
preponderance of depressive episodes. A small naturalistic study showed that lamo-
trigine was effective in preventing symptom relapse during pregnancy and the post-
partum period when women with BD were continued on the drug during pregnancy 
(Prakash et al. 2016). A population-based cohort study found that lamotrigine was as 
effective as lithium in preventing postpartum relapse in women with BD; this study, 
however, was limited by its small number of participants and observational design 
(Wesseloo et al. 2017). Because participants in this study with a history of acute mania 
were more likely to be taking lithium, wh ereas participants with  a vulnerability to -
ward depressive episodes were more likely to be taking lamotrigine, the results 
should be understood in the context of potential confounding by indication. 
Early pregnancy. Although an initial publication from the North American Anti-
epileptic Pregnancy Registry reported a small increased risk for oral clefts in 684 in -
fants exposed to lamotrigine in the first trimester (Holmes et al. 2008), more recently, 
a population-based case-control study of 10.1 million births did not observe an asso-
ciation between lamotrigine in pregnancy and overall malformation, including no in-
creased risk of cleft palate (Dolk et al. 2016). This large study is consistent with several 
international registries that reported no elevated risk of malformations with lamotrig-
ine use in the first trimester (Campbell et al. 2014; Cunnington et al. 2005; Tomson et 
al. 2018). 
Obstetrical outcomes. A recent systematic review and meta-analysis did not find 
an increase in rates of spontaneous abortions, stillbirths, preterm deliveries, or small for 
gestational age infants with exposure to lamotrigine in pregnancy (Pariente et al. 2017). 
Neonatal outcomes. Lamotrigine exposure during pregnancy has not been associ-
ated with adverse neonatal effects (Epstein et al. 2014). 
Long-term effects. Available data on infant neurodevelopmental outcomes demon-
strate no significant concerns on cognitive scores (Baker et al. 2015; Epstein et al. 2014; 
Meador et al. 2013). 
Management during pregnancy. Lamotrigine is metabolized almost completely 
via glucuronic acid conjugation to its inactive metabolite in the liver. Estradiol upreg-
ulates hepatic glucuronidation; thus, rising estradiol levels across pregnancy result in 
increased clearance of lamotrigine and decreased serum lamotrigine concentrations 
(Clark et al. 2013). The metabolism of lamotrigine increases as early as 8 weeks’ gesta-
tion, continues progressively into the third trimester, then declines precipitously over 
the first 2 weeks postpartum. Although individual variability exists, lamotrigine clear-
ance potentially increases more than 300% throughout pregnancy (Deligiannidis et al. 
2014). Because the therapeutic serum concentration range for lamotrigine maintenance
