In perimenopause, declines in estrogen contribute to physiological changes that af-
fect sexual functioning. Androgens decline gradually with age; in women in their late 
40s or early 50s, testosterone levels are approximately half what they were when the 
women were in their early 20s. Decreased lubrication with arousal is often the first no-
ticeable change with menopause (North American Menopause Society 2021). Subse-
quent vascular, connective tissue, and musc ular changes occur over time that also 
affect sexual function (e.g., decreased va scularization affects genital arousal). For 
some women, declining levels of free testosterone, associated with declining ovarian 
function, cause decreased sexual drive.
