Cannabis 
The endocannabinoid system is present early in fetal development, and it plays a key 
role in mediating neuronal maturation and development of key neurotransmitter sys-
tems in the CNS. Cannabis exposure in pregnancy during critical periods of brain de-
velopment can induce subtle and long-lasting alterations in neurodevelopment (Jaques 
et al. 2014). Current evidence does not support an association between marijuana ex-
posure and any specific congenital birth defects (Metz and Stickrath 2015). Cannabis 
exposure in pregnancy has been associated with stillbirth, preterm birth, fetal growth 
restriction, and lower birth weight, but these findings have been inconsistent and con-
founded by frequent co-use with tobacco and other substances and sociodemographic 
factors (Metz and Stickrath 2015). Also noteworthy is that the potency of marijuana has 
been steadily increasing, from about 3% in the 1980s to 12% in 2012, so data from pre-
vious studies may no longer be relevant (Volkow et al. 2014). Cannabis is lipophilic; 
therefore, it readily crosses the placental barrier and passes into breastmilk; breastmilk 
levels of delta-tetrahydrocannabinol can a ccumulate up to eightfold in comparison 
with plasma levels and can be found in breastmilk for up to 6 days following maternal 
use (Ordean and Kim 2020). 
Three long-term prospective longitudinal human studies followed offspring from 
the neonatal period into childhood and ad olescence. Subtle neurobehavioral distur -
bances have been demonstrated in cannabis-exposed neonates, such as exaggerated 
and prolonged startle responses and sleep-cycle problems (Jaques et al. 2014). Greater 
externalizing behaviors, including impulsiv ity and hyperactivity, were reported in 
children, as well as impairments in early executive function, abstract and visual rea -
soning, and visuoperceptual functioning in adolescents (Huizink 2014). It is not pos-
sible to determine the degree to which these effects were directly related to prenatal 
cannabis exposure or to potential confounding variables. In addition, a large retro -
spective analysis of all live births in Ontario, Canada, over a 5-year period recently 
found an increased rate of autism in the children of mothers who used cannabis in 
pregnancy (Corsi et al. 2020). 
Sedative-Hypnotics 
Research regarding benzodiazepine exposure  in pregnancy is limited to prescribed 
benzodiazepines. The data regarding fetal risks associated with in-utero exposure to 
benzodiazepines are mixed and inconclusive (McLafferty et al. 2016). In addition to 
the potential risks of direct exposure, there are concerns about the effects of benzodi-
azepine withdrawal on the fe tus, as discussed later in the “Treatment” section. For 
further detail on the uses and risks of benzodiazepines in pregnancy and lactation, 
please see Chapter 19, “Anxiety Disorders and Insomnia in the Perinatal Period.”
