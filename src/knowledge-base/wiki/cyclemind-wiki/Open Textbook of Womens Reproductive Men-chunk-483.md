Delivery effects. There is a paucity of data regarding neonatal outcomes in the set-
ting of SGA exposure during pregnancy, and the studies that have been reported are 
difficult to interpret due to concomitant exposure to maternal mental illness, comor -
bidities (e.g., smoking and poor nutrition), and polypharmacy. Although causality 
cannot be established, SGAs as a class have been associated with EPS, neonatal with-
drawal symptoms, and NICU admission (Galbally et al. 2014; Habermann et al. 2013; 
Kulkarni et al. 2014; Sadowski et al. 2013). As previously discussed, both SGAs and 
FGAs may be associated with transient EP S after neonatal exposure. Neonatal with -
drawal symptoms have also been described, including respiratory distress, feeding 
difficulties, hypotension, and somnolence (Epstein et al. 2014). 
Long-term effects. Neurodevelopmental studies of the effects of SGAs in utero are 
limited. One prospective controlled study compared 22 infants age 6 months who had 
been exposed to either SGA or FGA medications in utero with 85 nonexposed infants 
and found that both antipsychotic exposure and underlying maternal mental illness 
(depression and psychosis) were associated with significantly lower scores on a stan-
dard test of neuromotor performance (Johnson et al. 2012). Another study followed 76 
infants with in-utero SGA exposure who were matched to 76 unexposed infants born 
to women without mental illness and found delayed development in cognitive, motor, 
social-emotional, and adaptive behavior at 2 months of age but not at 12 months. This 
study suggests transient ne urodevelopmental effects among infants born to women 
taking antipsychotic medications but cannot rule out the contribution of the underly-
ing illness (Peng et al. 2013). 
Clozapine 
Although clozapine is categorized as an SGA, it warrants separate mention because 
it is the gold standard medication for tr eatment-resistant schizophrenia. Due to po -
tential serious side effects (e.g., seizures, myocarditis, agranulocytosis, weight gain, 
excess sedation) and the need for routine serum monitoring, its use is reserved for pa-
tients for whom other treatments have been ineffective or intolerable (Warnez and 
Alessi-Severini 2014). Therefore, patients who are appropriate candidates for cloza -
pine are highly likely to require maintenance medication treatment throughout preg-
nancy to remain at their baseline level of function and symptom control (Friedman and 
Adityanjee 2003). Reports of clozapine use in pregnancy are very often confounded by 
polypharmacy, including known teratogens (Friedman and Adityanjee 2003). Mehta 
and Van Lieshout (2017) noted regarding the current state of the literature, “in the ab-
sence of control participants, it is unclear whether these risks are due to clozapine, the 
experience of serious psychotic illness, previous treatment, or a combination of these” 
(p. 2). Due to fluctuating clozapine levels during pregnancy, frequent monitoring of se-
rum levels and white blood cell count is recommended regardless of psychiatric sta -
bility (Nguyen et al. 2020).
