Rates 
According to a study of U.S. women (Shifren et al. 2008), distressing difficulties with 
desire (12.3% in midlife women) and arousal (7.5% in midlife women) tend to be high-
est among midlife women compared with younger women, and difficulties in orgasm 
(5.7% in midlife women) are more prevalent in midlife women than younger women. 
Risk Factors 
Perimenopause is often associated with specific psychosocial stressors, including role 
transitions such as retirement, that may affect female sexual function. With age, women 
become more likely to be affected by partner variables that can impair sexual function, 
such as decreased partner availability or a partner’s own diminished sexual function 
(Thomas and Thurston 2016; Thomas et al. 2015). However, many women experience 
improvement in sexual function with perimenopause (e.g., due to changes such as chil-
dren moving out of the home) and effectively adapt to physical changes with age and 
menopausal transition to maintain sexual activity (e.g., by placing more emphasis on 
emotional closeness aspects of sex). Research on factors specifically contributing to sex-
ual functioning resilience in midlife women is scarce. However, several psychosocial 
and interpersonal variables, including self-esteem, general life satisfaction, optimism, 
emotional support, absence of psychiatric conditions such as depression and anxiety, 
and higher general satisfaction with one’s partner and quality of relationship commu-
nication, are likely protective for midlife women’s sexual health (Brotto et al. 2016; 
Mernone et al. 2019). 
Research indicates that early menopause is associated with greater impairments in 
sexual function, and that rates of HS DD are higher among women with surgical 
menopause, which results in more abrupt reduction in circulating ovarian hormone 
levels than does natural menopause (Kingsberg et al. 2020). After surgical menopause, 
women’s androgen levels are approximately one-third those of women with natural 
menopause. Providers should be mindful of the increased risk with age of chronic dis-
eases (e.g., hypertension, diabetes) and use of medication(s) that can negatively impact 
women’s sexual functioning. Reduced physical function and activity that may occur 
with age (particularly in women with obesity or osteoarthritis) can negatively affect both 
body image and sexual activity. Moreover, vasomotor symptoms (e.g., hot flashes, night 
sweats) may indirectly impair sexual functioning (Thomas and Thurston 2016; Thomas
