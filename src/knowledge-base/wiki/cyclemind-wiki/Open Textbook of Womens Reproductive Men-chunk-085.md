Chapter 6, “Premenstrual 
Mood Syndromes,” progesterone is metabolized to allopregnanolone, and thus peri-
menopause is associated with fluctuating levels of allopregnanolone. Some women 
may not have GABA A receptors that adapt to this hormonal volatility; therefore, in 
perimenopause, as in premenstrual dyspho ric disorder and postpartum depression, 
this dynamic change in allopregnanolone is associated with dysphoria and anxiety 
(Gordon et al. 2015; Hantsoo and Epperson 2020). Genetics may play a role in response 
to allopregnanolone alterations: GABAA receptor subunit polymorphisms have been 
found to be associated with an increased risk of depression, and it has been proposed 
that this be investigated in perimenopausal depression (Gordon et al. 2015). 
Psychological Factors 
Psychological contributions to perimenopausal mood and anxiety disorders include at-
titudes toward menopause, personality traits, and cognitive styles, especially optimism 
and a self-critical cognitive style (Süss and Ehlert 2020). Most studies that include mea-
sures of attitudes toward menopause report that negative attitudes play a role in the 
etiology of menopausal depression and anxiety symptoms (Ayers et al. 2010). A bidi-
rectional relationship likely exists, because women who have more depressive symp-
toms also have more negative attitudes (Ayers et al. 2010). Similarly, the psychological

Social and Cultural Considerations 
In addition to biological and psychological factors, many social changes occur during 
the perimenopausal transition, and these may contribute to onset of depression and 
anxiety symptoms in vulnerable women. Role stress often escalates for women during 
this time as they attempt to juggle increased and varied responsibilities, which may in-
clude caring for aging family members, changing childcare needs, and new challenges 
at work and in their intimate relationships (Maki et al. 2018). To our knowledge, no 
large studies have attempted to quantify the contribution of these social and cultural 
differences to the etiology of perimenopausal depression. It is highly likely that differ-
ing cultural views of aging, and beliefs about the responsibilities associated with role 
changes, factor into the experience of stress and contribute to depressive symptoms ex-
perienced at this time of life. In their review of “empty nest psychosocial stress,” Bou-
gea et al. (2019) described the varying expectations that different families and cultures 
have regarding the care of elders, including whether living arrangements are indepen-
dent or shared, and how these expectations shift in times of economic or social crisis 
(Bougea et al. 2019). Social stresses associated with increased depression at other times 
of life, such as interpersonal violence, raci al discrimination, and poverty, likely also 
play a role in perimenopausal depressive symptoms. For instance, in a study of peri-
menopausal women in New York City, neighborhood characteristics of decreased 
safety and cohesiveness and increased raci al discriminatory actions were associated 
with increased depressive symptoms (Gerber and Sievert 2018). 
Treatment 
Complete Evaluation That Identifies Contributing Factors 
Treatment of a perimenopausal woman who has psychiatric symptoms begins with a 
comprehensive clinical interview that encompasses her current symptoms; prior his-
tory of psychiatric symptoms and treatment; medical history, including reproductive 
history; and social and family history (Stute et al. 2020). The interview should include 
a detailed review of current physical symptoms; list of current medications, including 
over-the-counter supplements; typical diet, sleep, and exercise habits; recent and ongo-
ing stressors; and consumption of caffeine, alcohol, marijuana, and other substances. 
Information about recent menstrual cycle patterns, sexual function, and VMS should 
also be elicited (Canonico et al. 2007; Joffe et al. 2003; Krause and Nakajima 2015). A 
one-time hormone level is not terribly informative in the assessment and treatment of
