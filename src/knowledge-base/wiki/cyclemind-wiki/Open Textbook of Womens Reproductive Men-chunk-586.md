Evaluation and Differential Diagnosis 
In addition to using the screening scales mentioned earlier, psychiatric practitioners 
must adequately rule out common medical comorbidities that might mimic or exacer-
bate obsessive and compulsive symptoms in the perinatal patient. In particular, prac-
titioners should conduct a thorough me dical history and physical examination, 
supplemented with laboratory studies when indicated. Referral to the patient’s obstet-
rical provider or other appropriate specialist is indicated with any abnormality in his-
tory, physical examination, or laboratory evaluation. 
The differential diagnosis of OCD in the perinatal period is listed in Table 20–3 
(Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). OCD in the postpartum period is often misdiag -
nosed as either generalized anxiety disorder (GAD) or PPD. The primary difference 
between OCD and GAD is the nature of th e thoughts; in GAD, thoughts focus on 
worry over routine events and are characterized by apprehension and catastrophic 
thinking. Obsessional thinking is common in PPD. Perinatal depression typically 
presents with classic depressive symptoms, including sadness or a feeling of being 
numb or detached; neurovegetative symptoms , such as altered sleep, appetite, and 
energy; and, at times, the presence of hopelessness  or suicidal ideation (see Chapter 
13, “Depressive Disorders”). It can also present with high levels of comorbid anxiety 
that can include panic attacks, anxious rumination, psychomotor agitation, and even 
obsessions. Perinatal depression with obsessions presents with depressive rumina -
tions, content reflective of real-life circumstances and worries, and typically shifts in 
content over time; perinatal OCD, on the other hand, presents with senseless intrusive 
thoughts and concern about disastrous outcomes, with content that is stable over time 
(Abramowitz et al. 2003; Wisner et al. 1999 ). Depression is often erroneously diag -
nosed when OCD is the primary diagnosis (Abramowitz et al. 2010b). The crucial im-
plication of this difference is inadequate treatment choices; depression and OCD are 
often both treated with selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs), but OCD typi-
cally requires more aggressive dosing and exposure therapy (Abramowitz et al. 
2010b). 
An important distinction must be made  between postpartum OCD and postpar -
tum psychosis (PPP), especially in the cont ext of thoughts about harming the infant.
