Other well-established risk factors include low socioeconomic status, single moth-
erhood, adolescent pregnancy, low education status, unwanted pregnancy, childhood 
trauma, intimate partner violence, and obstetrical and delivery complications (Gelaye 
et al. 2016; Kimmel et al. 2015; Lancaster et al. 2010; Stuart-Parrigon and Stuart 2014). 
Some risk factors vary according to cultural context; for example, stressful life events 
in one large meta-analysis were an import ant risk factor only in non-Asian samples, 
and the sex of the child may be a risk factor in some cultures (Robertson et al. 2004). 
Although many studies indicate higher rates in Black and Latinx populations, a lack 
of social support, lack of access, and lack of trust in the medical establishment may ex-
plain some of this discrepancy (Cannon and Nasrallah 2019). In addition, some studies 
find that ethnicity and race are no longer predictive once marital status, poverty, edu-
cation, and subjective socioeconomic status are taken into account (Dolbier et al. 2013). 
Other studies have indicated that social support can buffer symptom severity and that 
those buffering effects do not differ according to race or ethnicity (Pao et al. 2019).
