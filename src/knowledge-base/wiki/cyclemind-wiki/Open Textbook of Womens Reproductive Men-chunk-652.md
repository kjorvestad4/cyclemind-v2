Maternal and Infant Risks Associated With 
Eating Disorders in Pregnancy 
Adequate maternal nutrition and weight gain are crucial for a healthy pregnancy and 
fetal development, so an active ED puts both the mother and the fetus at high risk (Table 
22–4). Suboptimal nutrition in pregnancy may contribute to the risk of nutritional dis-
orders, such as anemia, Korsakoff psychosis, Wernicke’s syndrome, and scurvy (Card-
well 2013). Severe calorie restriction increases the likelihood of intrauterine growth 
restriction, low birth weight, small head circumference, microcephaly , and small for 
gestational age infants (Koubaa et al. 2005) . Additionally, these infants do not catch 
up with peers, and developmental delays persist throughout life (Park et al. 2003). Per 
several studies, gestational age was lowest among children of women with AN and 
highest among children of women with BED. Children of women with AN are at in -
creased risk of very premature birth and have a fourfold increased risk of perinatal 
death (Linna et al. 2014). An especially challenging pregnancy is that of women with 
BN and gestational diabetes, in which the patient may use insulin injection as a purg-
ing method (Cardwell 2013).
