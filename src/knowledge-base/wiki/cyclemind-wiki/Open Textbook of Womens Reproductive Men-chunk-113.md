Treatment Setting 
Given the severity and high-risk nature of PPP , inpatient admission is warranted for 
rapid implementation of a safety plan, psychiatric stabilization, and treatment (Fried-
man et al. 2019). Ideally, hospitalization would occur in a dedicated mother–baby psy-
chiatric unit that provides support for bonding, breastfeeding, and mother–infant 
dyadic treatment. This type of facility is considered best practice in most countries 
worldwide, although such units are scarce in the United States (Friedman et al. 2019). 
Treatment 
Mood stabilizers and antipsychotics are the mainstay of the acute treatment of PPP . The 
choice of a medication regimen for a particular patient will depend on the clinical sit-
uation, symptom severity, previous response to medications, and adverse effects (Jones 
et al. 2014). Introduction of lithium early in the treatment course is recommended (Os-
borne 2018). One naturalistic study treate d women with PPP with sequential use of 
benzodiazepines, antipsychotics, and lithium, achieving a 99% remission rate and 80% 
sustained remission rate after 9 months (Bergink et al. 2015). Electroconvulsive therapy 
(ECT) is also an effective treatment for PPP and can be a first-line treatment for high-
risk patients in whom rapid improvement of symptoms is needed, including patients 
with catatonia, severe agitation, refusal to eat or drink with compromised nutritional 
status, severe suicidality, or treatment resistance (Focht and Kellner 2012). 
Acute Agitation 
Acute agitation is a perinatal psychiatric emergency that might compromise the safety 
of the patient, the pregnancy, the patient’s supports, and the treatment team. Agitation 
broadly refers to a behavior set that poses a risk to the safety of the patient or caregiv-
ers, impedes the implementation of care, or acutely impairs functioning (Yildiz et al. 
2003). Patient agitation is also a leading cause of workplace injury for hospital staff and 
members of the treatment team (Gates et al. 2006; Lam 2002). Agitation can encompass 
a wide range of behaviors and underlying etiologies, including both psychiatric and

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Relatively little comprehensive information has been written about the epidemiology 
of agitation, particularly in pregnancy. A systematic review estimated that in the 
United States, up to 1.7 million patient visits to emergency departments per year may 
involve episodes of agitation (Zeller and Rhoades 2010). A structured, retrospective 
multicenter chart review of patients pres enting to an emergency department with 
psychiatric complaints revealed that 52% the patients had agitation as part of the clin-
ical picture (Boudreaux et al. 2009). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors that predict agitation in the general adult population, the adult psychiat-
ric population, or the perinatal population are scarcely described in the literature. One 
European expert consensus protocol descri bed several categories of risk associated 
with agitation in the adult psychiatry population. These included demographic fac -
tors, such as male gender; psychosocial fact ors, such as a recent stressful life event; 
and clinical factors, such as active substance abuse (Vieta et al. 2017). 
Screening 
To our knowledge, no screening tools are commonly used to detect agitation either in 
the general population or specifically in pregnant patients. One multicenter research 
collaboration (Lukens et al. 2006) measured  agitation objectively using the Agitated 
Behavior Scale (Corrigan 1989), a well-vali dated, comprehensive scale to prospec -
tively assess and quantify agitation. 
Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
DSM-5 does not have a formal set of diagno stic criteria for agitation. Agitation has 
been described as the physical manifestation of internal distress due to an underlying 
medical condition, such as delirium, or to a psychiatric condition, such as acute sub-
stance intoxication or withdrawal, mania, psychosis, or a personality disorder (Wil -
son et al. 2015). 
Clinical Presentation 
Regarding the presentation of agitation, a clinician might say that “I know it when I 
see it”—an understandable response to be haviors that have broad presenting symp-
toms and a wide range of etiologies. Agitatio n typically refers to a state of increased 
arousal characterized by uncooperative or combative behavior, physical restlessness, 
increased verbal and motor activity, and ex treme irritability (Nordstrom et al. 2012; 
Rodriguez-Cabezas and Clar k 2018; Zeller and Rhoade s 2010). Agitation symptoms

how she had been invaded by aliens that resulted in her pregnancy, attracting concerned 
looks from other patients in the emergency department. She started to pace around the 
room, waving her arms around wildly and intermittently kicking the legs of a chair in the 
corner of the room with her bare foot. She refused any attempts by the nursing staff to 
evaluate her, including the taking of vital signs. When the attending physician entered the 
room, the patient became highly agitated and started waving her hands near the physi-
cian’s face, shouting that angels were telling her that the emergency department doctors 
were there to harm her and her baby. A quick-thinking medical student retrieved a blan-
ket, a bread roll, and some orange juice from outside the evaluation room. He offered it 
to the patient at arm’s length, saying quietly, “Are you hungry or cold? I’m happy to get 
you more food if you want it.” The patient noticeably calmed. A few minutes later, she 
was sitting in the chair, wrapped in the blanket and eating the roll. She remained highly 
suspicious but eventually accepted 0.5 mg of lorazepam and 2.5 mg of haloperidol orally. 
An hour later, she allowed the medical staff to take her vital signs and draw her blood. 
Course and Prognosis 
Because the symptoms of agitation can rang e from mild to severe, fluctuate signifi -
cantly, and arise from a number of different etiologies, the course and prognosis can be 
unpredictable. Clearly, however, uncontrolled agitation compromises the safety of the 
pregnant patient and the fetus and has been found to be associated with obstetrical 
complications such as preterm delivery, sp ontaneous abortion, postnatal death, and 
placental dysfunction (Aftab and Shah 2017). 
Pathophysiology 
Although agitation is a common feature of many psychiatric conditions, such as schizo-
phrenia, substance use disorders, bipolar disorder, and dementia, little is known about 
agitation itself as a unique entity (Sachs 2006). A unifying hypothesis for the etiology 
of agitation has not yet been described, but discrete factors have been linked to specific 
psychiatric disorders. For example, frontal lobe dysfunction, alterations in the catechol-
O-methyltransferase gene involved in dopamine metabolism, and catecholamine inac-
tivation have been implicated in the genesis of agitation in schizophrenia and bipolar 
disorder (Sachs 2006). A review of the pathophysiology of agitation suggests that var-
ious underlying etiologies are mediated by dysregulation of neurotransmitter systems 
to produce agitation symptoms, including the serotonergic, dopaminergic, noradren-
ergic, and GABAergic systems (Lindenmayer 2000).

Assessment

ment of the airway, breathing, and circulation; and measurement of oxygenation and
