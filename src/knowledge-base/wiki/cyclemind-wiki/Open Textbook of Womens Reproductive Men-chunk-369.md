349 Depressive Disorders 
Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria and Evaluation 
Ms. T is a 28-year-old first-time mother with no prior psychiatric history and a desired 
pregnancy. She is no longer involved with the baby’s father, but she lives with her sister, 
who is strongly supportive, and has other family members close by. She works as a 
teacher’s aide and is highly conscientious, with very firm ideas prior to birth of what 
an “ideal birth experience” will be. At 37 weeks, she presents for a routine check and is 
found to have elevated blood pressure and protein in her urine. She is hospitalized for 
induction, and eventually has a cesarean section delivery after failing to progress. Her 
baby is small for gestational age and has to spend 24 hours in intensive care for low 
blood sugar. 
In the initial days after the birth, Ms. T feels somewhat “giddy,” both laughing and 
crying easily. She experiences some difficulty with latching and poor milk supply, and 
within 3 weeks she is frequently tearful and confides to her sister that she is “a terrible 
mom.” She constantly reviews the events of the birth, convinced that she should and 
could have done something to prevent the cesarean section: “I should have known I had 
high blood pressure”; “I should have tried harder in labor.” She ruminates about these 
events and about her breastfeeding difficulti es and begins to have thoughts that her 
child might be better off without her. 
The diagnosis of perinatal depression is at once simple and complex: simple be -
cause the core diagnostic features of MDD can also be used in the perinatal period, 
complex because unique clusters and emphases of symptoms occur in this period and 
because not all clinicians and governing bo dies agree on the definition. The current 
definition, according to DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association 2013), is the onset 
of a major depressive episode during pregnancy or within 4 weeks of childbirth (see 
Box 13–1). This definition conflates AD and PPD, which may be two biologically dis-
tinct entities, and although onset within 4 weeks postpartum likely captures those 
women for whom parturition is a biological trigger for symptoms, it fails to capture 
the significant population who may have onset later as a result of psychosocial trig -
gers or hormonal disruption due to weaning (Burke et al. 2019). 
Box 13–1. DSM-5 Criteria for Major Depressive Disorder 
A. Five (or more) of the following symptoms have been present during the same 2-week 
period and represent a change from previous functioning; at least one of the symptoms 
is either (1) depressed mood or (2) loss of interest or pleasure. 
Note: Do  not include symptoms that are clearly attributable to another medical condition. 
1. Depressed mood most of the day, nearly every day, as indicated by either subjec-
tive report (e.g., feels sad, empty, hopeless) or observation made by others (e.g., 
appears tearful). (Note: In children and adolescents, can be irritable mood.) 
2. Markedly diminished interest or pleasure in al l, or almost all, activities most of the 
day, nearly every day (as indicated by either subjective account or observation). 
3. Significant weight loss when not dieting or weight gain (e.g., a change of more than 
5% of body weight in a month), or dec rease or increase in appetite nearly every 
day. (Note: In children, consider failure to make expected weight gain.) 
4. Insomnia or hypersomnia nearly every day.
