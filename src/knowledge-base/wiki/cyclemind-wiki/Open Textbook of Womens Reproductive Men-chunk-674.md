Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
The classic clinical presentation of ADHD includes two major symptom domains: in-
attention and hyperactivity/impulsivity. Examples of inattention symptoms include 
failing to pay close attention to details, making careless mistakes in tasks, appearing 
not to listen or hold attention, failing to finish tasks, not following through, and hav-
ing difficulty with organization. The doma in of hyperactivity/impulsivity includes 
symptoms such as fidgeting, squirming in seat, being “on the go,” talking excessively, 
blurting out an answer, having trouble waiting their turn, and interrupting others 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
Despite having the same DSM diagnostic criteria, adult ADHD symptoms can 
present differently than in childhood. The more overtly impairing symptoms (hyper-
activity and impulsivity) that may be particularly prominent in childhood seem to be-
come less obvious over time (Kooij et al . 2010). More subtle—yet still impairing— 
symptoms such as inattention, disorganizat ion, and inner restlessness tend to pre -
dominate in adulthood, as well as significant problems with executive functioning 
(Adler et al. 2017; Biederman et al. 2000; Fi scher et al. 2005; Hart et al. 1995; Kooij et 
al. 2001; Wender et al. 2001). This cluster of symptoms leaves a footprint in the prag-
matic aspects of adult life; adult patients often struggle with time management and 
procrastination, a feeling of internal restlessness, feeling overwhelmed or distracted, 
and starting but not finishing tasks (Fredriksen et al. 2014; Halmøy et al. 2009; Minde 
et al. 2003; Young et al. 2020). Anxiety, insomnia, and demoralization follow (Agarwal 
et al. 2012; Piñeiro-Dieguez et al. 2016; Seo et al. 2014), and the impulsivity inherent 
to ADHD often leads to comorbid substance misuse (Capusan et al. 2016; Piñeiro-
Dieguez et al. 2016) or binge eating (Brunault et al. 2019). 
Other domains also appear to play an important role in adult ADHD, including dif-
ficulties with cognitive impairment and emotional regulation. Most adults with ADHD 
have measurable cognitive dysfunction (Holst and Thorell 2017), although the degree 
of impairment can be highly variable; patients with significant executive dysfunction, 
working memory difficulties, or impulsivity tend to have the highest degree of func-
tional impairment in their daily lives (Mostert et al. 2015; Skodzik et al. 2017; Theiling 
and Petermann 2016). Cognitive impairment in ADHD is underappreciated yet im -
