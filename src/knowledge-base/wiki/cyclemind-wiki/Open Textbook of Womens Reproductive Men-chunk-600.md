Given the limited data in perinatal populations, it is recommended to follow CBT 
treatment guidelines for nonperinatal OCD: 13–20 weekly sessions for most patients, 
with “booster” sessions for 3–6 months afterward for more severely ill patients, those 
with a history of relapse, or those showin g signs of early relapse (Koran et al. 2007; 
Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). Session length can vary from 45 minutes to 2 hours, 
and treatment can be delivered in individual, group, or family therapy contexts. In the 
absence of available CBT, providers can rely on self-help treatment guides and are also 
recommended to direct patients to OCD support groups. With perinatal patients, whose 
schedules are often limited due to childcare responsibilities, work responsibilities, or 
the physical burdens of pregnancy, it is important to have a realistic discussion about 
what frequency of therapy sessions is reasonable given each woman’s circumstances. 
Treatment Challenges 
Although treatment strategies in perinatal OCD are largely informed by guidelines de-
veloped for nonperinatal popu lations, there are important modifications to note. In 
general populations, the selection of CBT, an SSRI, or combined treatment depends on 
the nature and severity of the patient’s symptoms, nature of co-occurring psychiatric 
and medical conditions, the availability of CBT, the patient’s past treatment history, cur-
rent medications that the patient is taking, and the patient’s preferences (Koran et al. 
2007). Indeed, these all come into play in perinatal cases; however, the risk-risk analy-
sis, as described in detail in Chapter 8, is expanded to include potential risks to a fetus— 
both as a result of the treatments themselves and from lack of (or under-) treatment. 
Once the risks of treatment and the risks of no treatment/undertreatment are clarified 
(Spinelli 2012), initial steps in decision-m aking can be more easily taken. Cases in 
which medication (an SSRI) would be heav ily considered include high severity of 
symptoms (especially leading to safety concerns or mark ed impairment of function-
ing), a history of prior good response to an SSRI, and a history of prior ineffective trial 
of CBT alone. Because many cases do not necessarily include these elements, and often 
fall in a “gray zone” of severity, other factors to consider include the patient’s prefer-
ence, willingness and ability to engage in close follow-up, presence or absence of family 
and social support, and time/financial resources (with regard to pursuing psychother-
apy). Given the potential for CBT to decrease relapse rates when medication is discon-
tinued (which is always a possibility in perinatal cases), it is generally recommended 
that patients who are proceeding with SSRI treatment also pursue CBT if possible.
