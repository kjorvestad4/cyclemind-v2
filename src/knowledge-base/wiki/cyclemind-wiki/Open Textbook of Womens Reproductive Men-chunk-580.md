OCD tends to be highly comorbid with other psychiatric disorders, including mood 
disorders and other anxiety disorders. Some studies cite the comorbidity with depres-
sion at 70.6% and with anxiety disorders at 27.5% (Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). 
There are also some reports of comorbidity with bipolar disorder, but this is less well 
studied in the literature. Obsessive-compulsive symptoms are also more common in 
women with perinatal mood disorders and have been identified in women with these 
disorders even in the absence of full criteria for OCD (Abramowitz et al. 2010b). 
Risk Factors 
Female sex and times of major reproductive transition are two major risk factors for 
the development of OCD. Other risk factors have been identified for OCD in general 
and for perinatal OCD specifically. For general OCD, family and twin studies show 
increased risk of OCD in families, and thus a genetic component to the illness is sug-
gested (Vasconcelos et al. 2007). Environmen tal factors also appear  to play a role in 
the development of OCD and are discussed la ter in this chapter. Gestational factors 
in pregnancy and parturition can influence the offspring’s risk of developing OCD 
later in life. Obstetrical complications, in particular prolonged labor and edema 
during pregnancy, can be a risk factor for the development of OCD in the offspring 
(Vasconcelos et al. 2007). Delivery-related complications, such as the use of forceps, 
neonatal complications, preterm birth, breech presentation, and delivery by cesarean 
section, are also associated with an increased risk of OCD in offspring (Brander et al. 
2016; Vasconcelos et al. 2007). 
In addition, certain risk factors specific  to pregnancy may exacerbate the risk of 
perinatal OCD. Obstetrical complications have been cited as a risk factor, but results 
vary among studies. Some studies have shown that primiparity, obstetrical complica-
tions, and mode of delivery are risk factors for development of postpartum OCD, but 
others have not shown these findings (Lab ad et al. 2005; Maina et al. 1999; McGuin -
ness et al. 2011). Having family member s with mood and substance use disorders 
(Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015) is also a risk  factor for the development of postpar -
tum OCD. First-time motherhood is a high -risk time for the development of obses -
sional thinking due to the new responsibili ties associated with caring for an infant 
(Abramowitz et al. 2003; Fairbrother and Abramowitz 2007).
