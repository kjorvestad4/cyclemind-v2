Key Points 
• Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a treatable psychiatric emergency that requires 
immediate evaluation. 
• Symptoms include mood fluctuation, alterations of consciousness, and abnor-
malities of thought and perception arising rapidly within the first days to weeks 
after childbirth. 
• Risk evaluation is critical given high rates of suicide and infanticide. 
• A personal history of bipolar disorder is the biggest risk factor for PPP , and 
many women may eventually be diagnosed with bipolar disorder. 
• Lithium, antipsychotics, benzodiazepines and ECT have evidence for treat -
ment of acute PPP . 
• Lithium is the recommended medication for prophylaxis. 
• Psychosocial support includes psychoeducation for family, attention to sleep, 
and mother–infant bonding.

Schizophrenia is a psychiatric illness that requires ongoing treatment through-
out the lifespan. It is characterized by abnormalities of perception, thought, behavior, 
affect, and cognition (American Psychiatric Association 2013). Although the onset for 
both men and women is typically in early adulthood, women are more likely than men 
to experience onset in middle age and to have a better overall disease prognosis (Abel 
et al. 2010). An understanding of the role of estrogen in the disease course of schizo -
phrenia is growing, and emerging data sugge st that estrogen plays a protective role 
against psychosis and that periods of estrogen decline are associated with new onset 
or exacerbation of psychotic symptoms (Riecher-Rössler et al. 2018). 
Historically, women with schizophrenia have had reduced fertility relative to the 
general population. Deinstitutionalization of persons living with mental illness and 
advances in psychopharmacology over several decades, however, have contributed 
to an increase in fertility rates (Vigod et al. 2012). At present, most women with schizo-
phrenia will have at least one pregnancy (Howard et al. 2001). Thus, proficiency in the 
psychiatric care of women with schizophrenia during the perinatal period is an essen-
tial skill for a psychiatric clinician.

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Schizophrenia is a relatively uncommon disorder with a lifetime prevalence of 7.2 per 
1,000 persons (Jablensky 2017). It is more common in men than women, with a male-
to-female incidence ratio of 1.4:1 (Aleman et al. 2003; McGrath et al. 2008). Although 
some have argued that the reported increased risk for men may be due to methodolog-
ical limitations of existing research (i.e., studies focused on inpatient settings show a 
sample bias toward men, who on average have a more severe disease course), the sex 
ratio appears to persist even when contro lling for pertinent factors (Aleman et al. 
2003). Because women, on average, show a less severe disease course and a later onset 
of illness course than men, it remains poss ible that detection bias is a contributor to 
the sex ratio. Interestingly, although the peak age of onset for men is in the early 20s, 
women show a wider distribution in age of onset, with a peak in the early 20s fol -
lowed by a secondary peak in middle age. Thus, there is a switch from male to female 
predominance at older ages (Abel et al. 2010).
