Reproductive Loss 
The course of trauma-related disorders rela ted to reproductive loss can be compli -
cated by a history of previous losses, the circumstances of the pregnancy, and the de-
gree of support from the partner/father of the baby. Evidence indicates that the length 
of gestation does not determine the course  or intensity of trauma symptoms, and an 
early gestational loss can be just as dist ressing as a later one (Leis-Newman 2012). 
Similarly, losing an unwanted pregnancy can be as distressing as losing a wanted one 
because the degree of attachment to a pregnancy can change over time, according to 
circumstances, and across developmental ph ases within the reproductive lifespan 
(Leis-Newman 2012). 
Traumatic Delivery 
Although longitudinal trajectories for PTSD in the population at large have been well es-
tablished, data on the clinical course of childbirth-related trauma are limited due to high 
attrition rates and multiple confounders. Aprospective study in the Netherlands enrolled 
348 participants, of whom 21% reported delivery as traumatic; however, at 10 months, 
only 0.35% of the entire sample met criteria for PTSD. Notable limitations include attrition 
of one-third of the sample and a lack of control for prior trauma and PTSD (Haagen et al. 
2015). The largest study to date examined 950 women from a community sample over 
a 6-month period, 226 of whom identified delivery as traumatic. Four trajectories 
were identified: resilient (61.9%), recovere d (18.5%), chronic PTSD (13.7%), and de -
layed PTSD (5.8%) (Dikmen-Yildiz et al. 2018). The high rates of recovery implore ex-
amination of resilience in addition to risk factors to develop more targeted treatments.
