ADHD is highly comorbid with other mental health conditions. ADHD alone is un-
common in the adult population, and it may co-occur with other disorders as much as 
75% of the time (Faraone and Biederman 1998; Rösler et al. 2006). Common comorbid-
ities with adult ADHD include autism spectrum disorder, depressive disorders, bipo-
lar disorder, anxiety disorders, OCD, eating disorders (i.e., anorexia nervosa, bulimia 
nervosa, binge eating disorder), PTSD, learning disorders, personality disorders, and 
impulse-control disorders (Capusan et al. 2016; Huntley and Young 2014; Kessler et 
al. 2006; Nazar et al. 2016; Pliszka 1998; Sobanski 2006). 
Of particular importance is the relationship between ADHD and substance use dis-
orders. In adulthood, patients with ADHD are more likely to use tobacco (Brassett-
Grundy and Butler 2004), to engage in hi gher rates of substance use (Huntley and 
Young 2014), and to develop alcohol and substance use disorders (Charach et al. 2011). 
Notably, evidence has shown at least equal—if not higher—risk of alcohol and canna -
bis use disorders in women with ADHD compared with men (Ottosen et al. 2016). In 
addition, the presence of comorbid ADHD may portend a less favorable prognosis; for 
example, comorbid ADHD and bipolar disord er is associated with earlier onset of 
symptoms and a more chronic and disabling course than bipolar disorder alone (Brus 
et al. 2014). A recent systematic review also showed an association between ADHD 
and suicidality in all age groups and in both sexes that was mediated by the presence 
of psychiatric comorbidities (Furczyk and Thome 2014). 
There appear to be significant sex differences in the course and prognosis of ADHD 
throughout the lifespan (Young et al. 2020). Internalizing disorders, such as anxiety and 
depression, are more often reported in women than in men with ADHD (Cortese et al. 
2016; Edvinsson et al. 2013; Gershon 2002), as are borderline personality disorder traits 
(Stepp et al. 2012) and self-harming behaviors (Hollingdale et al. 2014), likely prompted 
by impulsivity. The impulsivity inherent in ADHD can lead to an increase in risk-taking 
behavior and undesired consequences in women (Quinn and Madhoo 2014). For exam-
ple, adolescent girls with undiagnosed ADHD may engage in at-risk behaviors such as 
early sexual activity, have an increased number of sexual partners, and experience a 
greater frequency of sexually transmitted infections (Barkley et al. 2006; Quinn 2005). 
Women with ADHD also have a higher risk of teenage, early, or unplanned pregnancies 
(Skoglund et al. 2019). They may be vulnerable to sexual harassment, exploitation, and 
abusive relationships; the Adult Psychiatric Morbidity survey in the United Kingdom 
found that 27% of women who experienced extensive physical or sexual abuse had 
ADHD traits (McManus et al. 2009). Along with the other mental health comorbidities, 
such as depression and PTSD, alcohol and other substance use disorders are highly co-
morbid with ADHD in women, often beginning at an early age (Wilens et al. 1995). 
No systematic studies have yet been published on the course of ADHD through the 
perinatal period (Baker and Freeman 2018). Women with ADHD who are pregnant 
have a higher risk of continued tobacco use and of being obese or underweight (Sko-
glund et al. 2019). Women with undiagnosed ADHD are hypothesized to struggle 
more with parenting, balancing work with household management, and maintaining 
partnerships and interpersonal relationsh ips (Nadeau and Quinn 2002; Young et al. 
2020), although empirical data on this topic are scarce. It has also been hypothesized 
that, in the perinatal period, women may experience significant psychosocial stress 
related to the large number of tasks associ ated with the transition to motherhood; 
however, this has not yet been examined rigorously (Freeman 2014; Young et al. 2020).
