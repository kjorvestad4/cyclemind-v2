575 Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder 
No randomized controlled trials of trea tments for perinatal OCD have been pub -
lished. As such, clinical guidelines for th e treatment of OCD in general populations 
largely inform treatment options and decisions, with important adaptations relevant 
to the perinatal period, such as the risk profile of medications in pregnancy and risks 
to the pregnancy of untreated illness. In general populations, CBT and SRIs (SSRIs and 
clomipramine) are recommended on the basis of clinical trial results as safe and effec-
tive first-line treatments for OCD (Bandelo w et al. 2008). Each of these treatments, 
along with others, is discussed in the following sections. 
Psychopharmacology 
Both treatment and psychiatric illness in the perinatal period carry risks. Because the 
medications used for OCD are by and large the same used for other disorders (partic-
ularly depression), we do not repeat the discussion of risks here. Please see Chapter 8 
and Chapter 9, “A Clinical  Approach to Psychiatric Diagnosis and Treatment in the 
Postpartum Period,” for an overview of the perinatal treatment approach and Chapter 
13 for specific risk information about antidepressants. Fluoxetine, fluvoxamine, parox-
etine, and sertraline are approved by th e FDA for treatment of OCD and are recom -
mended pharmacological agents (American Psychiatric Association 2013; Bergeron et 
al. 2002; Bisserbe et al. 1997; Denys et al. 2003, 2004; Goodman et al. 1989a, 1996; Greist 
1995; Hoehn-Saric et al. 2000; Hollander et al. 2003; Jenike et al. 1997; Joffe and Swin-
son 1987; Koran et al. 2007; Smeraldi et al. 1992; Tollefson et al. 1994). Escitalopram 
and citalopram, although they lack FDA approval for this indication, are also recom-
mended agents on the basis of recent evidence (Dougherty et al. 2009; Montgomery et 
al. 2001; Pampaloni et al. 2010; Stein et al. 2007). Clomipramine, a mixed serotonin -
norepinephrine reuptake inhibitor, as we ll as a cholinergic and histaminic blocking 
agent, also has FDA approval for treatmen t of OCD. Meta-analyses of placebo-con -
trolled trials suggest greater efficacy for clomipramine, but the results of head-to-head 
trials comparing clomipramine with SSRIs do not support this. As such, all of the 
aforementioned agents are considered equally effective in treatment of OCD (Vulink 
et al. 2005), although an in dividual patient may respond better to one over another. 
Regarding tolerability, SSRIs are routinely better tolerated given their more benign 
side effect profile (Mundo et al. 2000) an d, as such, are reco mmended as first-line 
agents. In general populations, an SSRI alone is recommended for a patient who is not 
able to cooperate with CBT, has previously responded well to a given drug, or prefers 
treatment with an SSRI alone. SSRI monoth erapy can also be considered in cases of 
severe OCD because the medication may di minish symptom severity sufficiently to 
allow the patient to engage in CBT (Koran et al. 2007). 
Second-line approaches include the use of  antipsychotics, which are used as aug -
mentation for SSRIs; modest evidence suppor ts the use of haloperidol, risperidone, 
quetiapine, olanzapine, and aripiprazole (Bystritsky et al. 2004; Denys et al. 2004; Er-
zegovesi et al. 2005; Hollander et al. 2003; McDougle et al. 1994, 2000; Selvi et al. 2011).
