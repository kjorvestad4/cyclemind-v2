Early pregnancy. Overall, more data support the reproductive safety of bupropion 
in pregnancy than support stimulants, although there is less information as compared 
with, for example, selective serotonin reup take inhibitors. There has been concern 
about an association with bupropion exposure and cardiovascular malformations; for 
example, one retrospective case control study using data from a birth defect registry 
noted a small increase in the relative risk of cardiovascular left ventricular outflow de-
fects (with an absolute risk calculated to be  2 out of 1,000 pregnancies) (Alwan et al. 
2010). However, overall studies have been reassuring. For example, prospective reg-
istry data found that the birth defect rate with bupropion exposure in the first trimes-
ter was 3.6%, similar to that found in the unexposed population, and there was no 
clear pattern of birth defects (Bupropion Pregnancy Registry 2008). 
Another prospective study found no eviden ce of a higher rate of malformations 
with bupropion exposure as compared with groups with other antidepressant expo -
sure or no exposure (Chun-Fai-Chan et al. 2005). Furthermore, a large epidemiological 
study of more than 150,000 women compared women with bupropion exposure in the 
first trimester with bupropion exposure in the second trimester and exposure to an -
other antidepressant in the first trimester; no increase in birth defects with first trimes-
ter bupropion exposure was noted (Cole et al. 2007). A recent systematic review aimed 
to consolidate these findings; this review supported the finding of a small increase in 
relative risk of cardiac malformations, al though the absolute risk was low, and con -
founders such as the indication for smo king cessation may have  contributed (Hen -
drick et al. 2017). One small study noted an elevated risk of miscarriage with 
bupropion exposure as compared with a control group; however, the rate remained 
within that of the general population (Chun-Fai-Chan et al. 2005; Hendrick et al. 2017). 
Obstetrical outcomes. Data about other pregnancy or delivery outcomes with bu-
propion exposure are scarce. One small study of 30 pregnant women taking bupropion 
for smoking cessation showed no statistical differences in birth weight, Apgar score, 
or length of gestation in exposed infants (Nanovskaya et al. 2017).

Other Medications 
Scant reproductive safety information is available about other medications sometimes 
used for the treatment of ADHD, including atomoxetine and guanfacine. To our 
knowledge, no systematic studies in human pregnancy are available that have exam-
ined the safety of these medications (Freeman 2014). A few case reports and a registry 
analysis show no specific malformations or adverse pregnancy outcomes for either 
agent thus far (Alessi and Spalding 2003; Källén et al. 2013; Philipp 1980). 
Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
No known perinatal-specific psychotherap ies have been developed for the manage -
ment of ADHD. However, data are accumulating regarding nonpharmacological ap-
proaches in the general adult population, specifically cognitive-behavioral therapy 
(CBT) (Mongia and Hechtman 2012). CBT tailored for adult ADHD typically focuses 
on a combination of building organizational skills, such as prioritization and time man-
agement, and challenging self-defeating cognitions (Jensen et al. 2016; Morgensterns 
et al. 2016; Solanto et al. 2010; Vidal-Estrada et al. 2012). A recent meta-analysis exam-
ining CBT for ADHD in adults demonstrated  e f f e c t  s i z e s  c o m p a rable with those of 
well-established behavioral therapies for children with ADHD (Knouse et al. 2017). 
Further work remains, however, for identifying the core principles of treatment-related 
change in adults so that more tailored treatments can be developed and applied to both 
the adult and the perinatal po pulation (Knouse et al. 2017). Last, executive function 
coaching (Prevatt and Yelland 2015) and exercise (Den Heijer et al. 2017; Vysniauske 
et al. 2020) also have some evidence of benefit in adult ADHD. 
Specific attention may be required to address the complex situations faced by 
women with ADHD in the perinatal period and early parenting, as they aim to bal -
ance multiple occupational demands, ho me management, and family/parenting re -
sponsibilities (Young et al. 2020), especially because household and parenting duties 
tend to be disproportionately borne by women (Treas and Tai 2016). Solutions have 
been cited in the literature, such as reducing the workload if possible and taking pub-
lic transportation instead of driving (Baker and Freeman 2018). Comprehensive pro-
grams of support have yet to be developed, but suggested interventions cited include 
the development of targeted support to manage feelings of stress and distress, manage 
and regulate emotions, support sexual health and safety, cope with rejection and isola-
tion, manage interpersonal conflict, learn assertiveness skills, and develop compromise 
and negotiation strategies in order to improve occupational and social outcomes and 
women’s ability to cope with everyday stressors (Young et al. 2020).

Conclusion 
ADHD is a common, lifelong neurodevelopmental disorder that has a significant im-
pact on functioning in daily life in school, work, and social settings and on physical 
and mental health throughout the lifespan (Agnew-Blais et al. 2016; Barbaresi et al. 
2013; Faraone et al. 2015). Contrary to a prior understanding of ADHD solely as a 
childhood disorder, it is now clear that ADHD persists through the lifespan (Asherson 
2010) and that there may be a distinct adult-onset version. However, a comprehensive 
understanding of ADHD in adulthood, in women specifically, and in the perinatal pe-
riod is just beginning to be elucidated (Freeman 2014; Kooij et al. 2010). 
Because of its high prevalence and significant impact, ADHD is an important con-
dition to address in the setting of reproductive mental health. Women of reproductive 
age are increasingly diagnosed with ADHD (Leong et al. 2017; Louik et al. 2015), and 
they are at substantially increased risk of comorbidities, including depression, anxiety, 
and substance use disorders (Owens et al. 2017) in addition to significant difficulties 
with work, school, interpersonal relationships, and everyday functioning (Corbisiero 
et al. 2017; Eddy et al. 2019). When wo men with ADHD enter pregnancy and new 
motherhood, they may be under increased psychosocial stress, although data regard-
ing this time period are very limited (Freeman 2014). The use of stimulant medications 
by women of reproductive age has increased significantly, and in turn these medica-
tions are among the most commonly prescrib ed in pregnancy (Anderson et al. 2018; 
Haervig et al. 2014; Louik et al. 2015; Renoux et al. 2016). Yet our understanding of the 
reproductive safety profile of prescribed medications is limited and is complicated by 
significant confounding variables (Bro et al. 2015; Diav-Citrin et al. 2016; Haervig et 
al. 2014; Källén et al. 2013; Newport et al . 2016; Wright et al. 2015). Because prenatal 
factors are thought to be environmental risk factors for ADHD in the offspring, a ro-
bust understanding of ADHD in women in the perinatal period would serve to ad -
dress the mental health needs of their children in an intergenerational manner. 
A great deal remains to be discovered regarding the epidemiology, diagnosis, clini-
cal presentation, diagnostic criteria, screening and assessment, and treatment of adults 
with ADHD generally, and women with ADHD specifically—both within and outside 
of the perinatal period. Future directions for an understanding of ADHD and repro-
ductive mental health remain a wide-open field, and a greater understanding of this 
highly prevalent and impairing disorder could improve the mental and physical health 
of women and their offspring for multiple generations.
