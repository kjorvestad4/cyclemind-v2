393 Integrative Approaches to Perinatal Depression 
Some intervention studies have demonstrated efficacy in improving perinatal sleep. 
A yoga program that included poses, breath ing practices, and meditation led to less 
sleep disturbance and decreased anxiety, depression, stress, and low back pain (Babbar 
and Shyken 2016). In addition, regular phys ical activity, massage, and acupuncture 
have been associated with improved sleep quality and reduced insomnia during preg-
nancy (Hollenbach et al. 2013). Cognitive-behavioral therapy for insomnia (CBT-i) is a 
nonpharmacological insomnia-focused psychotherapy that  i s  r e c o m m e n d e d  a s  t h e 
first-line treatment for insomnia by American and European guidelines (Riemann et al. 
2017; Schutte-Rodin et al. 2008). CBT-i has also been identified by pregnant women as 
their treatment of choice, compared with pharmacotherapy and acupuncture (Sedov et 
al. 2017). 
Another promising area of research is “critically timed wake therapy” to improve both 
antenatal and postpartum depressive symptoms. For prenatal depression, “early wake 
therapy,” in which women are kept awake late into the night and then permitted to sleep 
between 3 A.M. and 7 A.M. (for 1 night only) led to delayed melatonin onset and advanced 
sleep onset the following night. For postpartum women, “late wake therapy,” in which 
sleep was permitted between 9 P.M. and 1 A.M. and then the patient was awakened (for 
1 night only), led to increased total sleep time the following night. These improvements, 
when they were further augmented by brig ht light therapy, expedited symptom im-
provement such that 1 week of combined critically timed wake therapy plus bright light 
therapy was equivalent to 6 weeks of bright light therapy alone (Parry et al. 2011, 2019). 
General sleep hygiene recommendations that can safely be recommended to preg-
nant women include keeping the sleeping ar ea dark and cool (60°–68°F), avoiding 
blue-wavelength light from screens for 1–3 hours before bedtime, being exposed to 
natural sunlight before noon daily, avoiding caffeine and nicotine in the afternoon or 
evening, getting regular physical activity (after reviewing with their obstetrician), and 
limiting daytime naps to 20–30 minutes (B urkhart and Phelps 2009; Harding et al. 
2019; Walker 2017). 
Special Considerations 
Nausea and vomiting are commonly experienced in early pregnancy (Einarson et al. 
2013). For mild cases, ginger, pyridoxine (vitamin B6, 25 mg every 8 hours), antihista-
mines, and metoclopramide have all been associated with greater benefit than placebo 
(Firouzbakht et al. 2014). For moderate cases, pyridoxine-doxylamine, promethazine, 
and metoclopramide have been associated with greater benefit than placebo (McParlin 
et al. 2016). Ginger, chamomile, pyridoxine, and acupuncture are all recommended for 
the relief of nausea in early pregnancy, based on a woman’s preferences and available 
options, per the World Heal th Organization (2016) firs t-line recommendations for 
treating nausea and vomiting during pregnancy. Taking a multivitamin early in preg-
nancy, and even prior to conception, has been associated with decreased nausea and 
vomiting (Emelianova et al. 1999). Hypnosis can be helpful for nausea and vomiting 
generally as well, although it has not been  studied specifically during the perinatal 
period. Many studies on self-hypnosis have been related to mitigating chemotherapy-
induced nausea and vomiting. A meta-analysis showed conflicting data on the use of 
self-hypnosis and its use for hyperemesis gravidarum (McCormack 2010).
