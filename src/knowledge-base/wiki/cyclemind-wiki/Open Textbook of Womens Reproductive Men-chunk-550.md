Intrusive thoughts, particularly of harm coming to the baby, are common in the 
postpartum period and, although potentially highly distressing, may not be patholog-
ical. Thoughts of harm to baby that are ego-dystonic, fleeting, and do not interfere with 
daily functioning may exist in the context of the normal worrying of new parenthood 
and must be distinguished from more severe symptomatology that increases the risk 
of harm (discussed in Chapter 10, “A Treatment Approach to Psychiatric Emergencies 
in the Perinatal Period,” and Chapter 20). It can be difficult to differentiate normal con-
cerns from pathological worry, espe cially during the first trimester in primiparous 
women. Clinicians should be alert for the presence of an anxiety disorder if the worries 
have been present for an extended time, result in functional impairment, consume a 
lot of time, or have an irrational or intrusive quality (Misri et al. 2015). 
Perinatal anxiety, specifically perinatal GAD, can be differentiated from OCD pri-
marily by the nature of the anxious thoughts. OCD is characterized by unwanted, in-
trusive, repetitive thoughts (obsessions) and ritualistic behaviors (compulsions) 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013). Wi th GAD, the anxious thoughts are wor -
ries about real-life routine matters, whic h are approached with apprehension and 
thought distortions (e.g., catastrophizing and generalizing); the content of these tends 
to shift over time. With OCD, thoughts ar e intrusive and unrelated to everyday con-
cerns, mostly focused on disastrous outcomes such as harm coming to the infant, and 
obsessions tend to have stable content over time (Abramowitz et al. 2003). Of note, 
postpartum psychosis may also contain highly distressing thought content, such as 
harm coming to the infant; however, the th oughts are ego-syntonic (i.e., delusional) 
rather than ego-dystonic (i.e., recognized as irrational).
