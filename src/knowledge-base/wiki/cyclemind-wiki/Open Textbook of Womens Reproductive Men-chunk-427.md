Preconception. The potential for pregnancy introduces an additional level of com-
plexity in the psychiatric management of women with BD. Approximately 50% of preg-
nancies in the United States are unplanned, and women with BD are even more likely 
to have unplanned pregnancy than women in the general population (Finer and Zolna 
2016; Marengo et al. 2015). Many commonly used mood stabilizers carry a risk of tera-
togenicity at the earliest stages of fetal development; thus, it is critical that discussions 
about family planning occur early in the treatment of any woman of reproductive age 
as part of routine care. Psychiatrists should inquire about all women’s obstetrical his-
tory, plans for pregnancy, and contraceptive practices and preferences. Women with BD 
should be counseled about the risk of hypersexuality or high-risk sexual behaviors as 
a component of mania, as well as about the availability of long-acting reversible con-
traceptive options and methods to protect against sexually transmitted infections. 
When prescribing medication treatment, potential teratogenic effects should be in-
cluded in any discussion with women of reproductive age. Patients should be encour-
aged to engage proactively in preconception planning in collaboration with the treating 
psychiatrist. Preconception planning is more fully discussed in Chapter 8, “A  Clinical 
Approach to Psychiatric Diagnosis and Treatment During Pregnancy,” but should in-
clude consideration of risks related to the underlying disease and the risks of clinically 
indicated pharmacological treatments. Comorbid substance use disorders or medical 
conditions should also be discussed. A desire for pregnancy may provide an important 
opportunity to encourage healthy behaviors and adherence to treatment. 
Pregnancy and Postpartum. The onset of mood episodes in women with BD has 
been strongly associated with reproductive events. In one study of 887 mothers with 
BD, 28% experienced their first lifetime episode of BD in relation to childbirth (Di Florio 
et al. 2018). Current evidence suggests that pregnancy is not protective against mood 
episodes for women with BD, and the postpartum period has been identified as partic-
ularly high risk. Women with BD are seven times as likely to experience a first-time psy-
chiatric admission and twice as likely to experience a psychiatric readmission in the 
first postpartum month as women in the general population (Terp and Mortensen 
1998). Viguera et al. (2000) found that, among women with BD who discontinued lith-
