Lamotrigine 
Breastfeeding with lamotrigine is associated with an estimated RID of 9.2%–18.3%, and 
lamotrigine is not a contraindication to breastfeeding in full-term healthy infants. Wide 
ranges of lamotrigine concentrations in infants are reported, likely due to interindivid-
ual variation in lamotrigine metabolism by mother and infant (Clark et al. 2013). Al -
though most infants have not exhibited adverse reactions, adverse reactions in infants 
exposed to lamotrigine via breastfeeding have occasionally been reported. When lam-
otrigine is prescribed to a breastfeeding woman, maternal serum lamotrigine concen-
tration should be monitored after delivery, and infants should be monitored for apnea, 
rash, drowsiness, or poor sucking. If clinically indicated, infant serum lamotrigine lev-
els, platelet count, and liver function may be monitored (Clark and Wisner 2018). If an 
infant exposed to lamotrigine via breastmilk develops a rash, the mother should cease 
breastfeeding until the infant has been clinically evaluated and the source has been de-
termined (Lactmed 2020). Limited data suggest no adverse effects on growth or devel-
opment due to lamotrigine exposure through breastmilk (Meador et al. 2013). 
Valproate 
Valproate has very low passage into breast milk and is considered compatible with 
breastfeeding. Although no definite adverse reactions have been reported in infants 
exposed to valproate via breastmilk, one qu estionable case of thrombocytopenia has 
been reported, and there is a theoretical risk of hepatotoxicity . When valproate is pre-
scribed to a breastfeeding woman, her infant should be monitored for jaundice, abnor-
mal bleeding, and abnormal bruising. Monitoring of infant valproate levels, platelets, 
and liver enzymes may be performed if clinically indicated (Lactmed 2020).
