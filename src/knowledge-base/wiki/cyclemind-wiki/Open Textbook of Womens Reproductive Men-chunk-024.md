59 Gynecological Conditions and Mental Health 
TABLE 2–7. Genito-pelvic pain/penetration disorder: important points 
Screening Provider-initiated full sexual history, including questions about libido 
and discomfort or pain during vaginal intercourse, pelvic 
examinations, or tampon insertion 
Inquire about avoidance behaviors 
Obstetrical history, including inquiries about birth trauma 
Risk factors Prior mood or anxiety disorders 
History of abuse, relationship factors, negative sexual attitudes, and 
lack of sexual education 
In postpartum women, perineal trauma and deliveries requiring 
instrumentation 
Clinical presentation Avoi dance of sexual activity 
Intentionally limited sexual relationships 
Avoidance or fear of vaginal penetration (may not volunteer this 
information without provider-initiated questions) 
Reduced sexual interest during pregnancy and postpartum 
Symptoms that overlap with those of postpartum depression 
Abstaining from or avoiding sexual intercourse out of fear of pain 
Differential diagnosis Genito-pelvic pain disorder (including vaginismus and dyspareunia) 
Female sexual interest and arousal disorder 
Female orgasmic disorder 
Vulvodynia/Vestibulodynia 
Persistent genital arousal disorder 
Infection or vulvar dermatological condition 
Clinical points Genito-pelvic pain cond itions common postpartum; women with 
perinatal trauma at higher risk 
Provoked vestibulodynia most common cause of dyspareunia; 
symptoms include burning or itching sensations and can be localized 
to vestibule region (in which case condition is vestibulodynia) 
Persistent genital arousal disorder is relatively newly described 
condition characterized by unwanted genital arousal in absence of 
subjective desire or interest; has been associated with selective 
serotonin reuptake inhibitor initiation and discontinuation 
syndrome 
Trying to conceive and the childbearing process may trigger a 
worsening of mood or anxiety symptoms for patients 
Patient often presents with more than one type of female sexual 
dysfunction and will require an interdisciplinary team, such as a 
gynecologist, sex therapist, and pelvic floor physical therapist, to 
achieve symptom remission
