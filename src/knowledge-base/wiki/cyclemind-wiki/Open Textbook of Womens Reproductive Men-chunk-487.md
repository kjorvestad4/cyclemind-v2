TABLE 17–6. Management of adverse effects 
Adverse effect Usual management Considerations in pregnancy/lactation 
Extrapyramidal symptoms (e.g., 
dystonia, parkinsonism, akathisia) 
Benzodiazepines Early studies suggested increased risk of cleft lip/palate; subsequent research is reassuring. 
Regular use late in pregnancy may cause neonatal withdrawal, respiratory depression, or 
floppy baby syndrome (Organization of Teratology Information Specialists 2021). May 
cause sedation in infant; shorter-acting agents (e.g., lorazepam) are preferred during 
lactation. 
Benztropine Limited evidence to guide use. 
Diphenhydramine No clear risk of fetal malformation. Rare reports of uterine hyperstimulation in the context 
of high levels in late pregnancy (Li et al. 2013; Organization of Teratology Information 
Specialists 2021). Limited evidence for use in lactation; may contribute to somnolence. 
Propranolol Limited information on first-trimester use.Some association with reduced fetal growth (may 
be confounded by hypertension). Late in pregnancy, may cause neonatal bradycardia and 
hypoglycemia (Organization of Teratology Information Specialists 2021). Probably 
compatible with breastfeeding (Hale 2017); relative infant dose of 1%, peak milk 
concentrations 2–3 hours after oral dose. 
Metabolic syndrome Metformin No clear association with malformations or pregnancy complications. Low breastmilk 
transmission (Butalia et al. 2017; Organization of Teratology Information Specialists 2021). 
Tardive dyskinesia Benzodiazepines See above as stated in management of extrapyramidal symptoms. 
Valbenazine Limited to no human data. Increased risk of stillbirth and postnatal death in animal studies. 
Breastfeeding is not recommended either during treatment or for up to 5 days after last 
dose (Neurocrine Biosciences 2017).
