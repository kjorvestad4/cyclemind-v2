Other Pharmacological Treatment 
Other options include buspirone, antihistam ines such as hydroxyzine or diphenhy -
dramine, sedating second-generation antipsychotics such as quetiapine, and sedating 
antidepressants such as trazodone (Thorsness et al. 2018). Although minimizing poly-
pharmacy is optimal, some women may benefit from the short-term use of additional 
medications for anxiety or sleep in conjunction with or in place of SSRI or SNRI med-
ications. These are each discussed separately. 
Human reproductive safety data regarding buspirone are scarce, although available 
animal data do not suggest increased risk for malformations (Armstrong 2008). Given 
this lack of reproductive safety data, other agents may be preferred. Sedating antihista-
mines, such as diphenhydramine, hydroxyzine, and doxylamine, are commonly used 
during pregnancy for anxiety and sleep, as well as for pruritis, nausea and vomiting, and 
allergic symptoms. Reproductive safety data are limited on these agents despite their 
widespread use; however, they do not appear to be associated with congenital malforma-
tions (Li et al. 2013). Second-generation antipsychotic medications, such as quetiapine, 
may be used at times for anxiety or insomn ia; the reproductive safety of these medi-
cations is addressed in Chapter 17, “Schizophrenia and Related Disorders.” Sedating 
antidepressants, such as trazodone, are commonly used off-label for insomnia at dos-
ages of 50–200 mg. Reproductive safety data are limited but overall reassuring and do 
not suggest increased risk of congenital malformations (Einarson et al. 2003, 2009). 
Behavioral and Somatic Treatments 
Data regarding specific psychotherapies for perinatal anxiety are evolving. For exam-
ple, a systematic review of 78 studies of depression, anxiety, and trauma-related dis-
orders noted that although evidence of benefit of both CBT and interpersonal therapy 
(IPT) for perinatal depression is strong, the evidence for psychotherapy for perinatal 
anxiety is still quite limited (Nillni et al. 2018). However, several forms of psychother-
apy have been studied specifically for anxiety in the perinatal population. Several 
studies, including a preliminary meta-analysis, have shown benefit of CBT for peri -
natal anxiety (Green et al. 2015; Maguire et al. 2018). In addition, mindfulness-based 
CBT has been shown in several studies to have efficacy in reducing pregnancy-related 
anxiety symptoms and improving the use of adaptive coping strategies and psycho-
logical well-being (Goodman et al. 2014b; Zemestani and Fazeli Nikoo 2020). IPT is a 
time-limited, manualized psychotherapy that focuses on addressing current interper-
sonal issues related to universal experiences of losses, changes, or conflicts (Stuart 
2012). Substantial evidence supports its use in both the prevention and treatment of 
perinatal depression, and a meta-analysis also supported the use of IPT for the treat-
ment of perinatal anxiety (Sockol 2018). Other psychotherapies currently being stud-
ied for their efficacy in perinatal anxiety include acceptance and commitment therapy, 
which emphasizes mindfulness, acceptance, and recognition of internal values (Bo -
nacquisti et al. 2017). Mobile- and internet-based interventions are also showing some 
promise in this population, although data are still early and limited (Hussain-Shamsy
