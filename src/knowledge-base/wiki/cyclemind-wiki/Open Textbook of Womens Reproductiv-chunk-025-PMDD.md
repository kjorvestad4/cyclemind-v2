Clomiphene citrate. Clomiphene citrate is a selective estrogen receptor modulator 
with mixed agonist-antagonist properties. Taken orally on days 3–7 of the menstrual 
cycle, it creates a central perception of low estradiol, which leads to increased FSH and 
LH via negative feedback and then to ovarian stimulation and increased follicular de-
velopment (Kousta et al. 1997). Clomiphene citrate is associated with mood lability, 
often characterized as “super PMS” (preme nstrual syndrome), as well as insomnia 
and decreased libido (Aussedat et al. 2017; Blenner 1991; Choi et al. 2005). It has been 
associated with case reports of transient psychosis occurring in patients with and 
without a previous psychiatric history (Altmark et al. 1987; Seeman 2015; Siedentopf 
et al. 1997). In these case reports, sleeplessness and agitation were early symptoms, 
followed by auditory hallucinations, para noia, delusions, and ideas of reference. 
Treatment with neuroleptics, electroconvulsive therapy, or a combination of both re-
solved the symptoms (Siedentopf et al. 1997; Sinha and Garg 2014). 
Gonadotropins. Gonadotropins are used for controlled ovarian stimulation. These 
medications increase the number of ovaria n follicles and are associated with ex -
tremely high serum estradiol levels (Usa di and Merriam 2015). As with clomiphene 
citrate, women exposed to these medications frequently describe mood lability (Choi 
et al. 2005). Carefully controlled studies of women undergoing IVF with gonado -
tropins and progesterone supplementation  showed increased depressive symptoms 
as they moved from the estrogen-dominant (“follicular”) phase to the progesterone-
dominant (“luteal”) phase, suggesting that changing levels rather than absolute levels 
of these hormones were associated with symptoms (Bloch et al. 2011). Furthermore, 
women who had a previous Axis I diagnosis of anxiety or major depressive disorder 
(MDD) had even greater shifts in mood symptoms (Zaig et al. 2013).

GnRH agonists and antagonists. GnRH agonists and antagonists are used fre -
quently in IVF cycles to prevent premature ovulation; these medications lead to a tem-
porary hypogonadal state similar to menopause (Copperman and Benadiva 2013). 
Most studies of psychiatric side effects from GnRH agonists have included women 
who take the medications for fibroids and are thus exposed for prolonged periods. In 
these women, hot flashes, sleep disturbance, irritability, depressed mood, and difficulty 
with memory/concentration are common side effects (Warnock et al. 1998). Women re-
ceiving fertility treatment are likely to be exposed to these medications for a shorter pe-
riod of time and thus may experience fewer side effects. 
In a prospective study, patients did not have increased mood or anxiety symptoms 
during pretreatment with GnRH agonists. Instead, increased anxiety and depression 
symptoms emerged as estrogen levels were rapidly rising (Bloch et al. 2011). In a ran-
domized trial of the mood effects of GnRH agonist and antagonist treatments in women 
undergoing IVF, neither treatment was a ssociated with significant mood changes. 
However, pretreatment neuroticism was related to mental distress throughout treat-
ment, independent of protocol (Stenbæk et  al. 2015). GnRH agonist treatment has also 
been associated with increased daily mood lability in women with elevated baseline 
mood lability and neuroticism (Stenbæk et al. 2015, 2019). 
Progesterone. Progesterone is used in ART for endometrial support of implantation 
and early embryo development. Although most ART cycles use micronized progester-
one, which does not appear to have CNS effects, some cycles use synthetic progestins, 
such as medroxyprogesterone acetate, whic h have been associated with depression 
and anxiety symptoms in some women (Skovlund et al. 2016; Wang et al. 2016). 
Metformin. Metformin is an oral insulin-sensitizing medication prescribed to women 
with PCOS to decrease insulin resistance for ovulation induction (Practice Committee 
of the American Society for Reproductive Medicine 2017). Interestingly, metformin 
may be associated with improved depression symptoms in women with PCOS. In a pilot 
study of 19 adolescents and 25 adults with PCOS, treatment with metformin for 90 days 
was associated not only with decreased insulin resistance but also with decreased de-
pression and anxiety scores (Erensoy et al. 2019). 
Bromocriptine and cabergoline. Bromocriptine and cabergoline are ergot-de -
rived dopamine D 2 agonist medications that are used  in patients with hyperprolac -
tinemia (Usadi and Merriam 2015). Dopami ne agonists have be en associated with 
psychiatric side effects, including hypomania and psychotic symptoms; patients with 
preexisting affective or psychotic illnesses appear to be at greatest risk (Seeman 2015; 
Snellen et al. 2016). Dopamine agonists can also increase the risk of impulse-control 
disorders (Grall-Bronnec et al. 2016; Thondam et al. 2013). All infertile women with a 
personal or family history of mania or psychosis, if prescribed these medications,

Corticosteroids. Prednisone and prednisolone are oral corticosteroids prescribed in 
some ART cycles to enhance uterine receptivity, especially in women with a history of 
recurrent pregnancy losses or multiple failed IVF cycles. It is well established that these 
medications may have a range of mental health effects, including insomnia, irritability, 
anxiety, new onset of mania and psychosis, and acute depressive symptoms with sui-
cidality (Kenna et al. 2011). Mood and psychotic disorders associated with corticoste-
roids are treated with mood stabilizers, including lithium, valproic acid, and atypical 
antipsychotics (Brown and Suppes 1998). 
Impact of Mental Health Conditions on Fertility 
Depression and Anxiety 
It is difficult to determine the role of psychiatric illnesses as the primary risk factors 
for infertility because many of the biological processes thought to underlie increased 
risk for infertility, such as inflammation, are also found in mood and anxiety disor -
ders. Most studies of fertility rates in women with mood disorders have not controlled 
for important confounding variables, such as subclinical hypothyroidism or obesity 
(Williams et al. 2007). When studies report higher rates of stress, anxiety, and mood 
disorders in patients undergoing infertilit y evaluation and treatment, it may not be 
specified whether the fertility problems themselves led to psychiatric symptoms and 
disorders or the psychiatric disorders affected fertility (Rooney and Domar 2018). Pro-
posed mechanisms through which MDD could directly affect fertility involve physi -
ological changes in the depressed state, such as thyroid dysfunction and disruptions 
in the HPG and hypothalamic -pituitary-adrenal (HPA) axes. For instance, results 
from one small study of 10 women with and 13 women without depression suggest 
that women with MDD may have changes in LH pulsatility, leading to subtle men -
strual changes and subfertility (Grambsch et al. 2004). Changes in immune function 
associated with stress and depression may also adversely affect reproductive function 
(Haimovici and Hill 1998). Further studies are needed to distinguish the direct effects 
of MDD from symptoms and behaviors associated with these disorders, such as low 
libido, smoking, and alcohol use, which may also interfere with reproductive success. 
Eating Disorders 
Studies have shown high rates of current or past histories of eating disorders in 
women in infertility clinic populations. Anorexia nervosa is typically associated with 
ovulatory dysfunction; amenorrhea is estimated to occur in 66%–84% of patients and 
oligomenorrhea in 6%–11% (Kimmel et al. 2016). Fertility problems may persist even 
after recovery because ovulatory dysfunction may persist. Women with a history of 
bulimia nervosa also report high rates of amenorrhea (7%–40%) and oligomenorrhea 
(36%–64%) (Kimmel et al. 2016), and they are more likely than women without psychi-
atric disorders to undergo fertility treatment (Micali et al. 2014). Both anorexia nervosa 
and bulimia nervosa are also associated with higher rates of miscarriage (Boutari et al. 
2020). Binge-eating disorder can also be related to infertility because of its association 
with a high BMI (McCuen-Wurst et al. 2018), a risk factor for infertility (Silvestris et al.
