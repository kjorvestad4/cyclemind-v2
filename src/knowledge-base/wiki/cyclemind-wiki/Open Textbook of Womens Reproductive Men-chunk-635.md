Conclusion 
Pregnancy represents an important window of opportunity for engagement in treat-
ment of SUDs. Comprehensive treatment programs that provide coordinated prenatal 
care, addiction treatment, behavioral health treatment, and help addressing social de-
terminants of health show promise for improving pregnancy and birth outcomes, as 
well as long-term outcomes for women and their children. 
Key Points 
• Alcohol is a known teratogen. Fetal alcohol spectrum disorders are the most 
common and preventable cause of intellectual disability in the United States. 
• Aside from the use of benzodiazepines for management of alcohol withdraw-
al, medications for alcohol use disorder (disulfiram, acamprosate, naltrexone) 
are not commonly used in pregnancy due to lack of data regarding reproduc-
tive safety. 
• Tobacco use in pregnancy is associated with adverse pregnancy and bir th 
outcomes. Women with major depressive disorder are more likely to smoke. 
• Data supporting the efficacy of most smoking cessation treatments in pregnant 
women are as yet limited, but the harms of tobacco use are such that nicotine 
replacement therapy and bupropion are reasonable to consider in conjunction 
with behavioral interventions for smoking cessation. 
• Cannabis use in pregnancy has been associated with adverse pregnancy and 
birth outcomes and adverse neurobehavioral outcomes in children, although 
causality is difficult to determine due to risks of confounding in observational 
studies. 
• Women should be encouraged to avoid cannabis during pregnancy and while 
breastfeeding and should be offered other treatment options if they indicate 
using cannabis to address common pregnancy-related symptoms, such as 
nausea or insomnia. 
• The standard of care for opioid use disorder during pregnancy is medication-
assisted treatment with either buprenorphine (in buprenorphine or buprenor-
phine/naloxone formulations) or methadone; opioid detoxification during preg-
nancy is not recommended.
