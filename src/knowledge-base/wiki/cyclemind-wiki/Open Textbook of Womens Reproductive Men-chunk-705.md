Evaluation of parenting capacity for the courts is a forensic assessment. The forensic 
evaluation differs from a clinical evaluation in that the traditional doctor–patient rela-
tionship does not apply. Various clinical, legal, and ethical dilemmas arise when a men-
tal health professional attempts to serv e as both treating clinician and forensic 
evaluator. Forensic and clinical roles are fundamentally different and conflicting with 
regard to the overall goal of the work, the nature of the relationship between evaluator 
and evaluee, assessment strategies, and ethical principles entailed (Strasburger et al. 
1997). Clinicians not trained in forensic assessment should be wary of performing par-
enting capacity assessments for legal purposes. Mistakes commonly seen in parental 
fitness evaluations include failure to disclose the purpose of the evaluation and limita-
tions of confidentiality, overreliance on a single source of information, and overstated 
conclusions and recommendations (Budd 2005). Such deficiencies may have signifi -
cant ramifications in the lives of parents and children, resulting in crucial decisions re-
garding custody and parental rights being based on incomplete, invalid, or misleading 
information (Jacobsen et al. 1997). Given the inherent role of conflicts and important 
consequences in parenting capacity cases, mental health professionals should avoid 
acting as both treating clinician and forensic evaluator when questions of parental fit-
ness arise (Strasburger et al. 1997). 
Child Abuse Risk and Duty to Report 
Child maltreatment is usually conceptualized as including physical abuse, neglect, 
sexual abuse, and emotional abuse. Child maltreatment is usually defined by state law. 
Physical abuse includes intentional use of force against a child that may result in injury; 
neglect includes a caregiver’s failure to meet the child’s basic physical, medical, educa-
tional, and emotional needs; sexual abuse involves attempted sexual acts or exploita -
tion; and emotional abuse involves behavior conveying to the child that he or she is 
worthless or valued only to meet the caregiver’s needs (McEwan and Friedman 2016). 
There is an approximately tenfold difference between the prevalence of child maltreat-
ment self-report and the reports to child protection agencies (McEwan and Friedman 
2016). Approximately one-quarter of report ed cases are substantiated in the United 
States (McEwan and Friedman 2016). In the cases not substantiated, there may be a 
lack of evidence, poor cooperation, investigatory issues, or a lack of maltreatment. 
Although psychiatrists are mandated repo rters of child maltreatment, they often 
have concerns about breaking confidential ity. However, completing the compulsory 
reporting may contribute in a positive way to the therapeutic process, especially if the 
professional discusses the need to report with the parent. Clinicians should keep in 
mind, however, that parents may be reluctant to seek treatment or to disclose symp -
toms for fear of being reported to child protective services (CPS) and losing custody.
