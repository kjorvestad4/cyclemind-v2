Nonpharmacological Somatic Treatments 
Electroconvulsive Therapy 
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is an effe ctive option for the treatment of severe 
mood symptoms, especially in the context of associated catatonia, psychotic agitation, 
suicidality, or severe physical decline (e.g., malnutrition or dehydration) (Ward et al. 
2018). ECT offers a rapid treatment response and serves as an alternative to pharma-
cological treatment or as a second-line agent when pharmacotherapy fails. The safety 
and efficacy of ECT during pregnancy and postpartum have been established over de-
cades and support its role as a treatment option for perinatal women when clinically 
indicated (Ward et al. 2018). For a detailed discussion of ECT in pregnancy and post-
partum, please see Chapter 13, “Depressive Disorders.” 
Bright Light Therapy 
Bright light therapy is an effective and sa fe treatment option for bipolar depression 
when prescribed as augmentation with a mood-stabilizing medication. Sit et al. (2018) 
performed a randomized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trial in adults with BDI 
and BDII and found that midday 7,000-lux bright light therapy in conjunction with a 
mood-stabilizing medication led to a robust antidepressant response (68.2% remis -
sion rate of depression vs. 22.2% with placebo) after 4–6 weeks. Midday timing of light 
therapy (between 12 P.M. and 2:30 P.M.) was not associated with switching to mania, 
hypomania, or mixed states. Advantages of bright light therapy in the perinatal pe -
riod include the absence of drug transfer in utero or via breastfeeding and a minimal 
side effect profile. The bright light box should be a broad-spectrum, bright white light 
that provides ultraviolet filtration and emits 7,000 to 10,000 lux. It should be placed at 
an optimal distance of 12 inches from the eyes (Sit et al. 2018). Light dosing starts with 
15 minutes a day at midday for the first w eek, then increases by 15 minutes per day 
each week until the target dose of 45–60 mi nutes a day by week 4 is reached or until 
symptoms have fully remitted (Sit et al. 2018). 
Behavioral Treatments 
Psychoeducation and Psychotherapy 
Although pharmacotherapy is the mainstay  of treatment for BD, both psychoeduca -
tion and select psychotherapy modalities are useful adjunctive tools for the treatment 
of acute depressive episodes and prevention of relapse. Use of these modalities in the 
setting of acute mania is not supported by current evidence (Yatham et al. 2018). 
Models of evidence-based psychoeducation for individuals with BD may be deliv-
ered individually or in a group setting over numerous sessions and focus on develop-
ing skills to identify warning signs of depression and mania, manage stress, minimize 
denial of chronic illness, improve medication adherence, and develop healthy lifestyles 
(Yatham et al. 2018). Evidence-based psychotherapy modalities for BD include cogni-
tive-behavioral therapy, family-focused therapy, and interpersonal and social rhythms 
therapy. Peer support is an evidence-based modality in the maintenance phase of the 
illness only (Yatham et al. 2018). Although data regarding the use of these treatments
