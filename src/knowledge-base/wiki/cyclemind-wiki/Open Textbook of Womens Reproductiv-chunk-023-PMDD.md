For many women, pregnancy and becoming a parent represent major life milestones. 
However, the journey at times can be a challenging one—one that contains loss, grief, 
and unexpected hardship. Infertility and perinatal loss represent two such challenges. 
Collectively termed reproductive trauma, these issues can significantly affect mental 
health (Bhat and Byatt 2016). In this chapter, we review the fundamentals of infertility 
and perinatal loss, with a particular focus on their intersection with mental health. 
Infertility 
Infertility is common, and it is physically and emotionally challenging (Patel et al. 2018). 
Its impact on a woman’s mental health can be profound. Additionally, psychiatric condi-
tions may be related to infertility and its treatment in a bidirectional but complex manner. 
In the sections that follow, we review the epidemiology, pathophysiology, evaluation, and 
management of infertility. We summarize the use of assisted reproductive technology 
(ART). Of note, the use of ART in populations with infertility has different mental health 
implications than the use of ART in populations for whom it offers an important oppor-

Definitions 
Infertility is defined as the failure to establish a clinical pregnancy after 12 months of 
regular, unprotected sexual intercourse or the impairment of a person’s capacity to re-
produce, either as an individual or with a partner (Zegers-Hochschild et al. 2017). Pri-
mary infertility refers to meeting criteria for infertility with no prior clinical pregnancy; 
secondary infertility refers to meeting criteria for infertility but with a prior pregnancy 
in the past (Zegers-Hochschild et al. 2017). An infertility evaluation is recommended 
for women younger than age 35 years who have failed to conceive after 12 months of 
unprotected intercourse and for women age 35 years or older who have failed to con-
ceive after 6 months of unprotected interc ourse (American College of Obstetricians 
and Gynecologists 2019). Women age 40 and older and those who have a condition 
associated with infertility should be offered an immediate evaluation (American Col-
lege of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2019). 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Infertility is estimated to affect 8%–12% of couples of reproductive age, and the prev-
alence among women of reproductive age ranges across the world (Mascarenhas et al. 
2012; Ombelet et al. 2008). It is estimated that primary infertility occurs in one in every 
seven couples in the Western world and one in every four couples in developing coun-
tries. Primary infertility rates may reach as high as 30% in some countries of South 
Asia, Sub-Saharan Africa, the Middle East, North Africa, Central and Eastern Europe, 
and Central Asia (Mascarenhas et al. 2012). In the United States, some studies find no 
difference in infertility across racial, et hnic, or economic categories, whereas others 
find increased rates of infertility among Black women and American Indian/Alaskan 
Native women (Lynch 2019; Shirazi and Rosinger 2020). 
The way infertility is defined is, in itself, racially biased, because many definitions 
of infertility depend on a women’s cohabitation status, and rates of cohabiting differ 
across racial and ethnic groups. Regardless of whether there are discrepancies in rates 
of infertility, however, there are certainly discrepancies in access to infertility treat -
ment (which is expensive and often not covered by insurance) and in success of treat-
ment (Insogna and Ginsburg 2018; Janitz et al. 2019). Numerous studies have found 
that Black, Hispanic, and Asian women have lower clinical pregnancy rates and live 
birth rates after in vitro fertilization (IVF) compared with white women (Humphries 
et al. 2016). This is also true for pregnancies after oocyte donation (Zhou et al. 2020).
