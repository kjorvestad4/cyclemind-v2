Economic Considerations 
Economic factors play a critical role in the course of perinatal trauma. Having finan -
cial security to both care for and provide for the baby and to allow for sufficient time 
off from work to adjust to the demands of  a new infant in the home plays a critical 
role in the early postpartum course (Meltzer-Brody et al. 2017). 
Pathophysiology 
Stress-diathesis models of PTSD include biological, psychological, and social vulnera-
bilities that are triggered by acute trauma, with the course of illness mediated by risk, 
vulnerability, and resilience factors (Bomyea et al. 2012). Biological, psychological, and 
social factors mediate the pathophysiology of trauma and stress-related disorders. No 
data indicate whether the biological pathways of perinatal PTSD differ from those of 
PTSD from other causes, so the following di scussion on biological factors is drawn 
from the general literature. 
Biological Factors 
The role of hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis suppression by stress hormones is a well-
established hallmark of the fear response (Dunlop and Wong 2019), but the relationship 
between an acute fear response and the prolonged hyperreactivity of PTSD is less well un-
derstood. Animal models of extreme chronic stress demonstrate neuronal remodeling, 
with the most notable changes in the prefrontal cortex and hippocampus. In these areas, 
prolonged stress produces reductions in synaptic connectivity, disruption in glucocorti-
coid signaling, increased neuroinflammation, reduced levels of brain-derived neuro -
trophic factor (BDNF), and deficits in astrocytes (Abdallah et al. 2019; McEwen 2017). 
Reduced uptake of glutamate in the synapses leads to increased extracellular glutamate 
and excitotoxicity and ultimately to neuronal atrophy and synaptic loss (Abdallah et al. 
2019). In preclinical studies, these neuronal changes are associated with behavioral im-
pairment and anxiety dysregulation. Chronic stress in animal models also results in in-
creased synaptic connectivity and upregulated BDNF in the basolateral amygdala and 
nucleus accumbens, which is similarly associated with behavioral disturbances (Abdal-
lah et al. 2019; McEwen 2017). Human imaging studies looking at neurobiological find-
ings associated with PTSD have implicated reduced hippocampal volumes and structural 
differences in the dorsal and rostral anterior cingulate cortices, ventromedial prefrontal 
cortex, amygdala, and insula (Cirino and Knapp 2019; Fitzgerald et al. 2018). These 
pathways, and in particular the hypothalam ic-pituitary-adrenal axis, are altered in 
women with chronic PTSD and undergo changes during pregnancy and postpartum, 
further making women susceptible to peripartum PTSD (Cirino and Knapp 2019).
