Most female correctional facilities offer a parenting program, but most of these pro-
grams do not include visitation (Miller et al. 2014), despite contact during incarceration 
being an important predictor of reunification (Gilham 2012). However, most incarcer-
ated women keep in some form of regular contact with their children. Even for those 
mothers who are afforded in-person visits, many factors such as geographical distance, 
a physical environment that is not child-friendly, security procedures, restrictions on 
physical contact, and short length of visitation can be problematic (O’Malley and Dev-
aney 2016; Schubert et al. 2016). To address these challenges, some prisons have tried 
videoconferencing, with limited success (Cramer et al. 2017), and others have tried 
having the parent record bedtime stories and sending the recordings to their children 
(Poehlmann et al. 2010). Encouraging commu nication between incarcerated mothers 
and their children is important because prolonged separation has a significant impact 
on psychological outcomes for the children. Children of incarcerated mothers have 
been noted to be at increased risk for sleep disorders, depression, anger, and anxiety 
(Mignon and Ransford 2012). 
Infertility and the Law 
The increasing use of reproductive technologies has led to complex ethical and legal 
considerations. (For a complete discussion of infertility and assisted reproductive tech-
nologies, please see Chapter 5, “Infertility and Perinatal Loss.”) As an example, gesta-
tional surrogacy is increasingly used as a form of assisted reproduction, particularly for 
individuals who cannot or choose not to carry a child because of medical problems, age, 
marital status, male same-sex couples, tran sgender couples, or other reasons. In the 
United States, 18,400 infants were born from gestational carriers between 1999 and 2013, 
and most of these children were born after 2010 (Centers for Disease Control and Pre-
vention 2016). Gestational carriers are women who become pregnant and give birth to a 
child with the intention of relinquishing the child to the intended parents. The surro-
gacy can be traditional, in which the gestational carrier’s eggs are used, or gestational, 
in which an embryo is transferred into the gestational carrier’s uterus. However, only 
gestational surrogacy is widely practiced today (Söderström-Anttila et al. 2016). Ges-
tational carriers are typically paid for th eir medical costs and other reasonable preg -
nancy-related expenses. This arrangement is known as “altruistic surrogacy,” and a 
relative is often the gestational carrier in these arrangements. In contrast, in commercial 
arrangements, gestational carriers are paid for their services in addition to these typical 
reimbursements. 
Commercial surrogacy tends to be controversial because of concerns that the gesta-
tional carrier may be coerced or exploited, whereas altruistic arrangements are more 
broadly accepted. Commercial surrogacy is not allowed in some countries. For instance, 
both India and Cambodia ban commercial surrogacy. In Cambodia, a surrogate can be 
charged with human trafficking and may face up to 20 years in prison (Blomberg 2019). 
Other countries, such as Canada and New Zealand, allow only altruistic arrangements 
(Cornell Law School 2017). Still others, such as Iceland, Germany, and France, ban sur-
rogacy altogether. In the United States, no federal law governs surrogacy, leaving indi-
vidual states to delineate appropriate parameters. Legal scholars note that consequently
