sation, nicotine replacement therapy is not routinely recommended. However, it may 
be considered in a woman who has a history of successful smoking cessation with the 
use of this therapy in the past, after carefully weighing the risks of continued nicotine 
use compared with the use of nicotine replacement therapy. 
The primary treatment for perinatal nicotine use disorders includes behavioral in-
terventions, such as motivational interv iewing or contingency management, which 
have shown modest success in reducing smoking or relapse to nicotine use in pregnant 
women (Chamberlain et al. 2013; Heckma n et al. 2010). Co ntingency management 
with financial incentives appears to be the most effective for smoking cessation during 
pregnancy (Chamberlain et al. 2013; Lerfino et al. 2015); however, implementation of 
financial incentives can be challenging fo r treatment programs. Treatments aimed at 
relapse prevention in the postpartum period have included psychoeducation and mo-
tivational interviewing and have demonstrated modest success (Levitt et al. 2007). 
Alcohol Use Disorders 
No known studies have evaluated the efficacy of FDA-approved pharmacological in-
terventions for the treatment of alcohol use disorder (i.e., naltrexone, disulfiram, aca-
mprosate) among pregnant women in genera l or pregnant women with alcohol use 
disorder (Zagon et al. 1998). However, clinical data using naltrexone for the treatment 
of opioid use disorder during pregnancy are available, and, overall, naltrexone does 
not appear to be associated with poor birth outcomes (Wachman et al. 2019). Interpre-
tation of these data is limited by small sample sizes, lack of control groups, minimal 
control for confounding variables, and lack of long-term follow-up. Pharmacological 
treatments for perinatal alcohol use disorder are not routinely recommended due to 
the paucity of efficacy and safety data. However, patients and providers need to care-
fully weigh the well-known risks of alcoho l use in pregnancy against the potential 
benefits and both known and unknown risks of pharmacotherapy. 
No studies to our knowledge have systematically examined the effects of perinatal 
alcohol withdrawal or benzodiazepine tr eatment for perinatal alcohol withdrawal.
