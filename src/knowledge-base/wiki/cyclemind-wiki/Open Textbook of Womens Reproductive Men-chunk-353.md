Key Points 
• Early interactions between the infant and caregiver set a template for long -
standing effects on the well-being of the infant. 
• Parental mental illness can have adverse effects on parent–infant interactions. 
• Neurobiological mechanisms of stress transmission during pregnancy, and of 
responsive parenting behavior during infancy, can affect the child’s stress re-
sponse, self-regulation, and attachment into adulthood. 
• Negative patterns of interactions between parent and infant can be identified in 
the clinical setting and are amenable to intervention. Intervening during preg-
nancy and infancy offers an opportunity to achieve long-standing benefits for 
both the child and parents. 
• Evidence-based models of care are available that incorporate treatment for the 
mother, the infant, and their relationship with culturally informed and strength-
based approaches. 
• Parents of infants who require care in the neonatal intensive care unit (NICU) 
face particular stressors and may need additional supports specific to their 
experience; racial and ethnic disparities in NICU care augment these stress-
ors for families of color. 
• Fathers, LGBTQ+ parents, and adoptive parents are also at increased risk of 
depression in the period after the birth or adoption of an infant and also ben-
efit from screening and treatment.
