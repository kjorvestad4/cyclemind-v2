Other Medical Interventions: Neuromodulation 
The most systematically and rigorously studied form of neuromodulation in the treat-
ment of PTSD is repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) (Koek et al. 
2019). rTMS has been shown to be an effective treatment of PTSD in sham-controlled 
studies for reducing hyperarousal (Osuch et  al. 2009; Watts et al. 2012), reexperienc -
ing, intrusive memories, and avoidance and for improving scores on the PTSD Check-
list and Clinician-Administered PTSD Scale (Cohen et al. 2004; Kozel et al. 2018; Nam 
et al. 2013; Watts et al. 2013). Furthermore, a recent study found that rTMS led to more 
rapid PTSD symptom reduction and more sustained changes immediately preceding 
cognitive processing therapy (CPT) versus sham transcranial magnetic stimulation 
prior to CPT (Kozel et al. 2018). rTMS appears to be a safe and well-tolerated treat -
ment during pregnancy (Hizli et al. 2014; Kim et al. 2019). 
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) as a treatment for PTSD has limited data. A sys -
tematic reviewof studies from 1956 to 2016 found that individuals with PTSD who re-
ceived ECT also had comorbid depression, limiting conclusions on whether PTSD in 
the absence of comorbid depressive symptoms would respond to ECT (Youssef et al. 
2017). Given ECT’s potential side effect of retrograde amnesia, harnessing this effect 
in the treatment of PTSD through having individuals recall the traumatic memory 
prior to receiving ECT is a theoretical possib ility (Misanin et al. 1968). ECT is safe to 
use during all trimesters of pregnancy (Ward et al. 2018). 
Behavioral Treatments 
In 2017, the VA/DoD and American Psychiatric Association (APA) each published a 
treatment guideline for PTSD. The APA guideline is specifically for treatment of PTSD 
among adults and strongly recommends cognitive-behavioral therapy, CPT, prolonged 
exposure (PE), cognitive therapy, brief eclectic psychotherapy, narrative exposure ther-
apy, and written narrative experience. Trauma-focused psychotherapies such as CPT, 
PE, and eye movement desens itization and reprocessing (EMDR) are found to have 
greater effect sizes than non-trauma-focused therapies and medications (Chard 2005; 
Lee et al. 2016; Watts et al. 2013). 
There are six tenets that are common to the empirically supported therapies for 
PTSD (Table 18–10): psychoeducation, emotion regulation, imaginal exposure, cogni-
tive processing, emotions, and memory processing (Schnyder et al. 2015). The goals of 
psychoeducation are to provide information on the course of PTSD and stress reactions 
and strategies to manage distress. Emotion regulation skills are taught more implicitly 
over the course of treatment. Imaginal exposure is a major focus of PE and narrative 
exposure therapy, although it is still a tenet of all evidence-based psychotherapies for 
trauma-related disorders (Schnyder et al. 2015). Exposure addresses the challenges of 
separating the present safety from the past traumatic event(s) (Coventry et al. 2020). 
Cognitive processing and restructuring (i.e., integration of the lessons from exposure) 
is another element that can be found in almost all of the empirically supported psycho-
logical treatments for PTSD (Diehle et al. 2014; Schnyder et al. 2015).

Prolonged Exposure 
PE is based on emotional processing theory; patients learn that trauma-related mem-
ories are not perilous and experience these memories and feelings. The original inter-
vention protocol was described as 9–12 sessions, each 90–120 minutes in length, with 
use of both imaginal and in vivo exposure (Foa and Rothbaum 1998). The patient is 
challenged in a graduated fashion with the goal of achieving success in confronting 
feared stimuli and coping with evoked affect. PE has been found to be safe and effec-
tive in patients with co-o ccurring dissociation, borderline personality disorder, psy -
chosis, suicidal behavior, nonsuicidal self-injury, substance use disorders, and major 
depressive disorder (van Minnen et al. 2012). Patients randomly assigned to exposure 
therapy have significantly greater pre- to posttreatment reductions in PTSD symp -
toms compared with those who receive su pportive counseling, relaxation training, 
and treatment as usual, including pharmacotherapy (Asukai et al. 2010; Bryant et al. 
2003; Schnurr et al. 2007; Taylor et al. 2 003). Several meta-analyses have found that 
exposure therapy is more effective that non-trauma-focused therapies (Bradley et al. 
2005; Cusack et al. 2016; Powers et al. 2010; Watts et al. 2013).

Cognitive Therapy 
Several studies have investigated specific  cognitive therapies in the management of 
traumatic stress after childbirth. A randomized controlled trial of women after emer-
gent cesarean section found success in the use of an online visuospatial task aimed at 
reducing intrusive traumatic memories (Horsch et al. 2017). A four-session grief coun-
seling intervention in women who have experienced stillbirths found reduction in 
PTSD severity (Navidian et al. 2017). An internet-based cognitive-behavioral therapy 
intervention after childbirth was found to reduce PTSD symptoms in the women ran-
domized to receive the therapy (Nieminen et al. 2016). A recent systematic review and 
meta-analysis concluded that many trauma-focused psychological interventions can 
be helpful in reducing symptoms of postpartum PTSD in the short and medium term 
but that evidence is lacking on whether any particular intervention is more effective 
than others and on whether these interventions can help women with long-term re -
covery from clinically significant PTSD (Furuta et al. 2018). 
Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing 
EMDR was initially introduced in 1989 as an eight-phase manualized therapy with the 
objective of assisting patients in accessing and processing traumatic memories while 
bringing them to an adaptive resolution. Initial focus is on evaluation and planning fol-
lowed by desensitization and reprocessing, with end goals of installing positive cogni-
tions, replacing negative ones, and releasing associated somatic tension. The novel aspect 
of EMDR as compared with other trauma-focused therapies for PTSD is its inclusion of 
eye movements. Although experimental studies have shown that horizontal eye move-
ments are associated with a decrease in the vividness, emotionality, and arousal associ-
ated with memories, most of these studies are of healthy participants (van den Hout et 
al. 2001). Empirical evidence supports EMDR as superior to waitlist controls and com-
parable with PE and cognitive-based ps ychotherapies (Davidson and Parker 2001; 
Powers et al. 2010). Although one meta-analysis found no difference between EMDR 
and PE, another found PE to be superior to EMDR (Rothbaum et al. 2005; Taylor et al. 
2003). One small pilot study of four women that investigated EMDR after childbirth 
found it to be effective in the reduction of PTSD symptoms (Sandström et al. 2008). 
Treatment Challenges 
Treatment challenges fall into three broad categories: physician-/provider-specific, 
patient-specific, and system barriers. Additional challenges include the lack of studies 
specific to the management of perinatal PTSD.

Physician-/Provider-Specific 
A qualitative study specific to physicians’ perceived barriers for caring for sexual as-
sault survivors found internal barriers to ca re: fear of a patient disclosing, personal 
attitudes regarding sexual assault, and the emotional toll of caring for sexual assault 
survivors (Amin et al. 2017). Physicians and other providers may be unsure about 
what to do with a positive screen for PTSD. Therapists may lack comfort and compe-
tence in providing evidence-based therapies for PTSD. Similarly, physicians may lack 
knowledge about evidence-based treatment approaches for PTSD, particularly in the 
peripartum period. Myths continually propagate that trauma-focused therapies will 
lead to worsening of PTSD symptoms and dropout, a claim not supported by multiple 
studies (Feeny et al. 2003; Foa et al. 2002).

System Barriers 
Access to evidence-based treatments for PTSD is limited in many parts of the nation 
due to inadequate access to mental health services. In a sample of veterans with pos-
itive PTSD screens, most men (78.7%) and women (89.5%) would consider treatment 
for PTSD symptoms, with the largest proportion of both genders preferring counsel-
ing over a self-help computer program or phone application (Gutner et al. 2018). A 
small randomized controlled trial (N=20) evaluating the use of the PTSD Coach mo -
bile application with and without clinician support found that both the self-managed 
and clinician-managed versions led to PTSD symptom reduction based on the PTSD 
Checklist. The self-managed effect size was 0.41, which increased to 1.4 in the clinician-
supported PTSD Coach group, so although this intervention does not necessitate a cli-
nician, which improves access, involving a clinician improved the effectiveness of the 
intervention (Possemato et al. 2016). Indi viduals with PTSD st emming from prior 
medical treatment or discrimination by physicians or other providers may find com-
ing to the clinic or hospital for treatment of PTSD to be retraumatizing. Telemedicine 
may be a helpful tool for providing evidenced-based trauma-informed treatments to 
these individuals (Gilmore et al. 2016).

Special Considerations: 
Trauma and Psychological Distress Related to 
Voluntary Terminations of Pregnancy 
VTOPs occur when women desire to end pregnancies due to poor timing, lack of re -
sources, fetal anomalies, or fetal reductions. Although many women experience relief 
after VTOP , some may experience posttraumatic stress syndrome (PTSS) or PTSD. The 
etiology of PTSS/PTSD after VTOP remains inconclusive. Some studies have con -
cluded that stress after VTOP stems from premorbid or comorbid mental health dis -
orders and not from the termination itself, suggesting that mental health before VTOP 
predicts mental health afterward (Major et al. 2008; Robinson et al. 2009). Others have 
found that trauma stems from the unwant ed pregnancy and fact ors surrounding it, 
such as lack of partner or financial support (Fergusson et al. 2008; Mota et al. 2010). 
Still others conclude that stress emerges from the termination itself, as well as from a 
combination of variables (Broen et al. 2005; Coleman et al. 2010; Curley and Johnston 
2013). Alternatively, two recent studies found that being denied a VTOP contributed 
to more psychological distress (Biggs et al . 2017) and worse overall health than un -
dergoing one (Ralph et al. 2019). The etio logy of stress disorders after VTOP is con -
sidered multifactorial, stemming from any single to a combination of factors. 
Epidemiology 
In the United States, about 25% of wome n have experienced a VTOP . How many of 
these women go on to experience trauma or stress-related symptoms related to the 
VTOP is unclear. Some studies find no association between VTOP and PTSS/PTSD 
(Wallin Lundell et al. 2013). Others find rates of 12%–30% (Fergusson et al. 2008; Ham-
ama et al. 2010). 
Risk and Protective Factors 
Adequate information and screening for psychological risks, sufficient time for deci-
sion making, resources for alternatives to VTOP , and social support all protect against 
the development of stress reactions. Conver sely, biological vulnerabilities, such as 
prior mental health problems, substance disorders, and adverse early life events, can 
increase risk (Table 18–11). Women who are socially isolated, keep the termination and 
pregnancy secret, or feel pressured or ambivalent about their decision to abort are more 
vulnerable to develop negative outcomes (Speckhard and Rue 1992). Demographic 
factors, such as younger age, single status, lack of partner involvement, limited educa-
tion, and lowered socioeconomic status, have the potential to increase psychological 
risks. Obstetrical factors, such as prior termination(s), medical complications from the 
procedure, later gestational age of pregnancy, and viewing the embryo/fetus, can com-
pound risks (Major et al. 2008).

Clinical Features 
Often presenting as avoidant symptoms, PTSS/PTSD after VTOP can present with in-
creased substance use (Reardon et al. 2004) or with suicidal and self-injurious behav-
ior (Gissler et al. 1996; Morgan et al. 19 97), and high risk-relat ed PTSD can include 
manifestations of grief and loss, as well as survivor guilt (Speckhard and Rue 1992). 
Unresolved PTSD after VTOP can lead to disrupted attachment to subsequent preg -
nancies, babies, and children. 
Course and Prognosis 
The onset of PTSS/PTSD after a VTOP ranges from immediately after the procedure 
to decades later. Times of highest risk include the period between the procedure and 
the anticipated due date because of the continued physical effects of the pregnancy; an-
niversary times, such as the date of the procedure, the due date of the anticipated birth, 
or Mother’s Day; and seeing children of similar age as that of the aborted child (Speck-
hard and Rue 1992). Subsequent pregnancies may also reawaken affect and cognitions 
associated with a previous VTOP and disrupt the mother’s attachment to the target 
pregnancy. The course of PTSS/PTSD after VTOP waxes and wanes. Symptoms may 
manifest at vulnerable points across the reproductive lifespan, such as during subse-
quent pregnancies, subsequent pregnancy losses, problems with infertility or difficulty 
conceiving, and menopause. Symptoms range from mild distress to severe functional 
impairment, including high risk of suicidal, self-injurious, and self-destructive behav-
iors (Gissler et al. 1996; Morgan et al. 1997).

Research Limitations 
Research designs used to examine women’s responses to VTOP are limited by 
1. The inability to randomize 
2. High attrition rates among participants who are lost to follow up 
3. Withholding of accurate abortion data among participants who have had abortions, 
such as number of previous abortions and gestational age at the time of the abortion 
4. Use of nonequivalent comparison groups, such as comparing women who have 
wanted pregnancies (control group; deli very group) with women who have un -
wanted pregnancies (abortion group, obtained or denied) (Major et al. 2008) 
5. Misclassification problems, such as when participants who desire termination later 
change their minds in favor of parenting (Biggs et al. 2017; Fergusson et al. 2008) 
A highly debated diagnosis referred to as postabortion stress syndrome is not acknowl-
edged by the American Psychiatric Association or American Psychological Associa -
tion based on questions about the validity of the research describing this disorder. 
Treatment 
Treatment for PTSS/PTSD after VTOP follow s principles for treatment of trauma, 
such as using a phase-oriented approach th at includes stabilization, processing, and 
integration of the trauma narrative. Additionally, supportive readings for grief and 
loss tailored to the perinatal period can include On Death and Dying  by Kübler-Ross 
(1969) and Grief Counselling and Grief Therapy by Worden (2013). 
Conclusion 
Trauma and PTSD in the perinatal period require consideration of areas that include 
prior exposure to trauma (especially sexual trauma) and pregnancy factors that may 
cause trauma, including reproductive loss and traumatic birth experiences. Physicians 
and other clinicians must remain vigilant in screening carefully for trauma and using 
TIC principles in managing women with trauma in the perinatal period to ensure sen-
sitive, compassionate, patient-centered care.
