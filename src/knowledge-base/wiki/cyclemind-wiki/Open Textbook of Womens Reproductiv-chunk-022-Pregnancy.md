Key Points 
• Contraception drastically alters a woman’s reproductive destiny, which has 
downstream effects on her physical and mental health. 
• Negative effects on mood are not seen in the vast majority of women using 
contraception. 
• Contraception has noncontraceptive benefits, including but not limited to im-
provement in mental well-being for women who experience menstrual-related 
mood changes. 
• With the exception of antiepileptic medications, most psychotropic medica -
tions do not demonstrate clinically significant drug-drug interactions with hor-
monal contraception. 
• Mental health providers can optimize psychological functioning by playing an 
active role in contraceptive access, including but not limited to treatment re -
ferral and medication initiation. 
• Contraceptive decision making is often nuanced, and shared decision-making 
best respects the autonomy of the woman while maximizing opportunities for 
contraceptive benefit and satisfaction.
