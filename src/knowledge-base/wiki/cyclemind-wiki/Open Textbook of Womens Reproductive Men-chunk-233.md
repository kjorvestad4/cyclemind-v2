Practitioners screening perinatal or postpartum women for depression should pay 
close attention to the presence of anxiety symptoms. Studies indicate that perinatal de-

traditional screening tools for symptoms of depression do not typically evaluate for 
anxiety symptoms (Abramowitz et al. 2010b). Furthermore, perinatal OCD is often

Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
The diagnostic criteria for OCD from DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association 2013) 
are listed in Box 20–1 and are the same as the criteria for perinatal OCD. There is cur-
rently no specifier for “with peripartum onset” for the diagnosis of OCD in DSM-5; 
the manual does, however, discuss the possible onset and exacerbation of OCD symp-
toms and symptoms that can interfere with the mother–infant relationship in the peri-
partum period. Perinatal OCD has its onse t during pregnancy or within the first 
postpartum year, with the most common time  of within the first 4 weeks following 
delivery (Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). 
Box 20–1. DSM-5 Criteria for Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder 
A. Presence of obsessions, compulsions, or both: 
Obsessions are defined by (1) and (2): 
1. Recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges, or images that are experienced, at some 
time during the disturbance, as intrusive and unwanted, and that in most individuals 
cause marked anxiety or distress. 
2. The individual attempts to ignore or suppress such thoughts, urges, or images, or to 
neutralize them with some other thought or action (i.e., by performing a compulsion).

1. Repetitive behaviors (e.g., hand washing, ordering, checking) or mental acts (e.g.,

tress, or preventing some dreaded event or situation; however, these behaviors or

mental acts.

Clinical Presentation 
OCD is characterized by obsessions (intrusive thoughts, ideas, images, or impulses), 
compulsions (repetitive or ritualistic behaviors), or a combination of both. Symptoms 
are time consuming and cause clinically significant distress in important areas of func-
tioning, as well as in family life (Abramowitz et al. 2010b; Albert et al. 2010; Challa -
combe et al. 2016; Gezginç et al. 2008). OCD can present with variable levels of insight 
(American Psychiatric Associat ion 2013), but in postpartum women, insight is typi -
cally preserved (Hudak and Wisner 2012). The symptoms cannot be better explained 
by another medical, substance, or psychiatric condition (American Psychiatric Associ-
ation 2013). OCD with onset at menarche or in the premenstrual period has symptoms 
similar to those of OCD at other time points in a woman’s life or in other populations; 
no specific studies have yet looked at differences in clinical presentation in this context. 
OCD in the peripartum period typically presents with intrusive thoughts and im-
ages, often concerning infant harm (Abram owitz et al. 2003; McGuinness et al. 2011; 
Speisman et al. 2011). Postpartum women with OCD report more aggressive intrusive 
thoughts than nonpostpartum women with OCD (Hudak and Wisner 2012; Wisner 
et al. 1999). Typical symptoms with onset during pregnancy can include contamina -
tion obsessions, with compulsive cleaning and washing rituals that serve to “neutral-
ize” the obsessions (Forray et al. 2010; McGuinness et al. 2011). In the postpartum 
period, obsessions often center around ego-dystonic thoughts of infant harm and are 
accompanied by avoidance and checking compulsions. Thoughts of infant harm can 
include both thoughts of inadvertent harm to the infant and intrusive thoughts of de-
liberate harm to the infant (Hudak and Wi sner 2012; Sichel et al. 1993b). Obsessions 
can present as graphic visual images that cause extreme distress in the mother. Con-
