Summary 
Infertility is a common problem in women, and mental health professionals serve in 
a wide variety of roles in the evaluation and treatment of psychiatric conditions in this 
population. They perform indi vidual psychotherapy in the private practice setting 
and take on consultation-liaison and embedd ed mental health roles within fertility 
clinics. Mental health professionals need to be aware of the unique relationships be -
tween common causes of infertility in wo men and comorbid psychiatric conditions. 
Unique aspects of these conditions need to be integrated into psychological and psy-
chiatric treatment approaches. Mental health professionals also need to be aware of 
the potential for fertility medications to destabilize mood in women. Future research 
should focus on the identification of patients at greatest risk for this mood destabili-
zation, as well as the adaptation and development of evidence-based psychotherapy 
and psychopharmacology treatments for mood and anxiety disorders in this popula-
tion. Psychopharmacology research should continue the investigation of effects of 
psychiatric medications on the menstrual cycle; fertility treatments, including egg and 
sperm quality; implantation physiology; and pregnancy outcomes. 
Perinatal Loss 
Perinatal loss is a common and distressing experience for many women, and it can be 
associated with significant psychological and psychiatric sequelae. The goals of this 
section are to review the epidemiology, clinical features, mental health impacts, and 
treatment of perinatal loss.

Definitions 
Perinatal loss is defined as the nonvoluntary end of pregnancy or death of an infant 
from the time of conception until postpartum day 28 (Table 5–3) (Bhat and Byatt 2016). 
In the United States, spontaneous abortion, also termed miscarriage, is defined as preg-
nancy loss prior to 20 weeks’ gestation. Intrauterine fetal demise (IUFD), or stillbirth, is 
defined as loss after 20 weeks’ gestation or after the fetus reaches 14 oz in weight. Early 
neonatal death is defined as death of an infa nt prior to 7 days of life, and late neonatal 
death is defined as death between 7 and 28 days of life (Barfield 2011). Termination of 
pregnancy for fetal abnormalities or genetic indications is included in the definition 
of perinatal loss, whereas termination for elective reasons is not. Recurrent perinatal 
loss (RPL) is defined by the ASRM as two or more losses, and it can be further subdi-
vided into primary RPL, defined as pregnancy loss in women who have never had a 
viable pregnancy, and secondary RPL in women who have had a previous live birth 
(Barfield 2011; Bhat and Byatt 2016). 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Approximately 12%–20% of pr egnancies end in loss prior to 20 weeks’ gestation 
(Hemels et al. 2005; Hemminki and Forssa s 1999; Robinson 2014; Wilcox et al. 1988). 
Maternal age is a determinant for overall incidence of different types of perinatal loss. 
For instance, 27% of women ages 25–29 ex perience spontaneous abortion, compared 
with 75% of women ages 45 and older (Rob inson 2014). Many early first-trimester 
losses are never recognized as a pregnancy, and thus spontaneous abortion may occur 
in up to one-third of all pregnancies (Wilcox et al. 1988). Approximately 50%–80% of 
all women who experience perinatal loss will have a subsequent pregnancy. Recurrent 
perinatal loss occurs in 2%–3% of pregnancies (Bhat and Byatt 2016; Kolte et al. 2014). 
In contrast, IUFD after 20 weeks’ gestation is rare, affecting an estimated 4.6 per 1,000 
births, whereas neonatal death occurs in approximately 6.2 per 1,000 births (Bhat and 
Byatt 2016). The risk of IUFD is highes t in teenagers (0.07% –0.13%) and in women 
older than 40 years (0.11%–0.21%) (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecolo-
gists and Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine 2020).

Although research on racial disparities in spontaneous abortion is surprisingly scarce, 
some studies indicate higher rates of loss among Black women compared with white 
women (Price 2006). Because lower socioeconomic status, lower education, and income 
below poverty level are also risk factors for recurrent loss, this racial difference may be 
due in part to socioeconomic differences. Non-Hispanic Black women have the highest 
risks for IUFD and early infant death as compared with other populations (MacDor-
man and Gregory 2015; Muglu et al. 2019; Mukherjee et al. 2013). Some of this differ-
ence is likely attributable to the higher rates of preterm birth and pregnancy morbidities 
in the Black population, while some of it may also represent disparity in access to qual-
ity health care (Martin et al. 2019). 
Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
Miscarriage typically presents as acute vaginal bleed ing and pain. The loss is con-
firmed by ultrasound (Robinson 2014). Later in pregnancy, pregnancy loss may pres-
ent with decreased or absent fetal movement and may be identified by ultrasound at 
a routine prenatal visit, or it may occur despite management of conditions that are 
known to be related to late pregnancy loss, such as incompetent cervix or maternal in-
fection (Michels and Tiu 2007). 
Pathophysiology 
The most common etiologies of perinatal loss include chromosomal abnormalities, fe-
tal anomalies, death of a fetus during labor and delivery, preterm labor, hypertensive 
disorders in the mother, umbilical cord ab normalities, infection, and other medical 
conditions (Murphy et al. 2017). However, no clear cause is identified in up to 50% of 
cases (Wilcox et al. 1988).

Medical Management of Perinatal Loss 
The management of perinatal loss varies based on timing. Management of early preg-
nancy loss is based on a variety of factors, including the length of gestation, and medical 
or surgical intervention may be indicated based on the clinical presentation (Jurkovic 
et al. 1998; Nielsen and Hahlin 1995). Medical management—with mifepristone (an an-
tiglucocorticoid medication administered intravaginally or orally), misoprostol (a syn-
thetic prostaglandin analogue), or both—is used to shor ten the time for expulsion of 
pregnancy tissue in women without current infections or bleeding disorders (Ameri-
can College of Obstetrician s and Gynecologists 2018; Henshaw et al. 1993). Surgical 
evacuation of uterine contents is needed in the case of an incomplete miscarriage 
(Jurkovic et al. 1998), as well as molar pregnancy (Elias et al. 2019). 
Impact of Mental Health on Perinatal Loss 
Although studies have not conclusively demo nstrated that MDD, anxiety, or other 
mental illnesses are risk factors for spontaneous abortion or perinatal loss, a bidirec -
tional relationship may exist between psychiatric disorders and perinatal loss. Women 
with a history of depressive or anxiety disorders may have an increased risk of peri -
natal loss, and perinatal loss clearly increases the risk for mood and anxiety disorders 
(Farren et al. 2018; Gold et al. 2007; Klock et al. 1997). The mechanism of action be -
tween mental illness and pregnancy loss is unclear. One hypothesis involves the im -
pact of mental illness, specifically MDD, on the functioning of the HPA axis and the 
sympathetic nervous system. The release of maternal glucocorticoids with stress re -
sponse can promote placental prostaglandins, increase the effect of oxytocin on the 
uterus, and upregulate proinflammatory cytokines, which may be associated with the 
pathophysiology of pregnancy loss (Li et al. 2012). 
Although some meta-analyses and systematic reviews report an increase in sponta-
neous abortions in women taking SSRI anti depressants in pregnancy (Hemels et al. 
2005), others have not shown a significant association (Ross et al. 2013). Studies have 
generally not adequately controlled for important confounding variables, such as ma-
ternal age, BMI, thyroid disorders, or pr evious history of spontaneous abortion, nor 
have most been able to separate the role of MDD or depression severity in early preg-
nancy loss (Hemels et al. 2005). In a large population-based cohort study, SSRIs were 
not associated with an increased risk of st illbirth, neonatal death, or postnatal infant 
death (Stephansson et al. 2013). 
Impact of Perinatal Loss on Mental Health 
Many women and their partners experience the period immediately following a peri-
natal loss as an emotionally difficult time that includes a range of depressive, anxiety, 
and stress-related symptoms. Researchers have different perspectives regarding the 
assessment of severity and course of these symptoms, with some viewing the symp-
toms as normal emotional reactions and ot hers characterizing symptoms within a 
framework of psychiatric illness once symp toms pass a certain threshold or disrupt 
functioning (Gold et al. 2016). The risk of psychiatric illness is elevated in the first year 
following loss, particularly after IUFD (Lewkowitz et al. 2019).

Grief 
Epidemiology. Grief reactions are common after perinatal loss (Cassaday 2018). The 
incidence is difficult to assess because studies use different psychological measures and 
definitions of grief and investigate different types and timing of perinatal loss and differ-
ent time periods since the loss. When grief is prolonged and so intense that it is disabling, 
it has historically been termed “complicated grief.” It is estimated that complicated grief 
occurs in up to 30% of patients after perinatal loss (Kersting and Wagner 2012). 
Risk factors. Risk factors for the development of complicated grief after perinatal 
loss include a history of a prior mental illness, poor social support, ambivalent attitude 
toward the pregnancy, and a preloss neurotic personality, and it is more likely to hap-
pen in women experiencing sudden or traumatic loss (Badenhorst and Hughes 2007; 
Kersting and Wagner 2012; Lasker and Toed ter 1991). In contrast , studies have not 
shown a correlation between the severity of grief and greater maternal age, sex of the 
baby, socioeconomic status, or religious observance (Badenhorst and Hughes 2007). 
Some studies have shown the presence of other children to be a protective factor 
(deMontigny et al. 2017; Janssen et al.1997; Kersting and Wagner 2012). The quality of 
the relationship between partners and th e concordance between their grieving pro -
cesses can be protective factors (Badenhorst and Hughes 2007; Kersting and Wagner 
2012; Scheidt et al. 2012). Lack of percei ved social support, including dissatisfaction 
with the health care system during the pregnancy and after the loss, has also been as-
sociated with more intense grief and longer duration of grief (deMontigny et al. 2017; 
Kersting and Wagner 2012). 
Screening. The most common screening instrument for grief is the Perinatal Grief 
Scale (Toedter et al. 1988). This 33-item scale has high convergent validity and reliabil-
ity, has been translated into multiple languages, and is used worldwide. 
Diagnostic criteria. Although there are no formal diagnostic criteria for perinatal 
grief, there are recognized descriptions and definitions of the normative experience of 
grief in general (Shear 2012). Similar to grief at other times in life, perinatal grief is a 
psychobiological reaction to a loss (Brier 2008; Shear 2012). It is the loss of a developing 
infant and the expectation, hope, and vision of that child and thus is considered a pro-
spective loss (Leon 2018). Perinatal grief is considered an expected and normative pro-
cess associated with time-limited sadne ss and yearning for and preoccupation with 
thoughts of the lost baby (Rowland and Goodnight 2009). When grief is unusually in-
tense, more disruptive, or lasts longer than the timeline anticipated, it can evolve into 
complicated grief. Complicated grief is described in DSM-5 as persistent complex be-
reavement disorder, which is included in the section “Conditions for Further Study” 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
Clinical features. Grief reactions can include disruption in daily functioning, feelings 
of yearning, numbness, isolation, retreat from usual activities, and intrusive thoughts 
(Kersting and Wagner 2012). In perinatal grief, parents can have feelings of guilt, 
shame, inadequacy, failure, sadness, irritability, and anger (Kersting and Wagner 2012).

1 month to 2 years (Badenhorst and Hughes 2007) but typically declines by 6 months 
following the loss (Bhat and Byatt 2016). For many parents, however, grief can last for

Pathophysiology. To date, no studies have been published investigating the neu -
robiology of perinatal grief. From the few extant studies regarding grief in patients 
responding to other forms of attachment loss, such as loss of a marriage partner, it ap-
pears that when compared with patients who are adapting well, patients with com -
plicated grief demonstrate greater activity in the nucleus accumbens (O’Connor et al. 
2008). This increased activity is associated with greater self-reported yearning as well, 
suggesting that the experience of grief-related yearning is associated with a specific 
area of the basal ganglia (O’Connor et al. 2008). Grief associated with other forms of 
loss is also associated with immunological changes, including changes to proinflam -
matory interleukin-6, which may play a role in the mood changes seen in complicated 
grief because proinflammatory cytokines ha ve been found to be elevated in MDD 
(Cohen et al. 2015; Knowles et al. 2019). 
Throughout pregnancy, parents often deve lop future ideas about their child and 
the child’s characteristics and about themselves as parents. These mental representa-
tions are influenced by many factors, including personality structure, past experi -
ences of parenting, past trauma, and cultural differences (Stern 1995). These mental 
representations are further elaborated through medical technology, which has made 
it possible for parents to visualize the fetus at various points during pregnancy. These 
experiences contribute to increasing emotional attachment to the fetus, starting early 
in the prenatal period. Research has shown that prenatal parental representations and 
attachment to the infant are associated with postpartum parent–infant attachment 
(Dubber et al. 2015; Petri et al. 2018). Perinatal loss is a major disruption in these men-
tal representations, leaving parents with immense grief for the relationship they had 
dreamed about but did not get to experience (Leon 2018). 
A primary factor that affects the course and trajectory of grief is how the person makes 
meaning out of the loss. The process starts with a set of preexisting beliefs about oneself, 
the world, and the future; the death of the child shatters or alters these beliefs in significant 
ways (Keesee et al. 2008). When a discrepancy exists between the meaning of loss and 
one’s beliefs, one starts a process of adaptation to make sense of the experience; if one is 
unable to assimilate the loss successfully, one may experience more intense grief (Keesee 
et al. 2008). Schema, or core beliefs about the world, comprise three major components: 
benevolence, meaningfulness of the world, and worthiness of the self (Krosch and Shake-
speare-Finch 2017). Perinatal loss can alter or shake any of these components. Parents 
grieving a perinatal loss have reported themes of unfairness, guilt, and loss of control

Depressive Disorders 
Epidemiology. Following a perinatal loss, 10%–54% of women report depressive 
symptoms severe enough to meet criteria for MDD within 6 months of the loss (deMon-
tigny et al. 2017; Farren et al. 2018). Up to 20% of women have symptoms that are of 
moderate or greater severity at 6 weeks postloss (Farren et al. 2018). 
Risk factors. Women with recurrent miscarriage ha ve an especially elevated risk 
for MDD, with prevalence rates of 30% (Lok and Neugebauer 2007). Women with a 
previous history of MDD are at especially increased risk for recurrence after a perina-
tal loss, with prevalence rates of 54% (Neugebauer et al. 1997). Other risk factors for 
MDD following a perinatal loss include younger age, history of infertility and infer -
tility treatments, history of trauma and ab use, history of PTSD, being single, marital 
discord, feeling of lack of control over one’s life, or feeling responsible for the loss (Ta-
ble 5–5) (Bhat and Byatt 2016; Farren et al. 2018). The highest risk of MDD following 
pregnancy loss is for IUFD or pregnancy loss after 20 weeks’ gestation (Bhat and Byatt 
2016; Hogue et al. 2015).

with validated scales used to screen for de pression at other times in a woman’s life.

Studies–Depression Scale (Radloff 1977), Beck Depression Inventory (Beck et al. 1996),
