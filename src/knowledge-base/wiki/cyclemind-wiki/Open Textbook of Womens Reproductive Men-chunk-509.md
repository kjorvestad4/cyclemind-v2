Many women may feel ashamed or have other reasons to deny IPV. If clinicians are 
concerned about the potential presence of IPV , verbalizing these concerns to the pa -
tient may provide a path for future disclo sure. Potential IPV victims should be told 
that the clinician is always available to ta lk and that they may call or make another 
appointment at any time. Multiple inquirie s about any instances of IPV should con-
tinue across visits. Creating the habit of asking patients on multiple occasions may 
lead to disclosure with change in circumstances and comfort. 
Barriers to asking about IPV often include issues of lack of knowledge and concerns 
about being intrusive or offensive. Physicians and other providers cite lack of time and 
resources for screening during routine office visits (Sprague et al. 2012) and may not 
feel confident in knowing how to address a positive screening (Table 18–6). These bar-
riers may be decreased through education and training modules in IPV for medical stu-
dents, residents, social workers, therapists, and other mental health and physical health 
care providers. Physicians and other providers should be trained not to invite the part-
ner into the room during the examination, to make sure patients are fully clothed when 
discussing IPV , and to assure patients that what they say is confidential (in accordance 
with state laws). Physicians and other providers should develop strong therapeutic al-
liances with their patients and ensure a safe environment when asking about IPV . Once 
physicians and other providers feel comfortable with basic screening options, proce -
dures, and resources, they may be more likely to screen on a regular basis. 
Reproductive Loss and Traumatic Delivery Screening 
Ms. CC is a 26-year-old gravida 2 para 1 who presents 5 weeks after an emergency ce-
sarean section at 27 weeks’ gestation. She is referred by her obstetrician to a perinatal 
psychiatrist for evaluation of depression. When asked about her delivery, she shares 
that she thought her baby would die and continues to experience high fear and anxiety 
of harm to her baby. She frequently checks her baby to make sure she is breathing, has 
poor sleep due to the development of debilitating nightmares, avoids hospital settings 
and physicians, and has frequent thoughts th at the worst scenario will occur. She be-
comes tearful and shares that she is having difficulty bonding with her baby. She also 
reports that she has experienced this severity of fear twice in the past, once after a preg-
nancy loss and again after leaving a physically abusive relationship.
