Compulsions in this population typically take the form of contamination-related 
rituals, such as cleaning or sterilizing baby-related equipment, or of checking and or-
dering rituals, such as repetitively checking on a sleeping infant (Hudak and Wisner 
2012; Speisman et al. 2011). Checking comp ulsions are the most frequently reported 
symptom in postpartum women with OCD (Hudak and Wisner 2012). Women fre -
quently check to ensure that they have not harmed the infant (in the case of intrusive 
thoughts of infant harm), to ensure the infant is still breathing, or to ensure that no 
harm has befallen the infant. At times, the obsessions can be so extreme as to lead to 
avoidance of the infant or of common situat ions or activities that provoke intrusive 
thoughts, as opposed to true ritualistic behavior (Hudak and Wisner 2012; Koran et 
al. 2007). Women may avoid such activities as bathing or diapering, avoid objects 
such as kitchen knives and stairs, or limit contact with the infant in general. 
Although acting on urges to harm an infant in perinatal OCD is rare, indirect harm 
is possible; for example, the patient may avoid or neglect the child, “clean” the child 
inappropriately with bleach or other harmfu l substances, or insist on inappropriate 
neatness (Koran et al. 2007). Rarely, patients can become aggressive when others inter-
fere with their compulsions. In such cases, or any other cases in which the clinician has 
significant safety concerns, it may be necessary to involve social agencies. Regardless 
of imminent involvement, a thorough discussion about the role of social agencies is im-
portant given the degree to which patients fear legal action and avoid help as a result. 
The symptoms and severity of perinata l-onset OCD are time-consuming and dis -
tressing. They can often detract from mother–infant bonding and can lead to long-term 
impairments in attachment patterns (Arnold 1999; Challacombe and Salkovskis 2009). 
Women with worsening OCD symptoms in the premenstrual or postpartum period 
frequently have comorbid mood symptoms (Uguz et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 1999) and 
more frequently report a history of premenstrual worsening of mood symptoms, ma-
jor depressive disorder, or postpartum depression (PPD) (Labad et al. 2005). 
Intrusive thoughts are not specific to perinatal OCD; they have also been reported 
under normal circumstances and in other perinatal mental health conditions, most 
specifically PPD (Abramowitz et al. 2003, 2010b; McGuinness et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 
1999). Many reports have suggested that such thoughts are common in new parents 
and do not represent psychopathology when they are experienced as fleeting and mo-
mentary. These symptoms can include subt hreshold intrusive, distressing, and un -
