Other Perimenopausal Symptoms 
Sleep Disturbance 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Sleep disturbance, the second most common perimenopausal complaint after VMS, is 
often related to but not necessarily the direct result of VMS. Sleep disturbance typically 
consists of both trouble falling asleep and multiple awakenings (Kravitz et al. 2003). 
The percentage of women experiencing sleep disturbances increases from around 30% 
in premenopause (already a significant pr oportion of women) to 39%–60% in peri -
menopause and postmenopause, with the prevalence of sleep problems at its highest 
during the late perimenopausal transition (Kravitz et al. 2003, 2008; National Insti -
tutes of Health 2005). Sleep disturbance can be triggered or exacerbated by hot flashes, 
and hot flashes are not helped by sleep disturbance; research has shown that their re-
lationship is complex (Baker et al. 2018; Kravitz et al. 2008). One study that objectively 
measured both hot flashes and night awak enings using skin conductance and actig -
raphy found that 78% of objectively measur ed hot flashes were associated with an 
awakening but that only 36% of awakenings were associated with a hot flash (Thur -
ston et al. 2019). 
Risk factors for sleep disturbance duri ng perimenopause include VMS, surgical 
menopause (Kravitz et al. 2003), and breast cancer treatments, particularly chemother-
apy but also radiotherapy and hormonal therapy in some studies (Costa et al. 2014). 
One study found that both surgical menopause and bilateral oophorectomy after natu-
ral menopause were associated with a higher risk of developing obstructive sleep apnea 
compared with natural menopause (Huang et al. 2018). Finally , one large study found

Further Screening 
The PSQI, although not specific to perime nopause, can help identify the timing of 
sleep problems, their severity, and contributing factors (Carpenter and Andrykowski 
1998). Given the greater risk for obstructiv e sleep apnea with ag ing and higher BMI 
(Perger et al. 2019), the increase in BMI commonly observed at midlife and with meno-
pause (Montazeri et al. 2019), and the greater risk for obstructive sleep apnea among 
women with severe VMS (Gao et al. 2018) , all midlife women with sleep complaints 
should be screened for symptoms of obstructive sleep apnea, with a low threshold for 
referring patients to sleep medicine specialists for full evaluation if indicated. 
Course 
If due to perimenopause, symptoms such as sleep disturbance are generally most se-
vere during the perimenopausal transiti on and tend to improve after menopause 
(Santoro 2016). 
Pharmacological Treatment 
Sleep problems in perimenopause may be attributable to either VMS, perimenopausal 
depression, or both and may resolve with effective treatment of either. However, other 
sleep problems may not resolve, and other causes should be investigated. Evidence 
suggests that both sleep-di sordered breathing (includi ng obstructive sleep apnea) 
and restless legs syndrome may have an increased incidence in perimenopause and 
are important to diagnose and treat (Baker et al. 2018). 
One small, industry-funded RCT of both perimenopausal and postmenopausal 
women with sleep disturbance but without clinical depression found that eszopiclone 
increased total sleep time and improved other measures of insomnia in this popula -
tion (Joffe et al. 2010). 
Given the evidence that endogenous mela tonin decreases with age, particularly 
during perimenopause (Baker et al. 2018; Stute et al. 2020), an ongoing area of research 
is whether melatonin supplementation ca n help with perime nopausal symptoms. 
One small RCT found improvemen t in quality-of-life scores in the physical domain, 
including feeling worn out and tired, as well as in subjective reports of improved sleep 
in perimenopausal women randomized to melatonin 3 mg at bedtime (Kotlarczyk et 
al. 2012). 
Alternative Treatment 
CBT for insomnia (CBT-I) has compelling ev idence for efficacy in the population at 
large (see, e.g., Mitchell et al. 2012). In a study of 106 perimenopausal and postmeno-
pausal women also experiencing VMS, CBT-I was found to reduce insomnia (but not 
VMS); improvement in symptoms was maintained at 6 months (McCurry et al. 2016). 
Sexual Problems 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Sexual problems, including vaginal dryness and atrophy, which can lead to new-onset 
dyspareunia (pain with intercourse), are also commonly present in perimenopause.

There is evidence to suggest that overall sexual functioning declines around the time 
of the menopausal transition, with women experiencing increased pain and decreased 
sexual interest, arousal, ability to achieve orgasm, and emotional satisfaction. In one 
study, the steepest rate of decline in sexual functioning began around 20 months before 
the last menstrual period and continued for 1 year after the final menstrual period 
(Avis et al. 2017). Factors associated with th e decline in sexual function in this study 
included VMS, vaginal dryness, and depression or anxiety. Women undergoing a hys-
terectomy before their final menstrual period also had a decline in sexual functioning 
after their surgery (Avis et al. 2017). 
Vaginal dryness deserves particular atte ntion because it is a highly prevalent 
symptom that women often fail to report to physicians. This symptom increases with 
advancing menopausal stages. In a large longitudinal study, 19% of women ages 42–53 
reported vaginal dryness at baseline, and 34% of the same group reported the symptom 
at 17-year follow-up. Risk factors associat ed with vaginal dryness in this study in -
cluded age since menopause, surgically induced menopause, anxiety, and being mar-
ried (Waetjen et al. 2018). Vaginal dryness can be both the cause of dyspareunia and 
exacerbated by pain during intercourse. Among women who reported experiencing 
vaginal dryness in the prior 2 weeks in the study by Waetjen et al. (2018), 40% reported 
having pain sometimes or almost always with sexual intercourse, compared with 16% 
of women who reported no vaginal dryness in the prior 2 weeks. Both vaginal dryness 
and dyspareunia are underdiagnosed and therefore undertreated, although they can be 
improved by over-the-counter lubricants (Bygdeman and Swahn 1996), topical hor -
mones, and MHT. 
Hormonal Treatment 
If sexual problems are due to perimenopausal symptoms, vaginal estrogen cream or 
gel can be effective for dyspareunia and vaginal dryness (but not for hot flashes, 
which require systemic administration as described in the earlier section on treatment 
of VMS). Of note, evidence has shown that nonhormonal over-the-counter lubricants 
can be effective for the treatment of dyspareunia and vaginal dryness, and given their 
lower risk, they can be considered first-line treatment for women presenting with these 
symptoms alone or in women with contraindications to estrogen (Bygdeman and 
Swahn 1996). Women and their partners often have to try multiple lubricants to deter-
mine which is best for them. Water-based lubricants may require more frequent reap-
plication but are less irritating to sensitive skin. 
Cognitive Complaints 
Rates and Risk Factors 
Complaints of cognitive symptoms during the menopausal transition are common, but 
symptoms that may be attributed to perimenopause are typically subtle, have gradual 
onset, and are challenging to distinguish from the normal symptoms of aging. Abrupt 
and serious cognitive decline should always be investigated to rule out other causes. 
Typical findings of cognitive changes due to perimenopause are of self-reported prob-
lems with verbal memory, such as remembering words or names (reported by 72% of 
women in SWAN), or with forgetting things (reported by 50%) (Greendale et al. 2010; 
Woods and Mitchell 2016).

When objective measures of cognitive performance are used, identifiable changes as-
sociated with perimenopause are typically subtle and may be transient. The first report 
that menopause stage—independent of age at the time of testing, baseline cognitive per-
formance, race, and BMI—was associated with  significant declines in immediate and 
delayed verbal recall came from POAS (Epperson et al. 2013). Although declines oc -
curred across the transition, they became significant for immediate recall at the post-
menopause stage ( P=0.04) and for delayed recall in the early transition ( P=0.002), 
when women reported having had one skipped period in the previous year. 
One study examining processing speed, which is expected to improve over the 
course of repeated testing with the same in strument as subjects learn, found this ex -
pected improvement in women who were in premenopause, early perimenopause, or 
postmenopause but not in women at late perimenopause (Greendale et al. 2009). An-
other study, by Kuh et al. (2018), found that women who went through natural meno-
pause at a later age had higher verbal me mory scores but no changes in processing 
speed compared with women who completed menopause earlier. Although the au -
thors hypothesized that hormone therapy could boost cognitive function in women 
going through the menopausal transition, they found no significant benefit for those 
taking MHT on either verbal memory or processing speed except for those who had 
menopause due to bilateral oophorectomy, who did show some benefit. 
Not all cognitive domains are likely to be as negatively affected by the loss of estra-
diol. Given the impact of estradiol on neuronal morphology, receptors for various neu-
rotransmitters, and neurochemistry in the prefrontal cortex, it is not surprising that 
many menopausal women complain of difficulties within the executive function do-
mains of working memory, sustained attention, processing speed, and motivation for 
work (Epperson et al. 2015; Shanmugan and Epperson 2014; Shanmugan et al. 2020). 
Stimulant treatment may be beneficial for women with significant executive function 
complaints, but the potential adverse effects on blood pressure and heart rate should 
be considered, as well as the addictive potential of stimulant medications (Epperson 
et al. 2015). 
Risk factors for self-reported memory problems during pe rimenopause included 
higher perceived levels of stress and depressed mood (Woods et al. 2000). 
Further Screening 
The MoCA (Nasreddine et al. 2005) has high  sensitivity and specificity for detecting 
mild cognitive impairment. If this is id entified, causes other than perimenopause 
should be investigated. Women should be queried regarding undiagnosed ADHD if 
they present with significant symptoms of executive dysfunction. A recent study sug-
gests that childhood adversity and current depression and anxiety can increase risk 
for executive function complaints among women who have undergone risk-reducing 
salpingo-oophorectomy due to having had breast or ovarian cancer or being at genetic 
risk for such cancers (Shanmugan et al. 2020). Tools such as the Brown Attention-
Deficit Disorder Scale (Brown 1996; Sandra Kooij et al. 2008), Adult ADHD Self-Report 
Scale (Ustun et al. 2017), and Conners’ Adult ADHD Rating Scale–Long Version (Con-
ners et al. 1999; Sandra Kooij et al. 2008) ca n be used to assess severity of executive 
function difficulties both prior to and following stimulant treatment to assess the rel-
ative benefits and weigh these against the potential risks of long-term use.

Additional Strategies for Changes in Sleep, 
Sexual Function, and Cognition 
As discussed, successful treatment of mood disorders or VMS may result in improve-
ments in sleep, sexual functioning, and cognition. However, for changes that do not 
improve with successful treatment of anxiety, depression, or VMS or with the specific 
treatments described earlier in this chapter, further investigation, education, and re -
ferral may be warranted. Further specialized treatment options are beyond the scope 
of this chapter and vary depending on the cause of the problem. Thus, the first step 
is to further characterize the problem using a scale, a diary, or other measurement sys-
tem. Next, serious and treatable causes of the symptom should be explored and ruled 
out if possible so as to provide both reassurance and relief. Finally, the patient should
