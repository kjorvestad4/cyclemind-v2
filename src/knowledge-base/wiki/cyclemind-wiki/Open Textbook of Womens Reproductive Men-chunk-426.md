The effect of BD and associated treatments on the menstrual cycle is also a develop-
ing area of research (Reynolds-May et al . 2014; Teatero et al. 2014). Women with BD 
are more likely than healthy control subjects to retrospectively report menstrual abnor-
malities early in the reproductive years that predate the onset of BD (Joffe et al. 2006; 
Rasgon et al. 2000). Medications used to treat BD also may adversely affect the hypo-
thalamic-pituitary-gonadal (HPG) axis, resulting in menstrual and other hormonal ab-
normalities (Kenna et al. 2009). Valproate has been the mood stabilizer most strongly 
linked to HPG dysfunction. A study by Joffe et al. (2006) showed that 10% of women 
with BD treated with valproate developed oligomenorrhea with hyperandrogenism. 
It is important therefore to monitor women with BD for menstrual and hormonal ab-
normalities. Encourage patients to record monthly onset of their cycles (particularly in 
the first several months after starting a new pharmacological agent), which will help 
them detect more subtle abnormalities or changes. Women with BD should also be ed-
ucated about signs of hormonal dysfunction, such as virilization and changes in the 
menstrual cycle (Kenna et al. 2009). All women of reproductive age who are treated 
with valproate must be counseled about the risks of valproate exposure in pregnancy 
(as detailed later) and the need for adequate contraception. 
To date, there are few studies of the treatment of exacerbation of mood symptoms 
in the premenstruum in patients with BD. For women who do not have contraindica-
tions to oral contraception (see Chapter 4, “Contraception”), suppression of the HPG 
axis with an oral contraceptive may be considered. To date, limited existing studies sug-
gest that oral contraceptives are not associated with destabilization of mood in BD (Pa-
gano et al. 2016). Case studies and small observational studies have also reported that 
lamotrigine may be an especially useful mood stabilizer to consider in this subpopula-
tion of women with BD. Women receiving lamotrigine therapy are reported to have 
fewer menstrual cycle mood fluctuations than women receiving other mood stabilizers, 
and lamotrigine and oral contraceptives appear to be an especially useful combination 
(Robakis et al. 2015). When using this combination, be aware that lamotrigine levels are 
likely to decrease during the active oral contraceptive pill weeks because estradiol in-
creases the clearance of lamotrigine via induction of hepatic glucuronidation (Reddy 
2010). Changes in the pharmacokinetics of mood stabilizers across the menstrual cycle 
should also be considered in the differential diagnosis of women with BD with premen-
strual mood exacerbations, although to date studies regarding the influence of the men-
strual cycle on lithium and valproate metabolism are limited (Carmassi et al. 2019). 
Menopause. Most women with BD will experience menopause, yet the clinical dis-
ease course during this transition remain s understudied. In the general population, 
mood symptoms are common but not universal during perimenopause and can include 
irritability, anxiety, depression, lability, and difficulty with concentration and sleep
