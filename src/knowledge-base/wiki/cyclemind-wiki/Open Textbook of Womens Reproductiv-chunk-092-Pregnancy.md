Chapter 13, “Depressive Disorders.” 
Psychosocial Interventions 
Research into psychosocial interventions for pregnant or postpartum patients with 
primary psychotic disorders remains quite limited. Several psyc hosocial treatments 
show good efficacy for schizophrenia in ge neral and thus may also be useful in the 
perinatal period. Evidence supports the use of cognitive-behavioral therapy for psy -
chosis, cognitive remediation, illness self-management training, social skills training, 
assertive community treatment, family psychoeducation, and supported employment 
(Clark 2016; Mueser et al. 2013; Pharoah et al. 2010). Cook et al. (2014) completed a 
retrospective multisite study of integrated clinical, rehabilitatio n, and parenting ser-
vices for mothers with psychiatric disabilities in general. Their findings suggest that 
comprehensive integrated programming may improve maternal clinical outcomes, re-
duce custody loss, and support family reunification (Cook et al. 2014).

Denial of Pregnancy 
There are three types of pregnancy denial: pervasive, affective, and psychotic (Miller 
2003). In pervasive denial, the woman is not aware that she is pregnant. The physical 
changes of pregnancy, such as weight gain, amenorrhea, and changes in the body, ei-
ther are not present or are misconstrued (Friedman et al. 2007). The woman may even 
misinterpret the pain of labor as being abdominal cramping or a need to defecate. In 
contrast, those with affective denial have an intellectual awareness of pregnancy but 
make little preparation for the delivery, continuing to act as though they are not preg-
nant. Women with substance use disorders, for example, may experience affective de-
nial that may help to defend against guilt from harming the fetus due to substance use.

Delusions of Pregnancy, Simulated Pregnancy, 
and Pseudocyesis 
A woman may present for pregnancy care when she is not actually pregnant for var-
ious reasons, including delusions of pregnancy, a simulated pregnancy, or pseudocye-
sis. Couvade syndrome has also been described among men. Delusions of pregnancy, 
like delusions at other times in life, involve fixed, false beliefs. They will often occur 
in the absence of signs and symptoms of pregnancy. Most often, the woman with delu-
sions of pregnancy has an underlying diagnosis of schizophrenia. However, other psy-
chotic disorders, mood disorders, or dementia may also be primary diagnoses (Bera 
and Sarkar 2015; Uvais 2020). Galactorrhea and amenorrhea are side effects of certain 
antipsychotic medications and may be misa ttributed as signs of pregnancy among 
some women. Delusions of pregnancy may be associated with perceptual experiences 
of quickening or fetal movements or erotomanic delusions (Bera and Sarkar 2015; Ci-
priani and Di Fiorino 2015). 
In a systematic review of case reports of delusions of pregnancy, Bera and Sarkar 
(2015) noted 84 cases in the world literature that were published in English. Approx-
imately half of the patients were between the ages of 20 and 40 years, and one-quarter 
of patients were older than 50 years. Schizophrenia was diagnosed in more than one-
third of the cases, followed in frequency by bipolar disorder and depression. Four-
fifths of the delusional pregnancy patients reported having a single fetus. Almost half 
reported they perceived fetal movement. Approximately two-thirds had a good re -
sponse to treatment (Bera and Sarkar 2015). 
In distinction, a simulated pregnancy occurs when a woman is aware that she is not 
pregnant but presents herself as being pregnant for secondary gain. These secondary 
gains may include getting married or being able to take time away from work or even 
gains within the legal system when she is the defendant (Bass and Halligan 2014; Bera 
and Sarkar 2015).

Finally, pseudocyesis is a condition described since the time of Hippocrates and is cur-
rently included in DSM-5 as an “other specified” somatic symptom disorder. It is de-
fined as a false belief of being pregnant that is associated with objective signs and reported 
symptoms of pregnancy. In pseudocyesis, a patient’s conviction may or may not be swayed 
by laboratory testing, ultrasonographic evidence, or physical examination. Classically, 
the patient has no other signs or symptoms of a psychiatric disorder, and frequent an-
tecedents are pregnancy loss, infertility , isolation, naïveté, and a belief that childbearing 
is a woman’s crucial role. Pseudocyesis is a rare disorder with decreasing incidence; 
some hypothesize that the development of readily available objective methods of con-
firming (or ruling out) pregnancy in the earliest weeks of pregnancy (e.g., home preg-
nancy tests) explains the decrease in prevalence. Although data on its prevalence are 
lacking, pseudocyesis may be relatively more common in developing countries that 
place a high cultural value on childbearing (Brockington 1996; Stotland et al. 2005). 
When examining any woman who presents with a false belief that she is pregnant, 
it is important to remember that organic causes and contributors must be included in 
the differential diagnosis. Secondary amenorrhea can result from dysfunction of the 
hypothalamus, pituitary gland, or ovaries. Hyperprolactinemia from endocrine abnor-
malities or antipsychotic medications can clinically present as galactorrhea. Abdomi-
nal distention may result from constipation, metabolic or endocrine disorders, or liver 
disease. Gestational trophoblastic disease may result in multiple symptoms that mimic 
pregnancy. Astute clinicians will include a thorough history and review of systems in 
the examination of these beliefs, followed by an appropriate physical examination, lab-
oratory testing, and referral to other physicians when indicated (Bera and Sarkar 2015). 
Couvade syndrome also warrants brief mention in this differential. The name comes 
from the French word couver, meaning “to hatch,” and it is also known as a sympathetic 
pregnancy. In this syndrome, the partner of a pregnant woman experiences somatic 
symptoms of pregnancy. Relative to pseudocyesis, the scientific literature on couvade 
syndrome is scarce and limited to case reports, and no mention of couvade syndrome 
appears in DSM-5. In the literature that does exist, couvade syndrome is hypothesized 
to represent a somatic expression of anxiety, identification with the fetus, ambivalence 
about fatherhood, or parturition envy (Piechowski-Jozwiak and Bogousslavsky 2018). 
To date, no case reports or other scientific literature on couvade syndrome among 
same-sex couples were identified in a literature search. 
Ethics and the Treatment of 
Schizophrenia in the Peripartum 
Ethical issues in perinatal psychiatry that merit special consideration when treating 
women with schizophrenia include the importance of being proactive rather than re-
active and the importance of informed consent, enhancing women’s decision-making 
capacities, and relational et hics (Miller 2009). When psychiatrists evaluate and treat 
any female patient of childbearing age, they should consider that the patient may be-
come pregnant at some point in her treatment. Being proactive in discussions about 
her desire for pregnancy, contraceptive options, and plans for treatment during preg-
nancy may allow for a more thoughtful approach than when the potential for preg -
nancy is unanticipated. Clinical experien ce indicates that both psychiatrists and 
patients may make hasty decisions about medications upon recognition of pregnancy,

When treating a pregnant woman with a psychotic disorder, one should discuss 
both the risks of the medication treatment and the risks of not treating the mental ill-
ness. It is critical that psychiatrists not overlook the risks of failing to treat a psychotic 
disorder. The benefits of medication, which the woman has likely experienced in the 
past, also should be discussed. Consideration of obstetrical and fetal risk should be re-
framed in the context of the mother–infant pair because the overall health and well -
being of the mother is in the infant’s best interest. Finally, psychiatrists should enhance 
a woman’s decision-making capacity by describing and helping her understand the 
risks and probabilities of various outcomes , rather than relying on her perceptions, 
which may be influenced by the experiences of friends or what is reported in the media 
(Friedman et al. 2018; Miller 2009). 
Considerations of Involuntary Medications 
When a pregnant or postpartum woman is acutely psychotic and refuses treatment de-
spite a clear need for that treatment, the psychiatrist should evaluate that patient’s ca-
pacity for decision-making about psychiatric treatment. This would include whether 
she can appreciate her diagnosis and the consequences, whether she can understand 
relevant information about treatment, whether she is able rationally to manipulate this 
information, and whether she can communicate a clear choice (Friedman et al. 2018). 
The psychiatrist should also collaborate with obstetrical providers, even in situations in 
which the patient is on an inpatient psychiatric unit and requiring forced medications. 
When completing an evaluation for invo luntary treatment in pregnancy, psychia-
trists should be aware of the laws in their jurisdiction, the patient’s diagnosis, the ra-
tionale for treatment, and the reason behind the lack of capacity. It is critical that they 
collaborate with obstetrical providers and have a clear understanding of rational psy-
chopharmacology in pregnancy. The least restrictive alternatives for appropriate 
treatment should be determined. When describing the issues to the court, they should 
clearly identify the risks of no treatment, because courts may only focus on harm from 
medications rather than on the risks of not treating (Friedman et al. 2018). 
Parenting 
Most women with schizophrenia will have at least one pregnancy over a lifetime, and 
many mothers with schizophrenia parent their own children. Although women with 
schizophrenia are likely to care deeply about their children and their responsibilities 
as mothers, as a group they demonstrate significant parenting challenges (Montgom-
ery 2005). On average, women with schizophrenia display less maternal sensitivity to-
ward their infants and are more likely to have infants or toddlers with insecure or 
disorganized attachment patterns (Wan et al. 2008a) than are healthy control subjects. 
Multiple factors related to core symptoms of the illness may affect emotional respon-
siveness, cognition, and ability to interpret accurately the motivations of others. 
Consistent with the negative symptom cluster, mothers with schizophrenia are 
more likely than healthy control subjects to show interactional deficits when with their 
infants, to display an impaired ability for “mentalization” (or the capacity to appreciate 
internal states of others) (Brüne et al. 2007), show a more passive or withdrawn par -

should take so that she can have a healthy baby. 
When considering the care of women with schizophrenia during and after preg -
nancy, it is important to appreciate that temporary or permanent custody loss is com-
mon in this population. Overall, approxim ately half of women with schizophrenia 
will experience some period  of custody loss (Seeman 2012) , particularly in times of 
illness exacerbation. In a study of women admitted to a British inpatient psychiatric 
mother–baby unit, women with schizophrenia were at highest risk for discharge with 
some degree of formal child protective services supervision, with more than half of 
women with this diagnosis (OR 5.16) referred prior to discharge. In this study, moth-
ers with supportive partnerships and mentally well partners had more positive par-
enting outcomes (Howard et al. 2003). 
Support for parenting is an essential co mponent of a treatment plan for mothers 
with schizophrenia. Because supportive relationships are associated with positive par-
enting outcomes for these mothers, a focus on building family and other supports is 
recommended (Abel et al. 2005). Early childhood intervention programs may offer ser-
vices to high-risk mothers, such as developmental monitoring of the infant and parent 
coaching. Evidence-based interventions to support parenting among women with 
schizophrenia are a critical need. In a review of mother–child relational interventions, 
approximately nine studies were identified that included mothers with a mental disor-
der, none of which included women with psychotic disorders specifically (Wan et al.
