A prospective Dutch cohort study showed three distinct subgroups of patients with 
PPP , with approximately 41% classified as depressed, 34% classified as manic, and 25% 
classified as “atypical” (Kamperman et al. 2017). Dysphoric mania was more common 
than euphoria, and all groups had high levels of anxiety and agitation. Only the atypical 
group demonstrated the classic delirium-like symptoms of disturbance of conscious-
ness, disorientation, or derealization. Visual hallucinations and delusions of childbirth 
were much more common than other psychotic symptoms, such as thought insertion, 
broadcasting, withdrawal, or auditory hallucinations with running commentary. 
Suicidal ideation and homicidal thoughts are common in PPP , with rates ranging 
from 8% to 50% (Chandra et al. 2002; Kamp erman et al. 2017). Up to 4% of mothers 
with untreated psychosis in the postpartum period commit infanticide (Brockington 
2017). Overall, most maternal  filicides (child murder by mothers) are categorized as 
altruistic and associated with the belief that death would be in the child’s best interest 
or as acutely psychotic with no comprehensible motive. Up to 29% of maternal fili -
cides end in completed suicide by the mother as well (Hatters-Friedman and Resnick 
2007). Given these high risks for suicide and infanticide, it is critical to assess directly 
for suicidal ideation, plans for the children if the mother completed suicide, thoughts 
or fears of harming the infant, altruistic homicidal ideation, and delusions that are fo-
cused on the infant (Bergink et al. 2016; Chandra et al. 2006; Hatters-Friedman and 
Resnick 2007). 
Course/Prognosis 
Even with early treatment, symptoms may persist for up to 1 year postpartum in one-
quarter of women, especially if a relapse o ccurs or an episode of depression follows 
resolution of acute psychosis (Blackmore et al. 2013; Burgerhout et al. 2017). Up to 20% 
of marriages will end after an initial epis ode (Blackmore et al. 2013). However, most 
women will have functional improvements in work, interpersonal relations, and recre-
ation and will resume premorbid employment and household responsibilities within 9 
months (Burgerhout et al. 2017). Among women who experience an incipient PPP ep-

Differential Diagnosis 
The differential diagnosis of PPP is broad, especially given the potential overlap with 
medical complications in th e immediate postpartum peri od. In addition, PPP may 
have overlap with symptoms of delirium, such as a waxing and waning course and 
acute confusion; this makes it vital to rule out medical causes of altered mental status. 
Neuropsychiatric syndromes, such as auto immune encephalitis, should be consid -
ered, particularly when accompanied by neurological symptoms. See Tables 16–3 and 
16–4 for a more thorough differential diagnosis and recommended laboratory and ra-
diological investigation in the setting of PPP .

ity, weepiness, or anxiety that may be di stressing but does not significantly impair 
function. Perinatal depression is characterized by a major depressive episode with on-
