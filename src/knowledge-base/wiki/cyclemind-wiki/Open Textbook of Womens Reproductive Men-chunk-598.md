The extant literature on the pharmacotherapy of OCD in perinatal populations in-
cludes only open-label studies (Sharma an d Sommerdyk 2015). In one case series, 
two-thirds of patients with postpartum OCD treated with fluvoxamine showed a pos-
itive response, defined as 30% or greater decrease in total Y-BOCS score (Arnold 1999). 
In another case series, 15 patients with postpartum OCD treated with fluoxetine, clo-
mipramine, desipramine, or a combination of these drugs showed significant respon-
siveness to SSRIs following 12 weeks of treatment, with improvement maintained 
with continued treatment over a 1-year period (Sichel et al. 1993a). In another case se-
ries, 64.7% (11/17) of patients with treatment-resistant postpartum OCD treated with 
quetiapine as augmentation for SSRIs (par oxetine, fluoxetine, sertraline) and SNRIs 
(venlafaxine) showed responsiveness to this regimen, with a mean dose of 112.5 mg/ 
day and a mean reduction of 59.6% in Y-BOCS scores (Misri et al. 2004). In a case series 
of four women with postpartum OCD, symptoms improved with fluoxetine (Buttolph 
and Holland 1990). 
In general populations, successful medica tion treatment should be continued for 
1–2 years before being gradually tapered by 10%–25% every 1–2 months while ob -
serving for symptom return or exacerbation (Koran et al. 2007). This is also recom -
mended for perinatal cases, although ch anges to the regimen may be indicated in 
certain contexts, such as breastfeeding. In general, for breastfeeding patients who are 
taking SSRIs, the relatively benign side effect profile of SSRIs in lactation (including al-
most negligible secretion into breastmilk in some cases, such as sertraline) favors the 
use of pharmacological approaches. 
Nonpharmacological Somatic Treatments 
Somatic therapies used in the treatment of adult OCD include deep brain stimulation 
and other forms of neurosurgery, transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS), and elec-
troconvulsive therapy (ECT). None of these therapies is considered a first-line treat -
ment for OCD because most of the evidence for them exists in the form of case reports/ 
series and unblinded studies (Greenberg et al. 2003, 2005; Jenike et al. 1991). The risk 
for significant morbidity associated with neurosurgical techniques renders them indi-
cated only for treatment-refractory cases. Similarly, given the limited evidence base of 
ECT as an OCD treatment strategy in genera l populations, ECT is reserved only for 
those cases in which there is a co-occurring disorder for which ECT is a known effec-
tive treatment. A recent randomized controlled trial of deep TMS for OCD suggested 
positive results, but more research is needed (Carmi et al. 2019). None of these strate-
gies has been investigated in perinatal OCD cases.
