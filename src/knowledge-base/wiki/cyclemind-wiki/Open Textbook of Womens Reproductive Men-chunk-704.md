Parenting Capacity Evaluations 
Parenting capacity involves the ability of parents to care for and meet the needs of their 
children appropriately. Assessment of parenting capacity may arise in various legal 
contexts, including termination of parental rights proceedings, custody disputes, and 
allegations of child abuse or neglect (Grisso 2003). Mental health professionals may be 
asked to provide an opinion regarding an individual’s ability to parent. Determining 
fitness to parent is an inherently difficult task because there is no clear consensus re-
garding what constitutes minimal parental fitness (Budd 2005). Furthermore, future 
behaviors, such as child abuse and violence, are difficult to predict, and parenting val-
ues and childrearing styles differ across families and cultures (Grisso 2003). Compli-
cating matters, parenting capacity evaluations typically occur within the legal arena, 
which has its own set of challenges (Budd 2005). 
Assessment of parenting capacity should focus on an individual’s ability to parent, 
the parent–child relationship, and whether parenting is sufficient to meet the child’s 
basic safety and emotional needs (Budd 2005). Additionally, parenting skills should 
be evaluated with respect to the specific developmental needs of the children involved. 
Risk factors that may be linked to child maltreatment include active psychiatric symp-
toms or substance abuse, childhood history of abuse, social isolation, unrealistic expec-
tations, failure to appropriately interpre t and respond to the child’s cues, parental 
stress, unsafe home environment, and a history of violence. Protective factors include 
good social support, attunement to the child’s needs, positive parental role models, in-
sight into mental health difficulties, treatment adherence, and the ability to recognize 
how the parent’s mental illness or history of abuse may impact the child (Jacobsen et 
al. 1997). In addition, the psychiatric evaluator should be aware that socioeconomic 
factors may represent “equal or greater risk to children than the individual risk of pa-
rental mental illness” (Benjet et al. 2003) . Evaluators tend to advocate for “optimal 
family environments for children” rather than focus on what is simply adequate (Ben-
jet et al. 2003). Further complicating psyc hiatric assessment, courts may ask evalua -
tors to comment on what constitutes minimally adequate care for the child or if a child 
is at imminent risk of harm, both conditions that an evaluator may have difficulty de-
termining (Benjet et al. 2003). 
Although no definitive data have linked mental illness with parental unfitness, per 
se, courts may explicitly or implicitly presume unfitness based on a parent’s substance 
use disorder, intellectual di sability, or other psychiatric illness (Melton et al. 2007). 
Some laws explicitly identify conditions that are grounds for termination of parental 
rights. For instance, Nebraska law states that termination may occur if a parent is unfit 
due to “habitual use of intoxicating liquor or narcotic drugs” (Nebraska Rev Stat 43 -
292 2020). Missouri law presumes unfitness if a parent has pled guilty or been con -

mended treatment (Mo Ann. Stat. 211.447 2011). Other states list factors for the court
