Long-term effects. Limited data show that in-utero  exposure to lithium does not 
adversely affect IQ scores (Forsberg et al. 2018). 
Management during pregnancy. Lithium freely crosses the placental barrier and 
thus maternal lithium therapy does result in fetal exposure (Poels et al. 2018). Excretion 
of lithium occurs almost exclusively via the kidneys; thus, its pharmacokinetics are influ-
enced considerably by intravascular volume and glomerular filtration rate (GFR). Lith-
ium has an established therapeutic drug level of 0.6–1.2 mEq/L, representing a narrow 
therapeutic index. During pregnancy, as renal blood flow, GFR, and plasma volume in-
crease, the clearance of lithium increases by 30%–50%, causing plasma levels to decrease 
substantially. The decline in lithium serum level presents a risk for illness recurrence. Af-
ter delivery, lithium clearance quickly returns to pre-pregnancy levels (Deligiannidis et 
al. 2014). No consensus has been reached on how frequently to monitor lithium levels in 
pregnancy, but levels should be checked regularly throughout pregnancy and anytime 
the patient is at risk for dehydration (e.g., hyperemesis gravidarum) or has impaired renal 
function (e.g., preeclampsia). For routine monitoring, one common strategy is to check the 
patient’s lithium level monthly starting early in pregnancy, then weekly starting 4 weeks 
prior to expected delivery. Split daily dosing of lithium can serve to minimize peak serum 
levels and thus theoretically reduce teratogenic risk (Wesseloo et al. 2017). 
Close monitoring of maternal serum lithium levels proximal to delivery is critical, 
and adequate hydration should be provided if labor is prolonged. Although some ex-
perts recommend cessation of lithium 24–48 hours prior to a scheduled cesarean deliv-
ery or at onset of labor as a strategy to mitigate the risk of neonatal toxicity, no current 
evidence supports that this strategy decrease s the risk of complication in the infant, 
and thus any management plan should also include consideration of risk of relapse 
postpartum (Molenaar et al. 2020; Poels et al. 2018). It is prudent to measure a postpar-
tum lithium level approximately 24 hours after delivery and to consider restarting the 
patient on the pre-pregnancy lithium dosage after delivery if the dosage has been in-
creased incrementally during pregnancy (Deligiannidis et al. 2014) because a quick re-
turn to baseline GFR and rapid decrease in plasma volume in the postpartum period 
can place the patient at risk of toxicity. 
Women who are maintained on lithium during pregnancy should have periodic 
monitoring of their thyroid and renal function. Nephrotoxic medication and nonsteroi-
dal anti-inflammatory medications should be avoided (Khan et al. 2016). Although the 
recent evidence summarized here has questioned the association between lithium and 
cardiac malformations, it is recommended that women maintained on lithium in the 
first trimester have a high-r esolution ultrasound and fetal echocardiography at 16– 
20 weeks’ gestation to evaluate for cardiac malformations (Poels et al. 2018). Due to the 
risk of neonatal symptoms, some experts recommend that women maintained on lith-
ium in late pregnancy deliver in a hospital setting with access to high-level neonatology 
services. An umbilical cord blood sample should be obtained for measurement of infant 
lithium level and thyroid function (Poels et al. 2018). See Figure 15–2 for a visual rep-
