Clinical Considerations 
Choosing a Contraceptive Method: 
Shared Decision Making 
The contraceptive choice will depend on each patient’s needs, limitations, and goals. For 
example, patients who have difficulty adhering to a frequent contraceptive dosing sched-
ule may best be served with a long-acting reversible contraceptive, such as the IUD or arm 
implant, or with a quarterly injectable contraceptive, such as DMPA. Studies have shown 
that these options have superior rates of pregnancy prevention (99.5% for arm implant, 
99% for IUD, and 97% for depot injection) compared with daily oral contraceptives, 
whose success rates range from 92% to 98% depending on the reliability of the patient’s 
use (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2020). It is not uncommon for patients, 
especially those who are afraid of adverse medication reactions, to desire a contracep-
tive that they can easily start and stop. Fo r these patients, the increased risk of unin-
tended pregnancy with the daily methods must be balanced by the principle of patient 
autonomy. Providers may offer contraceptive pills with shorter or less frequent placebo 
periods, the contraceptive patch, or the contraceptive ring as a way to increase contra-
ceptive effectiveness without taking away the patient’s locus of control. 
Contraceptive choice may also vary based on which noncontraceptive goals the pa-
tient wishes to achieve. For example, the combined hormonal methods will provide the 
best suppression of androgenic manifestat ions, such as hirsutism and acne. Women 
hoping to decrease menstrual flow and its associated discomforts would benefit most 
from methods that produce good ovarian suppression, endometrial atrophy, or both. 
Contraceptive methods with high rates of amenorrhea include DMPA injections 
(50%–60% at 1 year), IUDs containing 52 mg progestin (50% at 1 year), and continuous 
combined hormonal contraception (70% at 1 year) (Hillard 2014). 
Some studies have found that women with  mood disorders have higher rates of 
contraceptive discontinuation due to experienced side effects, such as weight changes 
and moodiness (Hall et al. 2012, 2013a). Providers should discuss potential side effects 
up front both to allow for effective management and to build patient–provider trust. 
Moreover, efforts should be made to arrange follow-up visits so that adverse side ef-
fects and other patient concerns may be addressed promptly. Reports of adverse side 
effects, even if not common or expected, should be taken seriously to build trust and 
to avoid retraumatization of patients who have experienced invalidating power rela-
tionships in their lives. Plans should be made to address any reported side effects, and 
the patient should be allowed to switch contraceptive method if she desires. Emer -
gency contraception should also be offered if there is a risk that the patient might self-
discontinue contraception.

Contraception and Psychotropic Drug Interactions 
In patients with psychiatric diagnoses, some contraceptive methods will be preferred 
due to drug-drug interactio ns. The estrogen and progestin components of common 
contraceptive methods are metabolized by intestinal and hepatic oxidation, glucuroni-
dation, and sulfation. Cytochrome (CY) P450 3A4, in particular, appears to play a large 
role in the oxidative metabolism of ethinyl estradiol, the major estrogen component in 
most combined hormonal contraceptives. Meanwhile, combined hormonal contracep-
tives may moderately inhibit CYP1A2 and weakly inhibit CYP3A4, CYP2C19, and 
CYP2D6 enzymes. Because these enzymes are implicated in the metabolism of psycho-
tropic drugs, drug-drug interactions may exist (Berry-Bibee et al. 2016). 
In general, there is low concern that hormonal contraception interferes with the ef-
ficacy of selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors or serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake 
inhibitors, or vice versa. Tricyclic antidepressants and combined hormonal contracep-
tives are also not expected to have any clinically significant interactions. One good-
quality pharmacokinetic study showed a modest decrease in bupropion serum levels 
in users of combined hormonal contraception, but whether this clinically produces a 
change in depression symptoms is unknown (Palovaara et al. 2003). The data for anti-
psychotics and hormonal contraception are limited but overall reassuring that these 
medications have no clinically significant drug-drug interactions. Benzodiazepine lev-
els may be modestly affected (with some increased and some decreased) by hormonal 
contraception, but these interactions are unlikely to have much clinical significance 
(Berry-Bibee et al. 2016; Osborne and Payne 2017). 
Of the psychotropic drugs, antiepileptic drugs have the most evidence of interac -
tions with hormonal contraceptives (Reddy  2010). Specifically , estrogen-containing 
contraceptives are known to decrease the serum concentration of both lamotrigine 
and valproate, and doses may need to be uptitrated to effect in women newly initiated 
in combined hormonal contraception. The reverse is not true, however: use of lamo-
trigine or valproate does not decrease the contraceptive efficacy of hormonal contra-
ception (Reimers 2019; Wegner et al. 2014). Fortunately, serum levels of both drugs are 
readily available in most laboratory results. Serum levels may be used to guide med-
ication adjustments in patients who seem to demonstrate mood changes when begin-
ning to use hormonal contraception. It ma y be prudent to draw a “reference serum 
level” prior to contraception initiation to create a target, assuming the patient was 
symptomatically stable prior to initiation. When the patient appears clinically stable, 
antiepileptic drug levels should not be adjusted based on serum levels alone. Clinical 
response is still considered the most reliable indicator of dose adequacy (Jacob and 
Nair 2016; Paulzen et al. 2015). Finally, mo st combined hormonal contraceptives in -
clude a placebo phase during which withdrawal bleeding occurs. Because this phase 
has no estrogen, the patient may temporarily experience a surge in lamotrigine levels 
and experience medication-related side effects. To prevent this, it is recommended 
that women taking antiepileptic drugs skip the placebo phase of their combined hor-
monal contraceptives and use the medications in a continuous fashion. 
Other psychotropic drugs, again mostly in the antiepileptic class, may decrease the 
effectiveness of hormonal contraception by lowering serum concentrations of either its 
estrogen or progestin components. Carb amazepine, oxcarbazepine, topiramate, and 
modafinil are among those included in this  class. Studies have shown breakthrough
