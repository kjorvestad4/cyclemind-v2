It is important to understand the course and treatment of insomnia in pregnancy as 
a related clinical problem. Insomnia is a common perinatal symptom often assumed to 
be part of the normal physiology of pregnancy. However, recent evidence has pointed 
to the detrimental effects of perinatal insomnia itself, such as its relationship to preterm 
birth (Felder et al. 2017). In addition, perinatal insomnia plays an important role in ma-
ternal mental health because it can be both a symptom of an underlying mood or anx-
iety disorder and a trigger for these conditions. There is a complex interplay between 
insomnia, depression, and anxiety—making insomnia an important area to address for 
clinicians who treat women in the perinatal period. 
This chapter focuses on the epidemiology, clinical presentation, diagnostic criteria, 
clinical features, differential diagnosis and assessment, course and prognosis, and treat-
ment of perinatal anxiety disorders in women. We discuss perinatal anxiety disorders 
both as a group and as distinct DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association 2013) anxiety 
disorders; we also discuss related entities cited in the literature, such as pregnancy-
specific anxiety. Of note, disorders that contain anxiety symptomatology but are not 
DSM-5 anxiety disorders, per se, such as OCD and PTSD, are covered comprehensively 
in other chapters; please see Chapter 18, “ T r a u m a  a n d  R e p r o d u c t i v e  H e a l t h , ” a n d  
Chapter 20, “Obsessive-Compulsive Disorder,” for details. We also  include a summary 
of insomnia in pregnancy , including its epidemiology, clinical presentation, and treat-
ment options, because this common condition has significant overlap with the symp-
toms and management of perinatal anxiety disorders.
