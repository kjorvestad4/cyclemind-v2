Although the sex ratio of BDI is approximately 1:1, there are sex differences in the 
clinical presentation. Compared with men, women are more likely to experience hypo-
manic episodes, and the prevalence rate of BDII is higher among women (Baldassano 
et al. 2005; Moses-Kolko et al. 2019). Women may be slightly older than men on initial 
presentation and are more likely to experience comorbid thyroid disease (Baldassano 
et al. 2005), anxiety disorders (Saunders et al. 2012), and eating disorders (McElroy et 
al. 2016). Alternatively, men are more likely than women to present with comorbid sub-
stance use disorders (Grover et al. 2019). Although some studies have indicated that 
women are more likely than men to experience predominant depressive episodes (Alt-
shuler et al. 2010; Grover et al. 2019) and rapid cycling subtype (Di Florio and Jones 
2010; Erol et al. 2015), this literature is mixed, and other studies have not found such 
associations (Baldassano et al. 2005). 
Course/Prognosis 
BD is a chronic illness that typically re quires ongoing management throughout all 
stages of the disease course. On average, people living with BD experience symptoms 
of the disease for approximately half of their lives (Yatham et al. 2018). Although the 
psychosocial functioning of people with BD varies greatly among individuals, overall, 
BD is associated with significant morbidity and is one of the leading causes of years lost 
to disability worldwide. Factors associated with more  significant functional impair -
ments include higher frequency of acute episodes, prominent depressive symptoms, 
and lower cognitive status (Yatham et al. 2018). Patients with BD have an elevated risk 
of suicide relative to the general popula tion, and approximately 6%–7% of patients 
with BD die by suicide (Schaffer et al. 2015).
