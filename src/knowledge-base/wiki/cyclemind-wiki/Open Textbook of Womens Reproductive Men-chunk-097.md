Key Points 
• Perimenopause is a time of increased risk for depression, especially for wom-
en with a prior history of depression. 
• Typical symptoms of per imenopause can also include vasomotor symptoms 
(VMS), sleep disturbances, sexual dysfunction, and cognitive changes, among 
others. Executive function complaints should be assessed for severity and im-
pact on daily functioning. 
• The symptoms of depression and the symptoms of perimenopause can over-
lap, requiring a careful history and investigation to determine which treat -
ments are recommended. 
• Antidepressants can be effective for VMS as well as anxiety and depression. 
• Menopausal hormone treatment is an effective treatment for VMS but has 
some contraindications. 
• Evidence supports some alternative treatments as effective for VMS, but ev-
idence also shows some alternative treatments to be ineffective or potentially 
harmful.

sant?” All clinicians who treat women of reproductive age are likely to hear this ques-

Many clinicians continue to recommend stopping all psychiatric medications during 
pregnancy out of concern for potential harm  to the fetus. However , increasing evi -
dence shows that untreated psychiatric il lness during pregnancy poses significant 
risks to both the untreated mother and the developing child.

Approach to Diagnosis 
With any patient, a clinician cannot design a treatment plan without obtaining a proper 
understanding of the patient’s symptoms and likely diagnosis. Arriving at the correct 
diagnosis is crucially important in the perinatal period. Because untreated psychiatric 
illness carries risks to both mother and child, and some psychiatric medications also 
carry risks, it is important to be sure that the illness is being treated appropriately. Preg-
nancy itself can induce physical symptoms, such as fatigue and appetite changes, that 
may be confused with mood symptoms. Also , the tendency of our society to equate 
motherhood with “glowing” and happiness may lead some women, and some clini -
cians, to downplay mood symptoms because they do not fit with deeply held expecta-
tions about pregnancy. Women living with considerable psychosocial stress may arrive 
in the perinatal period carrying prior diagnoses that may not be accurate but instead 
reflect inadequate evaluation or prejudice on the part of previous providers.
