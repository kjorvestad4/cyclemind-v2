A later and much larger study by Huybrechts et al. (2016) examined the risk of con-
genital malformations in first-trimester exposure to SGA medications. Using a sample 
of 1,360,101 pregnant women from a Medicaid database, they examined the pregnancy 
outcomes of 9,258 women who filled a prescription for an SGA in the first trimester of 
pregnancy. Analyses were done to compar e cumulative data from all exposures to 
SGAs as well as individual agents (quetiapine, n=4,221; aripiprazole, n=1,756; risper-
idone, n=1,566; olanzapine, n=1,394; and ziprasidone, n=697) with women with no 
antipsychotic exposure. Although the unadjusted analysis did show a small but statis-
tically significant increase in the rate of total major malformations, once the data were 
adjusted for confounding variables, the rate did not differ significantly from that of the 
unexposed group (RR 1.05; 95% CI 0.62–1.31) for overall malformations or for cardiac 
malformations. When this group of SGAs was examined individually, only risperidone 
was associated with an overall small but statistically significant increased risk of mal-
formations (RR 1.26; 95% CI 1.02–1.56) (Huybrechts et al. 2016). 
Cumulatively, the data suggest that, as a class, SGAs do not substantially increase 
the risk of congenital malformations and should be considered as a treatment option 
when clinically indicated. Newer SGAs (e.g., lurasidone, iloperidone, and brexpipra-
zole) have not been adequately represented in these studies to draw any conclusions 
about their teratogenic potential. 
Obstetrical outcomes. Because the SGAs have a long-recognized association with 
metabolic side effects, there has also been concern about their potential to contribute 
to excess gestational weight gain or gestational diabetes, two complications of preg -
nancy that are significantly associated with adverse pregnancy outcomes (Oken et al. 
2009; Sweeting et al. 2016). Although a registry analysis done by Freeman et al. (2018) 
did not show a difference in mean weight gain associated with SGA exposure during 
pregnancy, women in this study who were taking SGAs were more likely to begin 
pregnancy with a BMI in the obese range (P<0.0001) compared with control subjects 
with similar psychiatric morbidity, illustrating the importance of strategies to address 
SGA-associated weight gain in women of reproductive age as part of routine practice. 
In contrast, however, in an analysis of a Medicaid database of 1,543,334 pregnancies, 
Park et al. (2018) analyzed the risk of developing gestational diabetes among women 
who continued an SGA medication throughout pregnancy relative to women who had 
previously been taking an SGA medication but discontinued prior to pregnancy. In this 
study, women who continued treatment with either olanzapine (absolute risk reduc-
