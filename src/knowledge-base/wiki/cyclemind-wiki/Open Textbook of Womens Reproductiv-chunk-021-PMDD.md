Mood Effects of Contraception 
Recent studies analyzing the relationship between hormonal contraception and mood 
disorders are overall reassuring, with adverse mood effects being possible but infre -
quent (Böttcher et al. 2012; Sc haffir et al. 2016). A few studies have found an associa-
tion between hormonal contraception use and lifetime diagnoses of depression, 
particularly among adolescents (Gregory et al. 2018; Skovlund et al. 2016; Zettermark 
et al. 2018). Some studies suggest that the mood effects of hormonal contraception are 
related to individual sensitivity to the pr ogestin component (Merki-Feld et al. 2017; 
Svendal et al. 2012). However, multiple ot her studies have not found such an effect 
(McKetta and Keyes 2019; Pagano et al. 2016; Worly et al. 2018). In fact, some studies 
have found that current use of combined hormonal contraception improves mood, a 
finding that may relate to the benefits of  contraception, which include reproductive 
and menstrual control, decrease in premenstrual symptoms, and improvement in 
conditions such as acne and gynecological pain (Svendal et al. 2012; Toffol et al. 2012). 
The risk of contraception producing adverse mood effects must be weighed against 
the possibility that unintended pregnancy itself may lead to psychological distress or 
clinical depression. A large percentage of pregnancies in the United States are un -
planned or mistimed. An anal ysis of more than 100,000 mothers between 2009 and 
2011 found that only 32% of the women in the sample were desiring pregnancy at the 
time of conception (Gauthreaux et al. 2 017). Among individuals with mental health 
disorders, up to 92% of pregnancies may be unplanned, because individuals with af-
fective disorders may be more prone to risky sexual behaviors with ineffective con -
traceptive use (Adan Sanchez et al. 2019; Cunningham et al. 2016; Hall et al. 2012, 
2013a, 2014). Several studies have found th at mothers with unplanned pregnancies, 
even if the pregnancy is ultimately accept ed, experience higher rates of postpartum 
depression (Abajobir et al. 2016; Barton et al. 2017; Gauthreaux et al. 2017). Most of 
these studies cite a two- or threefold increase in postpartum depression risk. Preexist-
ing affective disorders, social support, and race may all influence the strength of this 
association (Barton et al. 2017; Suh et al. 2016).

contraception has been used to improve mood in some patients with PMDD. For this

Psychiatrist’s Role in Contraceptive Access 
For many women with mental health disorders, the psychiatrist will be the most con-
sistent point of medical contact in their lives. For this reason, psychiatrists play a cru-
cial role in helping women understand th e interplay of reproductive health and 
mental health and are positioned to be an active force in enabling reproductive choice. 
Although the placement of IUDs and admi nistration of contraceptive injections 
may be out of the purview of the general psychiatrist, most psychiatrists should be able 
to prescribe combined hormonal contraception with some basic education. Table 4–2 
lists some common contraindications for using combined hormonal contraception. 
The psychiatrist should prescribe contraception if the patient desires it, lacks contrain-
dications, and is likely not pregnant (a useful Quick Start Algorithm for determining 
the likelihood of pregnancy is available from the Reproductive Health Access Project 
at www.reproductiveaccess.org/resource/quick-start-algorithm). The risks of unin -
tended pregnancy generally outweigh the risk of taking oral contraception when al -
ready pregnant. These medications are neit her teratogens nor abortifacients. Their 
main risk when used during early pregnancy is delaying the diagnosis of pregnancy.

atrist may want to consider, as well as emergency contraceptive options.

Special Considerations: 
Voluntary Termination of Pregnancy 
Not all women want to carry a pregnancy to birth. This makes voluntary termination 
of pregnancy (VTOP) a medical reality, regardless of societal or legal support. In 2014, 
almost 20% of pregnancies in the United States ended in VTOP (Jones and Jerman 
2017). Most women seeking VTOP are poor or have low income (Jerman et al. 2016). 
Reasons commonly cited for obtaining VTOP include feeling unable to raise a child 
based on personal or economic circumstance s, believing that a child would interfere 
with the woman’s obligations to herself or others, or lack of a supportive partner with 
whom to share the responsibility of parenthood (Finer et al. 2005). The decision to ter-
minate a pregnancy is rarely an eagerly made choice, even when a woman thinks it is 
the right choice. Accordingly, some women do experience negative feelings following 
a VTOP , and some experience clinically significant disorders such as depression and 
VTOP-specific PTSD. 
In examining the causal relationship of VTOP with mental health, however, meth-
odology is vitally important. Historical studies that have compared rates of depres -
sion among women seeking VTOPs with those of women in the general population 
who have live births fail to account for the mediating factors of patient choice and psy-
chosocial context (Steinberg et al. 2012; Stotland 2019). A more appropriate compari-
son would be women with unwanted pregnancies who chose VTOP , live birth with 
parenting, or live birth with adoption. Nonpregnant women are never an appropriate 
control group for women who have had VTOPs. Relatedly, in examining prevalence 
rates for women’s mental health disorders following VTOP , the comparison should 
be made with women’s mental health disorders after giving birth; postpartum rates 
of disorders such as anxiety and depressi on are commonly cited at 15%–20% in the 
literature (Kendig et al. 2017). Finally, investigators should strive to delineate whether 
the mood symptoms arose from the act of VTOP itself or from the circumstances that 
led the woman to contemplate VTOP in the first place. 
The American Psychological Association (2008), the American Psychiatric Associ -
ation (2018), and the American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists (2020b) all 
refute a causal relationship between the act of VTOP and mental health disorders such 
as depression, anxiety, or PTSD. These position statements arise from several meth -
odologically appropriate studies examining large, longitudinal data sets (Coleman et 
al. 2009; Cougle et al. 2003; Steinberg et al . 2018; van Ditzhuijzen et al. 2018). Other 
studies have shown that external and internal stigma play a large role in the meaning 
that women attribute to their VTOPs, and their subsequent emotional sequelae are in-
formed by that narrative (Cozzarelli et al . 2000; Hanschmidt et al. 2018; Steinberg et 
al. 2016). Choice also informs whether VT OP is considered traumatic, and women

Conclusion 
Although contraceptive care has not traditionally been within the scope of mental 
health care, the documented interplay between reproductive health and mental health 
clearly indicates that clinical excellence in psychiatric care includes competency in con-
traceptive counseling and management. Psychiatrists and other mental health provid-
ers are well positioned to educate women about the risks and benefits of contraception 
and the ways in which mood symptoms may be influenced by various methods. At a 
minimum, prevention of unwanted pregnancy should be a core component of compre-
hensive psychiatric care.
