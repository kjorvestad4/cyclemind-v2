Various restrictions on surrogacy have led intended parents to seek surrogacy in for-
eign countries, a practice known as “gestational tourism” (Deonandan 2015). This prac-
tice is not without its risks. A recent example is that of Aki Mukai, a media personality 
in Japan who, along with her husband, sought out a surrogate in Nevada given Japan’s 
prohibition of surrogacy. Even though Mukai’s own embryos were used, the Japanese 
government refused to recognize her as the legal mother of the twin children, resulting 
in a protracted legal battle that ultimately led to Mukai adopting her own biological 
children (Sanchanta 2011). Another example that made recent headlines is the strand-
ing of hundreds of babies born to gestational surrogates in Ukraine when international 
borders closed during the COVID-19 pandemic (Varenikova 2020; Widdicombe 2020). 
Medical guidelines regarding the eligibility of surrogates exist, but it is at a clinic or 
agency’s discretion to choose to follow these standards. The American Society of Repro-
ductive Medicine (ASRM) recommends that a gestational carrier be between the ages 
of 21 and 45 years, have at least one child, have had full-term and uncomplicated pre-
vious pregnancies, and ideally have had no more than five total previous deliveries, at 
most three of which were via cesarean section. Potential gestational carriers should also 
have psychosocial consultations with a mental health professional, who is also often the 
ethical gatekeeper determining whether a woman is psychologically fit to be a surrogate 
(Braverman 2015). In contrast, standards regarding eligibility of intended parents are 
poorly defined. When mental health professionals are involved with intended parents, 
it is in three primary roles—psychoeducator, evaluator, and ethical gatekeeper—similar 
to the roles they play for potential surrogates (Braverman 2015). However, mental 
health professionals are often not involved with intended parents at all. For instance, in 
a 2005 survey, only 18% of ASRM clinics consistently had intended parents meet with 
mental health professionals (Braverman 2015). 
Sometimes questions arise regarding the medical provider’s ethical and legal obliga-
tions to the surrogate in light of the competing interests of the intended parents. For in-
stance, what if a surrogate discloses that she has been drinking alcohol, not taking her 
vitamins, or engaging in behavior that might put the fetus at risk? Although the ASRM 
supports disclosure to the intended parents in limited circumstances, others have as-
serted that a surrogate’s physician does not have a medical or legal relationship with 
the intended parents, and thus the ongoing duty to preserve confidentiality remains 
with the surrogate (Horner and Burcher 2018). 
Other legal questions may arise regarding the custody of the child. Rarely, a surro-
gate refuses to relinquish the baby to th e intended parents. Courts have come down 
on either side of this issue, but in general, in cases in which the intended parents are 
the genetic parents, it is likely that the court will find them to also be the legal parents.
