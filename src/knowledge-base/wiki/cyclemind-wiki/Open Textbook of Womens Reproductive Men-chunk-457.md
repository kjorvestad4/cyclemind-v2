Ms. X is a 26-year-old woman brought into the emergency department by her partner. 
She is 4 days postpartum after the birth of her first child via spontaneous vaginal deliv-
ery at 39 weeks’ gestation. Her pregnancy was planned and desired and without med-
ical complication. She has no formal psychiatric history, and family history is notable 
for a sibling with bipolar disorder. 
Per report from her partner, Ms. X was “great” in the first couple of days after deliv-
ery. Although she sometimes seemed over whelmed and short temp ered, overall, she 
was “full of energy” and “captivated” by he r infant and caretaking role. In the past 
24 hours, however, her behavior has become increasingly odd, unusual, and erratic. He 
reports that she has become “obsessed” with concerns that their daughter is showing 
signs of “neurologic damage” despite a reassuring visit to her pediatrician. This morn-
ing, Ms. X was found feeding the baby a bottle of water. When the partner asked what 
she was doing, Ms. X appeared confused and disoriented, but eventually he understood 
that she was attempting to “flush out the baby’s system” because she believed the baby 
had been fed contaminated breastmilk. She also had set the thermostat to “max heat,” 
stating that the baby had been hypothermic overnight and needed to be “warmed.” 
On examination, Ms. X appeared guarded,  with mild psychom otor agitation. Her 
thought process was tangential, and her mood was irritable. She became quickly pan-
icked when she was told she would need to stay for further evaluation, stating that her 
baby was gravely ill, and “I’m the only one who knows what’s really going on with her.” 
Differential Diagnosis 
The differential diagnosis of PPP is broad, especially given the potential overlap with 
medical complications in th e immediate postpartum peri od. In addition, PPP may 
have overlap with symptoms of delirium, such as a waxing and waning course and 
acute confusion; this makes it vital to rule out medical causes of altered mental status. 
Neuropsychiatric syndromes, such as auto immune encephalitis, should be consid -
ered, particularly when accompanied by neurological symptoms. See Tables 16–3 and 
16–4 for a more thorough differential diagnosis and recommended laboratory and ra-
diological investigation in the setting of PPP .
