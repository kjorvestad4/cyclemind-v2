Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Estimates of the rates of both AD and PPD vary widely. In the United States, most stud-
ies and current data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention show rates 
of 10%–15% during pregnancy (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2015; 
Gaynes et al. 2005), although some recent studies show rates as low as 6% in previously 
healthy women (Ashley et al. 2016). One factor that complicates the calculations of the 
incidence and prevalence of perinatal depre ssion is prior history of depression, be -
cause perinatal depression can represent either a new onset of a mood disorder or an 
exacerbation of a prior disorder. 
Rates around the world are similar to those of the United States and even higher, with 
higher rates more common in low- and middle-income countries (Al-Azri et al. 2016; 
Castro e Couto et al. 2016; George et al. 2016; Ogbo et al. 2018), with one recent study 
in Saudi Arabia finding a rate as high as 58% (Bawahab et al. 2017). In the postpartum 
period, a recent worldwide meta-analysis found an incidence of 12% and a prevalence 
of 17%, with rates being highest in the Middle East and lowest in Europe (Shorey et al. 
2018), whereas another meta-analysis focused on perinatal depression found a world-
wide prevalence of 11.9%, with rates highest in low- and middle-income countries 
(Woody et al. 2017). Recent country-specific studies find rates ranging from 14% to 
25% in low- and middle-income countries (Doe et al. 2017; Hartmann et al. 2017; Özcan 
et al. 2017; Upadhyay et al. 2017). In higher-income countries, rates are overall lower, 
but women in immigrant and minority groups have higher rates than women in other 
groups (Doe et al. 2017; Falah-Hassani et al. 2015). One recent meta-analysis found that 
Black women, overall, are more likely to report symptoms but are less likely to seek 
care due to cultural stigma about mental illness (Cannon and Nasrallah 2019).
