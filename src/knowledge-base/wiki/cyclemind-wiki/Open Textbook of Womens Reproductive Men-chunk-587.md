Sociodemographic Considerations 
OCD in general has a similar age of onset, gender distribution, and psychiatric comor-
bidity across the world, and the general symptom structure tends to be consistent 
(American Psychiatric Association 2013). Cultural and religious variation exists, how-
ever, in the subjective experience, course, and outcome of OCD across differing contexts 
(Stein and Rapoport 1996). The literature regarding sociodemographic considerations 
of OCD at times of reproductive transition is insufficient. Studies looking at popula-
tions outside of the United States show similar patterns of OCD in pregnancy and the 
postpartum period, as well as higher rates of onset and exacerbation of OCD symptoms 
at other times of reproductive transition (McGuinness et al. 2011). Few data have been 
published on racial, socioeconomic, or other characteristics specific to perinatal OCD. 
More research is needed into these important considerations to facilitate improved un-
derstanding, detection, and treatment of these illnesses. 
Pathophysiology 
Biological 
There are few published studies about the biological contributors to the pathophysiol-
ogy of perinatal OCD (Lord et al. 2011a; Serati et al. 2015). However, numerous studies 
have linked various biological systems to obsessions and compulsions in the general 
population, and others have linked biological systems to mood and anxiety symptoms 
in the perinatal period. By examining these two related types of symptoms, we may 
arrive at some hypotheses concerning the biological etiology of perinatal OCD. 
Genetic Factors 
No large-scale studies of genetic factors in perinatal OCD have yet been published. 
One case report of a woman with new-onset OCD in the postpartum period reported 
an incidentally found deletion (9q33.1) in genes affecting systemic inflammation and 
glial-guided neuronal migration; the same deletion has been associated with general 
OCD and with autism, ADHD, and other psyc hiatric problems (Serati et al. 2015). A
