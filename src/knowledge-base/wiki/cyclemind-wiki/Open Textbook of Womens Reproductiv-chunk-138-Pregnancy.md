Parenting Capacity Evaluations 
Parenting capacity involves the ability of parents to care for and meet the needs of their 
children appropriately. Assessment of parenting capacity may arise in various legal 
contexts, including termination of parental rights proceedings, custody disputes, and 
allegations of child abuse or neglect (Grisso 2003). Mental health professionals may be 
asked to provide an opinion regarding an individual’s ability to parent. Determining 
fitness to parent is an inherently difficult task because there is no clear consensus re-
garding what constitutes minimal parental fitness (Budd 2005). Furthermore, future 
behaviors, such as child abuse and violence, are difficult to predict, and parenting val-
ues and childrearing styles differ across families and cultures (Grisso 2003). Compli-
cating matters, parenting capacity evaluations typically occur within the legal arena, 
which has its own set of challenges (Budd 2005). 
Assessment of parenting capacity should focus on an individual’s ability to parent, 
the parent–child relationship, and whether parenting is sufficient to meet the child’s 
basic safety and emotional needs (Budd 2005). Additionally, parenting skills should 
be evaluated with respect to the specific developmental needs of the children involved. 
Risk factors that may be linked to child maltreatment include active psychiatric symp-
toms or substance abuse, childhood history of abuse, social isolation, unrealistic expec-
tations, failure to appropriately interpre t and respond to the child’s cues, parental 
stress, unsafe home environment, and a history of violence. Protective factors include 
good social support, attunement to the child’s needs, positive parental role models, in-
sight into mental health difficulties, treatment adherence, and the ability to recognize 
how the parent’s mental illness or history of abuse may impact the child (Jacobsen et 
al. 1997). In addition, the psychiatric evaluator should be aware that socioeconomic 
factors may represent “equal or greater risk to children than the individual risk of pa-
rental mental illness” (Benjet et al. 2003) . Evaluators tend to advocate for “optimal 
family environments for children” rather than focus on what is simply adequate (Ben-
jet et al. 2003). Further complicating psyc hiatric assessment, courts may ask evalua -
tors to comment on what constitutes minimally adequate care for the child or if a child 
is at imminent risk of harm, both conditions that an evaluator may have difficulty de-
termining (Benjet et al. 2003). 
Although no definitive data have linked mental illness with parental unfitness, per 
se, courts may explicitly or implicitly presume unfitness based on a parent’s substance 
use disorder, intellectual di sability, or other psychiatric illness (Melton et al. 2007). 
Some laws explicitly identify conditions that are grounds for termination of parental 
rights. For instance, Nebraska law states that termination may occur if a parent is unfit 
due to “habitual use of intoxicating liquor or narcotic drugs” (Nebraska Rev Stat 43 -
292 2020). Missouri law presumes unfitness if a parent has pled guilty or been con -
victed of a drug felony within the previous 3 years and has at least one other child who 
has been found to be abused or neglected or if the parent has failed to complete recom-
mended treatment (Mo Ann. Stat. 211.447 2011). Other states list factors for the court 
to consider in determining fitness rather than listing strict conditions. Under Massa-

Evaluation of parenting capacity for the courts is a forensic assessment. The forensic 
evaluation differs from a clinical evaluation in that the traditional doctor–patient rela-
tionship does not apply. Various clinical, legal, and ethical dilemmas arise when a men-
tal health professional attempts to serv e as both treating clinician and forensic 
evaluator. Forensic and clinical roles are fundamentally different and conflicting with 
regard to the overall goal of the work, the nature of the relationship between evaluator 
and evaluee, assessment strategies, and ethical principles entailed (Strasburger et al. 
1997). Clinicians not trained in forensic assessment should be wary of performing par-
enting capacity assessments for legal purposes. Mistakes commonly seen in parental 
fitness evaluations include failure to disclose the purpose of the evaluation and limita-
tions of confidentiality, overreliance on a single source of information, and overstated 
conclusions and recommendations (Budd 2005). Such deficiencies may have signifi -
cant ramifications in the lives of parents and children, resulting in crucial decisions re-
garding custody and parental rights being based on incomplete, invalid, or misleading 
information (Jacobsen et al. 1997). Given the inherent role of conflicts and important 
consequences in parenting capacity cases, mental health professionals should avoid 
acting as both treating clinician and forensic evaluator when questions of parental fit-
ness arise (Strasburger et al. 1997). 
Child Abuse Risk and Duty to Report 
Child maltreatment is usually conceptualized as including physical abuse, neglect, 
sexual abuse, and emotional abuse. Child maltreatment is usually defined by state law. 
Physical abuse includes intentional use of force against a child that may result in injury; 
neglect includes a caregiver’s failure to meet the child’s basic physical, medical, educa-
tional, and emotional needs; sexual abuse involves attempted sexual acts or exploita -
tion; and emotional abuse involves behavior conveying to the child that he or she is 
worthless or valued only to meet the caregiver’s needs (McEwan and Friedman 2016). 
There is an approximately tenfold difference between the prevalence of child maltreat-
ment self-report and the reports to child protection agencies (McEwan and Friedman 
2016). Approximately one-quarter of report ed cases are substantiated in the United 
States (McEwan and Friedman 2016). In the cases not substantiated, there may be a 
lack of evidence, poor cooperation, investigatory issues, or a lack of maltreatment. 
Although psychiatrists are mandated repo rters of child maltreatment, they often 
have concerns about breaking confidential ity. However, completing the compulsory 
reporting may contribute in a positive way to the therapeutic process, especially if the 
professional discusses the need to report with the parent. Clinicians should keep in 
mind, however, that parents may be reluctant to seek treatment or to disclose symp -
toms for fear of being reported to child protective services (CPS) and losing custody.

Although parental mental illness is often asserted as a risk factor for child abuse, 
literature supporting this claim is limited. In reality, most parents who abuse children 
are not mentally ill (McEwan and Friedman 2016). Methodological issues often exist 
in studies that purport to demonstrate a higher risk of child maltreatment in parents 
with a history of mental illness. The psychi atrist should consider that mental illness 
that is appropriately treated would not be expected to lead to an increased violence 
risk in general. Friedman and McEwan ( 2018) found that parents with psychiatric 
symptoms who were treated at a psychiatric hospital were at lower risk of violence 
toward children than other parents in thei r same communities. Mental illness, then, 
should be considered a dynamic, rather than static, risk factor regarding violence risk 
toward children. Considering specific symptoms of a mental illness that may lead to 
child maltreatment is of utility; for example, disorganized thinking in schizophrenia 
may lead to neglect, whereas hallucinatio ns could lead to vi olence (McEwan and 
Friedman 2016). Substance use and personal ity disorders are considered additional 
risk factors for violence against children and in general. 
Physicians working with children and families should have basic knowledge of their 
statutory duty to report suspected abuse or neglect. Mandated reporting laws began to 
appear across the United States during the 1960s. The federal Child Abuse Prevention 
and Treatment Act, which was enacted in 1974, further formalized these statutes by re-
quiring states, contingent on federal funding, to identify processes and procedures by 
which certain individuals were required to report suspicions of child maltreatment. To-
day, all states have mandated reporting statutes, but these laws vary in terms of the in-
dividuals or entities named as mandated reporters, the standards for making the report, 
the type of harm that is reportable, and whether the reporter’s identity is disclosed 
(Child Welfare Information Gateway 2019a). Not surprisingly, many states designate 
professionals such as social workers, physicians, police, teachers, or others who have 
routine contact with children as mandated reporters; however, 18 states require univer-
sal mandated reporting regardless of profession (Child Welfare Information Gateway 
2019a). State laws may also state that a designated professional must generally report 
“abuse or neglect” or more specifically de fine the prohibited harm. For instance, the 
District of Columbia requires reporting of suspicion of fetal alcohol spectrum disorder 
in infants younger than 12 mo nths. Multiple states require film processors to report 
photographs or other media depicting child pornography, and other states specifically 
require reporting of child victims of human trafficking (McTavish et al. 2019). 
Although the specific language varies, statutory standards generally require report-
ing from mandated reporters (e.g., physicians) when they suspect or have reason to 
suspect that a child has been abused or neglected (Landess and Friedman 2018). This 
is a relatively low standard, intended to overinclude cases of potential child maltreat-
ment, which conceivably results in high numb ers of false positives. Despite this low 
standard, existing literature confirms that physicians tend to underidentify and under-
report abuse (Flaherty and Sege 2005). Professionals may not report because they do 
not believe the information received is reportable, perceive problems with CPS, or fear 
malpractice suits or retaliation or due to other personal bias (Badger 1989; Lindberg et 
al. 2015). Physicians may feel that reporting to CPS leads to no action or, worse, to an

Infanticide and Violence Risk Assessment 
In analyzing the world literature, Friedm an and Resnick (2007) found that although 
many cases of maternal infanticide occurred in the context of child abuse, some moth-
ers made suicide attempts at the same time. Studies of maternal infanticide found that 
the mothers were often unemployed caregivers of their children in their early 20s who 
experienced psychiatric disorders. Amon g mothers who committed filicide (killing 
one’s child who is younger than 18 years) in the general population, mothers were of-
ten socially isolated, of lower socioeconomic status, and full-time caregivers who had 
relationship problems or had been victimized by intimate partner violence. In some 
cases, crying or other child factors were identified as precipitants. Some mothers had 
previously maltreated their children, whereas others were devoted mothers with men-
tal illness. Substance abuse was also seen in this population, as were psychosis and de-
pression (Friedman and Resnick 2007). When specifically considering psychiatric 
samples of maternal filicide, these mothers had often experienced depression, psycho-
sis, and suicidal thinking. Some had personality disorders. Significant stressors in life 
were often found. It was recommended that psychiatrists assess for risk of filicide sys-
tematically, such as they do for suicide, first entertaining the possibility that such an 
act could occur (Friedman and Resnick 2007). 
Motives for maternal filicide initially described by Resnick (1969) and further de -
scribed by Friedman and Resnick (2007) include altruistic (murder out of love), which 
may be part of an extended suicide or may occur for psychotic or nonpsychotic reasons 
(e.g., a child’s debilitating illness); acutely psychotic (with no comprehensible motive, 
e.g., responding to command auditory hallucinations); fatal maltreatment (the final out-
come of chronic neglect or abuse); unwanted child; and partner revenge (e.g., in the midst 
of a custody battle). Among the motives for filicide, altruistic filicide is often related to 
postpartum psychosis (PPP) or postpartum depression (PPD), and acutely psychotic 
filicide is often related to PPP (Friedman et al. 2019a). However, partner revenge, un-
wanted child, and fatal maltreatment cases are rarely related to PPP , although a mother 
with this diagnosis may experience difficulty providing for the infant’s needs because 
of her psychosis. Specifically, in PPP , a mother may be at risk of child maltreatment due 
to delusional thinking, irritable mood state, or confusion. PPP itself elevates the risk of
