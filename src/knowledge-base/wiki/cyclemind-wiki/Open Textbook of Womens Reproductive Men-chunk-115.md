Of the FGAs used to manage agitation, haloperidol is the most common, in part due 
to its long track record of efficacy and safety in the emergency setting (Ladavac et al. 
2007; MacDonald et al. 2010; Wilson et al. 2012). Expert consensus guidelines support 
the use of haloperidol as the first-line treatment of acute agitation in pregnancy when 
the patient has no previous experience with neuroleptics, especially because medium-
or high-potency FGAs are typically less sedating than low-potency ones, which may 
be important in the pregnant patient (Allen et al. 2005; Diav-Citrin et al. 2005). FGAs 
have the advantage of few adverse effects in terms of vital signs and anticholinergic 
side effects, and haloperidol has reassuring data about lack of teratogenicity from first-
trimester exposure (Einarson 2005; Wilson et al. 2012). However, FGAs do carry the 
risks of extrapyramidal side effects, such as acute dystonia and neuroleptic malignant 
syndrome, and cardiac effects, such as QTc prolongation and torsades de pointes (Wil-
son et al. 2012). Exposure to high-potency antipsychotics may also be associated with 
neonatal extrapyramidal symptoms during and after delivery (Galbally et al. 2014). 
SGAs such as quetiapine and olanzapine are increasingly used for the treatment of 
acute agitation (Allen et al. 2001). SGAs have several advantages over FGAs, including 
a reduced risk of acute extrapyramidal side effects and availability in a rapidly dissolv-
ing formulation, such as with olanzapine (Cohen et al. 2016). Early safety data on SGAs 
in pregnancy appear to be reassuring regarding risk of teratogenicity, with the possible 
exception of risperidone (Huybrechts et al. 2016). However, SGAs do carry side effects, 
including elevated risk of orthostatic hypotension, that may be of concern with acute 
use of these medications in pregnancy, particularly in a pregnant patient who may al-
ready have low resting blood pressure (Galbally et al. 2014; Wilson et al. 2012). 
Benzodiazepines are also commonly used for management of acute agitation, and 
in cases of severe agitation in pregnancy, the benefit of using these medications may 
outweigh the risks. Early studies indicated an association between first-trimester ben-
zodiazepine exposure and cleft lip and palate in the newborn, but further studies have 
not replicated this finding (Grigoriadis et al. 2019; Rodriguez-Cabezas and Clark 
2018). Third-trimester exposure to benzodiazepines carries a concern for withdrawal 
or respiratory problems in the infant; however, these adverse effects are more common 
in women with chronic benzodiazepine use and less likely to arise from a one-time 
emergency dose (Enato et al. 2011). 
Other medications to treat agitation may be useful in certain circumstances. For 
example, in a pregnant patient with mild agitation that is not related to delirium, oral 
medication such as diphenhydramine can be  an appropriate first step. Although di -
phenhydramine is generally considered low risk for congenital malformations, anti-
cholinergic medications can worsen symptoms of delirium, so this etiology is important 
to rule out (Li et al. 2013). 
Substance Intoxication and Withdrawal 
The prevalence of substance use disorders, such as opioid use disorder, has been rising 
at an alarming pace in the United States, particularly among women of reproductive 
age (Saia et al. 2016). Pregnant women, however, face important barriers to substance 
use disorder treatment, including lack of access to care, fear of legal or custody conse-
quences, and stigma (Saia et al. 2016). The use of substances during pregnancy can lead

Alcohol 
Alcohol intoxication is common in emergency settings. Key clinical features of intox-
ication include slurred speech, lack of coordination, impaired judgment, mood and 
personality changes, and somnolence (Pasha et al. 2020). These signs, familiar to most 
emergency physicians, are no different in pregnant women than in the general popu-
lation. Acute alcohol withdrawal poses significant, life-threatening risks to pregnant 
women. Symptoms include tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, restlessness, sei-
zures, altered mental status, hallucinations, and agitation (DeVido et al. 2015). Potential 
consequences to the pregnancy from alcohol withdrawal include fetal distress, low birth 
weight, placental abruption, and preterm labor (DeVido et al. 2015; Rodriguez-Cabezas 
and Clark 2018). If alcohol withdrawal is suspected in a pregnant patient, all efforts 
should be made to treat the withdrawal in a setting that allows close medical monitoring. 
Benzodiazepines are the mainstay of treatment for alcohol withdrawal in the general 
population (Aftab and Shah 2017). Data regarding their use for alcohol withdrawal in 
pregnant women are limited, but given the high risks associated with untreated with-
drawal, benzodiazepines are the most recommendable option for alcohol withdrawal 
in pregnant patients (DeVido et al. 2015). Gabapentin may be an alternative, with some 
data indicating relative safety in pregnancy (Fujii et al. 2013). Anticonvulsants such as 
valproate and carbamazepine are associated with higher rates of congenital abnormal-
ities and should be avoided (Heberlein et al. 2012). 
Cocaine and Methamphetamines 
Stimulants, such as cocaine or methamphetamines, are the second most widely used 
and abused substance category in the United States after tobacco (Smid et al. 2019). Ac-
cording to the 2019 National Survey on Drug Use and Health, cocaine use remained sta-
ble in women of all age groups (>12 years), but methamphetamine use had a statistically 
significant increase in 2018–2019 as compared with 2016–2017 (Substance Abuse and 
Mental Health Services Administration 2020). Pregnant women with cocaine or meth-
amphetamine toxicity can present with aggressive and irritable behavior, visual or other 
hallucinations, and grandiose and paranoid thought processes (Berkowitz and Mills 
2019). Acute cocaine toxicity , which can lead to severe hypertension, hyperreflexia, pro-
teinuria, edema, and seizures, may be mistaken for preeclampsia (Smid et al. 2019). 
Maternal complications of cocaine or methamphetamine intoxication include myo-
cardial infarction, renal failure, hepatic rupture, cerebral ischemia or infarction, and 
death (Kalaitzopoulos et al. 2018; Smid et al. 2019). Even small doses of cocaine may 
lead to cardiac morbidity and mortality in an otherwise healthy pregnant woman, po-
tentially due to elevated progesterone concentrations, which increase cardiac muscle 
sensitivity to cocaine (Smid et al. 2019).  -adrenergic antagonists should not be used

Opioids 
Repeated opioid intoxication and withdrawal during pregnancy can result in compli-
cations that include fetal distress, intraute rine growth restriction, placental insuffi-
ciency, and preterm labor (Rodriguez-Cabezas and Clark 2018). Opioid withdrawal 
can cause permanent damage to fetal brain development and fetal demise (Rodriguez-
Cabezas and Clark 2018). For pregnant women presenting with opioid withdrawal, the 
standard of care is acute agitation management and potential initiation of opioid main-
tenance therapy. Methadone and buprenorphine both have evidence of relative safety 
during pregnancy (Aftab and Shah 2017; Zedler et al. 2016). Medically assisted with-
drawal (“detoxification”) is not recommended for pregnant women because it is asso-
ciated with high relapse rates and inherent overdose mortality risk (Saia et al. 2016). 
Catatonia 
Catatonia is an important but easily missed perinatal psychiatric emergency. Signifi-
cant life-threatening risks associated with its symptoms include agitation and pro -
longed immobility, both of which are common features of this condition. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
In the general population, ca tatonia is not uncommon. It has been reported to occur 
in 10%–18% of all acute psychiatric presentations (Llesuy et al. 2018; Rasmussen et al. 
2016). However, catatonia in pregnancy has been described rarely. One retrospective 
chart review revealed only 3 case series (w ith 33, 15, and 13 patients in total) and 16 
more individual case reports in the literature (Gonzales et al. 2014). 
Risk Factors 
Risk factors include underlying psychiatric illness (typically affective states, such as 
major depression and bipolar disorder), neurological illness, and medical illness. 
Screening 
To our knowledge, screening for catatonia is not generally done in clinical practice. In 
terms of clinical assessment, the Bush-Francis Catatonia Rating Scale, among others, 
is widely used due to its validity, reliability, and ease of administration (Bush et al. 
1996; Sienaert et al. 2011).

Diagnostic Criteria

Clinical Presentation 
Symptoms of catatonia can be highly variable; more than 60 clinical features have been 
described (Weder et al. 2008). Symptoms of retarded catatonia include withdrawal,

prolonged immobility, and mutism (Espínola-Nadurille et al. 2007). Excited symptoms 
include restlessness, auditory and visual hallucinations, and disorganized speech and 
behavior, which can easily be misidentified as primarily psychotic in nature (Espínola-
Nadurille et al. 2007). Malignant catatonia is  characterized by severe agitation, auto-
nomic instability, delirium, and a rapidly progressive course (Park et al. 2017). 
Course and Prognosis 
The course of catatonia can be variable but can include serious, life-threatening com-
plications. Symptoms that cause considerable safety risks to patients include agitation, 
negativism, mutism, prolonged immobility, and stupor (Gonzales et al. 2014). Immo-
bility and withdrawal can lead to maternal malnutrition, dehydration, pressure ulcers, 
contractures, and aspiration pneumonia (Philbrick and Rummans 1994). Because preg-
nancy is a hypercoagulable state, immobility raises the potentially lethal risk of deep 
venous thrombosis and pulmonary embolism (Philbrick and Rummans 1994). Com -
plications from malignant catatonia include rhabdomyolysis, renal failure, cardiac isch-
emia, metabolic acidosis, disseminated intravascular coagulation, and death (Philbrick 
and Rummans 1994). Notably, catatonia is a risk factor for neuroleptic malignant syn-
drome, which has a fatality rate of up to 10% (Rasmussen et al. 2016; Strawn et al. 2007). 
Differential Diagnosis and Assessment 
It is important to distinguish catatonia from conditions with similar presentations, in-
cluding extrapyramidal side effects, neuroleptic malignant syndrome, nonconvulsive 
status epilepticus, and akinetic mutism (Rasmussen et al. 2016). Gonzales et al. (2014), 
in their retrospective chart review of cata tonia in pregnancy, noted a high incidence 
of medical etiologies in perinatal patients with catatonia. These included posterior re-
versible encephalopathy, neoplastic limbic encephalitis, cerebrovascular events, and 
eclampsia. The authors noted that catatonic patients in the perinatal period should be 
specifically ruled out for eclampsia because the seizures and posterior reversible en -
cephalopathy can be both associated with and causative of catatonia. NMDA enceph-
alitis is another condition diagnosed in a significant proportion of these cases; care 
should be taken to rule out this disorder  in cases with similar presentations (Kumar 
et al. 2010). 
Pathophysiology 
The pathophysiology of catatonia is not well understood. One hypothesis is that cata-
tonia is a movement-based disorder similar to parkinsonism; another hypothesis is that 
catatonia is a form of extreme anxiety or an evolutionarily based fear response (Ras -
mussen et al. 2016). The GABA and glutamate neurotransmitter systems are thought 
to be implicated, particularly in the basal ganglia (Northoff 2002; Rasmussen et al. 
2016). Other studies have examined structural (including the orbitofrontal, prefrontal, 
parietal, and motor cortical regions), genetic, and systemic contributors, such as the in-
nate immune system, infection, and autoimmunity (Northoff 2002; Rogers et al. 2019; 
Walther et al. 2019).
