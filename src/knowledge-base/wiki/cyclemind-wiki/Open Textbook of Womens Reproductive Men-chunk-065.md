In the United States, spontaneous abortion, also termed miscarriage, is defined as preg-
nancy loss prior to 20 weeks’ gestation. Intrauterine fetal demise (IUFD), or stillbirth, is 
defined as loss after 20 weeks’ gestation or after the fetus reaches 14 oz in weight. Early 
neonatal death is defined as death of an infa nt prior to 7 days of life, and late neonatal 
death is defined as death between 7 and 28 days of life (Barfield 2011). Termination of 
pregnancy for fetal abnormalities or genetic indications is included in the definition 
of perinatal loss, whereas termination for elective reasons is not. Recurrent perinatal 
loss (RPL) is defined by the ASRM as two or more losses, and it can be further subdi-
vided into primary RPL, defined as pregnancy loss in women who have never had a 
viable pregnancy, and secondary RPL in women who have had a previous live birth 
(Barfield 2011; Bhat and Byatt 2016). 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Approximately 12%–20% of pr egnancies end in loss prior to 20 weeks’ gestation 
(Hemels et al. 2005; Hemminki and Forssa s 1999; Robinson 2014; Wilcox et al. 1988). 
Maternal age is a determinant for overall incidence of different types of perinatal loss. 
For instance, 27% of women ages 25–29 ex perience spontaneous abortion, compared 
with 75% of women ages 45 and older (Rob inson 2014). Many early first-trimester 
losses are never recognized as a pregnancy, and thus spontaneous abortion may occur 
in up to one-third of all pregnancies (Wilcox et al. 1988). Approximately 50%–80% of 
all women who experience perinatal loss will have a subsequent pregnancy. Recurrent 
perinatal loss occurs in 2%–3% of pregnancies (Bhat and Byatt 2016; Kolte et al. 2014). 
In contrast, IUFD after 20 weeks’ gestation is rare, affecting an estimated 4.6 per 1,000 
births, whereas neonatal death occurs in approximately 6.2 per 1,000 births (Bhat and 
Byatt 2016). The risk of IUFD is highes t in teenagers (0.07% –0.13%) and in women 
older than 40 years (0.11%–0.21%) (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecolo-
gists and Society for Maternal-Fetal Medicine 2020).
