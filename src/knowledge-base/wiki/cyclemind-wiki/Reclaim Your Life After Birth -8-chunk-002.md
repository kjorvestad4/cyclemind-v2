Perinatal Mood & Anxiety Disorders
Like puberty, pregnancy and postpartum involve significant biological
changes, particularly the postpartum period, which is the most
significant hormonal shift a person can experience. However, this is
also a period of time that involves a host of psychological and social
stressors. 
Matresence, just like adolescence, is a time of transition, not just
hormonally but on a multitude of levels, including shifts to a woman's
role, responsibilities, and identity. This makes it a psychologically
vulnerable time in which PMADs can emerge. 
These disorders, which can emerge anytime during pregnancy as well
as up to a year postpartum, include not only postpartum depression
but postpartum anxiety, postpartum OCD, postpartum PTSD,
postpartum bipolar disorder, and postpartum psychosis. 
As many as 1 in 5 women will develop a PMAD.
 In fact, PMADs are the number one complication of pregnancy
and postpartum. 
Sadly, 75% of women with a PMAD do not receive help. 
6
PMADs are not an exclusively hormonal phenomena, but the result of
a combination of physiological (e.g., genetic predisposition, sleep
deprivation), psychological (e.g., relationship with one's own mother,
perfectionism), and social/environmental stressors (e.g., poor social
support, financial stress, relationship strain).
While PMADs can impact anyone, risk factors include a prior personal
or family history of a mood or anxiety disorder, past trauma/abuse,
and certain medical conditions (e.g., diabetes, thyroid imbalance, and
other endocrine disorders) as well as stress, lack of social support,
marital conflict, being a single parent, financial stress, prior losses
(i.e., miscarriages, stillbirths), a complicated pregnancy, even the
child's temperament (i.e., colic). Having PMS/PMDD can indicate you
might be more susceptible to the hormonal changes associated with
the postpartum period. 
While PMADs affect 1 in 5 women, they can also impact men, with 1 in
10 men developing a PMAD. This number increases to 50% if their
partner has PPD. In men, PMADs can manifest differently (e.g., anger,
substance abuse). 
PMADs include postpartum depression but also postpartum anxiety,
panic attacks, OCD, PTSD, bipolar disorder, and psychosis.

Although it is not technically a PMAD, the baby blues, which impacts
60-80% of new mothers, is a common phenomenon and something
that is important to know about. Unlike PMADs, it is an entirely
hormonal phenomenon that results from the sudden, drastic drop in
estrogen and progesterone. The baby blues is experienced in the
immediate aftermath or first two weeks of giving birth. Many people
experience it as an emotional rawness characterized by feelings of
stress, sadness, anxiety, loneliness, fatigue, and tearfulness. After the
first two weeks, the baby blues resolves on its own.

If low mood persists after 2 weeks, it is indicative of PPD, which
impacts approx—20% of new moms. If you are experiencing symptoms
of depression, such as feelings of sadness, guilt or worthlessness,
irritability, anger, agitation, or suicidal thoughts, you may have PDD,
which occurs in about 20% of women. Risk factors for PPD include a
history of trauma, prior experiences of depression or anxiety, a family
history of mental health issues, having a "difficult" baby or baby with
special needs (premature birth, medical complications, illness), other
stressors, such as financial or employment issues, isolation, and lack of
social support. Perfectionism and high expectations are also risk
factors. It is worth noting that PPD can begin prior to the postpartum
period, with 26.5% of women diagnosed with PPD having it prior to
pregnancy, 33.4% developing depression during pregnancy, and 40.1%
in the postpartum year.

Postpartum Anxiety 
Unlike postpartum depression, many people are unaware of postpartum
anxiety, which affects approximately 15% of new moms. Symptoms
include excessive worry that is difficult to control, restlessness,
irritability, agitation, sleep disturbances, and physical symptoms (e.g.,
muscle tension, racing heart, shortness of breath, GI distress).
Postpartum Panic Attacks 
Panic attacks affect approx—10% of new mothers. Panic attacks, which
are characterized by intense fear or discomfort that typically peaks
within 10 minutes, can occur postpartum. Symptoms of panic attacks
include shortness of breath, increased heart rate, chest pains, dizziness,
hot or cold flashes, numbness or tingling, restlessness, agitation,
irritability, fears of going crazy or losing control, etc.

Postpartum OCD
Postpartum OCD also occurs in approx—10% of new mothers. Symptoms
include obsessions, which are recurrent and persistent thoughts, urges,
or images that are intrusive and cause distress. Individuals typically seek
to ignore, suppress, or neutralize obsessions by engaging in a
compulsion, which is a repetitive behavior intended to reduce anxiety.
Obsession can include thoughts of harm coming to the baby, either
deliberately or accidentally, which can lead to intense distress as well as
feelings of guilt and shame. In the case of Postpartum OCD, mothers are
often alarmed or even horrified by these thoughts and take steps to
prevent harm, unlike the kind of thoughts of harm that can occur in
postpartum psychosis in which someone may intend to cause harm. In
fact, it is the protective part of the brain at play in postpartum OCD that
results in these types of thoughts. 
Michelle recently brought home her first baby. She has been
experiencing a lot of concerns about her baby's breathing, which
she frequently checks at night. She bought a device to monitor his
breathing at night but fears it may be unreliable. As he gets older

him in the deep freezer they use for breastmilk storage. She finds
these thoughts distressing and also shameful. When these thoughts
enter her mind, she holds her baby close and tries to rid herself of
them as quickly as possible, but they keep resurfacing.

Postpartum PTSD
Childbirth can be an extremely traumatic, sometimes life-
threatening experience. Every year, an estimated 1,200 women in the
US suffer complications during pregnancy and childbirth that prove
fatal, and another 60,000 suffer near-fatal complications. Tragically,
the CDC estimates that 80% of these deaths could have been
prevented with better access to care, more timely diagnosis, and
medical intervention. It is especially bad for women of color, with
black mothers being more than 4x likely to die. Black mothers are
also more likely to experience long-lasting mental health
consequences after childbirth and less likely to receive treatment.
The US CDC suggests that 60% or more of maternal deaths could be
prevented by addressing racial and socioeconomic disparities.
As many as 45% of women say their births were traumatic, with
about 5% going on to develop PTSD. Symptoms include intrusive
symptoms (e.g., flashbacks, nightmares, memories, physical
reactions), avoidant symptoms (e.g., social withdrawal, denial,
apathy, emotional numbing), negative changes in thoughts and
mood (e.g., guilt, depression, irritability, hopelessness, feelings of
self-blame), and arousal symptoms (e.g., sleep disturbance, poor
concentration, agitation, aggression, hypervigilance).

Postpartum Bipolar Disorder 
Postpartum bipolar disorder is distinguished from PPD in that it
includes a hypomanic or manic episode, which may include
feelings of euphoria or agitation, decreased need for sleep, racing
thoughts, increased productivity, speech, and energy. Hypomania
lasts for up to 4 days and often improves functioning, whereas, in
a full-blown manic episode, functioning is impaired due to the
severity of symptoms and lasts at least 7 days and/or requires
hospitalization. Like PPD, PPBD puts women at risk for suicide.
Women who stop mood stabilizers due to pregnancy have a 2x
greater likelihood of reoccurrence postpartum.
Miranda had a baby two weeks ago. She has since felt
overwhelmed, tearful, and even hopeless at times. But, at other
times, she feels energized, despite sleep deprivation, and excited
about future plans and projects she wants to work on. She stays up
late at night while the baby sleeps, buying things online related to
her plans. She has also been having sudden, intense bouts of
irritability towards her husband, who has not seen his wife acting
in this way in the past and is unsure what to make of her behavior.

Postpartum Psychosis 
Postpartum psychosis is very rare (it occurs in 1-2 in 1,000 women) but is
a very serious condition in which mothers have delusions and
hallucinations, sometimes putting their infants at risk. The onset is
usually sudden and typically occurs within the first 2-4 weeks
postpartum. Risk factors include a personal or family history of bipolar
disorder or psychosis, as well as sleep deprivation. Symptoms include
hallucinations (e.g., seeing, smelling, tasting, or hearing things that are
not present), delusions (e.g., false beliefs), disorganized thoughts and
speech (e.g., difficulty making sense of thoughts and communicating
clearly), and odd, bizarre, or inappropriate behavior (e.g., talking to
oneself without awareness). Whereas in postpartum OCD, parents
recognize that their thoughts are unhealthy and often experience distress
as a result of their intrusive, unwanted thoughts, in PPP, mothers may not
have this awareness or alarm. Additionally, whereas in postpartum OCD,
mothers take steps to protect or safeguard their infants, in PPP, women
might act on them. Of those with PPP, 5% die by suicide, and 4.5% commit
infanticide. For this reason, PPP is a medical emergency.
Carrie had a baby 2 weeks ago. Since coming home from the hospital, she
has become increasingly restless and unable to sleep. She is concerned
that her baby does not belong to her and had visions her of baby being
replaced by another child. As a result, she has become neglectful of her
baby's needs. She feels disconnected not only from the baby but also
from herself and her surroundings, as though none of it is real. 
If you are experiencing symptoms or have a partner or loved one who is
experiencing symptoms, please call 911 or go to the nearest ER.

Breastfeeding & D-MER 
Breastfeeding is a complex issue in that it can be a protective factor
from PMADs, with women who are exclusively breastfeeding being
less likely to be depressed. However, it can also be a source of stress
for women. When women find themselves struggling to breastfeed
for whatever reason (trouble latching, pain, low supply), choosing to
discontinue can be followed by feelings of guilt and shame.
Conversely, women may find that they want to continue to
breastfeed but are lacking the support needed to do so. PMADs can
also interfere with breastfeeding. For example, there is a
phenomenon known as D-MER or dysphoric milk ejection reflex,
which impacts up to 9% of women and results in an intense wave of
negative emotions just before milk is released, which can make
breastfeeding an emotionally difficult process.

Protecting your mental health may also include staying on or going on
medication. Women often discontinue medication when they become
pregnant, which can put them at risk for the development or worsening
of PMADs. Additionally, when women find themselves struggling with a
PMAD, they are often hesitant to begin medication due to fears about
the risks, most of which are unfounded. Due in part to a lack of
awareness, even among OBs, about the role that medications can play in
the treatment of PMADs, many women choose to "tough it out" or suffer
for the sake of their child, but this discounts the risks associated with
untreated PMADs during pregnancy and postpartum to their baby
including those related to attachment and development. While there are
risks associated with some medications, it is essential to discuss the
medications you are taking with your doctor, as they may require
adjustments. However, many medications, particularly those that are
working well for you, are generally safe to continue. If you are not on
medication currently and may benefit from it, please do not hesitate to
begin. Have a conversation with your doctor. There are also
psychiatrists who specialize in reproductive medicine that can help you
make the best decision for yourself and your baby.

Acceptance and Commitment Therapy 
ACT, which is pronounced as one word rather than A-C-T, to
emphasize the importance of ACTion was developed in the 80s by
Steven C. Hayes, Kelly Wilson, and Kirk Strosahl. It focuses on using
acceptance and mindfulness strategies to change the way you relate to
your thoughts and feelings while engaging in behaviors that align with
your values. With its unique balance of mindfulness and acceptance-
based interventions along with behavioral change or ACTion, ACT has
a tremendous amount to offer mothers. 
ACT can help mothers:
• Learn how to use mindfulness to defuse from distressing thoughts.
• Accept difficult emotions with compassion. 
• Identify their new values and engage in value-aligned ACTions.
Take "mom guilt', for example. This kind of guilt, if avoided, often
results in mothers neglecting themselves and their own needs,
leading to "mom rage." ACT can help mothers "unhook from thoughts
such as "I am being selfish" and/or avoiding feelings of guilt such that
they inhibit themselves from practicing self-care, but allow these
difficult feelings with mindfulness and compassion, and engage in
self-care ACTIons instead, which will help support them in being the
mother's they want to be.

The Six Core Processes
As mentioned briefly, ACT involves six core processes, which combine
mindfulness and acceptance processes with commitment and
behavior change or ACTion. The six core processes are acceptance,
defusion, self-as-context, contact with the present moment, values,
and committed ACTion. I primarily think of acceptance, defusion, self
as context, and contact with the present moment as mindfulness and
acceptance processes, whereas I think of values and committed
ACTion as commitment and behavioral change processes; although
there is overlap and truly all of these processes interrelate.

Cognitive Defusion
Cognitive defusion, or defusion, as it is called for short, is a
mindfulness process in which you can observe your internal
experience, including thoughts and feelings, from a distance. There
are all kinds of distressing thoughts and feelings that arise in
motherhood, like judgments and comparisons, feelings of failure, as
well as things like intrusive thoughts and even suicidal thoughts.
Defusion can help you learn how to hold these kinds of thoughts in
kind awareness without overidentifying with them in a way that results
in distress.
25
Acceptance 
Acceptance comes from the word acceptation, meaning the "action of
taking or receiving what is offered." It is an ACTive, not a passive
process. It is not giving up or resignation. In fact, it can lend itself to
ACTion. This doesn't mean that whatever happened is ok; it is just a
recognition of what is. It is also ongoing, meaning that it is a
continuous process. My favorite way of thinking about acceptance is as
a grieving process, specifically, grieving what we had hoped for vs what
we received. I think there is a lot of grief in motherhood, or "mom
grief," as I call it. This could be the loss of the fertility journey,
pregnancy, delivery, feeding journey, and/or parenting experience you
had hoped for. 
Acceptance of our circumstances, as well as our internal experience, is
important. Resisting our feelings, just like resisting our thoughts, only
heightens and maintains our distress. Accepting our emotions with
compassion is important for this reason, but also because our
emotions tell us something important about what we value, want, and
need. The word "emotion" comes from the Latin word "motus," which
means movement or motion. Your emotions are likely trying to get you
to move in a particular direction. If you feel stuck, for instance, it is
likely that you are ignoring what your emotions are trying to help you
move in.

Acceptance

or assert ourselves in some way.
Sadness can reveal what we truly care about and often indicates a

Jealousy and comparison can alert us to things we care about and

Present Moment 
Present moment awareness is the ability to be present rather than
being stuck in the past or living in an imagined future. Sometimes,
people think that being mindful or practicing mindfulness is "clearing"
your mind or exercising the kind of control that people may wish they
had over their thinking. Neither of these things are necessary or, I
would say, possible.
 The goal is not to stop your thoughts, clear or quiet the mind; it is not
to change your experience in any way; it is to be aware of it with
kindness and curiosity. 
Mindfulness is about making contact with the present moment as it is,
even if it is painful. It is being open or willing to experience the
present moment without judgment without resistance, as is the case in
acceptance and compassion. In motherhood, this means being present
in miserable moments like being spit up on, having your hair pulled,
dealing with a blowout, teething, sleepless nights, the "terrible twos,"
and also the magical ones like baby coos and cuddles, toddler hugs and
kisses, and dance parties before bedtime. 
28
Present Moment 
Mindfulness can be practiced formally and informally. Formal practice
includes a formal meditation like mindfulness of breath, a body scan,
loving-kindness, etc. Informal practice brings this same kind of
awareness to an activity of daily life, such as when you mindfully savor
a moment of connection with your baby or use mindfulness to cope
with a difficult moment. For example, recognizing your limits when
dealing with a baby who won't stop crying, being aware that you are
overstimulated from noise or "touched out" from being a human jungle
gym, and taking a "mommy time out." Another example is being aware
of resentment towards your partner who gets to use the bathroom
undisturbed and observing your thoughts, physical sensations, and
urges before reacting so that you can respond more intentionally and
skillfully. 
While mindfulness helps us be more aware of our internal experiences
and needs, thus making us less reactive and better able to make
intentional choices that align with our values, this won't happen 100%
of the time! There is nothing that tests your limits like having children.
You will have moments, many moments, of reactivity in which you fall
short of the values you have and are not the mother, partner, or person
you wish to be; this is where self-compassion comes in. Self-
compassion is the recognition of suffering within ourselves, just as we
would feel for another person. Often, people fear that practicing self-
compassion will let them "off the hook" and result in worsening
failures, but research shows that practicing mindful self-compassion
helps us take accountability and facilitates change.

Self-as-Context  
The way I prefer to frame self-as-context is as the observer self or the
part of yourself that is able to observe your experience. I see it as a
kind of witness to your life that remains constant and unchanged,
ever-present, a part of you that has always been there in childhood, as
you transitioned into adulthood, and even as you transition into
motherhood. Accessing this part of yourself can help you find stillness
in the storm of the postpartum period. Often, this perspective comes
later when we reemerge from the postpartum period, but trying to
maintain this perspective can be helpful. Whatever you may be feeling
postpartum is temporary. That doesn't mean it isn't real. Of course, it
is! But, just like the weather, it will pass. The clouds will part, and the
sun will come out again.

Values
Values refer to what matters to you most. It is different from goals,
which are things we hope to accomplish in that values are enduring and
without end. For example, we can achieve the goal of becoming a
parent, but the kind of parent we want to be (e.g., kind, gentle, patient)
is what values are referring to. Values often shift in matresence, which
can lead to a lack of clarity about what matters most to us. 
Societal and cultural messages can add to confusion this confusion.
These messages can also result in unrealistic expectations and feelings
feeling of failure. Even when it comes to personally held values, it is
important to note, especially in motherhood, that we never actually
achieve living in alignment with our values. Not only are the ideals
about motherhood unattainable, but everyone falls short of their values.
None of us are perfect. We are not perfect people. We are not perfect
parents. This makes it important to practice self-compassion here too.
Self-compassion is also important when it comes to conflicting values.
We can't do all things at all times, meaning that we have to make choices
about what we prioritize in a given moment. The most notable example
of this is being a working mother. Mothers often feel torn between
being there for their children and the demands of their work, but work
is also a way of providing for your family, and work may be something
you value. All mothers have things that matter to them outside of
motherhood. And no one benefits when mothers sacrifice other things
that matter to them and "lose" themselves to motherhood, which often
results in anger and irritability, or "mom rage," which children tend to
feel is their fault, meaning they suffer as well.

Committed ACTion 
Committed action is engaging in behaviors that align with your values.
Committed ACTion is any step you take that moves you toward your
values. Engaging in committed ACTion is often difficult because it is not
always easy to do things we care about. Therefore, it is essential to stay
connected to your values as a way to stay motivated. 
Unlike goals or achievements, we are not focused on the outcome,
meaning that you cannot fail when it comes to committed-ACTion. You
can be more or less engaged, but you cannot fail. You just keep trying!
Every day, you keep trying to show up as the mother you aspire to be,

Defining Defusion 
Cognitive defusion, or defusion, as it is called for short, is a
mindfulness process in which we are able to observe our internal
experiencing including thoughts and feeling, as though from a distance.
One of the reasons that people struggle so much with their thoughts
and feelings is because they are fused or overidentified with them. In
this way, they may not have a sense of their thoughts and feelings as
experiences, transient experiences, but of their thoughts as facts and
their feelings as permanent.
We want to work towards experiencing thoughts and feelings as
impermanent events allowing us to be present and intentional such that
we can engage in our lives in a meaningful way.
Having a thought doesn’t make it true. Unfortunately, if people are
fused with thoughts, they can feel very real.This can lead people to feel
the need to stop, change, or control their thoughts, which typically
backfires, just like struggling in quicksand. It also takes away from our
ability to be present in the moment. This has considerable implications
for women who are postpartum and experiencing things like
judgmental thoughts, intrusive thoughts, or even suicidal thoughts.
36
Defusion Strategies
Defusion strategies include noticing:
• I am noticing the thought that it is my fault in reference to going into
premature labor.
• My mind is telling me that I failed because I had a c-section.
• I am seeing the image of ______ in reference to intrusive thoughts of
harming the baby
• I am having the thought that I want to die.
It can also help to defuse from difficult emotions, recognizing them as
transient physical experience in the body: 
• I am noticing feelings of hopelessness and heaviness in my body.
• I am experiencing sensations of panic like a tingling in my body.

Defining Acceptance
Acceptance comes from the word acceptation, meaning the “action of
taking or receiving what is offered”
It is active, not passive. It is not giving up or resignation. 
In fact, it can lend itself to ACTion.
Acceptance doesn’t mean whatever happened is ok, it just is a
recognition that it is. It is also ongoing, meaning that it is a continuous
process.
My favorite way of thinking about acceptance is as a grieving process;
specifically, grieving what we had hoped for vs what we received.
I think there is a lot of grief in motherhood or “mom grief.” This could
be the loss of the fertility journey, pregnancy, delivery, feeding journey,
and/or parenting experience you had hoped for.

Why Accept Difficult Emotions
The word emotion comes from the latin word motus, which means
movement or motions.
You shouldn’t ignore or suppress your feelings, not only because doing
so is unhealthy, but also because they are likely trying to get you to
move in a particular direction.
Similar to distressing thoughts, difficult emotions are not the enemy,
they too are trying to help you. If you feel stuck, which is often the case
when people go to therapy, it is often a good indication that emotional
avoidance is happening. It is often difficult emotions especially that are
trying to get us to make a change as discomfort is often needed to make
a change. If we avoid these feelings, we may not be listening to what
they are trying to communicate and the direction they want us to move
in.

because for many people it is the area of their lives that they care about

Defining Mindfulness
I like the more succinct definition favored by Jack Kornfield, “loving
awareness.”
Sometimes people think that being mindful or practicing mindfulness
is “clearing” their mind or exercising the kind of control that people
may wish they had over their thinking. Neither of these things are
necessary, or, I would say, possible.
The goal is not to stop your thoughts, clear or quiet the mind, it is not to
change your experience in any way, it is to be aware of it, in the way JKZ
described, with kindness and curiosity
“Paying attention in a particular way: on purpose, in the present
moment, and nonjudgmentally.”
-Jon Kabat-Zinn’s definition of mindfulness
59
Mindfulness in Motherhood
Mindfulness is about making contact with the present moment, as it is,
even if it is painful.
It is being open, or willing, to experience the present moment without
judgment, without resistance as is the case in acceptance and
compassion.
In motherhood, this means being present in miserable moments like
being spit up on, having your hair pulled, dealing with a blowout,
teething, sleepless nights, and the “terrible twos” as well as the magical
ones like baby coos and cuddles, toddler hugs and kisses, and dance
parties before bedtime.

Practicing Mindfulness in
Motherhood
Mindfulness can be practiced formally and informally
Formal practice includes a formal meditation like mindfulness of
breath, a body scan, loving-kindness, etc. 
I have free meditations available on my website at
www.drcarissagustafson.com.
Informal practice is bringing this same kind of awareness to an activity
of daily life, like when you mindfully savor a moment of connection
with your baby or are using mindfulness to cope with a difficult
moment. 
For example, recognizing your limits when dealing with colic and
taking a “mommy time out”, recognizing being overstimulated from
noise or “touched out” from being a human jungle gym, being aware of
resentment towards your partner who gets to go to the bathroom
undisturbed and observing your thoughts, physical sensations, and
urges prior to ACTing on them.

There is nothing that tests your limits like having children. You will have

Defining Self-Compassion
I like Sharon Salzberg’s definition the best. She defines compassion as
“the trembling or quivering of the heart in response to seeing pain or
suffering.”
She elaborates, stating that when we sympathize with or feel the pain of
another and wish to alleviate that suffering, that is compassion.
When that suffering is in oneself, it is self-compassion.

Oftentimes, people fear that practicing self-compassion is letting
themselves “off the hook” and will result in worsening failures, but
research shows that practicing mindful self-compassion actually helps
us take accountability and facilitates change.
In your own experience, does judging, labeling, or berating yourself
help you to be better? Or does recognizing, with compassion, that you
fell short of your values, and reflecting on what you might need in to do
order to do better?
For example, does labeling yourself a bad mom and/or partner for
yelling vs recognizing that is not the kind of behavior you want to
engage in and when you notice yourself getting overstimulated, voicing

Defining Self-as-Context
The way I prefer to frame self-as-context is as the observer self or the
part of yourself that is able to observe your experience.
I see it as a kind of witness to your life that remains constant and
unchanged, ever present, a part of you that has always been there, in
childhood, as you transitioned into adulthood, and even as you transition
into motherhood.
The observer self is the part of you that can observe your own
experience in the moment (e.g., having thoughts, noticing your thoughts,
experiencing an emotion vs. noticing your emotional experience).  It is
stable and unchanged throughout your life, including the radical
transition of motherhood Accessing this part of yourself can help you
find stillness in the storm of the postpartum period. Often, this
perspective comes later when we reemerge from the postpartum period,
but try to maintain enough perspective to know that how you may be
feeling right now, even as it might be overpowering, is temporarily. That
doesn’t mean it isn’t real. Of course it is! But, just like the weather, it will
pass. The clouds will part and the sun will come out again.
Postpartum may be a stormy season of life, but it is not forever.
This doesn’t mean you should just “wait it out”. You absolutely should
take ACTion like joining a support group, therapy, and/or medication. In
fact, oftentimes, this changes the whether! But know that the postpartum
period is not the rest of your life.

Defining Values
Values refers to what matters to you most.
It is different than goals, which are things we hope to accomplish. Values
are enduring and without end. For example, we can achieve the goal of
becoming a parent, but the kind of parent we want to be (e.g., kind,
gentle, patient) is what values refer to.
Values often shift in matresence, which can lead to an increased lack of
clarity. Societal and cultural messages can also add to confusion about
what we value as opposed to what we are told to value. These messages
can also result in unrealistic expectation and result in feelings of
judgment, comparison, and failure.
The perinatal period is an important time to reflect on changing values
and distinguishing ones that are personally held from those that aren’t.
71
Self-Compassion in Values
Even when it comes to personally held values, it is important to note,
especially in motherhood, that we never actually achieve living in
alignment with our values. Not only are the ideals about motherhood
unattainable, but everyone falls short of their values.
None of us are perfect. We are not perfect people. We are not perfect
parents.
How could it be any other way? Like I said, the things we care the most
deeply about are also the things that can cause us the most pain.
So, if it feels like you are constantly failing, it is because you care!
Allow yourselves to be imperfect while committing to showing up as the
mother you aspire to be, knowing that you will never totally “get it right”
because there is no getting it right.
And if you do “get it wrong” meaning falling short of your values, which
you will, practice self-compassion to work towards being more in
alignment with your values without holding yourself to an unachievable
standard.
72
Conflicting Values in Motherhood
Self-compassion is also important when it comes to conflicting values.
We can’t do all things at all times meaning that we have to make choices
about what we prioritize in a given moment.
Also, sometimes, values can feel at odds with each other but actually
support each other. For example, taking time away from parenting,
which often leads to feelings of guilt, can help support us in being closer
to the parents we aspire to be because it reduces parental burnout and
helps us attend to our own needs and other areas of our life that you care
about.

Defining Valued-ACTion
Committed ACTion is any step you take that moves you towards your
values.
Engaging in committed ACTion is often difficult because is not easy to do
things we care about, so it is important to stay connected to your values
as a way of staying motivated.
Unlike goals or achievements, we are not focused on the outcome,
meaning that you cannot fail when it comes to committed-ACTion. You
can be more or less engaged in it but you cannot fail. You just keep
trying!
The reason we cover valued-ACTion last is because it may be important
to use mindfulness skills (acceptance, defusion, self-as-context, present
moment) and have clarity on your values in order to engage in valued-
ACTion. 
For example, you you may need to be able to identify, with mindful
awareness, that you are feeling angry in order to engage in committed
ACTions that help you to advocate for yourself or assert yourself with
others in skillful ways (i.e., voicing your needs to your partner such that
resentments do not build, establishing boundaries at work). Or you may
need to defuse from certain kinds of thoughts and feelings like guilt in
order to take time away from your children to attend to yourself or
another area of your life that you care about.
