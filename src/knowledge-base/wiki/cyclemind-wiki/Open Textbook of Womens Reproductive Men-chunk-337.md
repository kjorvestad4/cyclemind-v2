As discussed in detail in Chapter 11 and Chapter 19 (“Anxiety Disorders and Insom-
nia in the Perinatal  Period”), delineating the differential impact of different types of 
perinatal distress can be challenging, given the overlap of—and comorbidity among— 
distress, perceived stress, anxiety disorders, pregnancy-related anxiety, depressive dis-
orders, and other disorders, such as PTSD. That said, specific maternal mental health 
conditions, such as anxiety and depressive disorders, have been associated with many 
of the same infant neurobiological changes produced by chronic stress in pregnancy, 
such as HPA stress axis alterations, decreased hippocampal size, and increased amyg-
dalar reactivity (Qiu et al. 2015; Rifkin-Graboi et al. 2013, 2015). In humans, infants ex-
posed to maternal depression and anxiety show altered stress responses detectable 
even in later childhood, for example, at age 10 (Lupien et al. 2009). Interestingly, in ro-
dent models, selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors (SSRIs) reverse the effects of pre-
natal stress exposure on hippocampal volume (Rayen et al. 2011). 
After birth, the parent–infant relationship continues to be a highly dynamic combi-
nation of nature and nurture. Two main neurobiological pathways are thought to be in-
volved in the parent–infant attachment bond: 1) the dopaminergic “reward” pathway 
and 2) the oxytocinergic system (Scatliffe et al. 2019; Strathearn et al. 2009). Oxytocin 
is a neuromodulatory hormone produced in the hypothalamus that plays a key role in 
physiological processes, such as labor and lactation. Oxytocin is also believed to be crit-
ical to the emergence and maintenance of the behavior that forms the basis of the par-
ent–infant bond (Kim et al. 2014). Oxytocin is released in response to stimuli such as 
infant suckling or the sight or sound of a nursing mother’s infant (McNeilly et al. 1983). 
Evidence suggests a relationship between oxytocin and prosocial effects, such as en -
hanced social memory, improved eye gaze when looking at faces, increased recognition 
of faces and facial expressions, and increased manifestations of trust (Baumgartner et 
al. 2008; Domes et al. 2007; Guastella et al. 2008a, 2008b; Kosfeld et al. 2005; Savaskan 
et al. 2008). Likewise, oxytocin is thought to mediate multiple behavioral components 
of the parent–infant relationship, including physical contact, synchrony, and respon-
siveness. These effects are notably seen in both mother–infant and father–infant pairs 
(Kim et al. 2014; Scatliffe et al. 2019; Vittner et al. 2018). Oxytocin receptors are located 
in the ventral striatum, an important dopaminergic brain region, and animal models 
have demonstrated that oxytocin receptor binding is associated with maternal prosocial 
behaviors (Olazábal and Young 2006). Thus, it is thought that oxytocin may link social 
cues to dopamine-associated reinforcement pathways (Strathearn et al. 2009).
