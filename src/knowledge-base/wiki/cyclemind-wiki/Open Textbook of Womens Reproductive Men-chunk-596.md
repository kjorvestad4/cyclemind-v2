As with any psychiatric assessment, a complete history and mental status examina-
tion are necessary. Key features include assessment for co-occurring conditions, par-
ticularly those with higher incidence in OCD populations (e.g., mood disorders, 
anxiety disorders, tics, and Tourette syndrome); a thorough review of past psychiatric 
and treatment history, including medication and psychotherapy trials; a general med-
ical history (especially in the rare event th at OCD is a manifestation of a medical ill -
ness); family history; and social history—with  a particular focus on the patient’s social 
support system and her overall level of functioning in the home and work/school do-
mains. Family members can be important allies in treatment efforts (Koran et al. 2007); 
they also may need help themselves, in which case family therapy may be indicated. 
As described earlier in the “Clinical Features ” section, it is critical to differentiate 
OCD symptoms from delusions, which represent a psychotic process and, if involving 
infant harm, are more strongly associated with actual risk to the infant (Abramowitz 
et al. 2003; Sharma and Sommerdyk 2015). Su ch symptom evaluation can be part of 
the overall safety assessment indicated in all psychiatric evaluations; it should also in-
volve inquiry into self-harm and suicidalit y and may rely on collateral information 
from family members. This is also an opportunity for psychoeducation because many 
patients are reluctant to share their disturbing intrusive thoughts out of fear of legal 
action and fear that their children will be taken from their care (Brandes et al. 2004; Jen-
nings et al. 1999). 
Also as described earlier, symptom evaluation should distinguish obsessions and 
compulsions from other disorders and should also involve a systematic approach to 
symptom severity. If no rating scale is used, quantification of the number of hours per 
day spent in obsessing and in performing compulsions, and the degree of effort ap -
plied to trying to escape the obsessions and resist the behaviors, is recommended (Ko-
ran et al. 2007). Such information will inform treatment goals, which should be 
modest at the start given the extended pe riod needed for marked clinical improve -
ment. Reasonable goals include <1 hour per day spent obsessing and performing 
compulsive behaviors, mild OCD-related anxiety, and little to no interference of OCD 
with tasks of ordinary living, including childcare (Koran et al. 2007). 
In general, patients should be cared for in the least restrictive setting that is able to 
provide safe and effective treatment (Koran et al. 2007). Most OCD can be managed in 
the outpatient setting; hospital or partial hospitalization may be indicated, however , 
when the patient is not able to care for herself or if there is significant risk for harm to 
self or others, including a child. Even if outpatient, treatment intensity can be rigorous, 
including daily psychotherapy. Regardless of setting, treatment should be coordinated 
with physicians treating co-occurring medical conditions, other clinicians, and social 
agencies. In prenatal cases, coordination with the patient’s obstetrician is critical not 
only to discuss the risks of treatments versus no treatment but also to share informa-
tion on the patient’s overall well-being and ability to function because these can im -
pact obstetrical outcomes.
