Table 21–3 lists options for validated su bstance abuse screening tools developed 
specifically for the peripartum population. Other screens, such as the Drug Abuse 
Screening Test or the National Institute on Drug Abuse Quick Screen, may also be ap-
propriate because these are validated in general medical settings. They may all be ad-
ministered in person or remotely by a medi cal staff member or by the patient. Urine 
and blood drug testing may be used to confirm or monitor substance use but should 
not be used as a population-level screening method, due to methodological concerns. 
Many of the perinatal screening tools are highly sensitive but less specific because 
they are brief and ask broad questions about at-risk exposures or behaviors. In short, 
they are designed to facilitate conversati ons about substance use. A positive screen 
should prompt further evaluation, with the goal of defining factors such as the time 
period, frequency, and amount of use and severity of functional impairment. 
Providers should screen patients for substance use in an open-ended, empathetic, 
and nonjudgmental manner, provide education on the harmful effects of substance 
use, assess and support motivation to change, and refer to specialty or a higher level 
of care as indicated (Wright et al. 2016). This process is formally known as screening, 
brief intervention, and referral to treatment (SBIRT). SBIRT is recognized as the stan-
