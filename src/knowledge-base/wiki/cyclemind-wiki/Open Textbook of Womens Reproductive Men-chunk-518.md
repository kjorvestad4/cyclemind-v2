Course/Prognosis 
Trauma as manifested in the perinatal period exists on a spectrum of severity, ranging 
from adjustment disorders to variants of  PTSD and other specified trauma-r elated 
disorders. Women may enter the perinatal period with preexisting PTSD or may de -
velop symptoms after exposure to the actual or threatened pregnancy-related event, 
including the antepartum, labor and delivery, and postpartum phases. In addition to 
an acute course, trauma symptoms in resp onse to perinatal adverse events can also 
emerge as delayed or chronic at any point in time across the lifespan (Kim et al. 2015). 
Psychiatric morbidity may persist beyond the 12-month time frame that typically de-
fines the postpartum period. Perinatal trauma, both preexisting and de novo, carries a 
high incidence of comorbid conditions that complicate the trauma trajectory. Perinatal 
mood and anxiety disorders (e.g., generalized anxiety disorder and major depressive 
disorder) and substance use disorders often co-occur with perinatal PTSD (Grekin 
and O’Hara 2014; Vignato et al. 2018). 
The presence of premorbid or comorbid disorders, previous traumatic events, re -
petitive trauma such as recurrent pregnancy losses, or twin or multiple gestational 
pregnancies may complicate or prolong the course (Grekin and O’Hara 2014). Addi-
tionally, hormonal fluctuations, such as starting or stopping breastfeeding, resuming 
menses, or introducing contraception, may act to protect against or complicate trauma 
symptoms (Grekin and O’Hara 2014). Medical complications during the pregnancy, 
labor, or delivery that threaten the health or welfare of the mother or baby or require 
neonatal intervention may intensify symptoms (Grekin and O’Hara 2014; Seng et al. 
2009). Delayed or inadequate prenatal care and providing childcare to other young 
children in the home also contribute to a poor prognosis (Grekin and O’Hara 2014).
