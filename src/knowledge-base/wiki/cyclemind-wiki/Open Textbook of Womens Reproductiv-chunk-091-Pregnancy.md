First-Generation Antipsychotics 
Haloperidol and chlorpromazine have the most robust (although still limited) data re-
garding use in lactation. Both medications are secreted into breastmilk in small amounts. 
Limited data suggest no adverse effects in infants exposed to haloperidol monotherapy. 
Sedation was noted in some infants exposed to chlorpromazine through lactation. Al-
though reports of EPS as a result of lactation exposure are rare, they remain a theoretical 
risk in infants exposed to FGAs (Iqbal et al. 2005; Smith and Dubovsky 2017).

Estrogen Replacement Therapy 
Although no clear consensus has been found on the role of estrogen replacement ther-
apy in the treatment of women with schizoph renia, data suggest that it has promise 
as an augmentation strategy to antipsychotics. A 2012 meta-analysis of four random-
ized, double-blind, placebo-controlled trials totaling 214 patients found that estrogen 
therapy was more effective than placebo in reducing both positive and negative symp-
toms (Begemann et al. 2012). Two subsequent randomized controlled trials of 183 
(Kulkarni et al. 2015) and 100 (Weiser et al. 2019) women with schizophrenia reported 
that 200 µg of transdermal estradiol improved mainly positive symptoms, with mod-
erate effect sizes of ~0.4 compared with pl acebo. Interestingly, Weiser et al. (2019) 
noted that this improvement was specific for participants who were 38–46 years of age. 
Although there is much rationale to suggest that estrogen therapy might be most ef -
fective in women for whom estrogen levels have already begun to decline, this hypoth-
esis was tempered by the fact that Weiser et al. (2019) found no difference in baseline 
estrogen levels between younger and older participants. 
Other studies have shown improvement in negative and cognitive symptoms and a 
reduced need for anticholinergic medication in women with schizophrenia taking anti-
psychotic medication with adjunctive estradiol (Akhondzadeh et al. 2003; Kulkarni et 
al. 2015). Most data on estrogen augmentation have been in women 45 years of age and 
younger and thus may not be applicable to postmenopausal women. Among postmeno-
pausal women with schizophrenia, data are sparse and limited by design flaws. Studies 
in this population have focused on the disease severity and course among women with 
schizophrenia who are receiving hormone replacement therapy for nonpsychiatric 
symptoms of menopause compared with age-matched women with schizophrenia not 
receiving hormonal treatment. Nevertheless, one study showed that women who were 
taking hormone replacement therapy for menopausal symptoms required lower doses 
of antipsychotic medication than the control group and that the two groups did not dif-
fer significantly in terms of age, ethnicity, education, age of onset, and other factors (Lin-
damer et al. 2001). 
More recent research has focused on raloxifene, a selective estrogen receptor mod-
ulator used as a preventive treatment for postmenopausal osteoporosis. Raloxifene has 
both agonist and antagonist properties on estrogen receptors, with tissue-specific es-
trogen antagonism in uterine and breast tissue (Sadovsky and Adler 1998). Multiple 
studies have reported a decrease in both positive and negative symptoms among post-
menopausal women with schizophrenia with adjunctive use of raloxifene (Kianimehr 
et al. 2014; Kulkarni et al. 2010; Usall et al. 2011, 2016). A 2018 meta-analysis of ran -
domized controlled trials confirmed raloxifene’s utility in augmentation for improving 
both positive and negative symptoms in postmenopausal women (Wang et al. 2018).
