Perinatal depression—an umbrella term that includes both antenatal depression (AD) 
and postpartum depression (PPD)—is a seri ous mental health disorder affecting at 
least 15% of women in the developed world and 20%–40% of women worldwide (Ben-
nett et al. 2004; Gaynes et al. 2005; Le Strat et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 2002). With more 
than 4 million live births in the United States each year, at least 600,000 families will be 
affected, making depression the most common complication of childbirth (Grace et al. 
2003)—far outstripping such common complications as gestational diabetes (Dabelea 
et al. 2005), preterm birth (Saigal and Do yle 2008), and preeclampsia (Umesawa and 
Kobashi 2017). Perinatal depression affects the entire family, increasing partner con-
flict, disrupting mother–infant bonding, leading to poor occupational and social func-
tioning, and affecting the cognitive, emotional, and social development of the child 
(Conroy et al. 2012; Grace et al. 2003). The societal costs of untreated perinatal depres-
sion are severe, with a recent estimate of $14.2 billion for all births in the United States 
in 2017 (or $32,000 per mother–child pair) (Luca et al. 2019).

now know that pregnancy itself confers ne ither protection nor risk—a woman is no

both physicians and the public about how to  balance the potential risks of treatment 
with the potential risks of lack of treatment lags behind the evidence, leading to often 
unnecessary suffering on the part of mothers and children. 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Estimates of the rates of both AD and PPD vary widely. In the United States, most stud-
ies and current data from the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention show rates 
of 10%–15% during pregnancy (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention 2015; 
Gaynes et al. 2005), although some recent studies show rates as low as 6% in previously 
healthy women (Ashley et al. 2016). One factor that complicates the calculations of the 
incidence and prevalence of perinatal depre ssion is prior history of depression, be -
cause perinatal depression can represent either a new onset of a mood disorder or an 
exacerbation of a prior disorder. 
Rates around the world are similar to those of the United States and even higher, with 
higher rates more common in low- and middle-income countries (Al-Azri et al. 2016; 
Castro e Couto et al. 2016; George et al. 2016; Ogbo et al. 2018), with one recent study 
in Saudi Arabia finding a rate as high as 58% (Bawahab et al. 2017). In the postpartum 
period, a recent worldwide meta-analysis found an incidence of 12% and a prevalence 
of 17%, with rates being highest in the Middle East and lowest in Europe (Shorey et al. 
2018), whereas another meta-analysis focused on perinatal depression found a world-
wide prevalence of 11.9%, with rates highest in low- and middle-income countries 
(Woody et al. 2017). Recent country-specific studies find rates ranging from 14% to 
25% in low- and middle-income countries (Doe et al. 2017; Hartmann et al. 2017; Özcan 
et al. 2017; Upadhyay et al. 2017). In higher-income countries, rates are overall lower, 
but women in immigrant and minority groups have higher rates than women in other 
groups (Doe et al. 2017; Falah-Hassani et al. 2015). One recent meta-analysis found that 
Black women, overall, are more likely to report symptoms but are less likely to seek 
care due to cultural stigma about mental illness (Cannon and Nasrallah 2019).

Risk Factors 
The biological risk factors for perinata l depression, about which we have limited 
knowledge, are still evolving and are di scussed later (see “Pathophysiology”). Al -
though any woman can develop depression during pregnancy or postpartum, and the 
commonly cited “one in seven” figure for perinatal mood and anxiety disorders refers 
to the general population, identified risk factors increase a woman’s likelihood of be-
coming ill (Table 13–1). The strongest psychosocial risk factors for PPD are anxiety and 
depression in pregnancy, history of prior psychiatric illness, stressful life events during 
the perinatal period, and poor social support (Robertson et al. 2004). For women with 
prior affective illness, particularly prior MDD, studies show rates as high as 40% for 
PPD and 75% for the combined antenatal and postpartum periods (Guintivano et al. 
2018; Kimmel et al. 2015). Family history also plays a strong role: in the same study, 
53% of those with a family history of PPD developed the illness, compared with only 
11% of those without a family history. 
Other well-established risk factors include low socioeconomic status, single moth-
erhood, adolescent pregnancy, low education status, unwanted pregnancy, childhood 
trauma, intimate partner violence, and obstetrical and delivery complications (Gelaye 
et al. 2016; Kimmel et al. 2015; Lancaster et al. 2010; Stuart-Parrigon and Stuart 2014). 
Some risk factors vary according to cultural context; for example, stressful life events 
in one large meta-analysis were an import ant risk factor only in non-Asian samples, 
and the sex of the child may be a risk factor in some cultures (Robertson et al. 2004). 
Although many studies indicate higher rates in Black and Latinx populations, a lack 
of social support, lack of access, and lack of trust in the medical establishment may ex-
plain some of this discrepancy (Cannon and Nasrallah 2019). In addition, some studies 
find that ethnicity and race are no longer predictive once marital status, poverty, edu-
cation, and subjective socioeconomic status are taken into account (Dolbier et al. 2013). 
Other studies have indicated that social support can buffer symptom severity and that 
those buffering effects do not differ according to race or ethnicity (Pao et al. 2019).

Screening Methods 
The most important aspect of screening for perinatal depression as a routine compo-
nent of obstetrical care is that it be done. Implementing some method of screening is 
vastly preferable to not implementing it, so clinicians should not hesitate to put screen-
ing procedures into practice even if the ideal cannot be achieved. Screening can and 
should be carried out by any clinician wh o treats a perinatal woman, including not 
only obstetrical clinicians but also primary care and mental health clinicians. By far, the 
most commonly used tool is the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS; Figure 
13–1), which was created by a perinatal ps ychiatrist and has b een validated in both 
pregnancy and the postpartum period and translated into more than 50 languages 
(Cox et al. 1987).

questions), which can be used both to assess the anxious symptoms that are often part 
of perinatal depression and to evaluate for anxiety itself. Second, it excludes neuroveg-
etative and other physical symptoms of depression, such as changes in sleep patterns, 
appetite, and weight, that can be confused with symptoms of pregnancy and thus lead 
patients at times to dismiss them and clinicians to discount them even if endorsed. Fi-
nally, the EPDS is long enough to be relatively comprehensive while also being short 
enough to not be a burden; many patients can complete it in 5 minutes or less. (See Ta-

and ethnic groups. Although the factor structure of the scale appears to differ slightly 
among ethnic and racial groups (Chiu et al. 2017; Hartley et al. 2014; King 2012), no 
data have suggested that it is not valid as a screening tool in any of these populations. 
Although other screening tools lack the advantages described for perinatal patients, 
they can nonetheless be useful and are worth administering if the EPDS is not feasible 
(as in electronic medical records that have just one screening tool built in). Both the 
Beck Depression Inventory and the Zung Self-Rating Depression Scale are comprehen-
sive rating scales for depression, but at more than 20 questions each—including con -
stitutional symptoms—they may be burdensome to complete and have not been 
validated in this population (Schaefer et al. 1985; Wang and Gorenstein 2013). 
Screening in and of itself is not enough. A positive screening result on the EPDS or 
any other scale is not a diagnosis of depression; it must be followed by a clinical eval-
uation. Misuse of screening measures as diagnostic tools can lead to potential harm 
by leading the clinician either to underval ue a patient’s report of depressive symp -
toms or to misdiagnose a patient who does not meet criteria for a major depressive 
episode. In fact, studies on the EPDS show that nearly all patients with false-positive 
screening results, or patients who screened positive but did not have a diagnosis of 
depression on further evaluation, did ind eed have a diagnosable mental health con-
dition (Milgrom and Gemmill 2014). 
Although universal screening using the EPDS or other measures during and after 
pregnancy can increase the detection of perinatal depression, it is also important to ac-

Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria and Evaluation 
Ms. T is a 28-year-old first-time mother with no prior psychiatric history and a desired 
pregnancy. She is no longer involved with the baby’s father, but she lives with her sister, 
who is strongly supportive, and has other family members close by. She works as a 
teacher’s aide and is highly conscientious, with very firm ideas prior to birth of what 
an “ideal birth experience” will be. At 37 weeks, she presents for a routine check and is 
found to have elevated blood pressure and protein in her urine. She is hospitalized for 
induction, and eventually has a cesarean section delivery after failing to progress. Her 
baby is small for gestational age and has to spend 24 hours in intensive care for low 
blood sugar. 
In the initial days after the birth, Ms. T feels somewhat “giddy,” both laughing and 
crying easily. She experiences some difficulty with latching and poor milk supply, and 
within 3 weeks she is frequently tearful and confides to her sister that she is “a terrible 
mom.” She constantly reviews the events of the birth, convinced that she should and 
could have done something to prevent the cesarean section: “I should have known I had 
high blood pressure”; “I should have tried harder in labor.” She ruminates about these 
events and about her breastfeeding difficulti es and begins to have thoughts that her 
child might be better off without her. 
The diagnosis of perinatal depression is at once simple and complex: simple be -
cause the core diagnostic features of MDD can also be used in the perinatal period, 
complex because unique clusters and emphases of symptoms occur in this period and 
because not all clinicians and governing bo dies agree on the definition. The current 
definition, according to DSM-5 (American Psychiatric Association 2013), is the onset 
of a major depressive episode during pregnancy or within 4 weeks of childbirth (see 
Box 13–1). This definition conflates AD and PPD, which may be two biologically dis-
tinct entities, and although onset within 4 weeks postpartum likely captures those 
women for whom parturition is a biological trigger for symptoms, it fails to capture 
the significant population who may have onset later as a result of psychosocial trig -
gers or hormonal disruption due to weaning (Burke et al. 2019).

When evaluating a woman for depression, it is important to understand not only 
whether she meets criteria for the disorder but also how to talk with her about difficult 
and painful feelings and about risk. Some women may feel ashamed or unwilling to 
admit thoughts that are scary to them, and they must be approached in an open, non-
judgmental fashion. Some women may also be concerned about adverse consequences 
(e.g., referral to child protective services) that may result from being open about such 
thoughts. See Tables 13–3 and 13–4 for tips. 
Clinical Presentation 
The clinical presentation of perinatal depression has many of the features of depressive 
episodes outside the postpartum period, with low mood, anhedonia, poor energy and 
concentration, low motivation, feelings of low self-worth, and sometimes suicidal ide-
ation. Changes in sleep, appetite, and libido can also occur but are at times difficult to 
discern due to the effects of pregnancy (or to the patient’s or physician’s assumption of 
what pregnancy does). Anxious distress is a common feature of depression in this pe-
riod, with high rates of ru mination and worry—often ab out the mother’s or baby’s 
health—and intrusive thoughts (Collardeau et al. 2019; DeJong et al. 2016; Moulds et 
al. 2018). Recent research has begun to iden tify different clinical phenotypes within 
perinatal depression. The international consortium Postpartum Depression: Action To-

Ms. U is a 35-year-old married woman in a stable relationship. She has a 3-year-old son 
and a prior history of one episode of depres sion in college (treated to remission with 
citalopram and psychotherapy), mild premen strual mood lability, and an episode of 
PPD following the birth of her son. That episode was severe and resulted in a brief hos-
pitalization followed by 4 weeks in a day hospital program. In the hospital, Ms. U was 
treated with escitalopram and lorazepam; on discharge, the lorazepam was discontin-
ued, and she eventually stab ilized on 20 mg of escitalopram. Ms. U is now 21 weeks 
pregnant with her second child, and prior to the conception she elected to discontinue 
the escitalopram because “I was doing so well, and I didn’t want to hurt my baby.” 
For several months prior to conceiving and during her first trimester, Ms. U did well, 
maintaining a stable mood and prioritizing self-care with occasional sessions with her 
therapist and a daily exercise routine. At 15 weeks’ gestation, however, she began to feel 
the return of her depressive symptoms, characterized by low self-worth, low motiva -
tion, and significant anxious distress. She feared for the safety of her husband and son 
and began following the husband when he left in the morning to ensure that he dropped 
their son off at day care. She has been calling the daycare multiple times each day to 
check up on her son, and she calls her husband frequently at work to make sure he is 
all right. She knows rationally that this is not necessary, but she cannot help it. 
Differential Diagnosis 
Although diagnosis can be straightforward when classic depressive symptoms alone 
are present, several other disorders can be confused with depression in the perinatal pe-
riod (Table 13–5). “Baby blues,” which affects up to 75% of women (Nonacs and Cohen 
1998), refers to mild mood lability in the immediate postpartum period. Some women 
with perinatal mood and anxiety symptoms may mistakenly believe that their symp-
toms are consistent with the blues, but both patients and clinicians should know that 
this resolves by 2 weeks postpartum and is  never associated with severe symptoms, 
such as suicidality. Symptoms of anxiety disorders may overlap with those of depres-
sive disorders, with a high rate of comorbidity between the two (Jordan and Minikel 
2019). Medical disorders, such as hypothyroidism, anemia, or the rare Sheehan’s disor-
der, may present with symptoms such as lethargy and fatigue, which may be confused 
with symptoms of depression (Kang et al. 2020; Wilson et al. 2019). Either intoxication 
or withdrawal from substances can be confused with symptoms of depression, so sub-
stance use disorders must always be included in the differential diagnosis. Finally, any 
woman presenting with depressive symptoms in the perinatal period may in fact have 
bipolar disorder—in fact, some research in dicates that a high proportion of women 
with first-time depressive episodes in the postpartum later go on to be diagnosed with
