Sleep Deprivation 
In addition to dysfunctional beliefs an d behaviors, sleep deprivation postpartum 
(whether via modulation of hormonal or immunological factors or via separate mech-
anisms) is likely a strong risk factor for obsessive-compulsive symptoms in vulnerable 
women. At least one study has shown that most women with OCD onset or exacerba-
tion postpartum also have concomitant anxiety or mood disorders, which may drive 
sleep disturbances or be exacerbated by them (Arnold 1999; Miller et al. 2013). OCD, 
outside of the postpartum period, has been  associated with reduced sleep duration, 
more frequent sleep interruptions, delayed sleep phase disorder, and rapid eye move-
ment sleep disturbances, potentially making postpartum women who are vulnerable 
to OCD even more sensitive to the typical sleep impairment that comes from partici-
pating in overnight infant care and lactation (Nordahl et al. 2018; Paterson et al. 2013; 
Sharma 2019). 
Protecting healthy sleep behaviors from postpartum obsessive-compulsive symp-
toms is an added challenge. Extrapolating from research on general OCD, which has 
found that both obsessions and compulsions can compete with sleep, postpartum ob-
sessions involving worry about the baby may make it hard to resume sleep after inter-
ruptions for overnight care, and compulsive behaviors common to postpartum OCD, 
such as checking on an infant while sleeping or cleaning to avoid contamination, may 
decrease the hours available to sleep by using up time (Sharma 2019; Timpano et al. 
2014). Cultural expectations around a woman’s role as primary caregiver, especially 
overnight, may make it harder for women to ask for assistance in protecting their own 
sleep. In cultures that prioritize work outside of the home, women staying home with 
the baby in the immediate postpartum may err in protecting the sleep of their partners 
leaving for jobs; arguably the prioritization should be flipped in the postpartum pe -
riod to preserve sleep as much as possible for women at risk for mental illness. Regard-
less of its cause, sleep deprivation can both trigger and sustain obsessive-compulsive 
symptoms (Sharma 2019). 
Coping with medical comorbidities, such as autoimmune diseases, and their treat-
ment may also ignite flares of OCD in th e postpartum. A large registry-based study 
found that women with OCD are more likely to have any autoimmune disease, with 
the top five associations being found for Sjogren’s syndrome, celiac disease, Guillain-
Barré syndrome, Crohn’s disease, and Hashimoto’s thyroiditis (Mataix-Cols et al. 
2018). During pregnancy, many autoimmune diseases abate in intensity , with subse-
quent flares postpartum or with postpartum status increasing the risk for developing 
a new autoimmune disease (Adams Waldor f and Nelson 2008). Some proinflamma -
tory cytokines have been found to be el evated in the immediate postpartum period 
(Cheng and Pickler 2014; Osborne and Monk 2013). Thus, after delivery, when OCD 
risk is highest, inflammatory factors may play a role in triggering OCD, and symptoms
