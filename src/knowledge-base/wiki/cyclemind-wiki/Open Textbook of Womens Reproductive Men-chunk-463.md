Psychosocial Supports 
Despite the severity of PPP and the emphasis on safe and effective emergency inter -
ventions, psychosocial support is also a cr ucial part of management. Involving part-
ners and other loved ones in psychoeducation and approaching treatment with an eye 
toward family systems is crucially important. Providing support for the mother–infant 
dyad is critical both in the acute setting and beyond. Last, psychosocial interventions 
around behavioral management, such as the importance of sleep, are also important 
interventions (Sharma and Mazmanian 2003). 
Conclusion 
PPP is a rare condition that may present with a rapid onset of symptoms early in the 
postpartum period. PPP presents significantly elevated risk to mother and child if not 
swiftly diagnosed and treated. Because mothers with PPP most often require inpa -
tient treatment at initial presentation, careful attention to the mother–baby dyad, fam-
ily system, and maintaining lactation (if desired by the patient) are additional but 
critical components of care. Women with a prior history of PPP and women with bi -
polar disorder are at highest risk of developing PPP and should be treated prophylac-
tically in the postpartum period if not already receiving maintenance medication 
during pregnancy. Although benzodiazepines, antipsycho tics, lithium, and ECT all 
have evidence to support their use in the acute treatment of PPP , lithium has the stron-
gest evidence to support its use in prophylactic treatment. 
Key Points 
• Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a treatable psychiatric emergency that requires 
immediate evaluation. 
• Symptoms include mood fluctuation, alterations of consciousness, and abnor-
malities of thought and perception arising rapidly within the first days to weeks 
after childbirth. 
• Risk evaluation is critical given high rates of suicide and infanticide. 
• A personal history of bipolar disorder is the biggest risk factor for PPP , and 
many women may eventually be diagnosed with bipolar disorder. 
• Lithium, antipsychotics, benzodiazepines and ECT have evidence for treat -
ment of acute PPP . 
• Lithium is the recommended medication for prophylaxis. 
• Psychosocial support includes psychoeducation for family, attention to sleep, 
and mother–infant bonding.
