Other Medication Treatment 
Because PPD arises at a time of vast changes in reproductive hormones, hormonal rem-
edies have seemed an obvious route to try and have been cited as perhaps more ap -
pealing to women who are reluctant to try psychotropic drugs (Moses-Kolko et al. 
2009). Some early case reports and open-label trials showed promise for the use of es-
trogen (usually transdermal estradiol) to treat PPD (Gregoire et al. 1996), particularly 
in women with a deficiency of estradiol (Ahokas et al. 1999, 2001) or those who also 
had premenstrual mood symptoms (Studd 2014). A randomized trial in 2015 was de-
signed to compare transdermal estradiol with placebo but was unable to evaluate this 
question fully because serum concentrations of estradiol on a variety of doses were not 
higher than those of women given placebo (Wisner et al. 2015). A similar randomized 
trial in 2020 showed promising differences in response between placebo and estradiol 
groups, but the numbers were too small to determine whether these differences were 
due to chance (Li et al. 2020), leaving unanswered the question of whether transdermal 
estradiol might be effective as a treatment for PPD. 
While the field was failing to find an answer as to whether estradiol could be used 
as treatment, other researchers chose to investigate the progesterone system, focusing 
on the metabolite allopregnanolone. Allopregnanolone is an allosteric modulator of the 
GABAA receptor, and the link between this system and PPD made it an attractive treat-
ment target. Brexanolone, a synthetic version of allopregnanolone for injection, was re-
cently developed and tested in three do uble-blind, randomized, placebo-controlled 
trials, each of which showed a reduction in depressive symptoms compared with pla-
cebo (Kanes et al. 2017; Meltzer-Brody et al. 2018). Integrated data from all three trials 
showed a separation from placebo both at 60 hours (primary endpoint) and 30 days 
(secondary endpoint) (Meltzer-Brody and Ka nes 2020), and on the strength of these 
studies brexanolone became in 2019 the first drug to be FDA approved for the treatment 
of PPD. Although the excitement of having a “first-ever” drug has been substantial, 
critics have pointed out the large placebo effects in each trial. In addition, the difficulties 
of administration (a 3-day infusion requiring an inpatient stay, with few data yet about 
breastfeeding safety, and a price tag of $34,000) mean that this drug at the moment is 
usable by only a tiny minority of women. An oral compound is currently in testing. 
Nonpharmacological Somatic Treatments 
Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) is an effective option for the treatment of severe mood 
symptoms, especially in the context of associated catatonia, psychotic agitation, suicid-
ality, or severe physical decline (e.g., malnutrition or dehydration) (Ward et al. 2018).

ECT offers a rapid treatment response and serves as an alternative to pharmacological 
treatment or when pharmacotherapy fails. The safety and efficacy of ECT during preg-
nancy have been established over decades and support its role as a treatment option 
for pregnant women when clinically indica ted (Ward et al. 2018). ECT also has evi -
dence to support its efficacy in the treatm ent of postpartum affective disorders, and 
some suggest it should be considered a first-line treatment in women with severe 
symptoms in this setting (Forray and Ostr off 2007). As with pharmacotherapy, any 
risks of ECT in pregnancy must be weighed against the risks of untreated mental ill -
ness. The risks and adverse reactions in pregnancy are similar to those in nonpregnant 
patients and include memory loss, prolonged seizure, and risks related to anesthesia 
(Ward et al. 2018). ECT is not associated with increased risk of congenital malforma -
tions, miscarriage, preterm delivery, or infant neurodevelopmental concerns. The most 
common reported maternal risk is premature contractions, which have been found to 
occur in up to 24% of patients and is seen most frequently in the latter half of pregnancy 
(Ward et al. 2018). The most reported fetal risk is transient fetal bradyarrhythmias, 
likely due to hypoxia (Anderson and Reti 2009). 
Although ECT is a safe and efficacious treatment option for the treatment of depres-
sion during pregnancy, its accessibility to pregnant women is often limited by logistical 
considerations and by the comfort level of community ECT providers in treating preg-
nant patients. ECT during pregnancy should be done in a setting that allows for obstet-
rical monitoring and support and for access to neonatal care if necessary. Further 
specific and practical protocol recommendations regarding ECT during pregnancy can 
be found in a recent comprehensive review (Ward et al. 2018). Modification of medica-
tions (e.g., tapering off anticonvulsants) may be required for patients considering ECT. 
Repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS), although a much newer tech-
nique than ECT, is also building a body of evidence for both safety and efficacy in 
pregnancy and the postpartum. rTMS is approved by the FDA for use in patients who 
have failed at least one antidepressant. It has the potential to be widely useful in the 
perinatal period because it avoids questions of fetal risk posed by some medications 
and is also less invasive than ECT. It nevertheless represents a significant patient bur-
den because treatment is generally carried out 5 days a week. Randomized controlled 
trials are lacking, but the open-label pilot studies and case series that have been con-
ducted have been associated with an improvement in symptoms, with no indication 
of adverse effects for the fetus (Felipe and Ferrão 2016; Johansen et al. 2019). rTMS has 
also been studied for PPD, with promising initial results (Garcia et al. 2010). 
Behavioral Treatments 
Psychotherapy 
For many women with perinatal depression, particularly those with mild to moderate 
symptoms, psychotherapy will be the first-choice treatment. There is considerable ev-
idence of the effectiveness of manualized , evidence-based psychotherapies for both 
prevention and treatment of perinatal depression, with the best evidence for cognitive-
behavioral therapy (CBT) and interpersonal therapy. Two systematic reviews have ad-
dressed psychotherapy in both antenatal and postpartum populations (Loughnan et 
al. 2019; Sockol et al. 2011) and found good overall effect sizes, with some evidence that 
effect sizes may be greater for postpartum than for antenatal women. For postpartum

Sleep Interventions 
Both education about sleep hygiene and CBT for insomnia have some evidence of ef-
ficacy in the perinatal population, but research in this area is still young. Studies have 
focused largely on infant sleep regulation, and those that have examined maternal de-
pressive symptoms have found a positive effe ct (Hiscock et al. 2014; Johansen et al. 
2019; Phillips et al. 2010; Smart and Hiscock 2007). Two preliminary open pilot studies 
of CBT for insomnia in perinatal women have promising results (Swanson et al. 2013; 
Tomfohr-Madsen et al. 2017). 
Alternative and Complementary Treatments 
Psychotherapy and psychopharmacological agents at this point hold the greatest body 
of evidence for safety and efficacy in pregnancy and lactation, but evidence is building 
for some alternative and comp lementary therapies. Because patients often see these 
modalities as desirable, clinicians must understand the evidence base and know which 
modalities have evidence of safety and which may be helpful. Although many patients 
believe such approaches will be safe “because they are natural,” the reality is that such 
agents are outside of the FDA approval process and thus unregulated, so there is little 
control over them. Most vitamins, minerals, and nutraceuticals have little evidence of 
either safety or efficacy and should thus be avoided in pregnancy, with the possible ex-
ception of omega-3 fatty acids (Reza et al. 2018). Bright light therapy has been found to 
be both safe and acceptable and superior to a dim-light placebo for AD, but a random-
ized controlled trial in PPD failed to show a difference, and more research is clearly 
needed (Johansen et al. 2019). Physical activity in general has evidence of safety and ac-
ceptability in AD and PPD, although research is lacking on whether it is effective for ei-
ther treating or preventing perinatal depression (Reza et al. 2018). More evidence exists 
for yoga, which is also safe and acceptable and shows greater reduction of depressive 
symptoms when compared with waitlist, treatment as usual, massage, social support, 
and parenting education (Davis et al. 2015; Field et al. 2012, 2013a, 2013b). For further
