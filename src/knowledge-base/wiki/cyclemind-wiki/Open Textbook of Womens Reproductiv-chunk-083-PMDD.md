Key Points 
• Women with bipolar disord er (BD) may experience exacerbation of illness 
during times of reproductive hormone fluctuation, such as the premenstrual, 
postpartum, and perimenopausal periods. 
• The potential for pregnancy introduces added complexity in the treatment of 
women with BD, and family planning is a critical component of psychiatric care. 
• Treatment planning for women with BD dur ing and after pregnancy should nec-
essarily include psychological and pharmacological options. Adequate sleep 
should be a priority. 
• Decisions around maintenance medications in the perinatal period strongly 
influence the risk of recurrence for women with BD. 
• Lithium is one of the most established treatments for BD, and growing evidence 
regarding its safety profile supports its use in women who are unlikely to main-
tain euthymia with lamotrigine or antipsychotic monotherapy. 
• Lamotrigine’s relative reproductive safety and emerging evidence base in the 
perinatal period make it an emerging first-line option for treatment of BD in preg-
nancy, particularly for women with a preponderance of depressive episodes.

Postpartum Psychosis 
Caitlin Hasser, M.D. 
Jovana Martinovic, M.D. 
Pooja Lakshmin, M.D.

Melisa Olgun, B.A.

Lauren M. Osborne, M.D. 
Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a rare but severe perinatal psychiatric syndrome 
that is considered a true psychiatric emergency. Women with PPP typically present 
with a rapid onset of affective, psychotic, and cognitive symptoms in the early post -
partum period. Despite the name, the sympto ms of PPP often occur in the setting of 
known or new-onset bipolar illness or, less often, as a syndrome limited to the post -
partum period. Although PPP has been desc ribed in the medical literature for hun -
dreds of years, knowledge around its etio logy, treatment, and prevention has been 
slow to accumulate (Osborne 2018). Howeve r, recent data suggest important guide -
lines to inform optimal treatment with the potential to shorten th e course of illness 
and reduce risk of recurrence in subsequent pregnancies (Bergink et al. 2012, 2015). 
Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
PPP is rare, with an incidence ranging from 0.9 to 2.6 per 1,000 births according to in-
ternational data (Osborne 2018; VanderKruik et al. 2017). This incidence is not known 
to differ substantially in different cultures.

Risk Factors 
Prior history of PPP and personal or family history of bipolar disorder are the greatest 
risk factors for PPP (Table 16–1) (Bergink et al. 2016; Jones and Craddock 2001). Prim-
iparity is a risk factor for PPP (Di Florio et al. 2014); if a woman’s first delivery is not 
complicated by PPP , the likelihood of developing PPP in subsequent deliveries is 
markedly lower (Bergink et al. 2016). Life stressors have not been identified as risk fac-
tors, and studies of obstetrical risk are inconsistent (Brockington et al. 1990; Hellerstedt 
et al. 2013; Meltzer-Brody et al. 2017; Perry et al. 2016). It is important to clarify that 
many women have no prior risk factors. For example, in a large retrospective study, 
only 33% of women had an antecedent history of a mood episode (Blackmore et al. 
2013). Given the association with personal and family history of bipolar disorder, PPP 
is often conceptualized as a bipolar spectrum disorder, although somewhere between 
20% and 50% of cases appear to be limited to the postpartum (Bergink et al. 2016). 
Screening and Diagnostic Criteria 
No screening tools are available for PPP . Although the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression 
Scale can uncover symptoms of depression or the Young Mania Rating Scale symptoms 
of mania, neither these nor any other tool is designed for PPP in particular, and neither 
can capture both the affective and psychotic symptoms. DSM-5 (American Psychiatric 
Association 2013) does not recognize PPP as  a distinct disorder , although the “with 
postpartum onset” specifier is added to a brief psychotic disorder diagnosis if the onset 
is during pregnancy or within 4 weeks postpartum. Because PPP is an affective psycho-
sis, this method of classifying it is incomplete. PPP has been categorized in the Interna-
tional Classification of Diseases under category F53.1, used to describe severe mental and 
behavioral disorders associated with the puerperium (Di Florio et al. 2016). 
Clinical Presentation 
Among women with PPP , the typical time to onset generally has been 3–10 days, al -
though women with known bipolar disorder may, on average, experience onset more 
immediately postpartum (Bergink et al. 2016) (Table 16–2). Symptoms last an average 
of 1 month for women with manic features and 2.5 months for women with mixed or 
depressed features (Bergink et al. 2011) . The onset of symptoms within a period 
marked by transition and change can make early identification and treatment difficult

A prospective Dutch cohort study showed three distinct subgroups of patients with 
PPP , with approximately 41% classified as depressed, 34% classified as manic, and 25%

ness, disorientation, or derealization. Visual hallucinations and delusions of childbirth

broadcasting, withdrawal, or auditory hallucinations with running commentary.

from 8% to 50% (Chandra et al. 2002; Kamp erman et al. 2017). Up to 4% of mothers

Pathophysiology 
Childbirth is a clear biological trigger for PPP , but despite this, its etiology is poorly 
understood. The pathophysiology of PPP is an  area of active research that includes 
genetics, hormonal changes, immune response, and circadian rhythm disruption. Ge-
netic research on first lifetime onset of PPP is scant, but the literature investigating 
PPP in patients with an underlying bipolar disorder identifies genetic variants of the 
serotonin transporter gene and potential si gnal at chromosome 16p13 (Coyle et al. 
2000; Jones et al. 2007; Kumar et al. 2007). Genetic polymorphism of METTL13, which 
is involved in estrogen receptor–induced gene transcription, has also been implicated 
(Thippeswamy et al. 2017). Ho rmonally, significant changes occur in corticotropin -
releasing hormone, adrenocorticotropic hormone, estrogen, and progesterone levels 
around parturition, leading many to consider particularly the drop in estrogen and its 
modulation of dopamine as a potential etiology, although evidence for this is not strong 
(Bergink et al. 2016). Given that peripheral levels of reproductive hormones are not dif-
ferent in affected versus unaffected women, if an association exists, it is more likely that 
a subset of women is particularly vulnerable to the rapid hormonal changes. Immune 
activation in the postpartum period has also been considered due to overlap with au-
toimmune conditions such as  e n c e p h a l i t i s ,  t h y r o i d i t i s ,  and differences in levels of 
proinflammatory and immunosuppressive cells in women with histories of PPP (Ber-
gink et al. 2013, 2016; Kumar et al. 2017; Weigelt et al. 2013). One hypothesis posits that 
dysregulated immune functioning may affect remyelination processes postpartum 
(Dazzan et al. 2018). Finally, sleep disruption associated with labor, delivery, and the 
early postpartum period has been implicated, particularly in women with bipolar dis-
order who have histories of mania triggered by sleep loss (Lewis et al. 2018).
