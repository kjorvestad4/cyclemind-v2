Differential Diagnosis 
Even when presenting during times of reproductive events, BD is diagnosed based on 
a thorough psychiatric history. The differen tial diagnosis includes unipolar depres-
sion, especially anxious phenotype, as well as other illnesses with some overlapping 
mood symptoms, including PTSD and borderline personality disorder. Additionally, 
intoxication or withdrawal from substances such as cocaine or alcohol can mimic affec-
tive episodes, and therefore substance use disorders should also be considered. Aside 
from psychiatric illness, many medical illnesses (e.g., thyroid disorders) and medica-
tions (e.g., steroids) may also cause mood symptoms and therefore should be included 
in the differential diagnosis (Roberts 2019). See Table 15–4 for additional differential di-
agnostic considerations in the setting of acute mania (Freudenreich et al. 2019). 
Pathophysiology 
Clinical and epidemiological research has demonstrated that times of hormonal fluc-
tuation place women with BD at greater risk for relapse (Frey and Dias 2014; Gogos et 
al. 2019). This suggests that reproductive hormones such as estradiol and progesterone 
play significant roles in the pathophysiology of BD, and perinatal BD in particular. Un-
fortunately, there is a paucity of direct research on specific mechanisms underlying BD 
in the perinatal period. The reason for this might be the difficulty in understanding the 
pathophysiology of BD in general. Althou gh many psychiatric disorders likely have 
multiple biological pathways that lead to similar symptomatic manifestations, this 
might be particularly true for BD (Maletic and Raison 2014). As such, current research 
suggests a diverse set of neurobiological aberrations in BD, including genetic and epi-
genetic factors, inflammation and oxidativ e stress, circadian rhythm abnormalities, 
differences in brain networks regulating emotion, and impairments in neuronal signal-
ing and plasticity (Maletic and Raison 2014; Muneer 2016; Newberg et al. 2008). In this 
section, we provide a brief review of research on the pathophysiology of BD, focusing 
on potential interactions with reproductive hormones.
