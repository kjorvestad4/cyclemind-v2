Intimate Partner Violence/Sexual Trauma Screening 
The ACOG promotes universal screening for IPV at the first prenatal visit, once per 
trimester, and at postpartum visits (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecol-
ogists 2012). All nonpregnant patients should be screened at every routine gynecolog-
ical visit, as well as at family planni ng and preconception vi sits. The USPSTF also 
recommends universal screening of all women of childbearing age (Curry et al. 2018). 
Table 18–5 lists groups that are at particularly high risk of IPV and should receive pri-
ority for screening. 
Screening methods vary based on specific pregnancy and postpartum concerns. Be-
cause screening tools rarely account for ethnic or racial differences, the development of 
more culturally sensitive screening tools may provide better information on racial mi-
norities and PTSD. Black, indigenous, and Hispanic women may not disclose trauma 
as freely, may not conceptualize what they have experienced chronically as traumatic, 
and may screen negative on validated instruments that have largely been tested only 
on white populations. Clinical staff should be educated in principles of TIC to address 
these limitations in a culturally sensitive way.
