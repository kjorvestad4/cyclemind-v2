Attachment is specific to the infant and a particular caregiver. Infants can form different 
attachment styles to individual caretakers; they may have a secure attachment with one 
caregiver and an insecure attachment with another (Dagan and Sagi-Schwartz 2018). 
Many studies have looked at characteristics of children who were observed to have 
insecure attachment early in life. These ch ildren appear to be at increased risk for 
greater negativity in early childhood, slower cognitive development, and externalizing 
behaviors in adolescence (Boldt et al. 2017; Ding et al. 2014). In addition to its effect on 
interpersonal relationships, attachment also predicts ability to self-regulate (Davis et 
al. 2011). Infants with disorganized attachment demonstrate greater increases in corti-
sol in response to a stressful situation than infants with organized attachment (Bernard 
and Dozier 2010). 
Attachment provides the earliest model for human relationships. Thus, children 
with insecure attachment may grow up to be adults who struggle to form stable, lov-
ing, and trusting relationships with others. This research has led to efforts to charac -
terize attachment patterns in adults, and the Adult Attachment Interview (AAI) was 
created to address this need (George et al. 1985). This instrument asks adults to recount 
aspects of their childhood and childhood relationships with each parent, then scores 
their responses. Their ability to form a coherent and nuanced narrative, independent 
of the actual events experienced, is analyz ed to gain insight into adult individuals’ 
conceptualization of relationship with their own attachment figures and family envi-
ronment as a child and the degree to which an organized pattern of experience has 
been developed. AAI categorizes adults into four categories of secure-autonomous (F), 
dismissing (D), preoccupied (E), and disoriented/disorganized (U/D). For example, 
an adult whose AAI reveals patchy or broken narratives without much substance may 
be categorized as being in the dismissive category. 
The dynamic nature of attachment offers hope for the potential benefits of clinical 
intervention. Parents and caregivers who have psychiatric illnesses that impede their 
ability to respond warmly and consistently to the needs of infants and toddlers will 
benefit from standard treatment of those illnesses, as well as parent–child dyad ther-
apies or coaching about the types of parental behaviors necessary for healthy child de-
velopment. When infants have developed a disorganized attachment, interventions 
to address this are associated with improvements in the security and organization of 
attachment (Cicchetti et al. 2006; Facompré et al. 2018).

Neurobiology of Attachment 
The parent–infant relationship serves to set the neurobiological tone for the infant 
brain, which in turn affects how that infant deals with stress. Critical to infant neuro-
development—and foundational to the de velopment of attachment—are the opposing 
concepts of the promotion of self-regulation and the transmission of stress. Interest -
ingly, there is some evidence for genomic intergenerational transmission of trauma. 
Women who survived the Tutsi genocide while pregnant had children with alterations 
in the hippocampus-pituitary-adrenal axis (HPA), including lower cortisol and higher 
mineralocorticoid receptor signaling together with genomic changes in glucocorticoid 
receptors (Perroud et al. 2014). 
Surprisingly, there is also evidence of pa ternal transmission of a trauma history, 
presumably through genomic modifications of spermal chromatin. Children of male 
Holocaust survivors show a modification in a glucocorticoid receptor that is associ -
ated with an increase in stress reactivity (Yehuda et al. 2014). These findings have been 
validated in animal studies showing that male rodents exposed to an olfactory fear 
paradigm transmit odor-specific fear to th eir progeny. These progeny then, in turn, 
transmit that fear to their own progeny, meaning that the biological underpinnings 
(through epigenetic changes transmitted via spermal chromatin) exist for the transmis-
sion of trauma through multiple generations (Dias and Ressler 2014). Although stress 
is clearly transmitted along both parental lines to infants who may show evidence of 
that stress later in life, those markings can be offset by postnatal experiences such as 
parent–infant relationships that promote self-regulation and thus enhance resiliency.
