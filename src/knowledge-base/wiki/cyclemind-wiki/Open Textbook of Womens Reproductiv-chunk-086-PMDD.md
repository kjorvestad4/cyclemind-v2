Key Points 
• Postpartum psychosis (PPP) is a treatable psychiatric emergency that requires 
immediate evaluation. 
• Symptoms include mood fluctuation, alterations of consciousness, and abnor-
malities of thought and perception arising rapidly within the first days to weeks 
after childbirth. 
• Risk evaluation is critical given high rates of suicide and infanticide. 
• A personal history of bipolar disorder is the biggest risk factor for PPP , and 
many women may eventually be diagnosed with bipolar disorder. 
• Lithium, antipsychotics, benzodiazepines and ECT have evidence for treat -
ment of acute PPP . 
• Lithium is the recommended medication for prophylaxis. 
• Psychosocial support includes psychoeducation for family, attention to sleep, 
and mother–infant bonding.

Schizophrenia is a psychiatric illness that requires ongoing treatment through-
out the lifespan. It is characterized by abnormalities of perception, thought, behavior, 
affect, and cognition (American Psychiatric Association 2013). Although the onset for 
both men and women is typically in early adulthood, women are more likely than men 
to experience onset in middle age and to have a better overall disease prognosis (Abel 
et al. 2010). An understanding of the role of estrogen in the disease course of schizo -
phrenia is growing, and emerging data sugge st that estrogen plays a protective role 
against psychosis and that periods of estrogen decline are associated with new onset 
or exacerbation of psychotic symptoms (Riecher-Rössler et al. 2018). 
Historically, women with schizophrenia have had reduced fertility relative to the 
general population. Deinstitutionalization of persons living with mental illness and 
advances in psychopharmacology over several decades, however, have contributed 
to an increase in fertility rates (Vigod et al. 2012). At present, most women with schizo-
phrenia will have at least one pregnancy (Howard et al. 2001). Thus, proficiency in the 
psychiatric care of women with schizophrenia during the perinatal period is an essen-
tial skill for a psychiatric clinician.

Epidemiology 
Rates/Incidence 
Schizophrenia is a relatively uncommon disorder with a lifetime prevalence of 7.2 per 
1,000 persons (Jablensky 2017). It is more common in men than women, with a male-
to-female incidence ratio of 1.4:1 (Aleman et al. 2003; McGrath et al. 2008). Although 
some have argued that the reported increased risk for men may be due to methodolog-
ical limitations of existing research (i.e., studies focused on inpatient settings show a 
sample bias toward men, who on average have a more severe disease course), the sex 
ratio appears to persist even when contro lling for pertinent factors (Aleman et al. 
2003). Because women, on average, show a less severe disease course and a later onset 
of illness course than men, it remains poss ible that detection bias is a contributor to 
the sex ratio. Interestingly, although the peak age of onset for men is in the early 20s, 
women show a wider distribution in age of onset, with a peak in the early 20s fol -
lowed by a secondary peak in middle age. Thus, there is a switch from male to female 
predominance at older ages (Abel et al. 2010). 
Risk Factors 
Schizophrenia is a complex neurodevelopmental disorder with molecular, biological, 
and psychosocial risk factors at specific developmental periods (Table 17–1). It is a highly 
heritable condition; twin studies show 40%–50% concordance for monozygotic twins 
and 6%–10% for dizygotic twins (Cardno and Gottesman 2000). Large-scale genome-
wide association studies have identified dozens of susceptibility genes, each of which 
makes a small contribution to illness risk (Foley et al. 2017). Gene/Environment inter-
actions are critical for the conceptualization of schizophrenia risk. Several genes elevate 
risk potentially through epigenetic mechanisms in the context of specific environmental 
risk factors, such as prenatal exposure to infection or stress, obstetrical complications, 
nutrition, cannabis use, migration, urbanicity, and trauma exposure. Acquired brain in-
jury, anti-NMDA receptor encephalitis, and epilepsy are also risk factors for schizophre-
nia (Clarke et al. 2012).

Perinatal complications of various types have been associated with later develop-
ment of schizophrenia in population-based cohort studies. These include pregnancy-
related (e.g., preeclampsia and gestational diabetes) or delivery complications (e.g., 
emergency cesarean delivery), and abnormal growth measures (e.g., low birth weight) 
(Clarke et al. 2012). Although the exact mechanism is unknown, it has been hypothe-
sized that hypoxia experienced during feta l development, labor and delivery, or the 
neonatal period may interact with genetic vulnerability to increase schizophrenia risk 
(Cannon et al. 2000). Prenatal infections across a number of pathogens, including influ-
enza and rubella, may increase risk for schizophrenia, particularly with exposure in the 
first trimester, likely due to an immune-related mechanism (Clarke et al. 2012). Diverse 
prenatal stress–related exposures, such as war or death of the mother’s partner, also el-
evate risk, although multiple other confounders affect this finding, such as increased 
risk of infection, fetal hypoxia, or malnou rishment (Clarke et al. 2012). Maternal mi -
cronutrient deficiencies, such as vitamin D or choline deficiency, may increase risk for 
schizophrenia (Clarke et al. 2012; McGrath et al. 2010; Ross et al. 2013). Childhood 
trauma may increase the risk of psychosis up to fourfold (Heins et al. 2011). Cannabis 
use has an independent risk for development of schizophrenia, particularly in those 
with specific alleles of the catechol- O-methyltransferase (COMT) gene (Clarke et al. 
2012). In short, schizophrenia risk is related to a complex synergistic interaction be -
tween genetic susceptibility and environm ental exposures beginning in the prenatal 
environment and continuing through adulth ood. Given that schizophrenia is based 
on a stress-diathesis model, there may be sex-specific differences in stress response, 
neurotransmitter function, or epigenetics that influence the greater risk in men (Rem-
ington and Seeman 2015). 
Diagnostic Criteria 
Symptoms of psychotic disorders are genera lly clustered into po sitive and negative 
symptoms. Positive symptoms include delusions, hallucinations, and disorganization 
of thought or behavior. Delusions are fixed, false beliefs that persist despite clear evi -
dence to the contrary. They may be bizarre (clearly implausible and not derived from 
usual life experiences) or nonbizarre and may encompass a wide variety of themes. Hal-
lucinations are perception-like experiences, in any sensory modality, that occur in the 
absence of a true external stimulus. Disorganized thought may manifest as tangential, de-
railed, illogical, or even completely incomprehensible thought processes. Disorganized 
behavior may include odd, nonsensical, or unpredictable behavior; some patients may 
simply struggle to meaningfully plan or complete goal-oriented tasks. Negative symp-
toms include decreased emotional expression (e.g., blunted affect, poor eye contact, or 
impaired prosody of speech), avolition, and anhedonia (Lewis et al. 2017). 
Schizophrenia is categorized in DSM-5 in the chapter “Schizophrenia Spectrum 
and Other Psychotic Disorders” (American Psychiatric Association 2013), which also 
includes schizoaffective disorder, schizophreniform disorder, brief psychotic disor -
der, delusional disorder, and substance/medication-induced psychotic disorder. The 
diagnostic criteria for schizophrenia are summarized in Box 17–1. Although other di-
agnostic nuances can be found between br ief psychotic disorder, schizophreniform 
disorder, and schizophrenia (e.g., one or two required symptoms, presence of a require-

Clinical Features 
Clinical Presentation 
Symptom onset in schizophrenia may be gradual (more common) or precipitous and 
commonly includes a prodromal period characterized by prominent cognitive and neg-
ative symptoms. Although the classic course is one of exacerbations and remissions, a 
decline in baseline functioning after each relapse is typical. In general, schizophrenia is 
associated with profound personal, occupational, and social disability. Although ap-
proximately one-third of persons living with schizophrenia are able to maintain rela-
tively normative levels of functioning, most patients are significantly impaired by their 
illness (Sadock et al. 2015). A large majority of patients with schizophrenia are unem-
ployed, and patients with schizophrenia are 13 times more likely to die by suicide than 
the general population (Saha et al. 2007). Even after accounting for suicide, persons 
with schizophrenia have a life expectancy of 10–25 years shorter than control subjects 
(Lambert 2009). Negative symptoms are typically more stable throughout the course 
of the disease and have a higher negative predictive power relative to positive symp-

Estrogen, Menses, and Perimenopause 
Sex differences evident in the clinical presentation of schizophrenia have spurred inter-
est in the “estrogen hypothesis of schizophrenia.” Riecher-Rössler and Häfner (1993) 
further described the estrogen hypothesis as two separate but interrelated hypotheses: 
1) estrogen protects against psychotic illness and 2) psychosis itself may influence go-
nadal hormones, resulting in the dysfunction of the hypothalamic-pituitary-gonadal 
axis. Both hypotheses speak to the current understanding of schizophrenia pathophys-
iology as it relates to estrogen, which is described in detail later in the “Pathophysiol-
ogy” section. From a clinical perspective, the estrogen protective hypothesis predicts 
that women will have increased incidence an d severity of psychotic illness when es -
trogen is lower across the lifespan (Riecher-Rössler and Häfner 1993). Several lines of 
evidence support this hypothesis (Table 17–2). Regarding incidence, the second peak 
of onset for schizophrenia occurs among women in the perimenopausal years (coin -

Preconception 
Historically, women with schizophrenia were believed to be subfertile. This was likely 
related to reduced access to sexual encounters during institutionalization of the chron-
ically mentally ill and to hyperprolactinemia commonly induced by first-generation 
antipsychotics (Smith and Dubovsky 2017). Today, more than half of women with 
schizophrenia will become pregnant, and the fertility rate of women with schizophre-
nia approaches that of the general population (Howard et al. 2001; Vigod et al. 2012). 
Discussion of family planning goals and se xual practices is an essential aspect of 
psychiatric care of women with schizophrenia and should occur routinely throughout 
the reproductive years. In general, women with schizophrenia have higher rates of 
high-risk sexual activity, including transactional sex, involvement with multiple part-
ners, and lack of contraceptive use (Cournos et al. 1994) compared with healthy con-
trol subjects. Rates of unplanned or unwanted pregnancies are higher among women 
with schizophrenia than in the general population, likely due to inadequate education 
about contraception and reduced access to care (Miller 1997). The potential for unin-
tended pregnancy should thus be considered for all women of reproductive age, and 
if the patient does not desire pregnancy,  education around contraception should be 
provided as part of usual psychiatric care. When prescribing medication treatment, 
the informed consent process with all reproductive-age women should necessarily in-
clude the known obstetrical, neonatal, and developmental risks of the proposed phar-
macological treatment (see
