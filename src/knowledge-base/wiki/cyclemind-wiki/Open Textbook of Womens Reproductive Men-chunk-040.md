Flibanserin and Bremelanotide 
Two medications have FDA approval to treat FSDs. The medications are not indicated 
for lifelong HSDD but rather for acquired, generalized (loss of desire in all situations) 
HSDD in premenopausal women that is not better accounted for by a coexisting med-
ical or psychiatric condition, problems within the relationship, or effects of a medica-
tion or other drug substance. Flibanserin  is a multifunctional serotonin 1A (5-HT 1A) 
receptor agonist and type 2A (5-HT2A) receptor antagonist that is administered orally 
at bedtime daily. There is a risk evaluation and mitigation strategy related to the risk 
for hypotension and syncope (Sprout Pharmaceuticals 2016). Patients should be coun-
seled to discontinue alcohol use 2 hours prior to taking flibanserin at bedtime, avoid 
consuming alcohol until the following day after taking the medication, and skip the 
daily dose if they consume three or more standard drinks. Full response to flibanserin 
may take up to 8–12 weeks (Joffe et al. 2016). 
Bremelanotide consists of seven amino acids and is a cyclic me lanocortin receptor 
agonist. It is self-administered, as needed, via an autoinjector (29-gauge needle) into the 
skin of the thigh or abdomen at least 45 minutes prior to anticipated sexual activity and 
has approximately 12–16 hours of effect. Recommended maximums are one dose per 
24 hours and eight doses per month (Kingsberg et al. 2019a). No existing data examine 
the safety and risk of flibanserin or bremelanotide in pregnant or lactating women, and 
these medications are not currently recommended for use by these women.
