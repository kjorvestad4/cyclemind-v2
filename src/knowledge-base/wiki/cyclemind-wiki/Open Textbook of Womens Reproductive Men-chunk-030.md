Additional biopsychosocial changes associated with pregnancy and the postpartum 
period may affect sexual function. Bodily changes with pregnancy and during the post-
partum period may lower body image (e.g., breasts that used to be perceived as sexual 
are now used for feeding, stretch marks de veloped during pregnancy) or otherwise 
negatively affect sexual functioning. Overall physical discomfort and physical compli-
cations associated with pregnancy or the postpartum period (e.g., increased urination, 
pain, pelvic floor dysfunction or changes), decreased sleep, and increased fatigue can 
affect sexual function (Read 2004). Women and their partners are commonly navigating 
role transitions that may negatively affect their intimate relationship, and demands of 
caring for the infant can limit opportunities for sexual activity with a partner.
