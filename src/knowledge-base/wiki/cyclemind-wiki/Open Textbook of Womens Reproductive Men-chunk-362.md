Depressive Disorders 
Lauren M. Osborne, M.D. 
Catherine Birndorf, M.D. 
The reproductive years represent a period of heightened risk in women for major 
depressive disorder (MDD), with some women particularly vulnerable to mood symp-
toms that arise at moments of reproductive transition, such as menarche (Patton et al. 
1996; Shen et al. 2019), the premenstruum (Miller and Miller 2001), pregnancy and the 
postpartum period, and perimenopause (Bromberger and Epperson 2018). For exam-
ple, prior to puberty, MDD presents in equal rates in boys and girls. However, in the 
reproductive years, it is twice as common in women as in men. Postmenopause, the 
opposite is true: the rate of MDD in women dips below that of men. The focus of this 
chapter is on depressive disorders in pregnancy and postpartum. (See Chapter 6, “Pre-
menstrual Mood Syndromes,” and Chapter 7, “Perimenopause,” for discussion of other 
reproductive transition periods.) 
Perinatal depression—an umbrella term that includes both antenatal depression (AD) 
and postpartum depression (PPD)—is a seri ous mental health disorder affecting at 
least 15% of women in the developed world and 20%–40% of women worldwide (Ben-
nett et al. 2004; Gaynes et al. 2005; Le Strat et al. 2011; Wisner et al. 2002). With more 
than 4 million live births in the United States each year, at least 600,000 families will be 
affected, making depression the most common complication of childbirth (Grace et al. 
2003)—far outstripping such common complications as gestational diabetes (Dabelea 
et al. 2005), preterm birth (Saigal and Do yle 2008), and preeclampsia (Umesawa and 
Kobashi 2017). Perinatal depression affects the entire family, increasing partner con-
flict, disrupting mother–infant bonding, leading to poor occupational and social func-
tioning, and affecting the cognitive, emotional, and social development of the child 
(Conroy et al. 2012; Grace et al. 2003). The societal costs of untreated perinatal depres-
sion are severe, with a recent estimate of $14.2 billion for all births in the United States 
in 2017 (or $32,000 per mother–child pair) (Luca et al. 2019).
