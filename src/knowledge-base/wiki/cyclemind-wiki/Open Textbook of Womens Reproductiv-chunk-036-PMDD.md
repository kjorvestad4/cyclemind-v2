The menstrual cycle is the reproductive hormonal transition that occurs most often 
in women’s lives, with the average wo man experiencing approximately 480 men -
strual periods in a lifetime (minus any periods of pregnancy) (National Health Service 
2019). Up to 90% of women report experi encing undesirable premenstrual physical 
and emotional symptoms that vary from mild to severe and can be classified into three 
types (Mishell 2005; O’Brien et al. 2011). 
First, for most women, these symptoms take the form of the commonly known pre-
menstrual syndrome (PMS). PMS is a patte rn of physical and emotional/behavioral 
symptoms that occurs in the 5 days prior to menses in at least three consecutive cycles 
and resolves with menstruation. Affective symptoms of PMS include depression, an-
gry outbursts, irritability, anxiety, confusion, and social withdrawal, and physical 
symptoms include breast tenderness, abdominal bloating, headache, and swelling of 
the extremities (American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2014). Women 
can have emotional symptoms, physical symptoms, or both. Although it affects most 
women, no formal psychiatric diagnosis is associated with PMS. 
Second, in the case of women with psychiatric illness, PMS symptoms may take the 
form of a premenstrual exacerbation (PME) of their underlying illness (Haley et al. 
2013). In PME, which is not well studied,  women have a luteal-phase worsening of 
their typical affective symptoms; more than 50% of women with a mood disorder ex-
perience premenstrual worsening (Haley et al. 2013). 
Finally, a small minority of women (with or without other psychiatric illness) may 
meet criteria for premenstrual dysphoric disorder (PMDD), which is the primary fo -
cus of this chapter. From a historical st andpoint, the formal recognition of PMDD is

with the publication of DSM-5 (American Ps ychiatric Association 2013). Previously,

continue to ovulate and experience hormonal change. Transgender men may also con-

affirming therapy or even after, because a delay often occurs between the initiation of

late) (Pradhan and Gomez-Lobo 2019). 
Although many studies on the incidence and prevalence of PMDD and associated 
risk factors have been published, their results vary. Reasons for this variance in find-
ings include differing symptom criteria for diagnosis of PMS and PMDD (ranging 
from more inclusive to more exclusive crit eria), differing thresholds of the severity 
and timing of symptoms, and differing study designs (O’Brien et al. 2011). Many stud-
ies measuring prevalence are conducted vi a retrospective data collection, a method 
that appears to measure the pattern of symptoms reasonably well when compared 
with prospective studies; this method, however, introduces recall bias that can over -
estimate the number and intensity of symptoms (Rapkin et al. 1988; Rubinow and 
Roy-Byrne 1984). 
Rates/Incidence 
Premenstrual symptoms are quite common in menstruating women, with an estimated 
80%–95% of women experiencing some physical or emotional symptoms (O’Brien et 
al. 2011). Far fewer women have symptoms severe enough to meet DSM-5 criteria for 
diagnosis of PMDD (Box 6–1). According to studies that have co llected prospective 
data in women, PMS has an estimated prevalence of 20%–30% (Borenstein et al. 2007; 
Qiao et al. 2012), and a meta-analysis of 15 prevalence studies (covering both prospec-
tive and retrospective data collection) reported a pooled prevalence of 47.8% (95% CI 
32.6–62.9). PMDD, by contrast, is much less common, with an estimated prevalence of 
1.3%–7.7% (Cohen et al. 2002; Gehlert et al. 2009; Hussein Shehadeh and Hamdan-
Mansour 2018). In a large community sample investigation, the Harvard Study of

Risk Factors 
Most studies that identify risk factors for PMDD are either cross-sectional or post hoc 
in design, which limits their ability to determine causal links to the development of 
PMDD. Despite these limitations, some inte resting associations have been observed. 
The development of premenstrual disorders has a heritable component, with monozy-
gotic twins having more overlap of PMS symptoms than dizy gotic twins (Condon 
1993). One modeling study suggested that genetic factors account for 95% of the devel-
opment of PMS (Jahanfar et al. 2011). PMDD and PMS both appear to occur at higher 
rates in white women than in Black women (Pilver et al. 2011a). Researchers have in-
vestigated numerous candidate genes, often without finding a connection; those that 
have at least one positive finding include brain derived neurotrophic factor (BDNF), es-
trogen receptors 1 and 2 (ESR1, ESR2), and the serotonin transporter (SERT) (Comasco 
et al. 2014; Dhingra et al. 2007; Gingnell et al. 2010; Huo et al. 2007; Marrocco et al. 
2020; Praschak-Rieder et al. 2002; Takeo et al. 2005; Yen et al. 2014). Recent evidence 
has also implicated ESC/E(Z) complex genes (an ovarian steroid-regulated gene silenc-
ing complex), with more than half the ge nes in this complex being overexpressed in 
women with PMDD when compared with healthy control subjects (Dubey et al. 2017). 
There is a positive correlation between increasing PMS scores and increased preva-
lence of metabolic syndrome (Hashemi et al. 2016). This may be related to adiposity , as 
one study showed a linear relationship between BMI at baseline and the risk of devel-
oping PMS (1 kg/m2 resulted in 3% increased risk of developing PMS). In that sample, 
subjects with BMI  27.5 kg/m2 had a significantly higher risk of PMS (Bertone-Johnson 
et al. 2010).

Diet may play a role; high-potassium diet s may increase risk, whereas diets high in 
nonheme iron, zinc, thiamine, and riboflavin may lower risk (Chocano-Bedoya et al. 
2011, 2013). Nicotine use increases the risk (Bertone-Johnson et al. 2010; Cohen et al. 
2002). Women with lower education status have higher rates of premenstrual disor -
ders, and working from home (vs. outside the home) is associated with lower rates of 
PMS (Cohen et al. 2002).

Clinical Features 
Diagnostic Criteria 
PMS is not listed in DSM-5; however, a useful definition is provided by the American 
College of Obstetricians and Gynecologist s (ACOG) ( Table 6–2). PMS is defined as 
more than one physical or emotional symptom associated with either economic or so-
cial dysfunction occurring during 5 days before menses in three consecutive cycles. 
The DSM-5 diagnostic criteria for PMDD (see Box 6–1 earlier in chapter) emphasize 
mood symptoms that cause functional impairment. To meet criteria, the person must 
have five of these symptoms, at least one of which is a mood symptom. Symptoms must 
be accompanied by either behavioral or physical symptoms that begin during the luteal 
phase of the menstrual cycle, start to decrease after menstrual flow, and are minimal or 
absent during the early follicular phase (American Psychiatric Association 2013). 
An accurate diagnosis of PMDD requires prospective mood charting of at least one 
but preferably two menstrual cycles. A m ood chart consistent with PMS or PMDD 
will demonstrate symptoms that begin in the premenstrual period and resolve at the 
beginning or shortly after the onset of menses. Ongoing symptoms during the follic -
ular phase that worsen during the premenst rual phase are more consistent with the 
PME of another disorder, such as depression or anxiety. Although no consensus exists 
regarding the necessary percentage of change, a 30%–50% change in symptoms be -
tween luteal and follicular phases of the cycle is generally considered an important 
diagnostic guideline (Hall and Steiner 2015).

as her wife not putting her clothes in the hamper, and these same things are easily over-
looked earlier in the month. She says she ha s trouble at work during this week every 
month because she is easily annoyed by coworkers and has trouble concentrating. She 
has been put on probation at work as a result  of one of her outbursts. She feels “like a 
train hit her” and wants to sleep for longer than 10 hours a day. Ms. K blames her steady 
weight gain over the years on this time of her cycle because she craves carbohydrates 
and always eats more cookies before her menstrual flow. Her mood is consistently low, 
and although she has never been actively su icidal, she has thought often in the luteal

The physician asks Ms. K to chart her symptoms prospectively over two menstrual 
cycles using the DRSP . Her daily symptom ratings, ranging from 1 to 6, are shown in

PMDD is a cyclical disorder in which symptoms begin after ovulation and remit early 
in the follicular phase of the menstrual cy cle. The cardinal mood features include 
marked mood lability, irritability, anxiety, or tension (feeling “keyed up or on edge”), 
feelings of depression, and feeling “ove rwhelmed” and hopeless (American College 
of Obstetricians and Gyneco logists 2014; American Psyc hiatric Association 2013). 
Women with PMDD complain of many of th e vegetative symptoms seen in a major 
depressive episode, including changes in sleep (hypersomnia or insomnia), loss of en-
ergy, and problems with concentration. Hyperphagia in the luteal phase is a common 
symptom in PMDD, and women may have specific food cravings, often for highly 
sweet foods (Reed et al. 2008; Yen et al. 2018). Women with PMS also have distress 
from the emotional and physical symptoms that occur premenstrually, but they do not 
have as many symptoms as women with PM DD (i.e., more than five symptoms, in -
cluding at least one emotional symptom). In addition, women with PMS may have 
physical symptoms alone. Women with PME may have symptoms during the pre -
menstrual period that are similar to those in women with PMDD, but they also have 
symptoms outside the luteal phase. For women with a mood disorder whose symp -
toms in the premenstrual period differ in severity but not quality from those in other 
periods, PME is likely. In this case, fully treating the underlying mood disorder may 
also treat the PME (Osborne and Payne 2017). In contrast, women whose premen -
strual symptoms are markedly different in quality from their symptoms at other times 
are likely to have PMDD. 
Course and Prognosis 
PMDD is a stable condition that has low overall spontaneous remission rates. One 
study reported <10% remission over 2 ye ars (Wittchen et al. 2002). PMDD does not 
occur during anovulatory cycles or pregnancy but may return in the postpartum pe-
riod when women resume menstrual cycles (Takayama et al. 2020). For some women 
the disorder begins in adolescence, whereas for others it may begin later in adulthood.

More studies investigating the course of PMDD over the reproductive lifespan are 
needed; however, cross-sectional studies of PMDD during perimenopause do exist, 
and it is clear that the disorder persists th rough this time period (Chung et al. 2014). 
PMS and PMDD may worsen during the perimenopausal transition, when cyclicity be-
comes disjointed (Baker and O’Brien 2012; Pinkerton et al. 2010). Notably, women with 
a history of PMDD also appear to be at gr eater risk for other mood disorders during 
the perimenopausal transition (Freeman and Lewis 2004). PMDD ceases after natural, 
surgical, and medically induced menopause, as long as no add-back hormonal replace-
ment is utilized (Reid 2012; Wyatt et al. 2004). 
Comorbidity with other psychiatric illnesse s is high in individuals with premen -
strual disorders. In one study, only 26.5% of women with PMDD in a community sam-
ple did not have another psychiatric diagnosi s (Wittchen et al. 2002). Comorbid 
affective disorders are common in women with PMDD; comorbid major depression 
rates range from 30% to 50% (Endicott 1993). Comorbidity of PMDD and bipolar dis-
order is also common, ranging from 10% to 27% depending on study populations and 
methodologies (Teatero et al. 2014; Wittchen et al. 2002). Women with comorbid 
PMDD and bipolar disorder have an earlier onset of bipolar disorder, increased fre-
quency of mood cycling, and more lifetime depressive episodes (Slyepchenko et al. 
2017; Wittchen et al. 2002). Suicide risk may also be affected by PMDD diagnosis; one 
study found that women admitted to the ps ychiatric hospital for a suicide attempt 
were more likely to have PMDD (Shams-Alizadeh et al. 2018). 
Anxiety disorders also are common in women with PMDD. Comorbid panic dis -
order is common, occurring in 25% of women with PMDD, whereas rates of comorbid 
OCD range from 11% to 13% and social phobia from 19% to 23% (Kim et al. 2004). 
Rates of comorbid generalized anxiety disorder are more varied across studies, rang-
ing from 4% to 38% (Kim et al. 2004). Nicotine and alcohol use are also common 
(Fernández et al. 2019a; Hong et al. 2012; Ma rks et al. 1994). PMDD is also highly co-
morbid with bulimia nervosa. In a large epidemiological study of 8,693 participants, 
women with PMDD were seven times as l ikely as women without PMDD to have a 
comorbid diagnosis of bulimia nervosa (OR 7.2; 95% CI 2.3–22.4) (Nobles et al. 2016). 
Due to limitations in study design, it is unclear whether PMDD makes individuals 
more susceptible to developing comorbid psychiatric disorders (e.g., major depres -
sion) or vice versa. However, having premenstrual depressive symptoms appears to 
increase the risk of having a future major depressive episode (Graze et al. 1990). 
Untreated PMDD has a significant impact on a woman’s life, and the burden of ill-
ness is related both to the number of days per cycle that she suffers and to the severity 
of her symptoms. Women with PMDD report impairment in interpersonal relationships 
and roles, including more hours absent from work and decreased productivity (Dahl-
gren and Qvigstad 2018). When one adds up the days a woman with PMDD suffers 
over her reproductive lifetime, it totals approximately 3.8 years (Halbreich et al. 2003).

mood and physical symptoms to determine whether the woman has a PMDD diag -

alone would not explain the cyclicity of symptoms seen in PMDD (i.e., symptoms that 
occur solely in the luteal phase) (Yonkers and Simoni 2018). 
Sociodemographic Considerations 
PMDD appears to be more common among white women in the United States than 
among other groups. In one large study of 2,590 English-speaking premenopausal 
American women (Pilver et al. 2011a), ages 18–40 years, the prevalence of PMDD was 
2.9% in Black women and 4.4% in white women. There is little research on variations 
in prevalence among people of different ethnicities in the United States. 
Notably, most studies on premenstrual disorders have included individuals who 
self-identify as women. Little information is available regarding PMDD in persons 
who identify as male or nonbinary. As described earlier (see “Epidemiology”), PMDD 
can only occur in individuals who are having menstrual cycles (with or without men-
strual bleeding), but this group could include individuals who identify as male or 
nonbinary. Studies show that individual s who have undergone bilateral oophorec -
tomy, cycle suppression with gonadotropin-releasing hormone (GnRH) agonists, and 
hormone (e.g., estrogen) replacement can have improvement or remission of premen-
strual symptoms (Reid 2012; Schmidt et al. 2017; Studd 2014). This may be relevant in 
future investigations of individuals with nonfemale gender identification who expe-
rience PMDD. 
Pathophysiology 
Biological Factors 
Sex steroids are believed to be intimately involved in the etiopathology of PMDD. Be-
cause PMDD does not occur before menarche or after menopause, exposure to cycling 
levels of sex steroids is obligatory. In women with PMDD, symptoms are not precip-
itated by an excess or deficit of a specific hormone but, rather, by the individual’s re-
sponse to fluctuating levels of sex hormones. This has been clearly demonstrated in 
the literature; women who have undergone ovarian suppression have reemergence of 
their PMS or PMDD symptoms when add-ba ck hormone is utilized but not once 
steady-state levels have been reached (Schmidt et al. 2017).

Although allopregnanolone would be expected to exert a calming effect due to its 
positive modulation of GABAA, it may also exert a paradoxical disinhibitory effect sim-

cal response occur in 3%–6% of human subjects, with an additional 2%–30% exhibiting 
moderate symptoms characterized by anxiety, irritability , or aggression (Bäckström et 
al. 2011). In keeping with this paradoxical response, and similar to other GABAA pos-

individuals. As such, at moderate levels, some women may experience a paradoxical 
anxiogenic effect, whereas at higher doses, allopregnanolone would be expected to pro-
duce a more anxiolytic effect. Therefore, it is believed that rising allopregnanolone lev-

women may contribute to PMS and PMDD symptoms during the luteal phase.

Social Considerations 
Compared with control subjects, women with PMDD often report higher levels of psy-
chosocial stress (Fontana and Palfai 1994),  including work stress (Namavar Jahromi 
et al. 2011) or lack of work and lower so cioeconomic status (de Carvalho et al. 2018). 
As previously mentioned (see “Epidemiology”), history of trauma has also been asso-
ciated with risk for PMDD and is considered an important potential social factor in its 
etiology (Eisenlohr-Moul et al. 2016; Perkonigg et al. 2004). Social stresses may lead to 
greater vulnerability to stress responses, and studies of women with PMDD report al-
terations in hypothalamic-pituitary-adrenal axis function compared with control sub-
jects (Hantsoo and Epperson 2015).

Antidepressant Medications 
Selective serotonin reuptake inhibitors. SSRIs are considered the first-line treat-
ment for PMDD. (When treating a woman of reproductive age, even if that patient is 
not planning a pregnancy, clinicians should always consider the safety of proposed 
medications for future pregnancies.) More than 30 well-designed double-blind ran -
domized controlled trials (RCTs) have demonstrated the efficacy and safety of SSRIs for 
the treatment of PMDD (Marjoribanks et al. 2013). Therefore, much of the treatment of 
PMDD centers around the use of SSRIs. Within this class of medications, sertraline, flu-
oxetine, paroxetine, escitalopram, and citalopram have been specifically studied and 
deemed equally effective as treatment options; other SSRIs, however, should also be 
considered viable options as well. 
Because of the decrease in serotonergic function during the luteal phase of the men-
strual cycle, some patients diagnosed with PMDD are treated with SSRIs only during 
the 2 weeks prior to menses. Several studies have shown that this type of luteal-phase 
dosing is as effective as continuous dosi ng in reducing the symptoms of PMDD and 
PMS (Shah et al. 2008). Another treatment st rategy for PMDD is to administer SSRIs 
only with symptom onset. Several small but well-designed RCTs have shown that 
symptom-onset dosing is an effective st rategy in reducing PMDD symptoms, with 
minimal treatment days (Steiner and Li 20 13; Yonkers et al. 2005b). Interestingly, in 
this research no symptom worsening was noted with discontinuation of treatment, 
and even with short-acting SSRIs such as paroxetine, withdrawal phenomena were 
not notable. Although there is more evidence, and therefore more support, for contin-
uous-dosing strategies for SSRIs, both luteal-phase dosing and symptom-onset dos -
ing represent reasonable strategies involving shorter treatment periods (with shorter 
exposure to side effects) for appropriate patients. 
Serotonin-norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors. Much like SSRIs, the serotonin -
norepinephrine reuptake inhibitors (SNRIs) are treatment options for PMDD due to 
their inhibitory effect on serotoninreuptake. Research has focused on two SNRIs, ven-
lafaxine and duloxetine. A few studies have demonstrated that with continuous dos-
ing, symptom remission rates for these two agents are superior to those of placebo 
(Freeman et al. 2001; Mazza et al. 2008; Ra mos et al. 2009). Limited data exist on the 
intermittent use of SNRIs for PMDD. One study found intermittent dosing of venla -
faxine to be efficacious in the treatment of PMDD, but some women experienced mild 
to moderate withdrawal-related symptoms (Cohen et al. 2004). Therefore, SNRIs rep-
resent an effective treatment strategy for PMDD but may be best used in patients who 
prefer continuous dosing. 
Tricyclic antidepressants. Limited data exist to support the use of tricyclic antide-
pressants (TCAs) in the treatment of PMDD. In two studies with small numbers of sub-
jects, clomipramine was shown to be supe rior to placebo in reducing premenstrual 
irritability and dysphoria; in one of those studies, both continuous and intermittent 
dosing appeared to be effective (Sundblad et al. 1992, 1993). Despite the limited data 
supporting its use, as well as the less desi rable side effect profile for TCAs, clomip -

Hormonal Interventions 
Oral contraception. Oral contraceptive pills (OCPs) have been explored as a treat-
ment option based on the theory that cessation of ovulation would eliminate symp -
toms of PMS and PMDD. However, the evidence of any type of OCPs for treatment of 
PMDD is limited. In 2006, the FDA approved an OCP containing drospirenone for the 
treatment of PMDD. Drospirenone is a synt hetic progestin derived from spironolac-

postulated to be associated with mood  symptoms (Rapkin 2003 ). One multicenter, 
double-blind, placebo-controlled study evaluated the use of monophasic drospirenone

pills, in women with PMDD. They found that the treatment group’s response rate to 
this formulation was similar to that of women taking SSRIs. Of note, premenstrual 
physical symptoms improved with drospire none as well (Yonkers et al. 2005a). Al -
though these results are encouraging, it sh ould be noted that studies assessing dro -
spirenone have had large placebo effects and have often lost large numbers of patients 
to follow-up (Lopez et al. 2012). Additional high-quality research is desired in this area. 
In addition to the choice of agent, continuous versus cyclical dosing has also been 
studied. Some data have shown a benefit with continuous administration (without a 
hormone-free interval) of a formulation of the monophasic 19-nortestosterone deriv-
ative levonorgestrel and ethinyl estradiol in  treating both physical and mental pre -
menstrual symptoms, compared with placeb o (Halbreich et al. 2012). Other reviews 
have shown inconsistencies in symptom reduction with this type of treatment (Free-
man et al. 2012).

Gonadotropin-releasing hormone agonists. The GnRH agonist class of medica -

GnRH receptors, which leads to a decrease in both luteinizing and follicle-stimulating 
hormones and eventual decreases in production of ovarian estrogen and progesterone. 
That these hormonal changes result in anovulation, amenorrhea, and a menopause-
type state is the basis of this type of treatment for PMS and PMDD. Studies evaluating 
GnRH agonists have shown them to be more effective than placebo in reducing PMDD 
symptoms (Wyatt et al. 2004). 
Clearly, one issue with GnRH agonists is that by creating a menopausal state, they 
cause women to experience signs and symp toms of hypoestrogenism, such as hot  
flashes and night sweats, and present a ri sk for decreased bone mineral density 
(Dawood 1994). Therefore, some research has investigated the use of “add-back” es -
trogen and progesterone for women receiving GnRH agonists. Although research on 
this topic is limited, a small study demonstrated that when estrogen and progesterone 
were added back to the treatment, some symptoms of PMDD did occur. Addition of 
estrogen alone appeared to be most effective for prevention of menopausal symptoms 
without PMDD symptoms; however, this is not considered a safe option due to the risk 
for endometrial hyperplasia (Segebladh et al. 2009). Due to the complications associ-
ated with use of GnRH agonists and the unclear implications of long-term treatment 
with them, they are not considered a first-line treatment for PMDD and may come into 
play only as a temporary treatment for severe treatment-resistant cases. 
Danazol. Danazol is a synthetic steroid with androgenic and antigonadotropic prop-
erties that suppresses ovulation. It has been shown to reduce premenstrual physical 
and mood symptoms in some cases (Hahn et al. 1995). However, this form of treatment 
is complicated by multiple side effects, such as decreased high-density lipoprotein cho-
lesterol, weight gain, hirsutism, and acne. Consequently, as with GnRH agonists, da-
nazol is not recommended as a first-line treatment for PMDD. 
Spironolactone. Spironolactone is an aldosteron e receptor antagonist and potas -
sium-sparing diuretic. The best evidence for spironolactone appears to be in the treat-
ment of premenstrual physical symptoms, such as swelling, breast tenderness, and food 
cravings. It has also been shown to reduce irritability and depression (Wang et al. 1995). 
Because this medication may increase potassium levels, careful monitoring for hyper-
kalemia is necessary.

mine agonists that reduce prolactin levels. In doing so, they have been found to be ef-
fective in reducing premenstrual mastalgia. Additional symptom reduction has been 
inconsistent with bromocriptine. Cabergoline has been noted to have fewer side effects

Nonpharmacological Somatic Treatments 
In all treatment efforts, a careful assessment with the patient about risk versus potential 
benefit is crucial. General health-oriented interventions with very low risk may in -
clude minimizing use of caffeine, salt, and alcohol, especially during the premenstrual 
week. Increasing other positive behaviors, such as sleep and exercise, is also recom -
mended. Depending on symptom severity, interventions such as scheduling changes 
that reduce stress during the premenstrual week and other nonpharmacological ap -
proaches may be useful. A great amount of interest has been shown in dietary modifi-
cations for the treatment of PMDD. Areas th at have been explored include intake of 
soy isoflavones, fat, protein, and carbohydrates. Some observational studies show as-
sociations between diet and PMS or PMDD, but the evidence is not consistent (Hashim 
et al. 2019; Houghton et al. 2017, 2018). Although some open-label studies have found 
improvements in symptoms with dietary modifications, others have not, and random-
ized evidence is lacking (Esmaeilpour et al. 2019; Houghton et al. 2019). 
Behavioral and Other Nonpharmacological Treatments 
Support for psychotherapeutic treatments for PMDD remains inconclusive but prom-
ising. CBT has the most support, but many studies have had limitations in study design, 
including use of retrospective versus pr ospective PMDD diagnosis and existence of 
mainly open trials (Lustyk et al. 2009). In a randomized comparison study, women with 
prospectively confirmed PMDD were randomly assigned to 10 sessions of CBT alone, 
fluoxetine 20 mg/day alone, or combined CBT and fluoxetine. All groups demonstrated 
improvement in PMDD. Fluoxetine was associated with earlier improvement, and CBT 
was associated with greater maintenance of improvement at 1 year (Hunter et al. 2002). 
Recently, internet-based CBT was shown to be effective in addressing the functional and 
psychological impairments of PMDD (Weise et al. 2019). Although, to date, published 
studies on the use of mindfulness-based stress reduction therapies for PMDD are lack-
ing, one small open study of women with  menstrual-related mood symptoms found 
that participation in a mindfulness-based stress reduction class was associated with sig-
nificant reduction in premenstrual symptoms, including decreased depressive symp-
toms, irritability, anxiety, and mood swings (Bluth et al. 2015). Thus, some data suggest 
efficacy of other low-risk interventions, such as mindfulness, to assist patients in stress 
reduction and coping during periods of illness. 
Acupuncture has also been evaluated in  the treatment of PMDD, and when com-
pared with sham procedures, acupuncture and acupressure are associated with de -
creased PMS and PMDD symptoms in many trials. No studies have yet compared this 
modality with SSRIs, SNRIs, or OCPs (Armour et al. 2018).

Herbal Treatments 
Historically, herbal treatment options have been used for the treatment of PMS and 
PMDD. Although some herbs such as St. John’s wort (Hypericum perforatum) have been 
shown to be somewhat effective for PMS (Canning et al. 2010), they have not shown 
improvements in mood symptoms of PMDD. Chaste tree extract or chasteberry (Vitex 
agnus-castus) has shown some benefit in treating physical and mood-related PMS 
symptoms in multiple double-blind RCTs and in uncontrolled studies (Loch et al. 2000; 
Schellenberg 2001; Verkaik et al. 2017). The postulated mechanism of action for chaste 
tree extract is that it may bind to dopamine receptors and inhibit prolactin (Jarry et al. 
1994) and also activate opioid receptors (Webster et al. 2011). Other promising herbal 
treatment strategies include saffron (Agha-Hosseini et al. 2008) and Ginkgo biloba 
(Ozgoli et al. 2009). Herbal remedies represent an interesting future direction for re -
search, but additional research is needed before treatment recommendations can be 
made (Jang et al. 2014). As with any herbal treatments, which are not under the same 
governmental quality controls as pharmaceuticals, caution must be advised in the pro-
curement and consumption of these products; actual formulations may differ widely 
even within the same brand due to the unregulated nature of the herbal treatment in-
dustry. Additionally, limited guidance is available about advising patients how best to 
administer and dose these supplements.

estrogen replacement has been shown to be effective in patients with previously treat-
ment-refractory disease (Cronje et al. 2004). Because this is an irreversible procedure,

Treatment Challenges: Partial Response, 
Treatment Resistance, and Comorbid Bipolar Disorder 
No studies have been published on treatment-resistant PMDD, despite reports that 40%

To date, no RCTs are available to guide treatment of comorbid PMDD and bipolar 
disorder or of bipolar disorder with PME of mood symptoms. Current recommenda-
tions include first maximizing the mood stabilizer and then considering adding an 
OCP (Sepede et al. 2020). Support for this approach includes data from small studies 
of women with bipolar disorder who report  improvement in cyclical mood fluctua -
tions with OCPs (Frey and Minuzzi 2013) and with lamotrigine and OCPs compared 
with other mood stabilizers (Robakis et al. 2015). 
Furthermore, to date no sequenced treatment trials for PMDD have been published. 
Only one small, open-label study has examined the effectiveness of switching from one 
SSRI, if not effective, to citalopram 20–40 mg/day for either the luteal phase or the full 
cycle (Freeman et al. 2002). Clinical improvement, defined as Daily Symptom Rating 
Scale decreases of >50% from baseline, were  reported in 76% of the subjects, and no 
significant differences were found between the full- and half-cycle treatment groups. 
In a double-blind study, adjunctive quetiapine has been shown to reduce mood lability 
and depression in patients with PMDD who had only partial response to SSRIs (Jack-
son et al. 2015). Further studies are needed to guide treatment of partial response and 
treatment resistance. 
Conclusion 
The treatment of PMDD remains an area in need of additional research for the best 
evidence-based treatment decisions. Currently, the recommended approach is for cli-
nicians to involve patients in a multifaceted approach, including nutritional modifica-
tions, lifestyle choices, behavioral interventions and therapies, and, where appropriate, 
introduction of medical treatments. Working closely with patients to best understand 
their own unique values, risk assessment, and treatment choices is crucial to the most 
effective treatment strategy.
