Risk of Untreated Illness in the Perinatal Period 
When developing a treatment plan for women with schizophrenia during pregnancy, 
it is important to consider the risks of the underlying disease as a potential exposure. 
Women with schizophrenia are more likely to receive poor prenatal care, engage in 
substance misuse, have poor nutrition, an d be exposed to intimate partner violence 
than are healthy control subjects (Møller-Ols en et al. 2018; Yaeger et al. 2006). They 
also have higher rates of baseline medical comorbidities, such as diabetes, obesity , and 
hypertension (Liao et al. 2011). Although studies examining the obstetrical outcomes 
of women with schizophrenia are largely limited by design (e.g., many studies do not 
control for medication use or multiple confounders), an underlying diagnosis of schizo-
phrenia has been associated with several adverse outcomes, including preterm delivery, 
low APGAR scores, low birth weight, intrauterine growth restriction, congenital mal-
formation, placental abruption, stillbirth, and infant death (Abel et al. 2014; Friedman 
et al. 2016; Tosato et al. 2017). Although th ese associations are reported in general

Differential Diagnosis and Comorbidities 
A full psychiatric evaluation should be performed to diagnose schizophrenia. In some 
cases, psychotic symptoms may be either completely or partially attributable to sub-
stance use (e.g., co caine, organophosphates, alcohol withdrawal), medications (e.g., 
steroids, chemotherapeutic agents), or an underlying medical condition (e.g., epilepsy, 
paraneoplastic syndromes, endocrine disturbance). In the postpartum period, activa-
tion of the immune system requires particular consideration because conditions such 
as postpartum autoimmune thyroiditis and anti-NMDA encephalitis have been asso-
ciated with postpartum psychotic symptoms (Bergink et al. 2015). Differential diagno-
ses include other psychiatric disorders with a possible psychotic component, including 
major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, schizoaffective disorder, schizophreni -
form disorder, PTSD dissociative subtype, borderline personality disorder, or schizo-
typal personality disorder. See Table 17–4 for an expanded list of considerations. 
Medical comorbidities are common among women with schizophrenia and include 
diabetes mellitus, metabolic syndrome, cardiovascular disease, and pulmonary disease, 
which are believed to contribute to a reduced life expectancy (American Psychiatric As-

Pathophysiology 
Despite impressive progress in research, no definitive pathophysiology of schizophre-
nia has been determined. Instead, multiple biological abnormalities have been identi-
fied, from aberrant neurotransmitter function (Howes and Kapur 2009; van Rossum 
1966) to inflammation (Mongan et al. 2020) and structural/functional brain changes 
(Li et al. 2019; Shenton et al. 2001). Not only is it often unclear to what extent these find-
ings are causal but evidence also suggests that individuals may have separate biolog-
ical mechanisms (Schwarz et al. 2014). It is beyond the scope of this chapter (and book) 
to provide an overall overview of schizophrenia pathophysiology; this section instead 
focuses on the relevance of reproductive hormones, particularly estrogen. 
As discussed earlier, the estrogen hypothesis of schizophrenia posits both that estro-
gen is protective and that gonadal dysfunction is inherent in the pathology of schizo-
phrenia (Riecher-Rössler and Häfner 1993) . Thus, women with schizophrenia display 
hypoestrogenism independent of expected decreases in estrogen across the lifespan that 
may increase vulnerability. Compared with control subjects, even antipsychotic-naïve 
women with schizophrenia demonstrate elevated prolactin levels, decreased estrogen 
levels, and abnormalities in gonadotropins across the menstrual cycle (González-
Blanco et al. 2016; Riecher-Rössler and Kulkarni 2010). Evidence for this gonadal dys-
function in other psychiatric disorders is lacking, which suggests that it is specific to 
schizophrenia rather than a general consequence of emotional distress (Riecher-Rössler 
et al. 1998). In addition to decreased estrogen levels, the ability of estrogen to modulate 
brain function may be altered in schizophrenia. Schizophrenia is also associated with ge-
netic polymorphisms of the estrogen receptor (Wang et al. 2013) and decreased tran-
scription of the estrogen receptor within the dentate gyrus of the brain (Perlman et al. 
2005). 
There are multiple mechanisms by which low estrogen levels contribute to schizo-
phrenia. Estrogen has a ubiquitous role in the brain, interacting with the neurotrans-
mitters related to schizophrenia and having general procognitive and neuroprotective 
effects. Neurodegeneration in schizophrenia is associated with declines in gray and 
white matter and with ventricular enlargement (Hulshoff Pol and Kahn 2008). Estro-
gen, on the other hand, can increase neurogenesis, synaptic density, and neurotrophic 
factors (Hara et al. 2015) and offer neuropro tection from free radicals, excitotoxicity, 
and inflammation (Hara et al. 2015; McEwen and Alves 1999). In all, estrogen has an 
“antipsychotic” action while buffering against schizophrenia-induced cognitive im -
pairment and neurodegeneration. 
Regarding neurotransmitters, dopamine has perhaps the best-known relationship 
to schizophrenia. Although the dopamine hypothesis, in which schizophrenia is due 
to excess dopamine activity, has undergone revision, it continues to be supported by 
various lines of evidence (Howes and Kapur 2009). Estrogen has extensive interaction

Despite evidence for increasing mesolimbic dopamine activity, animal work sug -
gests that estrogen can both act on its own and augment the effects of antipsychotics 
in behavioral models relevant to psychosis. Prepulse inhibition, a measure of sensory 
gating that is deficient in schizophrenia, is often used as a model to test antipsychotic 
drug effects in animals. Estrogen prevents the deficits in sensory gating in rats in -

to psychotic symptoms (Glenthøj and Hemmingsen 1997). 
Estrogen also increases dopamine in the prefrontal cortex (PFC) in animals (Jacome et 
al. 2010), probably at least partially due to its ability to decrease activity of the degrada-
tion enzyme COMT (Schendzielorz et al. 2011) . Because schi zophr eni a (particularly 
negative symptoms and cognitive deficits) is linked to deficits in frontal cortex dopa-
mine (Howes and Kapur 2009), estrogen-induced increases in PFC dopamine might 
represent a more direct mechanism for estrogen’s therapeutic and protective action. 
Besides dopamine, the advent of atypical antipsychotics that antagonize serotonin 
receptors has emphasized the importance of serotonin in schizophrenia pathology. Es-
trogen acts to enhance serotonin activity, particularly in the PFC, which might buffer 
against the cognitive deficits seen in schizophrenia. Specifically, estrogen-treated rats 
have increased levels of serotonin in the PFC, with concurrent improvement in spatial 
working memory (Inagaki et al. 2010). A likely mechanism for this effect is the estrogen 
receptor’s ability to act as a transcription factor for the tryptophan hydroxylase gene im-
portant in serotonin synthesis (Hiroi and Handa 2013). However, even with tryptophan 
depletion, estrogen treatment can prevent decreases in PFC activity during a working 
memory task, suggesting additional pro-serotonergic effects (Epperson et al. 2012). 
Like estrogen’s behavioral effects on dopamine-induced behavioral models, estro-
gen treatment in animals and humans can prevent disruption of sensory gating audi-
tory processing via serotonin receptor agonis m (Gogos et al. 2006, 2010; Guille et al. 
2011). The ability of estradiol to promote serotonergic activity while simultaneously 
tempering the disruptive effects of direct serotonergic agonism likely speaks to a com-
plex modulatory function. This may be particularly important in schizophrenia, in 
which both antagonizing serotonin receptors and enhancing serotonergic activity are 
paradoxically therapeutic (Silver 2004). 
An additional mechanism by which estrogen might influence schizophrenia patho-
physiology is via interactions with the glutamate system. Hypoglutamatergic activity

Finally, although most data have related the gonadal dysfunction in schizophrenia to 
decreased estrogen, the potential role of progesterone has received less attention. In their 
review, Sun et al. (2016) pointed out that times of vulnerability to psychosis for women 
(menstruation, postpartum, and peri-/post menopause) are also times of decreased 
progesterone. Progesterone’s metabolism into neuroactive steroids such as allopreg -
nanolone plays an important role in GABAergic transmission, which is known to be 
disrupted in schizophrenia (Marx et al. 2011). On its own, progesterone has effects sim-
ilar to those of estrogen in preventing the deficits in sensory gating in animals that occur 
with dopamine agonists (Rupprecht et al. 1999) and NMDA antagonists (van den 
Buuse et al. 2015). These findings point to a need for additional research in schizophre-
nia that takes into account the progesterone and progesterone/estrogen interactions. 
Other hormones, such as dehydroepiandrosterone, as well as gonadotropins such 
as luteinizing hormone and follicle-stimulating hormone, may also contribute to the 
pathophysiology of schizophrenia in women (Hayes et al. 2012). Compared with es -
trogen, the available evidence on these other hormones is limited. Current evidence 
does suggest that estrogen interacts with the dopamine, serotonin, and glutamate sys-
tems in a way that provides protective and therapeutic action in schizophrenia. When 
combined with estrogen’s general effects on neuroprotection and cognition, this helps 
explain the pathological role of hypoestrogenism in patients with schizophrenia. 
Treatment 
Pharmacology in Pregnancy 
Although various treatment modalities contribute to the comprehensive treatment of 
psychotic disorders, pharmacotherapy remains at the core of care for both acute and 
maintenance treatment. In this section, we review safety data for first-generation an-
tipsychotic (FGA) and second-generation antipsychotic (SGA) medications, which are 
the mainstay of treatment for psychotic disorders, with special consideration given to 
clozapine and LAI (depot) formulations. Data surrounding antipsychotic exposure in 
pregnancy and lactation are generally reassuring but are limited in power relative to 
data concerning other major psychopharmacological agents (e.g., selective serotonin 
reuptake inhibitors). In addition, availa ble data are plagued by the methodological 
limitations inherent in studying pregnant or lactating women and are limited to ob -
servational case studies, manufacturer reports, registry studies, and, more recently, a 
select number of larger prospective cohort studies. 
When starting a new antipsychotic medication in pregnancy, consider agents with 
relatively more robust safety information, such as haloperidol, risperidone, olanza -
pine, aripiprazole, and quetiapine, versus newer agents, such as lurasidone, asenap-

In the context of acute agitation, medications with more sedating qualities may be pre-
ferred, whereas in a less acute scenario it may be prudent to avoid these same agents, 
which may impede the mother’s ability to wake overnight for infant care. 
Of note, high rates of polypsychopharm acology have been noted in women with 
schizophrenia during pregnancy (Møller-Ol sen et al. 2018; Sadowski et al. 2013), 
which is of concern due to the risk of negative synergy from various medications. Sa-
dowski et al. (2013) noted an elevated risk of adverse outcomes with the exposure to 
polypharmacy, including large for gestational age infants, prematurity, neonatal inten-
sive care unit (NICU) admission, neonatal issues with adaptation, and malformations. 
Thus, when clinically possible, psychiatric clinicians should aim to minimize poly -
pharmacy during pregnancy. 
Although all antipsychotic medications are known to cross the placenta, individual 
agents vary in their placental passage ratio and thus result in varying degrees of fetal 
exposure. Using maternal and umbilical co rd plasma samples co llected at delivery, 
Newport et al. (2007) quantified this rati o for four commonly used antipsychotics 
across 50 maternal–infant pairs. In this study, the placental passage ratio was highest 
for olanzapine (72.1%; SD 42%) and the lowest was for quetiapine (23.8%; SD 11.0%). 
Haloperidol (65.5%; SD 40.3%) and risperidone (49.2%; SD 33.0%) rates were also re -
ported. 
Pharmacokinetic changes related to pregnancy are important to consider in the psy-
chiatric care of women with schizophrenia. Pregnancy induces changes in the hepatic 
cytochrome (CY) P450 system, which may influence the metabolism of antipsychotic 
medications. There is decreased activity of CYP1A2 (e.g., reduced clearance of olanzap-
ine or clozapine), increased activity of CYP2D6 (e.g., greater clearance of risperidone, 
haloperidol, and chlorpromazine), and increased activity of CYP3A4 (e.g., greater clear-
ance of lurasidone, aripiprazole, and quetiapine) (Anderson 2005). Because the magni-
tude of these changes varies considerably among individuals, women maintained on 
antipsychotics during pregnancy should have close monitoring of clinical symptoms to 
determine side effect burden and any need for dosage adjustments. LAI antipsychotics 
bypass first-pass hepatic metabolism; as such, dosing of these may not be affected by 
pregnancy-related metabolic changes. 
Women with psychotic disorders are more likely than their healthy counterparts to 
smoke (Szatkowski and McNeill 2015). Because smoking induces activity of CYP1A2 
(Anderson and Chan 2016), a reduction or cessation in smoking due to pregnancy 
may lead to an effective increase in levels of olanzapine or clozapine at stable oral dos-
ing. In addition, patients are likely to require higher levels of nicotine replacement 
therapy in the second and third trimesters due to an increase in CYP2A6 activity (Ko-
ren and Pariente 2018; Szatkowski and McNeill 2015). 
First-Generation Antipsychotics 
FGAs have been an established treatment for psychotic disorders over decades and 
historically were also used for treatment of hyperemesis gravidarum. Although data 
regarding their relative safety in pregnancy have accumulated over several decades, 
most of these data are limited to case reports and reports of adverse effects compiled 
by pharmaceutical companies (A bel et al. 2014), yielding information that is limited 
and often inconsistent in findings.

Early pregnancy. Overall, the data on FGAs (including promethazine, chlorproma-
zine, haloperidol, perphenazine, trifluoperazine, loxapine, thioridazine, flupenthixol, 
and fluphenazine) suggest that they are not likely to be major teratogens (Diav-Citrin 
et al. 2005; Einarson and Boskovic 2009). Nonetheless, the association of early exposure 
to haloperidol with limb abnormalities cannot be definitely ruled out (Gentile 2010), 
and some studies that have shown an overall increased risk of major malformations are 
limited by multiple confounders. 
Obstetrical outcomes. Meta-analyses have shown small but statistically signifi -
cant increases in earlier delivery and low birth weight, although most of these studies 
were noted not to be able to separate out drug exposure versus confounders seen in 
women with severe mental illness (Coughlin et al. 2015; Diav-Citrin et al. 2005). There 
has been no noted increased risk of spontaneous abortions (Coughlin et al. 2015). 
Delivery effects. In 2011, the FDA issued a drug safety communication describing 
a concern about an association between third-trimester exposure to antipsychotic 
medication (FGAs and SGAs) and extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS) and withdrawal 
symptoms in the newborn (Kulkarni et al. 2015). This warning was based on 69 cases 
of infants who had been exposed to antipsychotics and had neonatal symptoms; how-
ever, it did not account for multiple conf ounders (e.g., polypharmacy or underlying 
illness) present in most of the cases (Kulkarni et al. 2015). EPS have been documented 
to last up to 10 months, but the vast majo rity of cases resolve within days, with no 
long-term adverse sequelae (Galbally et al. 2014). 
Long-term effects. There have been no differences in IQ or development when 
FGA-exposed infants were followed up at 5 years of age (Galbally et al. 2014). Prospec-
tive studies have shown some delay in gross motor skills at 6 months for some infants, 
but this effect has been transient and FGA-exposed infants showed no significant dif-
ferences from control subjects at 12–24 months (Poels et al. 2018). 
Second-Generation Antipsychotics 
Use of SGAs has increased dramatically in the general population since their intro -
duction in the 1990s, and recent evidence suggests this trend is seen among pregnant 
women as well (Epstein et al. 2013). Although SGAs have increasingly replaced FGAs 
as the mainstay of treatment for schizophrenia, the bulk of this increase cannot be ac-
counted for by use in persons with psychotic illness and is likely due to an increase in 
prescribing for wider indications (i.e., bipolar disorder) as well as off-label use (i.e., in-
somnia) (Galbally et al. 2014). With the exception of risperidone and paliperidone, 
SGAs present less risk for prolactin elevation than the FGAs and may be more favorable 
for women attempting to conceive. Advantages over FGAs include lower likelihood of 
EPS and tardive dyskinesia. However, SGAs pose an increased risk for gestational dia-
betes (Bodén et al. 2012) that can lead to poor outcomes for maternal and fetal health.

and found that the risk of any malformation was increased in the exposed group rela-

metabolic side effects, there has also been concern about their potential to contribute 
to excess gestational weight gain or gestational diabetes, two complications of preg -

2009; Sweeting et al. 2016). Although a registry analysis done by Freeman et al. (2018) 
did not show a difference in mean weight gain associated with SGA exposure during 
pregnancy, women in this study who were taking SGAs were more likely to begin 
pregnancy with a BMI in the obese range (P<0.0001) compared with control subjects 
with similar psychiatric morbidity, illustrating the importance of strategies to address 
SGA-associated weight gain in women of reproductive age as part of routine practice. 
In contrast, however, in an analysis of a Medicaid database of 1,543,334 pregnancies, 
Park et al. (2018) analyzed the risk of developing gestational diabetes among women 
who continued an SGA medication throughout pregnancy relative to women who had 
previously been taking an SGA medication but discontinued prior to pregnancy. In this 
study, women who continued treatment with either olanzapine (absolute risk reduc-
tion [aRR] 1.61; 95% CI 1.13–2.29) or quet iapine (aRR 1.28; 95% CI 1.01–1.62) during

study suggests transient ne urodevelopmental effects among infants born to women 
taking antipsychotic medications but cannot rule out the contribution of the underly-

Clozapine 
Although clozapine is categorized as an SGA, it warrants separate mention because 
it is the gold standard medication for tr eatment-resistant schizophrenia. Due to po -
tential serious side effects (e.g., seizures, myocarditis, agranulocytosis, weight gain, 
excess sedation) and the need for routine serum monitoring, its use is reserved for pa-
tients for whom other treatments have been ineffective or intolerable (Warnez and 
Alessi-Severini 2014). Therefore, patients who are appropriate candidates for cloza -
pine are highly likely to require maintenance medication treatment throughout preg-
nancy to remain at their baseline level of function and symptom control (Friedman and 
Adityanjee 2003). Reports of clozapine use in pregnancy are very often confounded by 
polypharmacy, including known teratogens (Friedman and Adityanjee 2003). Mehta 
and Van Lieshout (2017) noted regarding the current state of the literature, “in the ab-
sence of control participants, it is unclear whether these risks are due to clozapine, the 
experience of serious psychotic illness, previous treatment, or a combination of these” 
(p. 2). Due to fluctuating clozapine levels during pregnancy, frequent monitoring of se-
rum levels and white blood cell count is recommended regardless of psychiatric sta -
bility (Nguyen et al. 2020). 
Early pregnancy. Clozapine has been used as an FDA-approved medication since 
1990 in the United States and for longer in Europe, and no clear pattern of malforma-
tions has been noted. The rate of malformations does not appear to be elevated based 
on available data (Friedman and Adityanjee 2003; Mehta and Van Lieshout 2017). The

Long-Acting Injections 
The literature on LAI antipsychotic use in pregnancy is extremely sparse and limited to 
a handful of case reports on individual long-acting agents, including haloperidol, 
fluphenazine, aripiprazole, olanzapine, risperidone, and paliperidone (Ballester-Gra-
cia et al. 2019; Clinebell et al. 2017; Kim et al. 2007; Manouilenko et al. 2018; Özdemir 
et al. 2015; Sabo et al. 2018; Zamora Rodríguez et al. 2017). Although published cases 
have not described any reports of major malformations among LAI-exposed infants, 
there have been reports of adverse obstetrical outcomes and neonatal effects. A case re-
port of a woman taking LAI risperidone in addition to oral risperidone, benztropine,

Pharmacology in Lactation 
The postpartum period is one of elevated risk of recurrence for women with schizo -
phrenia. Ideally, discussion around infant feeding preferences and treatment planning 
should occur collaboratively and proactively with the patient early in her pregnancy 
and regularly as the pregnancy progresses. Psychiatrists should provide available ev-
idence about the risks and benefits of breastfeeding for the patient’s specific situation, 
as well as the associated risks of formula-feeding versus indicated medications. 
In the setting of acute psychotic symptoms, suicidality, or homicidality in the post-
partum period, an immediate safety assessment should take priority to determine the 
need for either inpatient hospitalization or measures to address safety while outpatient 
care is delivered. Women with acute symptoms may not be capable of safely breastfeed-
ing their infants. Furthermore, women with a range of acute or  historical symptoms 
may prefer to formula-feed to allow for more opportunities for consolidated sleep. 
The decision of how best to feed an infant is one that should be made in the context 
of the patient’s clinical presentation and history, as well as her personal preferences. 
When a woman with schizophrenia does choose to breastfeed her infant, the treating 
psychiatric clinician should proactively discuss a plan for monitoring medication ex-
posure effects on the infant that includes collaboration with the infant’s pediatrician 
and support for adequate sleep. Lactation support services should be part of the treat-
ment plan. When a patient has been treate d with an efficaciou s medication during 
pregnancy, consideration should be given to continuing that treatment; switching 
medication during a high-risk period will introduce the infant to multiple exposures 
and increase the patient’s risk of symptom relapse. Discussion of safety assessment in
