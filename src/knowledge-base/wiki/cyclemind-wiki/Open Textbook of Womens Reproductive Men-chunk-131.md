Special Considerations 
Premature/Medically Ill Infants 
Each year, about 8%–10% of infants born in the United States need admission to a neo-
natal intensive care unit (NICU) due to prematurity or medical complications (Harri-
son and Goodman 2015). NICU admission brings on many challenges for infants and 
their parents and possibly within their relationship. NICU infants experience early 
separation, unnatural environments, painful and intrusive procedures, problems with 
physiological regulation, and long period s of isolation (Maroney 2003). They have 
been hypothesized to be neurologically more susceptible to both positive and negative 
environmental factors (Krugers et al. 2016). 
In turn, NICU parents have a significantly higher rate of depression, anxiety, and 
PTSD, as well as generally higher levels of psychological stress (Roque et al. 2017). The 
transition to parenthood often happens in an abrupt, unexpected, and traumatic man-
ner for these parents. They may struggle with parental self-efficacy because they are 
often dependent on NICU staff to care for their infant. They must adjust to caring for a 
medically vulnerable infant and communicate with multiple medical providers (Vance 
et al. 2020), and they may have fears of possibly hurting their infant themselves. NICU

tion, improving parental presence and self-efficacy, and familiarizing parents with pre-
mature infant communication and needs (Scala et al. 2021). As an example, promoting 
and facilitating SSC between parent and infant is part of developmental care practices. 
SSC has proved to be highly beneficial, with significant health benefits specifically for 
premature infants (Campbell-Yeo et al. 2015). Benefits of SSC for premature infants 
include reduced mortality, improved physiology (thermoregulation and cardiovascu-
lar stability), improved behaviors (breastfeeding initiation, sleep), less procedural dis-
comfort, and improved neurodevelopment (Campbell-Yeo et al. 2015). 
A number of formal programs are available, each of which utilizes a different com-
bination of interventions to improve infant and parental outcomes for NICU families. 
The Newborn Individualized Developmental Care and Assessment Program, Family 
Nurture Intervention, Mother-Infant Transaction Program, Creating Opportunities for 
Parent Empowerment, Auditory Tactile Visual Vestibular Interventions, NICU Family 
Support Core Curriculum, and Close Collaboration With Parents are among the more 
established and studied formal NICU programs (Scala et al. 2021). Despite advances 
in addressing infant and parental distress in the NICU, a significant challenge remains 
for identifying interventions that can support parental involvement in the NICU, sup-
port infant development, and optimize preterm neurodevelopmental outcomes (Au-

Fathers 
Fathers and partners play a crucial—yet less well understood—role in infants’ psy -
chosocial and neurobiological development. At the same time, there is increasing rec-
ognition that the perinatal period is a vulnerable time for fathers’ mental health. Two 
meta-analyses found an 8%–10% prevalence rate of paternal depression (with the 
highest incidence between 3 and 6 months postpartum) compared with the 4% prev-
alence rate of depression in men in the general population (Cameron et al. 2016; Paul-
son and Bazemore 2010). A meta-analysis of studies from 12 countries underscored the

higher risk fathers experience during the perinatal period and showed high levels of 
paternal anxiety during this time (Lieferman et al. 2021). The rate of paternal anxiety 
during perinatal period was estimated to be 10.7%, which is a significant increase from

in the transition to parenthood, and there may be a correlation between paternal and 
maternal depression (de Magistris et al. 2013; Paulson and Bazemore 2010). 
Fathers can be key attachment figures, an d a positive perception of the parenting 
role is consistently associated with patern al caregiving activities and interpersonal 
warmth (Lucassen et al. 2011). As with maternal involvement, paternal involvement 
begins prior to birth; for example, internal parental representations during the mother’s 
pregnancy are positively associated with later child development (Maas et al. 2012; 
Vreeswijk et al. 2014). After birth, paternal caregiving is particularly important in in-
fancy, in part due to alleviating maternal stress (Kim et al. 2016). However, there is still 
much to learn about the father–infant relationship, particularly in early infancy (Ram-
chandani et al. 2013; Tamis-LeMonda et al. 2004). 
LGBTQ+ Parents 
A similar need for further study is in the ro le of other nonbirth parents, such as for 
LGBTQ+ families. A steadily increasing number of same-gender and nonbinary cou-
ples are building families and raising children, and this number is likely to continue to 
grow (American Academy of Pediatrics Committee on Psychosocial Aspects of Child 
and Family Health 2013). According to the U.S. Census, between 2 million and 3.7 mil-
lion children under age 18 have an LGBTQ+ parent, and approximately 200,000 of 
these children are being raised by a same-sex couple (Family Equality Council 2017). 
Extensive evidence supports the lack of relationship between parents’ sexual ori -
entation and children’s emotional, psychosocial, and behavioral development (Farr et 
al. 2010; Golombok and Badger 2010; Stacey and Biblarz 2001; Tasker 2005; Wainright 
et al. 2004). In contrast, many studies have attested to the normal development of chil-
dren in same-gender families when the ch ild is wanted, the pa rents have a commit-
ment to shared parenting, and the parents have strong economic and social support. 
This has led current researchers to conclude that the same set of factors determine 
healthy child development no matter what the sex or gender of their parents: 
In all, it is now well-established that the adjustment of children and adolescents is best 
accounted for by variations in the quality of the relationships with their parents, the qual-
ity of the relationship between the parents or significant adults in the children’s and ad-
olescents’ lives, and the availability of economic and socio-economic resources. (Lamb 
2012, p. 106) 
The American Academy of Pediatrics (AAP ) has clear policy statements supporting 
these facts: 
To promote optimal health and well-being of all children, the [AAP] supports access for 
all children to (1) civil marriage rights for their parents and (2) willing and capable foster 
and adoptive parents, regardless of the parents’ sexual orientation...the AAP has sup -
ported families in all their diversity, because the family has always been the basic social 
unit in which children develop the supporting and nurturing relationships with adults

These families nevertheless have a particular need for advocacy and support from 
mental health professionals and health care providers, because families headed by sex-

lack of provider knowledge, external societal stigma, and homophobia, despite greater 
visibility and wider cultural acceptance (Mayer et al. 2008; Webster and Telingator 
2016). Additionally, there are aspects specific to each subpopulation in this group, such 
as pregnancy, birth, and parenting in LGBTQ+ families. 
There remains a wide knowledge gap in the study of LGBTQ+ families and their ex-
periences through pregnancy, the transition to parenthood, the experiences of early par-
enting, and parent–infant interactions; this is in part because pregnancy and parenting 
research is still centered on a heteronormative framework (Charter et al. 2018; Darwin 
and Greenfield 2019). Some research has focused on lesbian experiences of reproduc-
tive and maternity care but very little on the experience of pregnancy and parenting; 
research focusing on the experiences of gay male parents and transgender parents re-
mains scarce (Darwin and Greenfield 2019; Hafford-Letchfield et al. 2019; Tornello 
and Bos 2017; Van Rijn-van Gelderen et al. 2018). In addition, although there has been 
increasing focus on paternal mental health, a heteronormative approach to these data 
risks conflating gender (manhood/fatherhood ) with the role of coparenting or co -
caretaking (Darwin and Greenfield 2019). 
Much remains to be understood in terms of the complexity of family structures, such 
as a lesbian couple with a child who is biologically connected to a nonbirth parent or the 
existence of more than one birth parent within a particular family (Darwin and Green-
field 2019; Goldberg 2006). Despite these limitations, important concepts are emerging. 
For example, recent research has started to examine the experiences of preconception, 
pregnancy, and new motherhood from the perspective of lesbian nonbiological moth-
ers (McKelvey 2014; Wojnar and Katzenmeyer 2014). One descriptive phenomenol -
ogy study concluded that nonbirth lesbian parents experience an overall theme of 
“feeling different” (e.g., legal and biological concerns, losing relationships, feeling in-
complete, and the need to carve out a unique role) that permeate their preconception, 
pregnancy, birth, and early parenting experiences (Wojnar and Katzenmeyer 2014). 
Adoptive Parents 
Adoption is an important area of overlap with the discussion of LGBTQ+ parents and 
a topic of extensive study unto itself. Th ere are approximately 1.5 million adopted 
children living in the United States, or ab out 2% of all U.S. children (Kreider and 
Lofquist 2014). Recent trends in adoption include changes in the demographics of in-
fants and children over the past two decades. International adoptions into the United 
States peaked with nearly 23,000 per year in both 2004 and 2005. Due to various legal 
and political changes, by 2019, just under 3,000 children were adopted into the United 
States (U.S. Department of State Bureau of Consular Affairs 2020). Increasingly, these 
children tend to be older and may have had more placements in foster care or orphan-
ages prior to adoption. The number of children in foster care in the United States has 
increased slightly over the past decade, although it was decreasing from 2009 to 2012.

Racial disparities also may affect the health and well-being of adoptive children. Ap-
proximately 15,000 infants per year are adopted directly from birth, and these are more 
likely to be white infants, most often through “open adoptions.” Most domestic adop-
tions in the United States are from foster care following abuse or neglect in the child’s 
original home. “Most of these children have suffered traumatic losses, are no longer in-
fants, are of color, are in sibling groups that should be placed together, and/or have 
special physical, learning, or behavioral health needs that have been identified or are 
yet to emerge” (Siegel 2015, p. 19). Lack of “secure and enduring relationships with 
committed and nurturing adults” (America n Academy of Pediatrics Committee on 
Psychosocial Aspects of Child and Family He alth 2013, p. 827) in early life, deemed 
necessary by the AAP , can have long lasting impacts for adopted children and their 
families. 
Like the many constellations of parents, adopted children comprise a heterogenous 
group. Preadoption experiences and degree of maltreatment or neglect, age at adop -
tion, length of time exposed to adversities prior to adoption, and in-utero exposures to 
substances all have a direct impact on child outcome (Harwood et al. 2013). Adoption 
type could be associated wi th some of these factors; for instance, children adopted 
through international or foster care pathwa ys show poorer school outcomes and in -
creased health care needs that are at least partially related to differences in preadoption 
placement settings (Harwood et al. 2013). However, most adopted children form se-
cure attachments with their adoptive pa rents and do well developmentally (van 
Londen et al. 2007). As with the general infant and young child population, the quality 
of parent–child relationships can at least partially mediate the effects of adverse pre-
and postnatal adversities and lead to better social and cognitive development (Stams 
et al. 2002). A study by Niemann and Weiss (2012) evaluated factors affecting attach-
ment 6 months after international adoption for children who were adopted late in the 
first or second year of life. Children wi th fewer placements prior to adoption and 
lower stress levels after adoption (as measured by basal salivary cortisol) were more 
likely to have attachment security with their adoptive mothers (Niemann and Weiss 
2012). It is hypothesized that a lower infant stress level may be a protective factor in 
promoting bonding with the adoptive moth er, and, alternatively, responsive care 
from the mother may buffer HPA axis dysregulation, leading to a lower stress level in 
the infant who is developing an attachment bond with the adoptive mother. 
As with all parent–infant dyads, the adoptee’s temperament can play a role in the 
dynamics of the relationship with the parent. Adopted children with a more difficult 
temperament have lower social and cognitive development and higher level of inter-
nalizing and externalizing behavior problems at age 7 (Stams et al. 2002). Multiple 
studies have evaluated short-  and long-term predictors of development in adoptive 
infants and children. Maternal sensitivity appears to be the strongest factor in promot-
ing secure attachment and positive social -emotional development, although respon-
sive and flexible parenting in general and adult attachme nt style are also factors 
(Roberson 2006). Maternal sensitivity is important both during early childhood and as 
children grow into adolescence and can help maintain a secure attachment. A large, 
longitudinal study of early infant international adoptees in the Netherlands also found

A randomized controlled trial was recently published of an intervention focused on 
increasing maternal sensitivity in 83 families with internationally adopted children in 
Italy. Video-Feedback Intervention to Promote Positive Parenting in Adoption and Fos-

parenting over 4–6 months and uses emotional availability as a measure of maternal 
sensitivity (Barone et al. 2019). Children showed improvement in emotional availabil-
ity and externalizing behaviors at 6-month follow up, mediated by maternal improve-
ments in emotional availability during the intervention. The highest benefit was noted 
in children with the most temperamental negative affect whose mother’s emotional 
availability score increased over the course of the study (Barone et al. 2019). This high-
lights one possible intervention for supporting adoptive parents in improving sensitive 
and responsive caregiving and thereby improving outcomes for their adopted children 
even beyond early childhood. 
Adoptive parents may also face unique challenges that increase their risk of de -
pressive symptoms, including infertility, financial stressors, and undergoing evalua-
tion by adoption agencies (F ields et al. 2010). For example,  rates of depression in 39 
adoptive mothers were 25.6% before adoption and 15.4% 6 weeks after adoption (Se-
necky et al. 2009). A longer-term study of 86 families also found decreasing rates of 
depression over the first-year following adoption, as measured by a modified version 
of the Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale. The rates were 27.9% at 0–4 weeks, 25.6% 
at 5–12 weeks, and 12.8% at 13–52 weeks postadoption. Stress and adjustment diffi -
culties predicted depression in this study (F ields et al. 2010). A larger study of rates 
of depression and anxiety symptoms in adoptive mothers compared with postpartum 
mothers found similar rates of depression between the two groups but less anxiety in 
adoptive mothers. Higher levels of reported sleep deprivation, history of infertility, past 
psychological disorder, and lower marital satisfaction predicted higher rates of depres-
sion (Mott et al. 2011). Foli et al. (2012) evaluated predictors and proposed a theory of 
postadoption depression, suggesting the assessment of factors including parents’ ex-
pectations of themselves as parents and of family and friends, their expectations for the 
child, feelings of rest, history of depression and low self-esteem, and interpersonal vari-
ables such as parent–child bonding, marital satisfaction, and perceived support. Further 
research is needed on optimal times to screen for postadoption depression and other pa-
rental mental health concerns, given the increased stressors these families often face.
