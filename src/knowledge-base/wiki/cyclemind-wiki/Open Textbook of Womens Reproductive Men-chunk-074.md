and valid (Allen et al. 1991). The Premenst rual Symptoms Screening Tool (Steiner et 
al. 2003) is another short 19-item retrospective tool that can be used in both adults and 
adolescents with PMDD. The 21-item Daily Record of Severity of Problems (DRSP; 
Endicott et al. 2006) was developed to aid in the diagnosis and evaluation of DSM-IV 
PMDD (included in DSM-IV’s “Criteria Sets and Axes Provided for Further Study”; 
American Psychiatric Association 1994). The DRSP is the most widely used screening 
and diagnostic tool and should be prospectively completed for at least two consecu -
tive menstrual cycles. It includes 11 of the symptoms of the DSM-5 criteria for PMDD, 
which are rated on a severity scale from 1 (none at all) to 6 (extreme). The Carolina Pre-
menstrual Assessment Scoring System (Eisenlohr-Moul et al. 2017) is a new diagnostic 
tool based on DSM-5 PMDD criteria that us es items from the DRSP to create a diag -
nostic algorithm; this standardized method of scoring can be completed manually or 
via computer and is available for SAS and Excel macros. 
Other older mood rating scales that have been used for the diagnosis of PMDD in-
clude the Calendar of Premenstrual Experiences (Mortola et al. 1990), the Visual An-
alogue Scales for Premenstrual Mood Symptoms (Steiner and Streiner 2005), and the 
Premenstrual Tension Syndrome Rating Scales (Steiner et al. 2011a). Many cellphone 
symptom trackers also exist, but these are not validated screening tools.

TABLE 6–2. Diagnostic criteria for premenstrual syndrome 
Affective symptoms Somatic symptoms 
Angry outbursts Abdominal bloating 
Anxiety Breast tenderness or swelling 
Confusion Headache 
Depression Joint or muscle pain 
Irritability Swelling of extremities 
Social withdrawal Weight gain 
Patient must report at least one of the symptoms above from each category during the 5 days 
before menses in each of her three previous menstrual cycles. 
Symptoms must be relieved within 4 days of the onset of menses, without recurrence until at 
least day 13 of the cycle. 
Symptoms must be present in the absence of pharmacological treatment, hormone ingestion, 
or drug or alcohol use. 
Patient must exhibit functional impairment in social, academic, or work performance. 
Source. Adapted from American College of Obstetricians and Gynecologists 2014.
