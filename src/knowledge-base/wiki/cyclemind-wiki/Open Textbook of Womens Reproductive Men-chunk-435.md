findings (Fornaro et al. 2020). This study also found an increased risk of overall mal-
formations for lithium-exposed infants (OR 1.81; 95% CI 1.35–2.41). Although the risk 
of cardiac malformations was increased in lithium-exposed infants when compared

icant (OR 1.59; 95% CI 0.91–2.77). Cumulatively, these data suggest that although lith-

occurs at a much lower magnitude than previously postulated. Although the overes-

challenges, it is likely that the underlying illness is also a confounding factor.

women with verified BDI. Although the authors noted that the study and control 
groups did not differ in age of illness onset (one indicator of disease severity in patients 
with BD), the medications utilized in the control group during pregnancy, acute mood 
episodes during pregnancy, and additional measures of disease severity were not re-
ported. Thus, underlying illness remains a potential confounder (Poels et al. 2020). 
Obstetrical outcomes. Lithium has not been found to increase the risk of preeclamp-
sia, gestational diabetes, rates of cesarean delivery, preterm birth, low birth weight, or 
postpartum hemorrhage when women taking  it have been compared with disease-
matched control subjects (Fornaro et al. 2020; Munk-Olsen et al. 2018). Two case reports 
describe polyhydramnios in women taking lithium during pregnancy (Poels et al. 
2018).
