Key Points 
• Estrogen likely plays a protective role in the disease course of schizophrenia, 
and periods of estrogen decline are associated with new onset or exacerba-
tion of psychotic symptoms. 
• Most women with schizophrenia will have at least one pregnancy, and discus-
sion of family planning goals and sexual practices is an essential component 
of psychiatric care. 
• Available evidence suggests that pregnancy is not protective against chronic 
psychotic illness; the postpartum period  represents a time of increased risk 
of relapse for women with schizophrenia. 
• Treatment planning for women with schizophrenia dur ing and after pregnancy 
requires consideration of risk of the untreated illness alongside the risks of ef-
ficacious medications, with the goal of risk reduction. It is not possible to elim-
inate all risk. 
• Although evidence on the safety of antipsychotic medications is limited by 
methodological constraints, available information suggests that these medi -
cations are not major teratogens. 
• Although many mothers with schizophrenia parent their children, as a group 
they are at high risk for custody loss. Support for parenting is an essential part 
of a treatment plan.
