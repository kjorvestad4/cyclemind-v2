Chapter 8, is expanded to include potential risks to a fetus— 
both as a result of the treatments themselves and from lack of (or under-) treatment. 
Once the risks of treatment and the risks of no treatment/undertreatment are clarified 
(Spinelli 2012), initial steps in decision-m aking can be more easily taken. Cases in 
which medication (an SSRI) would be heav ily considered include high severity of 
symptoms (especially leading to safety concerns or mark ed impairment of function-
ing), a history of prior good response to an SSRI, and a history of prior ineffective trial 
of CBT alone. Because many cases do not necessarily include these elements, and often 
fall in a “gray zone” of severity, other factors to consider include the patient’s prefer-
ence, willingness and ability to engage in close follow-up, presence or absence of family 
and social support, and time/financial resources (with regard to pursuing psychother-
apy). Given the potential for CBT to decrease relapse rates when medication is discon-
tinued (which is always a possibility in perinatal cases), it is generally recommended 
that patients who are proceeding with SSRI treatment also pursue CBT if possible. 
As is the case with perinatal depression and anxiety disorders, all SSRIs are reason-
able options with regard to risk profile in pregnancy and lactation (Table 20–5), with 
the possible exception of paroxetine, which had been linked with cardiac malforma -
tions in earlier epidemiological studies. Recent data have not supported this finding,

Once the decision is made to pursue medication treatment in perinatal OCD, fol -
lowing guidelines for nonperinatal OCD is generally recommended, with some mod-
ifications. Because the prospect of starting medication in pregnancy or postpartum is 
worrisome to many perinatal patients, it is not unreasonable to start at half doses to 
minimize side effects and provide reassurance. At the start, it is important to set the 
patient’s expectations appropriately, with the knowledge that substantial symptom 
improvement often does not occur until 4–6 weeks after initiation of medication, and 
many patients may need up to 10–12 weeks. Medication doses can be titrated up every 
5–7 days or as indicated by the manufacturer. Although careful avoidance of overmed-
ication is important in perinatal cases, undermedication also carries risks because the 
patient and her baby would be exposed to the double risk of the disease process and 
the medication itself, without reaping benefits of the treatment. It is thus important to 
pursue the minimum effective dose, which is likely to equate to higher SSRI doses (as 
would be the case for nonperinatal popula tions). Compared with nonperinatal pa -
tients, perinatal patients would benefit from more frequent follow-up visits and mon-
itoring during this uptitration process. This is especially the case for pregnant patients 
as they enter their third trimester, when plasma drug levels can drop due to increased 
metabolism, thereby necessitating higher doses for symptom control (Sit et al. 2006). It 
is also critical to closely monitor for side effects and to explain these fully to the patient 
ahead of time. Pharmacotherapy-based approaches to address side effect complaints 
such as insomnia and fatigue may be cons idered, but these should be pursued with 
caution given the general preference to avoid polypharmacy in perinatal cases and the 
unknown risk profile of many of these agents in pregnancy and lactation. 
As would be the case in nonperinatal populations, an alternative treatment should 
be considered when good response is not achieved after 13–20 weeks of weekly outpa-
tient CBT in perinatal cases. Strategies at this point include increasing to daily CBT or 
adding an SSRI (combined treatment). For perinatal patients taking SSRIs who have not 
achieved a good response after 8–12 weeks (including 4–6 weeks at the highest comfort-
ably tolerated dose), the following factors, which could be contributing to low or neg-
ligible treatment response, should be addresse d first, if relevant: interference by co-
occurring conditions, inadequate patient adherence, presence of psychosocial stressors, 
family members’ accommodation or reinforcing of symptoms, inability to tolerate med-
ication (Koran et al. 2007), and decreased therapeutic efficacy of medication due to 
physiological changes of pregnancy. If such factors have already been addressed, or 
are not relevant, then the following strategies can be considered: switch to a different

as gestational diabetes. It is reassuring, however, that antipsychotics do not appear to
