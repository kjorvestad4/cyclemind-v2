Key Points 
• Prenatal (di)stress includes daily hass les, perceived stress, and major life 
events and a range of mental health problems, such as depression and anxiety. 
• Prenatal (di)stress is associated with adverse neurobehavioral outcomes 
throughout fetal, infant, and child development. 
• Traumatic events pr ior to pregnancy can influence prenatal (di)stress. 
• The influence of prenatal (dis)tress can be moderated by factors such as so-
cioeconomic status and social support. 
• Evidence-based screening tools are available to identify prenatal (di)stress. 
• Evidence-based interventions are available for the treatment of prenatal de-
pression and other mood disturbances. 
• Careful considerations must be made to avoid blaming mothers and women 
for the influence of prenatal (di)stress on child outcomes.

Psychiatrists and other mental health clinicians treating women during the peri-
natal period have a unique opportunity to assess infant mental health and to provide 
early intervention during a sensitive developmental period. In the prenatal and imme-
diate postpartum periods, a complex interplay between biological and environmental 
factors sets the stage for the offspring’s lifelong physical, neurocognitive, and social-
emotional development. The quality of the main caregiving relationships is among the 
most influential environmental factors in infancy and early childhood. Infants grow in 
an interpersonal context, and they start to learn about themselves and the world they 
live in via their daily interactions with their primary caregivers. Of note, throughout 
this chapter we use the terms primary caregivers and parents interchangeably, although 
we realize that parents are not always the primary or the only primary caregivers for 
infants and young children.

and well-being. We then review key components of the parent–infant relationship as 
they affect infant mental health, including attachment, biological pathways that influ-

Infant Mental Health 
Infant mental health  has been defined as “the develo ping capacity of the child from 
birth to age three to experience, regulate, and express emotions; form close and secure 
interpersonal relationships; and explore the environment and learn—all in the context 
of family, community and cultural expectations for young children” (Zero to Three In-
fant Mental Health Task Force Steering Committee 2001). An understanding of infant 
mental health is important even for clinic ians who focus on adult mental health, be -
cause one of its founding principles is th at early life experiences—whether positive 
or adverse—shape the brain and have a lifelong impact on health, behavior, and cog -
nition into adulthood (Clinton et al. 2016). 
The Adverse Childhood Experiences study is one example that highlights the im -
portance of the early environment in affecting health and well-being into adulthood. 
This study showed that adverse childhood experiences (ACEs) such as abuse, neglect, 
and trauma had a dose-response, lifelong effect on the incidence of conditions linked 
to the risk of mortality, such as depres sion, suicide attempts, alcoholism, substance 
abuse, cigarette smoking, cardiovascular events, and cancer (Felitti et al. 1998). A sub-
sequent systematic review an d meta-analysis showed that the number of ACEs that 
co-occur (e.g., four or more) was strongly associated with poor mental, physical, and 
sexual health in adulthood and was linked with outcomes that affect families across

In contrast, a sound social and emotional base through secure attachment creates a 
foundation for robust physical, cognitive, and motor development (Clinton et al. 2016). 
The Parent–Infant Relationship 
Attachment 
Human infants, through repeated interactions with caregivers, learn to seek comfort 
and protection from a selective number of re liable caregivers (Finelli et al. 2019). At-
tachment describes this bond with a main caregiver, from whom the infant seeks safety 
and nurturance. Attachment between infants and caregivers is crucial for their devel-
opment because it increases the likelihood of infant survival and serves as the bedrock 
of cognitive, social, and emotional development (Insel and Young 2001). Attachment, 
in terms of behaviors that govern connections between infant and caregiver, can be 
observed throughout mammalian development (Insel and Young 2001). The concept 
of attachment was first named by John Bowlby, who defined it as “a lasting psycholog-
ical connectedness between human beings” (Bowlby 1969, p. 194) and concluded that 
“what is believed to be essential for mental  health is that the infant and young child 
should experience a warm, intimate, and continuous relationship with his mother (or 
permanent mother substitute) in which both find satisfaction and enjoyment” (Bowlby 
1951, p. 11). 
Caregivers promote development of secure attachment in their infant by meeting the 
infant’s needs with reasonable reliability and warmth. Similarly, following the infant’s 
lead in terms of engagement, with the appropriate amount of eye contact, expressivity 
of the face, and tolerance of the infant’s negative emotions, is a critical component of 
the caregiver’s role in this process (Sroufe 1985). Many parents have an expectation, 
reinforced by social myths, that bonding happens the instant the infant is placed on the 
mother’s chest after delivery or makes eye co ntact. In reality, the process of attach -
ment and bonding is much more nuanced and can change over time. This is important 
because it allows for interventions that can address any difficulty that may be present 
initially or develop over time.

Several concepts related to attachment and development of an optimal relationship 
between parents and infants are important in understanding the parent–infant relation-
ship. The first concept is infant self-regulation. When caregivers respond promptly to an 
infant’s distress, the infants adjust without needing to escalate their demands. In turn, 
this makes it easier for infants to self-soothe and self-regulate because their level of dis-
tress experienced is not severe. Thus, the ability to self-soothe and self-regulate is the 
result of a healthy attachment with a responsive caregiver, through a process of coreg-
ulation. Through repeated interactions with emotionally available caregivers, infants 
not only begin to learn how to manage basic biological rhythms (e.g., sleeping, waking, 
eating) but also develop early self-control through emotional regulation and negotia-
tion. Sameroff and Fiese (2000) proposed that, at birth, infants are primarily dependent 
on the “other” or primary caregiver for maintaining self-regulation. As the infants ma-
ture, they become increasingly capable and responsible for their own self-regulation, 
but they continue to function within the context of coregulation with significant others 
into adulthood and throughout life (Sameroff and Fiese 2000). 
The second concept is infant temperament. Broadly speaking, temperament refers to 
individual differences in emotional and motor reactivity that are mostly stable across 
situations and over time (Rothbart and Derryberry 1981). Processes that serve to mod-
ulate reactivity include attentional control, attention shifting, and inhibitory control; 
these processes serve as the basis for self-regulation (Gartstein and Skinner 2018). In 
the past, temperament was described as a fixed, “constitutional” set of traits and was 
thought to be a highly heritable platform for social and emotional development (Lewis 
et al. 2015). However, recent research has highlighted the dynamic processes involved 
in infant temperament formation, including such factors as the epigenetic effects of 
stress and the impact of maternal mental health disorders (Gartstein and Skinner 2018; 
Lewis et al. 2015). Variations in infant temperament highlight the importance of noting 
what infants bring to the relationship and the effects of that on the parents. This also 
leads to the concept of goodness of fit between a parent and an infant; a parent might be 
able to more easily establish an optimal coregulatory process and bond with one child 
but find another child more challenging, in part due to differences in their tempera -
ment (Chess and Thomas 1991). 
Our understanding of attachment as it relates to the parent–infant relationship has 
developed throughout the past several decades. Some early pioneers of this work were 
Mary Ainsworth and colleagues, who created a research method called the Strange Sit-
uation Protocol (SSP) to provide a standardized way of assessing toddlers’ attachment 
to a given caretaker (Ainsworth and Bell 1970). The SSP involves observing toddlers’ 
behavior in several different laboratory settings: with the caregiver, with the caregiver 
and a stranger, with the stranger alone, entirely alone, and upon reunion with the care-
giver. Based on the toddlers’ behavior in these settings—their reaction to separation 
and reunion with their primary caregiver—thei r attachment style to that caregiver can 
be described. Ainsworth and colleagues categorized attachment into two overarching 
styles: secure and insecure, with two bran ches of insecure or anxious attachment 
(avoidant and ambivalent/resistant) (Ainsworth and Bell 1970; Ainsworth et al. 1978).
