orders.” Although studies of first-generation antipsychotics have not shown positive 
results, a recent study of olanzapine showed a statistically significant increase in BMI 
over time compared with placebo (Attia et al. 2019). Despite improvement in weight,

no group differences were found in Yale-Brown Obsessive Compulsive Scale, Center

635 Eating Disorders 
Studies of other second-generation antipsychotics, including risperidone, queti -
apine, and aripiprazole, have shown mixed results, with olanzapine remaining the 
agent with the most consistent evidence for efficacy in AN (Davis and Attia 2017). Case 
reports have also described the use of aripiprazole in BN (Trunko et al. 2011), although 
other second-generation antipsychotics may be avoided by clinicians due to concerns 
that the side effect of appetite induction could contribute to binge episodes. 
Antiepileptics 
Topiramate has been shown to reduce both bingeing and purging in individuals with 
BN (Hoopes et al. 2003), although its associ ation with weight loss can inhibit its use 
in some individuals for whom weight loss may be contraindicated. Topiramate can 
also be used as treatment for BED, with demonstrated reductions in binge eating fre-
quency and weight that appear to sustain with longer-term use (McElroy et al. 2004). 
It has also been trialed in combination wi th phentermine in treatment of BED, with 
limited studies showing cessation of binge eating and significant weight loss (Davis 
and Attia 2017). However, initiation of these medications during pregnancy requires 
a risk-risk discussion given the potential im pact on both mother and fetus, particu -
