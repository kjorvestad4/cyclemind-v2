# Substance Use in the Perinatal Period

> **Related:** [[Perinatal-Depression]] · [[Trauma-Reproductive-Health]] · [[Postpartum-Depression]]

---

## Overview

Substance use disorders (SUDs) are among the most common and under-recognized complications of pregnancy. They frequently co-occur with depression, anxiety, and trauma — and require integrated, non-judgmental care.

**Key principle:** Stigma and criminalization prevent women from seeking care. Judgmental providers create dangerous barriers. Compassionate, evidence-based, non-punitive care saves lives and improves outcomes for both mother and baby.

---

## Prevalence in Pregnancy

| Substance | Prevalence in Pregnancy |
|---|---|
| Alcohol | ~10% report any use; 5% report frequent use |
| Cannabis | ~7% (increasing as legalization expands) |
| Opioids | ~0.5–1% opioid use disorder; much higher in some regions |
| Tobacco/Nicotine | ~7–8% |
| Stimulants | ~0.5% |
| Benzodiazepines (non-prescribed) | <1% |

## Screening

### Universal, Non-Judgmental Screening
ACOG recommends **universal screening** at first prenatal visit using a validated tool.

**Validated Screening Tools:**
- **4P's Plus** — simple 4-question screen: Parents, Partner, Past, Present use
- **AUDIT-C** — alcohol use (3 items)
- **CRAFFT** — adolescents (Car, Relax, Alone, Forget, Friends, Trouble)
- **NIDA Quick Screen** — any substance, 4 questions

### Framing Screening
Use non-stigmatizing language:
- ✅ "As part of routine prenatal care, we ask everyone about substance use. This helps us give you the best care."
- ❌ "You don't use any drugs, do you?"

## Alcohol

### No Safe Level
There is no established safe level of alcohol in pregnancy. Fetal Alcohol Spectrum Disorders (FASD) are entirely preventable and entirely caused by prenatal alcohol exposure.

### Clinical Management
- Brief intervention (5–15 min) reduces drinking in ~30% of pregnant patients
- For AUD: medication-assisted treatment options are limited in pregnancy
  - Naltrexone: limited data; generally avoided
  - Acamprosate: limited data
  - Benzodiazepines for acute withdrawal: short-term use in monitored setting
- Thiamine supplementation: essential in heavy drinkers
- Referral to substance use treatment program

---

## Cannabis

### Increasing Use, Insufficient Safety Data
Cannabis use has doubled in pregnant women over the past decade. Many women use it for nausea — often without knowing risks.

**Known risks:**
- Low birth weight, preterm birth associations
- Neurodevelopmental effects in offspring (emerging evidence)
- THC passes into breast milk

**Clinical approach:**
- Counsel to stop: no known safe level in pregnancy or breastfeeding
- Address underlying nausea: ginger, B6 + doxylamine, ondansetron (caution in first trimester)
- Non-judgmental inquiry: "I ask everyone — are you using cannabis for any symptoms?"

## Opioid Use Disorder (OUD)

### Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT) Is the Standard of Care
Abrupt opioid cessation in pregnancy is dangerous — can cause fetal distress and pregnancy loss. MAT is recommended.

| Medication | Use in Pregnancy | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Buprenorphine** (Subutex, Suboxone) | ✅ First-line | Safe, effective; lower NAS risk than methadone |
| **Methadone** | ✅ Alternative | Requires daily clinic visit; effective and well-studied |
| **Naltrexone** | ⚠️ Limited data | Some use post-detox; limited pregnancy data |

**Neonatal Abstinence Syndrome (NAS):**
- Occurs in 50–80% of buprenorphine/methadone-exposed infants
- NAS is manageable and does not cause permanent harm when properly treated
- Breastfeeding reduces NAS severity and duration (encourage if no other contraindications)

### Psychosocial Support is Essential
- Case management, housing stability, domestic violence screening
- Trauma-informed care (high trauma prevalence in OUD)
- Peer support specialists with lived experience

---

## Mental Health Comorbidities

Almost all SUDs in pregnancy co-occur with mental health conditions:
- 80% of women with OUD have trauma history
- 40–60% of perinatal SUD patients have comorbid depression or anxiety
- Integrated treatment (SUD + mental health + obstetrics) has better outcomes than siloed care

### Preferred Approach: Integrated Perinatal Care
- OB/midwife + addiction medicine + psychiatry + social work in same clinic or coordinated team
- Case management for housing, food security, childcare
- Trauma-informed care at every touchpoint

## Luna's Role

Luna approaches substance use without judgment:
- Will not lecture, shame, or moralize about substance use
- Provides accurate, compassionate information on risks and resources
- Immediately provides crisis resources if any safety concern is raised
- Encourages connection with prenatal care provider — framed as a support, not a threat
- Knows that asking is not reporting — help-seeking protects mother and baby

Luna will NOT:
- Suggest a specific medication for SUD management
- Provide detox guidance
- Make any statements that could deter a woman from seeking prenatal care
