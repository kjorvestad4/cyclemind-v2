# Perinatal Depression

> **Related:** [[Postpartum-Depression]] · [[Postpartum-Psychosis]] · [[Pregnancy-Anxiety-Disorders]] · [[Bipolar-Disorder-Reproductive-Context]] · [[Integrative-Perinatal-Depression]]

---

## Definition
Perinatal depression encompasses **major and minor depressive episodes** occurring during pregnancy (antenatal) or within the first year after delivery (postpartum). It is the most common serious complication of childbearing.

---

## Epidemiology
- Affects **10–15%** during pregnancy; up to **20%** postpartum
- One of the most common and **under-recognized** obstetric complications
- All socioeconomic groups affected; rates higher in underserved populations
- Paternal perinatal depression affects ~10% of fathers

---

## Risk Factors

### Strongest Predictors
- History of depression or anxiety (before or during pregnancy)
- Depression or anxiety **during the current pregnancy**
- History of PMDD or severe PMS

### Additional Risk Factors
- Stressful life events; lack of social support
- Intimate partner violence or abuse history — see [[Trauma-Reproductive-Health]]
- Unintended pregnancy
- Previous pregnancy loss or infertility
- Poverty, housing instability
- Adolescent age
- Thyroid dysfunction

---

## Screening

### When to Screen
- **At least once** during pregnancy (first trimester and third trimester recommended)
- At **postpartum visit** (2–6 weeks)
- Opportunistically at any prenatal visit if concern arises

### Gold Standard: Edinburgh Postnatal Depression Scale (EPDS)
- 10-item self-report questionnaire
- Validated in pregnancy AND postpartum
- Score interpretation:
  - **≥10:** Possible depression — further evaluation needed
  - **≥13:** Likely depression — refer to mental health provider
  - **Question 10 (self-harm) score >0:** Immediate safety assessment required

### Additional Tools
- PHQ-9 — widely used; validated in pregnancy
- GAD-7 — for concurrent anxiety screening

---

## Clinical Presentation
- Persistent sadness, emptiness, hopelessness
- Loss of interest in activities, difficulty bonding
- Excessive guilt ("I'm a bad mother")
- Fatigue beyond normal pregnancy/newborn demands
- Sleep disturbance (beyond infant wake-ups)
- Appetite changes
- Difficulty concentrating, indecisiveness
- In severe cases: suicidal ideation, psychotic features

---

## Pathophysiology
- Rapid estrogen and progesterone withdrawal postpartum
- HPA-axis dysregulation; elevated cortisol
- Serotonergic system changes
- Inflammatory cytokines elevated
- Thyroid dysfunction (postpartum thyroiditis) can mimic or co-occur

---

## Treatment

### Mild-Moderate Symptoms — First-Line
- **Psychotherapy** (preferred when accessible):
  - **CBT** — gold standard; restructures negative thought patterns
  - **IPT (Interpersonal Psychotherapy)** — addresses role transitions, grief, relationship changes
- Support groups, peer support

### Moderate-Severe Symptoms
- **SSRIs** — first-line pharmacologic treatment
  - **Sertraline:** most studied in pregnancy and lactation; preferred
  - **Escitalopram:** excellent tolerability
  - Most SSRIs considered relatively safe; risk-benefit discussion essential
- Combined therapy (SSRI + CBT/IPT) superior to either alone

### Severe/Refractory
- SNRIs (venlafaxine, duloxetine)
- Brexanolone (IV; FDA-approved specifically for PPD — use in hospital setting)
- Zuranolone (oral; FDA-approved for PPD)
- ECT — safe in pregnancy; rapid and highly effective for severe cases

---

## Untreated Depression: Risks to Mother & Baby
- Preterm birth
- Low birth weight
- Impaired maternal-infant bonding
- Suboptimal prenatal care adherence
- Child neurodevelopmental concerns
- Maternal suicide (leading cause of peripartum mortality)
