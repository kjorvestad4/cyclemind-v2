# Trauma & Reproductive Health

> **Related:** [[Perinatal-Depression]] · [[Postpartum-Anxiety]] · [[Pregnancy-Anxiety-Disorders]] · [[Pregnancy-Loss-Grief]]

---

## Overview

Trauma — particularly interpersonal and sexual trauma — is both more common in women and uniquely intersects with reproductive health. The reproductive lifespan (gynecologic exams, pregnancy, childbirth, postpartum) creates repeated opportunities for trauma to be activated, recognized, or exacerbated.

---

## Types of Trauma Relevant to Reproductive Health

| Trauma Type | Reproductive Health Intersection |
|---|---|
| **Childhood sexual abuse (CSA)** | Gynecologic avoidance, dyspareunia, birth trauma risk, dissociation during exams |
| **Sexual assault / rape** | All of the above; pregnancy possible; STI trauma |
| **Intimate partner violence (IPV)** | Coercive reproduction, pregnancy complications, postpartum risk |
| **Birth trauma** | PTSD after delivery; common, under-recognized |
| **Medical trauma** | Difficult NICU stays, obstetric emergencies, failed procedures |
| **Pregnancy loss** | See [[Pregnancy-Loss-Grief]] |
| **Infertility treatment** | Repeated invasive procedures, loss experiences |

---

## Prevalence

- **1 in 3** women worldwide have experienced physical or sexual IPV
- **1 in 6** women have experienced attempted or completed rape
- Birth trauma PTSD affects **6–8%** of all postpartum women; up to **30%** report distressing birth experiences
- Prior trauma history is present in **30–40%** of OB/GYN patients

---

## Birth Trauma

### What Constitutes Birth Trauma
Birth trauma is subjective — it is defined by the mother's experience, not by objective medical severity. A routine birth may be traumatic for one woman; an objectively complicated birth may not be traumatic for another.

**Common traumatic elements:**
- Loss of control over one's body during delivery
- Feeling unheard, dismissed, or violated during care
- Emergency procedures without consent or adequate explanation
- Prolonged or painful labor
- Infant in distress; NICU admission
- Feeling near death or believing baby was dying

### Birth Trauma PTSD
**Prevalence:** 3–4% DSM-5 PTSD; 30% subclinical PTSD symptoms at 1 month postpartum

**Symptoms:**
- Flashbacks of delivery (intrusive re-experiencing)
- Nightmares about birth
- Avoidance of birth-related topics, hospitals, subsequent pregnancy
- Hyperarousal: startle response, hypervigilance, difficulty sleeping
- Emotional numbing; difficulty bonding with baby

**Assessment:** PCL-5 (PTSD Checklist); City Birth Trauma Scale (City BiTS) specifically validated for birth trauma

## Trauma-Informed Obstetric Care

### Core Principles (SAMHSA Framework)
1. **Safety** — physical and psychological safety in the clinical environment
2. **Trustworthiness and transparency** — clear communication about every procedure
3. **Peer support** — survivor-led support
4. **Collaboration and mutuality** — power-sharing in care decisions
5. **Empowerment and choice** — patient agency maximized at every step
6. **Cultural humility** — recognizing identity dimensions

### Practical Trauma-Informed Strategies for OB Care
- Ask permission before every physical exam: "Is it okay if I examine you now?"
- Explain every step before touching: "I'm going to apply the speculum now"
- Establish a stop signal patients can use at any time
- Allow support person of patient's choosing at all appointments
- Birth plan for women with trauma history: document communication preferences, pelvic exam consent procedures, and delivery preferences
- Debrief after delivery — offer this to all patients, not just those with known trauma

## IPV Screening and Response

### Why Reproductive Health Providers Must Screen
- IPV is most common in women of reproductive age
- Pregnancy does not protect against IPV — rates actually increase during pregnancy
- Coercive reproduction (reproductive coercion): controlling contraception, forcing pregnancy, or forced abortion — common in IPV relationships

### Screening
- **ACOG recommends universal IPV screening** at first prenatal visit, each trimester, and postpartum
- Use validated tools: **HITS** (Hurt, Insult, Threaten, Scream) or **AAS** (Abuse Assessment Screen)
- Screen **alone** — never with partner present

### Safety Planning
If IPV disclosed:
1. Document in chart (with patient permission)
2. Provide **National DV Hotline:** 1-800-799-7233 / thehotline.org
3. Safety planning: safe places, items to take, who to call
4. Do not pressure leaving — leaving is the most dangerous time

## Evidence-Based Treatment

| Intervention | Indication | Evidence |
|---|---|---|
| **EMDR (Eye Movement Desensitization and Reprocessing)** | Birth trauma PTSD, sexual trauma | Strong — first-line for PTSD |
| **CPT (Cognitive Processing Therapy)** | PTSD, guilt, self-blame | Strong |
| **TF-CBT (Trauma-Focused CBT)** | PTSD, childhood trauma | Strong |
| **Prolonged Exposure (PE)** | PTSD | Strong — may need modification for perinatal patients |
| **Birth debriefing** | Distressing birth, birth trauma prevention | Moderate — helpful when offered promptly |
| **EMDR Perinatal Protocol** | Birth trauma specifically | Emerging — promising early data |
| **SSRIs** | PTSD + comorbid depression | Sertraline first-line in perinatal |

## Luna's Role

Luna will:
- Acknowledge disclosures of trauma with full empathy and without judgment
- Provide crisis resources immediately if any safety concern is raised
- Never probe for trauma details — let the user share what they choose
- Explain birth trauma is real and common — normalize and validate
- Suggest appropriate professional resources (therapists specializing in perinatal trauma, EMDR)
- Never minimize: "at least you have a healthy baby" is never appropriate
