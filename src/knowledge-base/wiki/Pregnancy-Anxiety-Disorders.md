# Anxiety Disorders & Insomnia in the Perinatal Period

> **Related:** [[Perinatal-Depression]] · [[Postpartum-OCD]] · [[Trauma-Reproductive-Health]] · [[Integrative-Perinatal-Depression]] · [[Prenatal-Stress]]

---

## Epidemiology
- Anxiety disorders affect **15–20%** of pregnant and postpartum women
- Often **comorbid with depression** — screen for both simultaneously
- **Insomnia** affects up to 75% of pregnant women; major risk factor for perinatal depression

---

## Anxiety Disorders in the Perinatal Period

### Generalized Anxiety Disorder (GAD)
- Excessive, uncontrollable worry about multiple topics
- Pregnancy-related worries (fetal health, birth, parenting ability) can amplify GAD
- **Screening:** GAD-7 (score ≥10 indicates moderate-severe)
- **Treatment:** CBT first-line; SSRIs for moderate-severe

### Panic Disorder
- Recurrent unexpected panic attacks + anticipatory anxiety
- May worsen in pregnancy due to physiologic changes (dyspnea, palpitations)
- Important to distinguish from cardiac/pulmonary causes
- **Treatment:** CBT (interoceptive exposure); SSRIs

### Social Anxiety Disorder
- Marked fear of social situations; may affect prenatal care engagement
- Underdiagnosed in perinatal period

### PTSD in Perinatal Period
- Prior trauma (childhood, sexual, IPV) may be triggered by pregnancy, gynecologic exams, or birth
- Birth trauma can precipitate new-onset PTSD
- See [[Trauma-Reproductive-Health]]
- **Treatment:** Trauma-focused CBT (TF-CBT, CPT, EMDR); SSRIs

### Perinatal OCD
- See [[Postpartum-OCD]] for full coverage
- Harm obsessions related to infant are hallmark of perinatal OCD

## Perinatal Insomnia

### Prevalence & Impact
- Worsens with each trimester; peaks in third trimester and postpartum
- **Sleep disruption → depression and anxiety risk** (bidirectional relationship)
- Hormonal changes, physical discomfort, fetal movement, nocturia all contribute

### CBT-I (Cognitive Behavioral Therapy for Insomnia) — First-Line ⭐
Key CBT-I components adapted for pregnancy:
1. **Sleep restriction** — consolidate sleep to increase efficiency (modified in pregnancy)
2. **Stimulus control** — bed for sleep only; get up if awake >20 min
3. **Sleep hygiene** — consistent schedule, dark/cool room, limit caffeine
4. **Cognitive restructuring** — address catastrophic beliefs about sleep
5. **Relaxation techniques** — progressive muscle relaxation, guided imagery

### Pharmacologic Options for Insomnia in Pregnancy
| Agent | Safety | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Doxylamine (Unisom) | Generally safe | First-line OTC; also antiemetic |
| Diphenhydramine | Generally safe | Short-term; tolerance develops |
| Zolpidem/eszopiclone | Avoid if possible | Limited data; use only if necessary |
| Benzodiazepines | Avoid | Neonatal sedation/withdrawal risk |
| Melatonin | Limited data | Low dose (0.5–1 mg); consult provider |

## Treatment Summary

### Non-Pharmacologic (Always First-Line)
- **CBT** for anxiety (individual or group; telehealth effective)
- **CBT-I** for insomnia
- Mindfulness-based stress reduction (MBSR/MBCT)
- Exercise — see [[Integrative-Perinatal-Depression]]
- Relaxation training

### Pharmacologic
- **SSRIs** — first-line for moderate-severe anxiety disorders in pregnancy
  - Sertraline preferred (most safety data)
  - Avoid paroxetine (if possible) due to CYP2D6 interactions and discontinuation risk
- **SNRIs** (venlafaxine, duloxetine) — alternative to SSRIs
- **Buspirone** — limited pregnancy data; option for GAD when SSRIs not tolerated
- **Benzodiazepines** — use with caution; short-term only; avoid in first trimester if possible

## Postpartum Insomnia
- Sleep disruption from infant care is normal; clinical insomnia is when sleep is poor WHEN baby is asleep
- CBT-I remains first-line
- Treat underlying depression/anxiety if present
- Partner involvement in nighttime care can help
